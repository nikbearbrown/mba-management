### Figure 2: Comparison Chart — Reward Type vs. Reinforcement Effect

**Location:** Concept 1, section on "Common misconception: 'Reinforcement is just a reward'"

**Purpose:** Show that a "reward" (supervisor praise, bonus, autonomy) can be a true reinforcer or a non-event, depending on whether it increases behavior.

**Visual concept:**
- Two columns: "Intention: Reward" vs. "Effect: Reinforcer?"
- Rows showing:
  - $500 annual bonus paid once | Does not reinforce if employee already assumed it was one-time
  - Supervisor says "That's interesting" after every idea suggestion | May reinforce if employee values recognition and frequency is right
  - Immediate praise for on-time arrival | Reinforces if employee values the recognition; may not if praise is generic
  - $5/hour bonus for suggestions | May not reinforce if bonus is small relative to salary and payment is delayed

**Design note:** Emphasizes the definition test: *Does the behavior increase?* If no, not a reinforcer.

**Location:** Concept 1, section on "The mechanism: Drive, Habit, and Reinforcement"

**Visual concept:**

### Figure 4: Extinction Curve — Behavior Over Time Under Different Schedules

**Location:** Concept 2, section on "Why do some people keep trying when others give up?"

**Visual concept:**
- Line graph with time on x-axis (weeks), behavior frequency on y-axis
- Four lines showing extinction patterns:
  1. Continuous reinforcement: rapid rise, then sharp drop when reinforcement stops
  2. Fixed-ratio: high plateau, then sharp drop
  3. Variable-interval: steady rise, gradual fade when reinforcement stops
  4. Variable-ratio: highest plateau, slowest fade when reinforcement stops

**Design note:** The "persistence" visual that justifies why variable-ratio is used (and problematic). The gap between variable-ratio and continuous shows what persistence buys.

### Figure 5: The Behavior-Modification Five-Step Process (Flowchart)

**Location:** Concept 3, section on "The mechanism: Behavior Modification at work"

**Visual concept:**
- Flow diagram:
  1. Define desired behavior (objective, measurable)
  2. Measure baseline performance
  3. Set achievable goal
  4. Provide feedback and reinforce
  5. Gradually increase standard (shaping)
- Feedback loop: return to step 4 until goal is reached, then advance to step 5

**Design note:** Shows the iterative nature of OBM — it is not one-and-done but a cycle that tightens standards over time.

### Figure 6: The Trade-off Matrix — External vs. Intrinsic Motivation

**Location:** Concept 3, section on "The trade-off: External control vs. intrinsic motivation"

**Visual concept:**
- 2x2 matrix:
  - Axes: "External reinforcement present" (yes/no) × "Task is already engaging" (yes/no)
  - Quadrant 1 (yes/yes): Crowding-out risk — external reward may decrease intrinsic drive
  - Quadrant 2 (yes/no): Safe — external reward does not crowd anything out
  - Quadrant 3 (no/yes): Pure intrinsic — task is engaging, no external reward
  - Quadrant 4 (no/no): Low motivation — boring task, no external reward

**Design note:** Helps readers diagnose when external reinforcement is safe vs. risky.
