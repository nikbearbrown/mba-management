# Bookmap: Chapter 3 — Perception and Job Attitudes

## How the chapter uses source material

This bookmap documents how the Feynman-voice conversion used, reworked, reframed, and departed from the source material (the six OpenStax modules covering perception, attribution, job attitudes, and organizational commitment).

---

## Source sections and how they were reframed

### Source: "Learning Outcomes" → Chapter: "Learning objectives"

**What changed**: Restated in active, outcome-driven language.

**Source phrasing**: "After reading this chapter, you should be able to answer these questions: How do differences in perception affect employee behavior and performance?"

**Chapter phrasing**: "Explain how the perceptual process works: selectivity, organization, and interpretation."

**Why**: Learning objectives should name what the student will do, not what questions they'll answer. "Explain" is action. "Answer the question" is passive.

### Source: Chaz-and-James scenario → Chapter: Sarah cold-open

**What changed**: Complete replacement of the opening scenario.

**What the source had**: Two employees (Chaz and James) with different work styles. Chaz works extra hours; James sees it as "kissing up." Manager Jerry facilitates understanding.

**What the chapter has**: Sarah leads a major project, delivers results, but her boss Martin files her as "detail-oriented" rather than "builder." The consequence is systematic underestimation that will shape her opportunities and become self-fulfilling.

**Why**: The source scenario was too neat — it resolved when they communicated. The chapter's scenario is about a deeper problem: how perception bias creates prophecies. It also foregrounds the actual risk (career trajectory) rather than team harmony.

**Connection to method**: The cold-open is a puzzle ("how does Sarah's invisible work become invisible?") not a lesson preview. Reader wants to know what mechanisms are at work.

### Source: Definition of perception → Chapter: "The perceptual process at work" section

**What changed**: Same definition but grounded in a specific scenario first.

**Source structure**: "By perception, we mean the process by which one screens, selects, organizes, and interprets stimuli..."

**Chapter structure**: Opens with the factory-floor managers example (one focused on machine, one on worker), then names: "Perception is not a recording. It is a construction."

**Why**: Concrete first, then abstraction. This is Attenborough style — the example lets readers feel the mechanism before the vocabulary names it.

### Source: "Perceptual Selectivity" section with external and internal influences → Chapter: "External selectivity" and "Internal selectivity" 

**What changed**: Same content, reorganized and simplified.

**Source structure**: Extensive discussion of physical properties, dynamic properties, response salience, response disposition. Seven distinct concepts with technical terminology.

**Chapter structure**: Grouped into two big ideas: External (what's in the world) and Internal (what you need). Dropped the jargon; kept the mechanism.

**Examples**: Source used abstract examples ("loud noise attracts attention"). Chapter used concrete workplace examples (salesperson outpacing the team; worker noticing the clock).

**Why**: Fewer concepts, clearer. Response salience becomes "your immediate needs shape what you notice." Response disposition becomes "your past experience shapes what you notice." The action is the same; the words are plainer.

### Source: Playing-card study (Bruner and Postman) → Chapter: Brief mention with the mechanism

**What changed**: Source gave full study; chapter simplified and reframed.

**Source**: "In one study, a group of individuals was presented with a set of playing cards with the colors and symbols reversed... individuals consistently described the cards as they expected them to be..."

**Chapter**: "The classic example: researchers showed people playing cards with reversed colors... The brain overrides the sensory input and reports the familiar version."

**Why**: The detail isn't the mechanism. The mechanism is: your brain is so committed to the model it built that it reports the model, not the input. That's one sentence. The study is evidence, not the point.

### Source: Social perception section with three influences → Chapter: Reorganized as part of Concept 1 synthesis

**What changed**: Source had extended section on characteristics of perceived person, characteristics of situation, characteristics of perceiver. Chapter folded this into the selectivity framework.

**Why**: These aren't three new ideas; they're examples of what factors influence what you notice (selectivity) and how you organize it. Keeping them as a separate section creates redundancy.

### Source: Stereotyping, selective perception, perceptual defense → Chapter: "Barriers to accurate perception" framework

**What changed**: Source gave definitions and examples. Chapter kept definitions but spent more emphasis on stereotyping and selective perception; deprioritized perceptual defense.

**Why**: Stereotyping and selective perception are more actionable for a manager. Perceptual defense is real (people distort information that threatens them) but it's harder to interrupt. The chapter assumes the reader is trying to *see* better, not trying to get people to see what threatens them.

**Added**: Worked example (Chen's hiring decision) showing how implicit bias shapes stereotype.

### Source: Attribution theory section → Chapter: Concept 2

**What changed**: Source gave Heider and Kelley's theory with diagrams and the golf-tournament example. Chapter kept the same structure but added worked examples and made the two biases the centerpiece.

**Why**: Attribution theory is important, but the chapter's claim is that attribution biases matter more than the theory. The theory is accurate but people use it imperfectly and in biased ways. The chapter focuses on what actually happens.

### Source: Fundamental attribution error and self-serving bias → Chapter: "Two biases that trap you"

**What changed**: Source described them separately. Chapter grouped them as a system that distorts judgment.

**Source structure**: Separate sections. Brief explanation of each.

**Chapter structure**: Titled as a system ("two biases that trap you"). Showed how they work together to create asymmetry: others' failures are their fault; your failures are the situation's fault. Others' successes are the situation; yours are your ability.

**Added**: Worked example (software team missing ship date) showing all three attributions at once.

### Source: Attitudes definition and components → Chapter: Concept 3 opening

**What changed**: Same definition but emphasized that satisfaction is one attitude among several.

**Source structure**: Attitude is defined, then three components (cognitive, affective, intentional) are explained, then branches into job involvement, organizational commitment, job satisfaction.

**Chapter structure**: Opens with Sarah's invisibility. "Satisfaction is not the same as commitment. It is not the same as engagement." Then defines all four (satisfaction, involvement, commitment, engagement) as distinct.

**Why**: Organizations often conflate these. The chapter's whole point is to **distinguish** them, not just define them.

### Source: Job satisfaction measurement section → Chapter: Condensed; emphasis on satisfaction-performance gap

**What changed**: Source gave extensive detail on the Minnesota Satisfaction Questionnaire, rating scales, norms, validity. Chapter simplified.

**Source structure**: "By far the most common means of assessing satisfaction is the rating scale. Several job satisfaction scales exist. One of the most popular is the Minnesota Satisfaction Questionnaire..."

**Chapter structure**: "Measurement of job satisfaction... the most common method is the rating scale. Extensive normative data exist. However..." Then discusses drawbacks (assumes willing/able reporters, assumes common interpretation).

**Why**: The chapter doesn't care about *how* to measure satisfaction. It cares about **what organizations miss by measuring satisfaction**.

**Added**: The satisfaction-performance correlation (r ≈ 0.30) as the central finding. Not in source material explicitly; derived from Locke, Porter & Steers, and contemporary research.

### Source: Southwest Airlines case → Chapter: Worked into Concept 3

**What changed**: Source gave the Southwest case in a sidebar, then asked reflective questions. Chapter moved it into the main text and used it to illustrate the path-forward mechanism.

**Source use**: Sidebar: "If you've ever flown on Southwest... [tells the story]. What considerations should Southwest take? What would you suggest to a new company?"

**Chapter use**: "Southwest Airlines is the canonical case. They consistently rank high on both satisfaction and retention... The path is visible."

**Why**: This case is evidence for a specific mechanism: commitment is driven by visible growth paths, not by satisfaction. Source treated it as an interesting example. Chapter treats it as the mechanism.

### Source: Three approaches to attitude formation → Chapter: Concept 3, attitude formation

**What changed**: Same three approaches (dispositional, situational, social-information-processing), but reframed as interacting, not competing.

**Source**: Three separate subsections. Each presents a view, each has evidence.

**Chapter**: "Both the dispositional and the social-information-processing views have merit... it's wise to recognize that... they interact..."

**Why**: The question is not "which is right?" It's "how much does each matter?" The chapter treats them as layers, not options.

### Source: Job involvement, organizational commitment, job satisfaction definitions → Chapter: Table 3.1 (four attitudes)

**What changed**: Source gave separate definitions in sequence. Chapter made a 4x4 table comparing satisfaction, involvement, commitment, engagement.

**Why**: The table makes visible the distinction. Same information, but the format says "these are different," not "these are all attitude concepts."

### Source: Behavioral justification and cognitive dissonance → Chapter: Brief mention in Concept 3

**What changed**: Source gave full section on cognitive consistency, Festinger, the manager-who-hates-his-job example. Chapter simplified to one sentence.

**Source example**: "A manager may hate his job but be required to work long hours... to become cognitively consistent, he can... change his attitude toward the job to a more positive one..."

**Chapter**: One mention in the attitude-formation section: "Attitudes reflect partly your genuine experience and partly the social signals you're receiving."

**Why**: Cognitive dissonance is real and matters, but it's not central to this chapter's mechanism. The chapter focuses on perception → attribution → attitude, not on attitude change through cognitive dissonance. Including it would dilute focus.

### Source: Five dimensions of job satisfaction → Chapter: Listed but not expanded

**What changed**: Source gave detailed section on work itself, pay, promotion, supervision, coworkers. Chapter mentioned them in passing.

**Why**: These dimensions are useful for measurement (what to ask in a survey), but they're not central to the chapter's mechanism. The chapter's claim is that *satisfaction itself* is the wrong metric, not that we're measuring it wrong. Including the dimensions would suggest the chapter is about better measurement, when it's really about different metrics.

### Source: Chapter review questions and exercises → Chapter: Graduated exercises (warm-up, application, synthesis)

**What changed**: Completely rewritten.

**Source structure**: Multiple choice and short-answer questions about definitions and facts. Then decision exercises and a case study.

**Chapter structure**: Nine graduated exercises moving from noticing the machinery (warm-up) to designing interventions (synthesis).

**Why**: This chapter is about seeing the mechanism at work, then using it. The exercises train the perception, not the memory.

## Ideas harvested from source material (for future chapters)

### Mechanisms and frameworks useful in other chapters

1. **Cognitive complexity** — The idea that people differ in how many dimensions they use to think about other people. High cognitive complexity (multiple traits) predicts more accurate perception. This could connect to Chapter 2 (individual differences) or Chapter 12 (leadership).

2. **Impression management** — Source mentioned how people manage impressions of themselves. This connects deeply to Chapter 13 (power and politics) and Chapter 12 (leadership as about managing impressions).

3. **In-group bias and social identity** — Source touched on this; it's foundational to Chapter 9 (group dynamics) and Chapter 5 (diversity).

4. **The role of feedback loops** — Source didn't explicitly name this, but the Pygmalion mechanism and the self-fulfilling prophecy are about feedback loops. This is important for Chapter 8 (performance appraisal) and Chapter 16 (organizational change).

### Angles for other chapters

1. **Chapter 6 (Perception and Managerial Decision Making)** uses many of these same heuristics and biases but in the context of problem-solving and choice, not person-perception. Could tie together.

2. **Chapter 8 (Performance Appraisal and Rewards)** could use the attribution framework: how do managers attribute performance differences? How do they avoid fundamental attribution error in evaluations?

3. **Chapter 12 (Leadership)** could use the Pygmalion mechanism: do leaders' expectations shape follower performance? That's a form of leadership influence.

### Research gaps worth noting

1. **Generalization of Pygmalion to adult organizational contexts**: The original study was in schools. How strong is the effect with adult employees who have more independent judgment?

2. **Reversibility of filed perceptions**: Once someone is filed as "detail-oriented," how hard is it to change that perception? What evidence would it take?

3. **The satisfaction-performance relationship in knowledge work vs. routine work**: Is the weak correlation the same across all job types, or is it stronger in jobs where discretionary effort matters more?

## How the chapter differs from the source textbook's approach

### Emphasis differences

**Source textbook emphasis**: How perception works (process and mechanism), how various barriers interfere with accuracy, how attributions are made

**This chapter emphasis**: How early perceptions become self-fulfilling prophecies, how attribution biases distort organizational decisions, how organizations measure the wrong attitude (satisfaction instead of commitment)

### Selectivity of content

**Source included but chapter deprioritized**:
- Detailed characteristics of perceived person (dress, verbal communication, nonverbal communication, ascribed attributes)
- Detailed external influences on perception (size, intensity, contrast, novelty, motion, repetition)
- Extensive discussion of perceptual defense
- Detailed measurement methods for job satisfaction

**Reason**: These are true but secondary. The chapter focuses on the mechanisms that shape opportunity and outcome.

### Audience adjustment

**Source textbook**: Neutral, encyclopedic coverage of the topic. Students learn the field's vocabulary and frameworks.

**This chapter**: Focused on managerial and personal decision-making. How does this mechanism affect what actually happens?

## What wasn't in the source material that the chapter added

### Novel worked examples:
1. Chen's hiring decision (showing stereotype in action)
2. Software team's missed ship date (showing attribution from multiple viewpoints)
3. New manager with five people (showing commitment-based intervention)

### Novel arguments:
1. The satisfaction-performance correlation is weak; organizations measure the wrong thing
2. Commitment and engagement are stronger predictors of retention than satisfaction
3. Visible growth paths are the strongest lever on commitment
4. The Pygmalion effect is the integrating mechanism across the whole chapter

### Novel frameworks:
1. Table comparing four attitudes (satisfaction, involvement, commitment, engagement) and what they predict
2. The self-fulfilling prophecy loop as the chapter's capstone diagram

### Research not in source:
1. The r ≈ 0.30 satisfaction-performance finding (from Locke, Porter & Steers meta-analyses)
2. Southwest case used mechanistically (not as feel-good example)
3. Three-component commitment model tied to actual behavior prediction
