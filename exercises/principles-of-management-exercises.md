## Chapter 01: Chapter 1 — Management and Organizational Behavior
*Source: `chapters/01-management-and-organizational-behavior.md`*

## Exercises

### Warm-up

**1. The Hawthorne experiment produced a result the researchers could not explain with their original hypothesis. Describe in your own words what the researchers expected to find, what they actually found, and why the discrepancy pointed toward a different independent variable. (Tests: understanding the observation effect and why the original hypothesis failed.)**

**2. Management is defined in this chapter as four activities: planning, organizing, directing, and controlling. A new restaurant opens. Identify one concrete example of each activity that the restaurant's owner-manager would perform in the first month. (Tests: mechanical application of the four-activity framework to a real context.)**

**3. Name the five functions of work described in this chapter. For each one, give a one-sentence example of a workplace condition that would satisfy it and a one-sentence example of a condition that would undermine it. (Tests: recall and basic application of the functions-of-work taxonomy.)**

---

### Application

**4. A mid-size software company promotes its best engineer to engineering manager. Six months later, the team's output has declined and two strong engineers have put in transfer requests. Using the three-skill framework (technical, human, conceptual), diagnose what has most likely gone wrong and what the company should have done differently at the point of promotion. (Tests: applying the skill-type framework to a realistic management failure.)**

**5. A manufacturing manager notices that her line workers seem disengaged — showing up on time but producing at minimum acceptable levels, not raising problems proactively, and not helping each other. She responds by increasing the performance bonus. Engagement does not improve. Using the five-functions-of-work framework, identify two functions other than the economic one that might be broken, and describe what evidence you would look for to test each hypothesis. (Tests: moving beyond the economic lever and using the taxonomy to generate alternative diagnoses.)**

**6. The human relations school was a genuine advance over scientific management, but it had a structural blind spot. Describe the blind spot precisely. Then identify a real workplace scenario — one you have experienced or observed — where a manager operating from pure human-relations assumptions would misread the situation. (Tests: understanding the limits of a school of thought, not just its contributions.)**

---

### Synthesis

**7. An employee in a marketing department has become increasingly disengaged over three months. Their manager has tried a direct conversation about performance, a small raise, and reassignment to a more interesting project — none of which produced lasting improvement. Apply the five-block model systematically: for each of the five blocks, write one specific hypothesis about what might be causing the disengagement and one question the manager could ask or one thing the manager could observe to test that hypothesis. (Tests: using the full diagnostic framework rather than defaulting to the individual block.)**

**8. Scientific management, human relations, systems thinking, contingency theory, and evidence-based management are presented in historical sequence. But they are not just history — each school's assumptions still live in real organizations. Identify a management practice you have observed (in a job, a school, a sports team, any organized group) and trace which school's logic it reflects. Then identify which school would offer the strongest critique of that practice, and why. (Tests: connecting historical schools to present behavior and using them as analytical lenses.)**

---

### Challenge

**9. The chapter argues that management is hard not because planning and organizing are technically complex, but because "the thing being managed is people, and people respond to the whole situation they are in." A skeptic might respond: that is true of every social science — medicine, law, teaching — and we do not conclude from it that those fields are fundamentally different from technical disciplines. Is the skeptic right? Construct the strongest possible version of their argument, then identify where you think it fails. (Tests: holding the chapter's central claim up to genuine scrutiny rather than accepting it.)**

**10. Evidence-based management argues that organizations should base decisions on what research actually shows. The chapter gives MBTI as an example: widely used, essentially no predictive validity for job performance. Identify one other management practice — a hiring technique, a performance review format, a team-building intervention, a leadership model — that you suspect is more widely used than its evidence base justifies. What would a rigorous test of its effectiveness look like? What practical obstacles would prevent most organizations from running that test? (Tests: applying the evidence-based standard beyond the examples in the chapter and engaging with why the standard is hard to meet.)**

## Chapter 02: Chapter 2 — Individual and Cultural Differences
*Source: `chapters/02-individual-and-cultural-differences.md`*

## Exercises

### Warm-up: Seeing the machinery

1. **The ability ceiling.** Think of a skill you tried hard to develop and hit a wall. Describe the wall. Was it an ability limit (the capacity was not there), a motivation limit (you stopped pushing), or a situational limit (the environment changed)? How would you know the difference? What would a manager need to understand to respond correctly to each type of limit?

2. **Big Five self-read.** Place yourself on each of the five OCEAN dimensions — not where you wish you were, but where you actually observe yourself behaving. For the trait where your honest self-placement surprised you, name one work situation where that trait has caused you a problem you did not expect.

3. **Cultural script identification.** Name one assumption you carry about how authority should work in an organization — something you treat as obvious. Now name a culture, industry, or organization where that assumption would be considered wrong. What does the disagreement tell you about which part of the assumption is cultural and which part might be universal?

### Application: Hiring and team decisions

4. **Ability assessment design.** You are hiring for a project manager role at a company running complex software implementations. List the mental abilities and personality traits you believe matter most. For each, write one sentence describing how you would assess it in a structured process — not "ask if they are organized" but a specific method that produces comparable data across candidates.

5. **The MBTI temptation.** A colleague proposes using MBTI to sort job candidates before interviews. Write a two-paragraph response that (a) explains specifically why MBTI is not appropriate for this use and (b) proposes a validated alternative. Do not just say "use Big Five" — name what Big Five measures, why it predicts job performance, and how you would actually implement it.

6. **Cross-cultural friction diagnosis.** A manager from a low-uncertainty-avoidance background is leading a team that includes several people from high-uncertainty-avoidance cultures. The team keeps asking for more detailed procedures than the manager thinks are necessary. Using Hofstede's framework, explain what each side is experiencing and propose one concrete adjustment the manager could make that respects both cultural orientations.

### Synthesis: The complicated hire

7. **Ability, personality, and culture combined.** Return to the four-interviewer scenario from the opening. You are the hiring manager. Using the frameworks from this chapter, design a process that would reduce the influence of each interviewer's personal lens on the final decision. Be specific: what would you measure, when, and how? What would you do with the four written evaluations?

8. **Complementarity vs. fit.** A startup founder is building her first team. She is high in openness and low in conscientiousness. She keeps hiring people similar to herself. What problem is she building toward? What specific Big Five profile should she be looking for in her next two hires, and why? What is the risk of hiring for "cultural fit" when the founder's own profile has known gaps?

### Challenge: Where the frameworks break

9. **When structure is not enough.** The chapter argues that structured hiring processes reduce bias and improve prediction. But structured processes can also encode existing biases into their criteria. Describe a scenario where a well-designed ability assessment or Big Five screening process might systematically disadvantage a group of qualified candidates. How would you detect this? What would you do about it?

## Chapter 03: Chapter 3 — Perception and Job Attitudes
*Source: `chapters/03-perception-and-job-attitudes.md`*

## Exercises

### Warm-up

1. **The reversed-cards experiment, applied.** Think of a belief you held confidently about a colleague that turned out to be wrong. Which stage of the perceptual process was the most likely culprit — selection (you didn't notice the disconfirming evidence), organization (you filed it into the wrong category), or interpretation (you assigned the wrong meaning)? What would have had to happen differently for you to catch the error earlier? *(Tests: perceptual process — selection, organization, interpretation)*

2. **Three-factor quick check.** A team member delivers excellent work on a client presentation — the best the team has seen from her in six months. Using consensus, consistency, and distinctiveness, walk through what each factor would tell you and what attribution it points toward. What additional information would you want before acting on that attribution? *(Tests: three-factor attribution model)*

3. **Bias identification.** A project you led succeeded well beyond forecast. A project a peer led came in under forecast. Write one sentence explaining each outcome as you'd be likely to explain it naturally. Now identify which bias is operating in each sentence and rewrite both sentences with the bias corrected. *(Tests: self-serving bias, fundamental attribution error)*

### Application

4. **The filing problem.** You manage someone who has been categorized — by yourself or by a previous manager — as "not a strategic thinker." What would it concretely take to revise that perception? Name at least three things: one change in assignment, one change in how you interpret their contributions, one change in what you pay attention to. Be specific about what the revised-perception version of each would look like. *(Tests: perceptual organization, self-fulfilling prophecy, intervention points)*

5. **Satisfaction vs. commitment.** You survey your team and find that average satisfaction scores are high (4.1 out of 5) but two of your five direct reports have been quietly interviewing elsewhere. Using the distinctions between satisfaction, commitment, and engagement, explain how both things can be true simultaneously. What would you measure differently next time to catch this earlier? *(Tests: distinction between job attitudes, retention predictors)*

6. **The new hire experiment.** You hire someone into a team that has a collective negative view of the organization — they believe advancement is illusory and leadership doesn't listen. The new hire arrives with high energy and no prior experience of this culture. According to the social-information-processing model, what happens over the next six months if you do nothing? What specific intervention would disrupt the transmission of the group's attitude before it completes? *(Tests: social-information-processing model, attitude formation)*

### Synthesis

7. **Run the loop forward.** Pick a person you manage or have managed. Describe the early perception you formed of them — what category you filed them in — and trace the loop forward: what assignments did that perception lead to, what signal did those assignments send, how might that signal have shaped their commitment and engagement, and what performance outcome did it produce? Where in the loop was there an intervention point you didn't take? *(Tests: full perception → attribution → treatment → attitude → performance loop)*

8. **Design a de-biasing intervention.** A company runs a post-project retrospective and finds that project managers consistently attribute successes to their leadership and failures to external factors (client changes, market shifts). Design a structured retrospective format that would surface the fundamental attribution error and self-serving bias without requiring managers to consciously catch themselves. What questions would you add? What information would you force them to gather before they explain anything? *(Tests: attribution biases, intervention design)*

9. **The uncommitted high-performer.** You have an employee with high engagement (she cares deeply about the work, brings full focus, generates strong output) but low affective commitment (she has no particular loyalty to this organization, would leave for a better opportunity without much hesitation). Using the Three-Component Model of commitment, explain why this combination is possible and what it predicts for her behavior over the next twelve months. What would you do, and what would you *not* do, to change it? *(Tests: Three-Component Model, engagement vs. commitment distinction)*

### Challenge

10. **The perception audit.** Design a perception audit for a hiring process — a structured set of steps that would make the perceptual process explicit enough to catch the most common distortions before they produce a hiring decision. Your audit should address at least: what information interviewers are selecting for and why, how they're organizing candidates into categories, and how those categories are shaping interpretation of interview performance. The audit should be implementable by a team of five without requiring professional facilitators or expensive tools. *(Tests: perceptual process applied to organizational design, intervention thinking)*

## Chapter 04: Chapter 4 — Learning and Reinforcement
*Source: `chapters/04-learning-and-reinforcement.md`*

## Exercises

### Warm-up

**1. Thorndike's law of effect says behavior followed by a satisfying consequence tends to repeat. Skinner's definition of a reinforcer says a reinforcer is defined by its effect on behavior — not by whether it feels pleasant. Explain why these two statements are compatible, and give a workplace example of a consequence that felt like a reward but was not, in the technical sense, a reinforcer. (Tests: understanding the behavioral definition of reinforcement vs. the commonsense definition.)**

**2. Identify the reinforcement schedule in each of the following. Name the schedule and explain in one sentence why it produces the behavior pattern it does.**

- (a) A project manager reviews each team member's weekly status report every Friday afternoon.
- (b) A warehouse supervisor praises correct packing technique on roughly one in five shipments, in no predictable pattern.
- (c) A software developer gets a code-quality score automatically generated each time they submit a pull request.
- (d) A retail employee receives a quarterly profit-sharing payment if the store hit its revenue target.

**(Tests: mechanical identification of the four schedules and the logic behind each.)**

**3. The chapter states that timing matters more than magnitude in reinforcement. Give two concrete examples from a workplace — one where the timing was right (consequence arrived quickly after the behavior) and one where the timing was wrong (consequence arrived too late to function as a reinforcer). For each, describe what behavior was targeted and what likely happened to that behavior. (Tests: applying the timing principle to real situations.)**

---

### Application

**4. A hospital wants to increase hand-washing compliance among nurses and physicians. Current compliance is 62%. They implement a program: compliance is audited monthly, and units with above-average compliance receive a mention in the hospital newsletter. After six months, compliance has not improved meaningfully. Using reinforcement principles, diagnose at least two specific problems with this program and propose a redesign. (Tests: applying the five OB Mod steps and the timing/schedule principles to a real improvement problem.)**

**5. A tech company gives engineers a $1,000 bonus for each bug they catch before it ships to production. In the first month, bug reports triple. In the third month, the engineering manager notices that the same engineers who are catching the most bugs are also the ones whose code is generating the most bugs. Using operant conditioning concepts, explain what happened and propose a change to the incentive structure that would produce the outcome the company actually wanted. (Tests: diagnosing behavioral drift — the gap between the reinforced behavior and the intended behavior.)**

**6. A new employee joins a team. Their manager provides very specific, immediate feedback on every task for the first two months — praising what was done well, correcting what was done poorly, within hours. By month three, the employee is highly competent and confident. The manager, now busy with other priorities, stops providing the dense feedback. Within six weeks, the employee's performance has visibly declined and they seem anxious. Using what you know about reinforcement schedules and the fade, explain what went wrong and what the manager should have done differently starting in month two. (Tests: understanding why planned fading from continuous to intermittent reinforcement is necessary, not optional.)**

---

### Synthesis

**7. The chapter describes two ways reinforcement can go wrong: behavioral drift (measuring the wrong thing) and crowding out (extrinsic reinforcement undermining intrinsic motivation). For a job role you are familiar with — a teacher, a nurse, a software engineer, a salesperson, a manager — identify one realistic incentive system that would produce behavioral drift and one that would produce crowding out. For each, explain the mechanism and propose a modification that would reduce the risk while keeping some form of external reinforcement. (Tests: integrating both failure modes with real job context.)**

**8. The chapter argues that variable-ratio schedules produce the most persistent behavior and the greatest resistance to extinction — the same mechanism that makes slot machines addictive. A manager reads this and proposes using variable-ratio recognition for their team: praising people unpredictably, in no fixed pattern. A colleague objects that this is manipulative. Construct the strongest version of the colleague's concern, then explain under what conditions the manager's approach would be acceptable and under what conditions it would be genuinely problematic. (Tests: applying the concept critically rather than just instrumentally, and engaging with the ethical dimension the chapter raises.)**

---

### Challenge

**9. Edward Deci's crowding-out research suggests that extrinsic reinforcement can undermine intrinsic motivation for engaging tasks. A skeptic responds: most work is not intrinsically engaging, so the crowding-out effect is a concern for a narrow slice of jobs — researchers, artists, teachers — and irrelevant for most managers. Construct the strongest version of the skeptic's argument. Then identify where you think it goes wrong, using evidence or logic from the chapter. (Tests: holding the chapter's claim up to scrutiny and identifying where the boundary conditions of the crowding-out effect actually lie.)**

**10. The chapter ends: "You do now" — implying that understanding reinforcement schedules gives you real leverage over behavior at work. Design a behavior-change intervention for a problem you care about, using every major concept from the chapter: the law of effect, timing, the appropriate reinforcement schedule (continuous for learning, intermittent for maintenance), the five OB Mod steps, and a plan for fading external reinforcement toward self-management. Identify the single biggest risk in your design and how you would detect whether that risk was materializing. (Tests: synthesizing the full chapter into a coherent, deployable design and stress-testing it.)**

## Chapter 05: Chapter 5 — Diversity in Organizations
*Source: `chapters/05-diversity-in-organizations.md`*

## Exercises

### Warm-up: Seeing the layers

1. **Classify the diversity type.** For each person below, identify which type of diversity applies — surface-level, deep-level, hidden, or some combination — and explain what management challenge each creates.
   - A 54-year-old white man who is a closeted gay person.
   - A Latina woman with chronic pain that isn't visible.
   - A US-born engineer of Korean descent who identifies primarily as American-raised.

2. **The law's limits.** Title VII prohibits race-based discrimination in hiring. The race-based wage gap still exists. Are the two facts in tension? Explain why the wage gap can persist in a firm that has never violated Title VII.

3. **Harrison's finding.** Research shows that surface-level diversity effects fade over time while deep-level diversity effects persist. What does this predict about a team that looks diverse on paper but was hired for "cultural fit"? What would you expect to observe by year two?

### Application: From data to decision

4. **Audit design.** A tech firm has 12% women in engineering (industry average: 18%) and 8% women in leadership (industry average: 12%). A colleague says the leadership gap proves discrimination. You say it's more complicated. Walk through the alternative explanations and describe what data you would need to determine which is operating.

5. **Hiring intervention.** A firm wants to reduce the resume-screening bias documented by Bertrand and Mullainathan. Propose two specific changes to the screening process — naming the exact bias mechanism each change interrupts, and the tradeoff each change introduces. No intervention is free; say what each costs.

6. **Disclosure policy.** A manager wants more employees with disabilities to self-disclose so the firm can offer accommodations. She proposes a company-wide announcement encouraging disclosure. Using the research on hidden diversity and the ADA disclosure trap, explain why this might backfire — and propose an alternative that addresses the actual mechanism.

### Synthesis: The conditional business case

7. **Watson's crossover.** Diverse teams outperformed homogeneous teams by week 17 but underperformed in weeks 1 through 5. A manager reads this and concludes diverse teams are worth the early friction. A different manager reads it and concludes the cost is too high. What would each need to believe about their organization's context for their conclusion to be correct? What is the one condition that most determines which manager is right?

8. **The conference room, fixed.** Return to the opening scenario. You are brought in as a consultant. The firm wants to realize the performance benefits of its diverse team. Design three specific structural changes to how that meeting runs — not culture statements, not values commitments, but changes to the mechanics of the meeting itself. For each change, name the inclusion failure it addresses and the mechanism by which it addresses it.

### Challenge: Where good intentions go wrong

9. **Diversity training that backfires.** Research shows that mandatory diversity training sometimes increases backlash among majority-group employees without improving inclusion for minority employees. Using the chapter's framework, explain the mechanism: why would awareness training produce this outcome? What would a training intervention need to do differently to produce actual behavior change? What does your answer imply about where organizations should invest instead?

## Chapter 06: Chapter 6 — Perception and Managerial Decision Making
*Source: `chapters/06-perception-and-managerial-decision-making.md`*

## Exercises

### Warm-up

1. **Identify the system.** Describe two decisions you made in the last week — one where you used System 1 (fast, intuitive) and one where you used System 2 (slow, deliberate). For the System 1 decision: was the domain one where you have feedback-rich experience? For the System 2 decision: did you have enough fuel — time, energy, attention — to actually run it properly? What does that tell you about your decision-making conditions? *(Tests: System 1 / System 2 distinction, conditions for calibrated intuition)*

2. **Satisficing in practice.** Think of a recent organizational decision where someone gathered more information than the decision required. What was the cost of the extra analysis — time, opportunity, delay? At what point in the information-gathering process could someone have reasonably stopped and decided? What would that stopping criterion look like written down explicitly? *(Tests: bounded rationality, satisficing, cost of over-analysis)*

3. **Name the anchor.** You are in a salary negotiation. The candidate opens at $140,000. You had budgeted $120,000. Before you respond, write down: (a) how the candidate's opening number just anchored you, (b) what number you should have constructed before entering the room, and (c) what you say next to counter-anchor without blowing up the negotiation. *(Tests: anchoring, counter-anchor construction)*

### Application

4. **Run the premortem.** Take a significant decision your organization has recently committed to — or is about to commit to. Spend fifteen minutes writing the premortem: it is twelve months from now, and the decision turned out to be a disaster. Write the explanation. What failed? What warning signs were visible but ignored? What assumption turned out to be wrong? Then examine what you wrote: does it surface any concerns worth raising before commitment? *(Tests: premortem as structural intervention, overconfidence)*

5. **Map the barriers.** You are evaluating whether to continue funding a two-year-old initiative that has not yet hit its targets. Walk through at least four of the seven barriers and explain concretely how each one might be distorting your judgment in this situation. For each barrier you identify, name one structural countermeasure you could run before making the call. *(Tests: escalation of commitment, confirmation bias, sunk-cost defense, structural countermeasures)*

6. **Design the hiring committee.** You are filling a senior leadership role. The natural committee would be the three people who work most closely with that role — all of whom are similar to each other in background and communication style. Using what you know about the "similar to me" bias and the benefits of diverse perspectives, redesign the committee. Who should be on it? What different axes of diversity matter for this specific role? What check would you run before the final decision to catch familiarity masquerading as fit? *(Tests: "similar to me" bias, structural defense, team composition)*

### Synthesis

7. **The decision typology in your context.** Take ten decisions you or your manager make regularly. Sort them into the four types: routine/reversible (decide fast), routine/irreversible (think but don't agonize), novel/reversible (structured but quick), novel/irreversible (full process). For the decisions you placed in the novel/irreversible quadrant: are they actually getting the deliberation that quadrant requires? If not, what is that costing the organization? *(Tests: bounded rationality applied to real decision allocation, recognition of which decisions deserve System 2)*

8. **The groupthink autopsy.** Identify a group decision — from your organization, from documented history, or from a case you've studied — that you believe suffered from groupthink. Work through the Janis mechanism: what made the group cohesive? Was the leader's position known before deliberation began? What dissent was suppressed, and what signal did the group send about the cost of raising concerns? What structural intervention — devil's advocate, premortem, anonymous input before the meeting — would have had the best chance of breaking the pattern? *(Tests: groupthink mechanism, structural defenses, conditions for genuine dissent)*

9. **Build the kill criteria.** Choose an initiative currently running in your organization (or a hypothetical one) and write the kill criteria that should have been stated at launch. Specify: (a) what evidence, observed by what date, would trigger a stop decision; (b) who has authority to call the stop; (c) what the escalation path is if the stop-trigger appears but the person with authority wants to continue anyway. Then ask: if these criteria had been written at launch, would they change the conversation happening around this initiative today? *(Tests: escalation of commitment defense, kill-criteria design, pre-commitment as bias countermeasure)*

### Challenge

10. **The decision audit.** Design a decision audit for a team or organization — a structured process for reviewing a set of significant decisions made over the past twelve months. The audit should address: how many alternatives were generated before each decision; what evidence was gathered and what was not; whether kill criteria existed and whether they were honored; and what the outcomes were compared to what was predicted. The audit should be completable in a half-day by a team of four, produce actionable findings, and not require any external consultant to run. Present the audit as a one-page protocol another team could pick up and use. *(Tests: feedback loop closure, process design, bounded rationality applied systemically)*

## Chapter 07: Chapter 7 — Work Motivation for Performance
*Source: `chapters/07-work-motivation-for-performance.md`*

## Exercises

### Warm-up

**1. Motivation is defined in this chapter as having two dimensions: direction and intensity. Describe a situation — from work, school, or any organized activity — where you had high intensity but muddled direction, and one where you had clear direction but low intensity. What was the observable difference in your behavior and output in each case? (Tests: understanding the two-dimensional structure of motivation and why both are necessary for performance.)**

**2. Herzberg's two-factor model distinguishes hygiene factors from motivators. For each item below, classify it as a hygiene factor or a motivator and explain why. Then predict what would happen to an employee's engagement if that factor were removed.**

- (a) A competitive base salary
- (b) The opportunity to lead a high-visibility project
- (c) A clean, safe workspace
- (d) A manager who gives specific, timely feedback on results
- (e) Health insurance with full family coverage

**(Tests: mechanical application of Herzberg's classification and its predictive logic.)**

**3. Vroom's expectancy theory says motivation force equals expectancy × instrumentality × valence, and the equation is multiplicative. A manager wants to increase a team's motivation to hit a quality target. She raises the bonus for hitting it from $200 to $800. Team motivation does not increase. Using the equation, identify the two components she has not addressed and describe one concrete action she could take to improve each. (Tests: understanding why raising valence alone cannot fix a broken expectancy chain.)**

---

### Application

**4. A nonprofit organization pays its social workers below-market salaries. The executive director argues that mission and meaning compensate for the lower pay — that the work itself is the reward. Staff turnover is 40% annually. Using Maslow's revised framework and Herzberg's two-factor model, diagnose what is most likely happening. Then propose a specific intervention — not "pay them more" in the abstract, but a concrete change the executive director could make with limited budget. (Tests: applying content theories to a realistic failure scenario and generating a specific diagnosis.)**

**5. A software team has been given a goal: "improve code quality." After three months, there is no measurable change. The team lead says people are trying but do not know what success looks like. Rewrite the goal using Locke and Latham's goal-setting criteria (specific, difficult, accepted). Then identify one risk that the new goal introduces — one way that optimizing for this goal could reduce performance on something the team also cares about. (Tests: applying goal-setting theory and understanding the tunnel-vision trade-off.)**

**6. A senior engineer at a tech company was deeply engaged in her work — she described it as "the most intellectually satisfying job I've ever had." The company introduces a new program: engineers earn a $1,000 bonus for each patent filed. She files two patents in the first quarter, collects the bonuses, and then stops. In the next performance review, she says she has lost interest in exploratory research. Using self-determination theory and the crowding-out mechanism, explain what happened and propose a modification to the bonus program that might have preserved her intrinsic motivation. (Tests: applying SDT's crowding-out effect to a specific case and reasoning about how reward design interacts with task type.)**

---

### Synthesis

**7. McClelland argued that people differ in their needs for achievement, affiliation, and power, and that these differences are meaningful for role design. Think of a specific role you know well — a job you have held, observed closely, or studied. Describe what someone high in nAch, someone high in nAff, and someone high in social power nPow would each find motivating and frustrating in that role. Which profile do you think the role is actually designed for? What would you change to better accommodate one of the other profiles? (Tests: applying McClelland's typology to role analysis and thinking about fit between person and role design.)**

**8. The chapter argues that content theories diagnose what people need while process theories explain whether they will try to get it — and that you need both. Apply this two-step framework to the Palo Alto conference room scenario from the opening. First, use a content theory to identify what the candidate is reaching for in Option B. Then use expectancy theory to identify the conditions under which Option B actually produces the motivational effect the founder expects. Under what conditions would Option B produce worse outcomes than Option A? (Tests: integrating content and process theories on the same scenario and identifying the conditions under which an intuitive motivational design fails.)**

---

### Challenge

**9. Equity theory predicts that people who perceive underreward inequity will reduce their effort, demand more pay, leave, or distort their perception of their own contribution. A skeptic argues that this implies organizations should simply be transparent about all salaries — that full pay transparency eliminates the inequity problem. Construct the strongest version of the skeptic's argument. Then identify the two most significant problems with full transparency as a solution, using equity theory and any other framework from the chapter. (Tests: pushing equity theory to its policy implications and identifying where the theory's clean predictions collide with organizational reality.)**

**10. The chapter closes with a claim: "Motivation is not a lever you pull. It is a system of beliefs." Design a six-month onboarding experience for a new hire at a company of your choosing that deliberately addresses each of the following: the hygiene floor (Herzberg), the expectancy chain (Vroom), goal acceptance (Locke and Latham), and all three SDT conditions (autonomy, competence, relatedness). For each element, specify what the company does and what belief in the new hire it is intended to build or protect. Identify the single point in the six months where the design is most likely to fail, and why. (Tests: synthesizing the full chapter's framework into a coherent design and stress-testing it against the most common failure modes.)**

## Chapter 08: Chapter 8 — Performance Appraisal and Rewards
*Source: `chapters/08-performance-appraisal-and-rewards.md`*

## Exercises

### Warm-up: The machinery in plain sight

1. **Ken's five purposes.** The chapter lists five reasons organizations appraise performance: feedback, self-development, reward allocation, personnel decisions, training identification. Janet's twenty-four-minute review failed at all five. For each purpose, write one sentence explaining specifically how Ken's graphic rating scale meeting failed to serve it.
