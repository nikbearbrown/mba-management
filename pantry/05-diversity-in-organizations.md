# Pantry — Chapter 5: Diversity in Organizations

## Key concepts and terms

**Surface-level diversity:** Visible characteristics (age, body size, visible disabilities, race, sex) that organizations can measure directly from hiring data, census demographics, and observation.

**Deep-level diversity:** Nonobservable traits (attitudes, values, beliefs, decision-making orientation, cultural background) that emerge through interaction and shape how people work together.

**Hidden diversity:** Characteristics that individuals can conceal or reveal at their discretion (sexual orientation, hidden disabilities, mixed racial heritage, socioeconomic background). Carries personal risk of disclosure.

**Diversity:** Identity-based differences among people that affect their lives as applicants, employees, and customers. Includes surface, deep, and hidden dimensions.

**Inclusion:** The degree to which employees feel accepted, valued, and believe their voice matters in organizational decisions. Psychological safety is a component.

**Psychological safety:** The belief that one can voice a minority opinion, admit an error, or ask a question without punishment or humiliation. Critical condition for diverse teams to realize performance benefits.

**Glass ceiling:** Invisible barrier based on prejudicial beliefs of organizational decision-makers that prevents women (and, more broadly, members of underrepresented groups) from advancing beyond certain levels.

**Business case for diversity (Cox and Blake framework):** Six mechanisms through which diversity creates competitive advantage: cost advantages (lower turnover from diversity-valuing cultures), resource acquisition (larger applicant pool), marketing (market access and customer insight), system flexibility (cognitive flexibility at organizational level), creativity and problem-solving (cognitive diversity reduces groupthink), strategic alignment (diversity benefits innovation-focused strategies).

**Cognitive diversity:** Differences between team members in expertise, experiences, and perspectives. Distinct from demographic diversity; correlates with but is not identical to it.

**Groupthink:** Dysfunction in decision-making where desire for consensus overrides realistic appraisal of alternatives. More common in homogeneous groups with high cohesion.

**Integration-and-learning perspective:** Organizational view of diversity as source of competitive advantage; assumes different life experiences and perspectives of diverse group members can improve decision-making and strategy. Research shows this perspective produces best outcomes.

**Access-and-legitimacy perspective:** Organizational view of diversity as way to access diverse markets and customer bases; treats diverse employees instrumentally.

**Discrimination-and-fairness perspective:** Organizational view of diversity as moral duty; committed to equal opportunity but often assumes assimilation into dominant culture.

**Homophily:** Tendency to prefer interaction with people similar to oneself. A mechanism of discrimination that doesn't require intent.

**Passing:** Decision to conceal a hidden identity (sexual orientation, disability, etc.) to avoid stigma or discrimination.

**Revealing:** Decision to disclose a hidden identity. Carries both benefits (reduced emotional strain, full integration) and risks (stigma, discrimination).

**Reverse discrimination:** Claim by dominant-group members that they are experiencing discrimination based on their race or sex. Statistically rare (1990s data: 100 of 3,000 discrimination cases); often reflects recalibration of labor market as diversity increases.

**Discrimination:** Treating people unfairly at work or in hiring based on identity group, condition, or personal characteristic.

**Disparate treatment:** Deliberately treating people differently based on protected status.

**Disparate impact:** Policies that apply to everyone but have disproportionate negative effect on people from protected classes (e.g., height requirements that affect women and people from certain ethnic groups differently).

**Workplace harassment:** Unwelcome conduct based on characteristics such as age, race, national origin, disability, gender, or pregnancy status.

**Sexual harassment:** Harassment based on person's sex; can include unwanted sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, or physical/verbal acts of sexual nature.

**Quid pro quo harassment:** Exchange of rewards for sexual favors or punishments for refusal.

**Hostile work environment:** Harassment that creates abusive climate through behavior (jokes, offensive comments, etc.).

## Key legislation

**Title VII of the Civil Rights Act (1964):** Made illegal discrimination in hiring, compensation, job assignment, performance evaluation based on race, color, national origin, sex, or religion. Created EEOC.

**Equal Pay Act of 1963:** Requires equal pay for equal work. Protects against wage discrimination where jobs are substantially similar in skill, effort, responsibility.

**Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA, 1967):** Forbids discrimination against individuals age 40 and above; covers hiring, advancement, compensation, terms and conditions.

**Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA, 1990):** Prohibits discrimination in employment, public services, public accommodations, telecommunications against people with disabilities. Requires reasonable accommodations.

**Pregnancy Discrimination Act (PDA, 1978):** Prohibits discrimination based on pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions in hiring, firing, compensation, training, job assignment.

**Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA, 1993):** Provides up to 12 weeks of unpaid leave (or paid if earned) for new parents and other specified needs. Protects job during leave.

**Supreme Court ruling on sexual orientation and gender identity (Bostock v. Clayton County, 2020):** Ruled that Title VII's prohibition on sex discrimination covers sexual orientation and gender identity.

**Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC):** Federal agency created by Title VII to investigate discrimination charges, enforce employment laws, issue guidelines.

## Misreadings to correct

**Misreading 1:** Diversity hiring causes reverse discrimination. 
*Correction:* Legal reverse-discrimination claims are statistically rare. When hiring rules change to include previously excluded groups, people from majority groups perceive loss of advantage as loss, but this is recalibration, not discrimination. The law allows preference for underrepresented groups if done non-rigidly.

**Misreading 2:** The wage gap is entirely about different job choices.
*Correction:* Job-choice differences account for some wage gap, but not all. Direct wage-gap studies controlling for job, experience, education show women earn less than men for identical work. This is discrimination.

**Misreading 3:** Diversity training eliminates bias.
*Correction:* Awareness training alone doesn't change behavior. Most effective diversity training combines awareness with skill-building, specific behavioral goals, and accountability. Poorly designed training can backfire.

**Misreading 4:** Diversity is what makes performance better (magic ingredient).
*Correction:* Cognitive diversity is what improves performance on complex tasks. Demographic diversity creates the possibility of cognitive diversity but doesn't guarantee it. Culture and leadership determine whether demographic diversity becomes cognitive diversity.

**Misreading 5:** Organizations that hire diverse people have solved diversity.
*Correction:* Hiring is representation. Keeping people and advancing them equally is inclusion. Most organizations confuse these. A firm can have 50% diverse hiring and 0% diverse promotion.

## Limitations and caveats

**Research on diversity and performance is correlational.** Most studies find associations between diversity and outcomes, but causality is hard to isolate. A firm with high diversity and high performance might have high performance because it's innovative, and innovation attracts diversity—not because diversity caused performance.

**Lab studies of diverse teams are short-term.** Watson's 17-week study is longer than most. Real organizational performance happens over years. The dynamics of diverse teams in long-term organizational contexts might differ.

**Psychological safety is hard to measure.** Research on psychological safety relies on surveys (self-reported). People's perception of safety might not align with actual consequences. Stated cultural values might not drive actual decisions.

**Intersectionality is not addressed.** This chapter treats dimensions of diversity separately (gender, race, age). But people experience simultaneous membership in multiple groups. A Black woman faces different discrimination than a Black man or a white woman. The chapter simplifies for clarity but undercounts complexity.

**US-centric.** All legislation and data are US. Diversity law, demographics, and organizational responses differ internationally.

## Exercises for further thought

1. Interview someone from an underrepresented group about their workplace experience. Ask about moments they felt included and moments they felt excluded. What distinguished the two?

2. Pull hiring and promotion data from your workplace or a company you know. Are the percentages equal at each level (hire rate, retention rate, promotion rate)? Where do diverse employees flow out?

3. Design a "diversity initiative" for a company. Then design one that focuses on inclusion instead of representation. What's different?

4. Read the Cox and Blake paper. What did they get right? What seems conditional or dated?

5. Examine the Uber case study (later in chapter). What would have prevented the sexual harassment? Structural changes, cultural changes, or both?
