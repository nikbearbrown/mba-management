# Chapter 15 — Companions: Culture-Change Cases and Trade-offs

## Pattern recognition across cases

The cases in the chapter (Kodak, Blockbuster) and extended examples (Gerstner's IBM, Southwest Airlines) illustrate specific patterns about when culture-environment fit breaks down and what it takes to recover.

---

## Kodak: Culture-Strategy Misalignment Under Pressure

### The setup (1975–1990)

Kodak's basic assumption was embedded in decades of dominance in film photography: **the company is the photography company; film is what we are.** This assumption was reasonable. Kodak had invented the technology, dominated the market, set the standard. Employees joined Kodak to work in film. Kodak's identity was film.

When Sasson invented the digital camera in 1975, the company faced a choice that was actually a test of its basic assumption. Digital could be treated as (a) the future we need to move toward, or (b) a threat to be contained while we protect the film business.

Kodak's response: contain it. Why?

**Not because Kodak was stupid.** Kodak's leadership understood the technology. Kodak understood the market. The constraint was cultural. Investing in digital photography meant:
- Cannibalizing film revenue (the cash cow)
- Admitting that the fundamental business model was obsolete
- Asking the organization to compete against itself

These are psychologically and organizationally hard. Kodak's executives who had built their careers on film success were being asked to build the business that would make their expertise obsolete. That's not a strategy problem. That's a culture problem.

**The trade-off Kodak faced:** invest in the future even though it threatens the present (high risk, uncertain payoff) vs. protect the present even though the future is coming (lower immediate risk, but delayed reckoning).

Most organizations choose to protect the present. The innovation dilemma is real. But what made it worse for Kodak was that the cultural assumption — "we are the photography company" — made the choice feel like an identity question, not a strategy question.

### The failure

Kodak eventually did invest in digital. It launched a digital camera division. It developed digital technology. But the investment remained marginal. The primary resources, attention, and talent continued flowing to film. By the time digital became competitive, Kodak had already ceded the market to Sony, Canon, and others.

The Kodak case teaches: **culture made it hard to do what strategy said needed doing.**

But the case also teaches the limits of the culture explanation. Kodak's failure was not purely cultural. It was also business-model structural. If Kodak moved aggressively to digital, it would cannibalize film revenue. The short-term hit to profits would be enormous. A company can't easily explain that to shareholders. So there was a real business constraint, not just a cultural one.

The honest reading: **business-model constraint + cultural resistance = paralysis.**

### What would have shifted the culture

Looking backward, what would it have taken for Kodak to move aggressively to digital?

1. **Existential threat perceived earlier.** If the threat had seemed more imminent (which it was, but took time to materialize), the basic assumption might have shifted sooner.

2. **Leadership change.** A new CEO from outside the photography industry, with no loyalty to film, might have made the shift. But Kodak promoted from within.

3. **Separation.** Creating a genuinely independent digital business with separate leadership, separate incentives, separate culture. This is hard because the parent company sees the digital business as a threat (or as a sideline). But it's sometimes necessary to let a new culture develop without interference from the old one.

4. **Success in the new model.** If the digital division had succeeded in winning market share, it would have provided evidence that the new strategy worked. But it was starved of resources, so it didn't get the chance to prove itself.

## Blockbuster: Silo Cannibalization

### The setup (1999–2010)

Blockbuster Video dominated video rental. The business model was simple: rent physical media from stores, charge late fees, profit from traffic. The culture was built around the store experience: convenience, location, in-store discovery, customer service.

Netflix's founding insight was: people don't want to rent immediately; they want convenience and avoiding hassle. The culture Netflix encoded from the start: **delivery and elimination of friction matter more than immediate access.**

Blockbuster saw Netflix coming. The company launched Blockbuster Online in 2004 — a subscription mail-rental service, essentially copying Netflix's model. But Blockbuster Online was treated as a separate business, competing with (not complementing) the core store business.

Here's the trap: **Blockbuster Online would cannibalize store revenue.** Every subscription customer who got movies mailed was a customer not coming to the store. So the store organization had every incentive to minimize Blockbuster Online's success. The incentive structure pitted the new model against the old one.

### The failure

Blockbuster Online grew, but slowly and without the resources or urgency it needed to truly compete with Netflix. By the time streaming became the real battleground (around 2007), Blockbuster was too invested in stores to make the transition. The company tried to launch a streaming service, but by then Netflix had already won.

### The trade-off Blockbuster faced

Unlike Kodak (which could have invested in a genuinely independent digital division), Blockbuster's core business model — stores and late fees — was fundamentally incompatible with Netflix's model. You can't charge late fees in a subscription service. You can't profit from store traffic in a mail service. Moving to the Netflix model meant not just changing operations but changing how the company made money.

Blockbuster's leadership understood this. They chose to invest more heavily in stores rather than online subscription. This was a rational choice given the installed base of profitable stores. But it was also a choice to bet that the current business model would remain profitable longer than it actually did.

The Blockbuster case teaches: **when the new model is genuinely incompatible with the old one, culture change alone won't solve the problem. You need a structural solution — separate company, separate incentives, willingness to let the old model fail.**

---

## Gerstner's IBM Transformation: Culture Change Under Crisis

### The setup (1993)

IBM in 1993 was in crisis. The company that had dominated computing was losing market share to smaller, more nimble competitors. The personal computer was fragmenting IBM's market. Unix and open systems were challenging IBM's proprietary approach. The company's stock price had tanked. Layoffs were coming.

IBM's culture was distinctly inward-focused. Employees took pride in "the IBM way." The company was hierarchical, process-heavy, protective of its proprietary technology. The basic assumption: IBM knows best, the IBM way is superior, we should protect our position.

This culture was adaptive when IBM faced predictable competitors and could compete on economies of scale and proprietary innovation. It was maladaptive when the market shifted to openness, speed, and service.

### What Gerstner changed

Gerstner didn't try to change culture first. He changed strategy, structure, and incentives. And over years, the culture shifted.

**Strategy:** IBM would become a services company, integrated solutions for customers, not a hardware vendor. This was a fundamental shift. Services are about understanding the customer's problem and solving it. Hardware is about making the best product. The thinking shifts from "what can we make" to "what does the customer need."

**Structure:** Gerstner broke up the fiefdoms. IBM had been organized by geography and by product line, with each unit protecting its own turf. He created customer-focused units and forced integration. Now the hardware division couldn't protect its margin if it meant a solution wasn't optimal for the customer. The structure created pressure for cooperation.

**Incentives:** He tied compensation to customer outcomes and overall company performance, not to individual unit success. Now a manager's bonus depended on solving the customer's problem, not on maximizing their division's revenue.

**Symbols and stories:** Gerstner himself made moves that showed the new basic assumption. He visited customers. He listened. He made decisions based on customer needs even when they threatened internal interests. He fired executives who couldn't let go of the old way. He promoted people who embodied the new assumption.

### What shifted

Over years, IBM's basic assumption shifted. The assumption "we know best, we should protect our position" gave way to "we need to understand the customer, we need to serve them, we need to let go of proprietary approaches if they don't serve the customer."

This shift didn't happen because Gerstner said "now think this way." It happened because:
1. The strategy worked — services became profitable
2. The structure now rewarded customer-focus
3. The incentives aligned with customer outcomes
4. The stories changed (from "IBM is great" to "customers matter most")

People observed all of this and absorbed the new assumption. Within a few years, the culture had genuinely shifted.

### Timeline

This is important to note: the shift took years. Gerstner was CEO from 1993 to 2002. By 1997–1998, the turnaround was visible. But the cultural assumption shift took the whole period and arguably beyond.

### The conditions Gerstner had

1. **Existential crisis.** IBM's survival was at stake. People were willing to let go of old beliefs because the alternative was failure.

2. **External leadership with authority.** Gerstner came from outside (he was a consultant), untainted by IBM culture, and had a clear mandate to fix things.

3. **A few years of runway.** The board gave him time to make changes before demanding results. That's rare and crucial.

4. **Success.** The services strategy worked. IBM's stock recovered. That success made the cultural shift stick.

## Southwest Airlines: Culture-Environment Fit as Advantage

Southwest's case is different because it's not a failure-and-recovery story. It's a case where culture and environment aligned from the start and remained aligned.

### The setup

Southwest was founded in the 1970s to serve short-haul, point-to-point routes where the competition was driving or bus travel, not other airlines. The basic assumption: **fast, low-cost, friendly service wins.**

The culture that encoded this: casual, friendly, efficient, fun. Employees were empowered to do what it took to get planes out on time and to solve customer problems. The dress code was casual. The communication was direct and playful.

### Why it worked

This culture was perfectly adapted to the competitive environment. Short-haul airlines need to get planes in and out quickly (efficiency). They compete on price (cost-focus). They compete on convenience (friendly, fast). The culture enabled all three.

Employees understood what mattered: get the plane out on time, make it cheap, make it pleasant. Those values were genuinely encoded in the basic assumptions, not just espoused. People knew what to do without asking.

### When it became a constraint

Southwest's advantage worked for decades. But as the industry changed — as full-service carriers moved into short-haul routes, as customers wanted amenities — Southwest's cost-focused, efficiency-first culture became less advantageous.

The culture that made Southwest brilliant at short-haul budget travel made it less compelling to customers who wanted meals, entertainment, and personalization. The same cultural assumptions (efficiency, cost, speed) that had been an advantage became, in some contexts, a limitation.

This is the important point: **culture is only as good as its fit with the environment.** Southwest is still profitable and admired, but the case shows that even excellent culture-environment fit is temporary if the environment shifts.

---

## Comparative analysis: why some cultures shift and others don't

Comparing these cases:

| Factor | Kodak | Blockbuster | IBM | Southwest |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Crisis severity | High (late) | High | High | Low (still successful) |
| External leadership | No | No | Yes (Gerstner) | No (internal promotion) |
| Structure change | Limited | Partial (stores vs. online) | Major (broke fiefdoms) | Incremental |
| Incentive realignment | Limited | Partial (stores incentivized to undermine online) | Major (customer outcomes, company performance) | Incremental (cost-focused throughout) |
| Culture-environment fit | Broke down; film → digital | Broke down; stores → streaming | Shifted to fit new environment | Worked for decades; now less optimal |
| Outcome | Bankruptcy | Bankruptcy | Turnaround success | Continued success but constrained growth |

The pattern: **culture shifts when (1) crisis is severe, (2) leadership is external and has authority, (3) structure changes to enable the new culture, and (4) the new approach succeeds early enough to show viability.**

Kodak and Blockbuster failed because the crisis didn't arrive early enough and the leadership didn't make the structural change necessary. IBM succeeded because Gerstner changed structure and incentives, and the new strategy worked. Southwest kept culture through internal promotion because the crisis was less severe and the environment remained favorable longer.

---

## The myth of "strong culture"

These cases illustrate why "strong culture is good" is overstated.

- **Southwest's culture was strong and adaptive.** The cost-focused, efficiency-first assumption served the company well for decades because it matched the environment.
- **Kodak's culture was strong and maladaptive.** The "we are photography" assumption was deeply embedded and resistant to change, and it caused the company to miss the future.
- **IBM's strong culture was maladaptive until Gerstner shifted it.** The inward-focused, proprietary culture made the company excellent at what it used to do and terrible at what the market now wanted.

The research supports this: **strong culture is an advantage when it fits the environment and a disadvantage when it doesn't.** Culture strength is not inherently good.

---

## Lessons for leaders

What these cases suggest:

1. **Diagnose regularly.** Use Schein's layers to assess whether your culture fits your environment. If the environment is changing and the culture isn't, you have a problem.

2. **Create structural options early.** If you think the environment might shift, consider separating the new business model (separate leadership, separate incentives, separate culture) from the old one. Don't make the new model compete with the old one for resources.

3. **Don't assume you have time.** Kodak and Blockbuster both thought they had more time than they did. By the time they acted, the window had closed.

4. **Change structure and incentives first.** Culture change is slow. If you change strategy without changing structure and incentives, the culture will undermine the strategy. Change structure and incentives; let culture follow.

5. **Look for early wins in the new model.** If the new approach starts succeeding (even partially), that success is what shifts the basic assumptions. Without early wins, people assume the new direction is temporary.

6. **Protect the cultural shift.** Once you've made the change, protect it. Don't revert to old symbols or old incentives. The culture shift is fragile for several years.

---

## Trade-offs in culture change

There's a real trade-off between **culture coherence and culture adaptability.**

- A coherent culture (strong, unified, deeply embedded basic assumptions) is excellent for execution. Everyone knows what to do. There's no confusion or politics.
- An adaptive culture (looser, more permissive of diverse views, more willing to question assumptions) is excellent for change. The organization can shift direction when the environment demands it.

Organizations often have to choose which matters more right now. In a stable environment, coherence wins. In a turbulent environment, adaptability wins. The hard part is recognizing when to shift which one you're optimizing for.

Southwest optimized for coherence for decades because coherence was what made the model work. Now that the environment has shifted, the company faces the choice: shift to adaptability (which means loosening some of the culture) or double down on what made it great (which limits growth).

Most organizations can't optimize for both at the same time. The ones that do are rare, and they usually have exceptional leadership and luck.

## Concept specification

Three core concepts identified for deep explanation:

1. **External environment and PESTEL.** The framework for reading what's pressing on an organization from outside. Named political, economic, sociocultural, technological, environmental, legal forces. Trade-off: comprehensive vs. static snapshot.

2. **Organizational structure (mechanistic vs. organic).** How organizations arrange themselves to respond to environment. Specific structures: functional, divisional, geographic, matrix, networked, virtual. Trade-off: tight control vs. nimble response.

3. **Organizational culture and Schein's three layers.** Artifacts (visible), espoused values (stated), basic assumptions (believed). Why the layers misalign and why that matters. Trade-off: strong culture for alignment vs. rigidity in changing environments.

---

## Primary sources

### Schein's three-layer model of organizational culture

**Source:** Edgar H. Schein, *Organizational Culture and Leadership*, 5th ed. (Jossey-Bass, 2016). Foundational work; originally proposed in 1985, revised and extended.

**Key claim:** Culture operates at three levels that often don't align. Only the basic assumptions (Layer 3) actually drive behavior; artifacts and espoused values are visible but not necessarily causative.

**Evidence:** Schein's work is qualitative, based on case studies and observation, not RCT. It is widely adopted in MBA and OB curricula. No major subsequent critique or refutation; competing frameworks (competing values, Detert & Schein) build on rather than replace the three-layer model.

**Caveat:** The model is descriptive (how culture looks) rather than predictive (which cultures perform better). Research linking culture to performance is mixed. See O'Reilly, Chatman, and Caldwell (1991) and subsequent meta-analyses below.

### Kodak's digital-camera invention and failure to capitalize

**Source:** Steven Sasson's 1975 prototype is documented in Sasson, S. J., and J. F. Compton. "The Dawning of the Digital Age." *SolidState Technology*, 2004. Kodak's failure to pursue digital at scale is covered in multiple business-case analyses.

**Key claim:** Kodak invented digital photography but failed to commercialize it as the primary business because the existing culture and incentive structure rewarded film.

**Evidence:** Documented by Christensen, Clayton M., *The Innovator's Dilemma* (Harvard Business School Press, 1997). Kodak bankruptcy filing, 2012. Kodak's digital division remained a small part of revenue while film remained the focus. By the time Kodak invested seriously in digital, competitors had moved ahead.

**Interpretation caveat:** The narrative of "they had the technology but the culture failed them" is the dominant reading. Alternative reading: Kodak faced a genuine innovator's dilemma — investing in digital meant cannibalizing film revenue, and the installed base of film was large enough that the payoff math didn't work until too late. Both are true. The culture exacerbated the economic constraint.

### Culture-performance link

**Source:** Multiple meta-analyses: O'Reilly, Chatman, and Caldwell (1991), *Administrative Science Quarterly*; Denison (1990), *Organizational Dynamics*; Kotter and Heskett (1992), *Corporate Culture and Performance*. Recent work: Hartnell, Ou, and Kinicki (2011), *Journal of Management*, meta-analysis finding moderate but significant positive relationship.

**Key finding:** Strong culture is associated with higher performance when the culture fits the environment and strategy. Strong culture mismatched with environment is associated with worse performance than weaker culture.

**Important caveat:** The link is correlational, not causal. High-performing companies can afford to invest in culture and symbols. Struggling companies pull resources from culture. The direction of causation is unclear.

**Trade-off:** The chapter asserts that culture-environment fit matters more than culture strength alone. This is supported by Denison and Neale (1996) and the competing values framework (Cameron and Quinn, 1999). But the evidence base is not as strong as the assertion might suggest.

### Blockbuster Video case

**Source:** Blockbuster's history and Netflix competition documented in Hastings, Reed, *No Rules Rules* (Penguin, 2020); Anderson, Chris, *The Long Tail* (Hyperion, 2006); multiple business-case studies in HBS case library.

**Key facts:** Blockbuster launched Blockbuster Online in 2004 but continued to prioritize in-store rentals. Netflix launched streaming in 2007; Blockbuster streaming service launched in 2007 but closed by 2010. Blockbuster filed bankruptcy in 2010.

**Interpretation:** The cultural explanation (Blockbuster's identity was tied to stores) is reasonable but not exclusively true. Blockbuster's revenue model (late fees, per-rental) also made Netflix-style subscription less profitable. Structural constraints and incentive misalignment both played roles.

### PESTEL framework

**Source:** Johnson, G., Whittington, R., Scholes, K., *Exploring Strategy*, 11th ed. (Pearson, 2017). Framework emerged in 1990s strategic management literature; not attributed to single author. Variants: PEST (original), PESTLE, PESTEL.

**Status:** Widely taught; used in strategy courses. Not research-backed in the sense of RCT validation; it is a heuristic for thinking about environments. Some critique that it encourages list-making over deep analysis.

**Usage in chapter:** PESTEL is presented as a diagnostic tool, not a complete analysis framework. The chapter uses it to illustrate that external pressure comes from multiple directions.

### Porter's five forces

**Source:** Porter, Michael E., *Competitive Strategy* (Free Press, 1980). Foundational work.

**Status:** Dominant framework in strategic management. Teaches competitive positioning analysis. Different purpose than PESTEL (five forces is competitive landscape; PESTEL is broader environment).

**Note in chapter:** Five forces is mentioned and distinguished from PESTEL. The chapter does not do a deep dive on five forces; that belongs in a strategy course.

### Mechanistic vs. organic structures

**Source:** Burns, T., and G. M. Stalker, *The Management of Innovation* (Tavistock, 1961). Foundational. Extended by Lawrence and Lorsch (1967), *Organization and Environment*.

**Key finding:** Organizations in stable environments tend toward mechanistic structures; organizations in turbulent environments toward organic structures. Organizations that mismatch environment and structure struggle.

**Status:** Widely cited. The mechanistic-organic continuum is standard in OB textbooks.

### Gerstner's IBM transformation

**Source:** Gerstner, Louis V., *Who Says Elephants Can't Dance?* (HarperBusiness, 2002). First-person account by the CEO who led IBM's turnaround in the 1990s.

**Key narrative:** IBM's culture was inward-focused (pride in the company, belief that IBM ways were superior). Market shifted to services and openness. Gerstner changed strategy (services), structure (broke up fiefdoms), and incentives (tied compensation to outcomes). Over time, culture shifted.

**Interpretation note:** The Gerstner narrative is used in the chapter to support the claim that strategy, structure, and incentives can shift culture. The causality (did culture shift because of the changes, or did people who believed in the new direction get promoted?) is not fully disentangled.

## Framework deep dives

### Schein's three-layer model — extended explanation

The model is presented briefly in the chapter. Researchers and practitioners find the model useful because it explains why organizational change is hard: artifacts and espoused values are easy to change (you can repaint the office, write a new values statement, do a culture workshop). Basic assumptions are hard to change because they operate outside conscious awareness.

Schein's claim: basic assumptions are absorbed through (1) what gets rewarded, (2) what gets punished, (3) who gets hired, (4) who gets promoted, (5) what stories leaders tell and repeat, (6) what the leaders actually do in moments of crisis.

Example: A company says it values "innovation" (espoused value). But when someone proposes a radical idea that threatens the existing business, the idea is shot down and the person's career takes a hit. The basic assumption is not "we value innovation." The basic assumption is "we protect the existing business." The innovation value is an artifact — a nice story, not a belief.

Competing frameworks:
- **Detert & Schein (2006):** Refined Schein to include "espoused theories" (what leaders say they believe) vs. "theories in use" (what leaders actually do). Pointed out that leaders themselves often don't see the misalignment.
- **Cameron & Quinn (1999):** The competing values framework maps cultures on two dimensions (flexibility vs. control; internal vs. external focus). Different approach, not a replacement for Schein. Both are useful.

### Culture change — empirical patterns

Research on culture change suggests several patterns:

1. **Existential threat accelerates change.** When survival is at stake, basic assumptions shift faster. IBM, General Motors, Kodak (too late) — all shifted culture only when the business was in crisis.

2. **Leadership from outside helps.** New leaders untainted by the old culture can shift things faster. But there's a window — two to three years — before the organization's immune system neutralizes the outsider.

3. **Early wins matter.** If the new strategy works, even partially, the culture shift has evidence. If it fails, culture reverts.

4. **Symbols and rituals matter.** If leadership says "we're changing" but keeps the same people in power, the same promotion criteria, the same rituals, people don't believe the change. The visible symbols have to shift for the deep assumptions to follow.

5. **Culture change is slow.** Even with all conditions met, cultural change takes years. Schein's estimate: 5–10 years for a deep shift in a mature organization.

---

## Contested claims and caveats

### Claim: "Culture eats strategy for breakfast"

**Origin:** Commonly attributed to Peter Drucker; sometimes attributed to Mike Tyson or others. Original Drucker source unclear.

**Interpretation:** Often read as "culture is all-powerful; strategy doesn't matter." That's wrong.

**Correction in chapter:** The chapter proposes that culture and strategy are locked in a feedback loop. Bad strategy breaks good culture. Bad culture breaks good strategy. What works is alignment.

**Evidence:** Not a direct testable claim. Qualitative case evidence (IBM, Kodak, Blockbuster) supports it.

### Claim: Strong culture is always better than weak culture

**Wrong.** The research is clear: strong culture is better when it fits the environment. Strong culture mismatched with environment is worse than weak culture. Southwest Airlines' strong culture worked when the industry rewarded speed and cost control. That same culture is less advantageous in markets where personalization matters.

**Source:** Denison (1990); Hartnell, Ou, and Kinicki (2011).

### Claim: Organizational structure is entirely determined by environment

**Overstated.** Structure is influenced by environment, but also by history, leadership preferences, and accident. Some companies thrive with unconventional structures. The relationship is tendency, not law.

**Nuance in chapter:** The chapter frames structure as "a response to environment" rather than "determined by environment." Leaves room for path dependence and leader choice.

## Myths corrected by name

1. **"Core values posters change behavior."** They don't. Behavior changes when basic assumptions change, which requires structural and incentive shifts, not symbols alone.

2. **"Culture eats strategy" unidirectionally.** Wrong. Bidirectional feedback loop. Strategy without culture support fails. Culture without strategy to anchor it is drift.

3. **"Strong culture is always competitive advantage."** Only if it fits the environment. Strong mismatched culture is disadvantage.

4. **"Organizational change is primarily cultural."** Often it's structural and incentive-driven. Culture follows the changes; it's not the lever to pull first.

# Chapter 15 — Companions: Schein's Three Layers in Detail

## Why this framework matters

Edgar Schein's three-layer model is the most useful frame we have for understanding why organizational culture is hard to change and why visible culture symbols often don't predict what people actually do. The model explains the gap between "the company says we value X" and "the company actually does Y."

---

## Layer 1: Artifacts

Artifacts are the visible, tangible expressions of culture. You can observe them.

Examples:
- **Physical space:** Open office vs. closed offices vs. assigned seating vs. hot-desking. The office layout sends a message about hierarchy, collaboration, control, and autonomy.
- **Dress code:** Formal (hierarchy, tradition), business casual (blended), casual (equality, informality).
- **Rituals and ceremonies:** Company gatherings, award ceremonies, holiday parties, onboarding processes. What the organization celebrates reveals what it values.
- **Language and jargon:** Does the organization use military language ("we're at war with the competition") or family language ("we're all on the same team") or sports language ("win the quarter")? The metaphors reveal assumptions.
- **Stories and legends:** The stories that circulate about the founder, about legendary mistakes, about heroes and villains. These stories encode basic assumptions.
- **Organizational rules and procedures:** What the official rules are (can vary widely from what people actually do).
- **Products and services:** What the organization produces says something about what it values.

The problem with artifacts: **they are interpretable, not self-explanatory.** An open office can signal "we value collaboration and transparency" or it can signal "we monitor you and you have no privacy." The artifact doesn't tell you which. Only the layer beneath it does.

Example: A company puts "We put customers first" on the wall and has a customer-first ritual where the CEO highlights a customer success story. That's an artifact. It might reflect a genuine basic assumption about customer focus. Or it might be performance art — the company saying what it knows it should say while the real decision-making protects internal interests. The artifact alone can't tell you.

---

## Layer 2: Espoused Values

Espoused values are what the organization and its leaders say they believe. They are usually stated in mission statements, values statements, company messaging, and what leaders articulate in speeches and interviews.

Examples:
- "We put the customer first."
- "We're a meritocracy — the best ideas win, regardless of source."
- "We value diversity and inclusion."
- "We empower our people to make decisions."
- "We take care of our employees like family."
- "We innovate fearlessly."

The important thing about espoused values: **they are usually sincerely believed by someone in the organization.** The founder might genuinely believe "we empower our people." The CEO might genuinely believe "we're a meritocracy." The problem is that the rest of the organization might not believe it, or might believe it in the breach.

The gap between espoused values and basic assumptions shows up in several ways:

**The stated value is a goal, not a description.** The company says "we value work-life balance" because that's what it's trying to become, not what it is. The organization is reaching for the value it wants to embody. Nothing wrong with that — growth requires aspirational values. But it means the espoused value is not yet a basic assumption.

**The espoused value is honored selectively.** A company says "we're data-driven" and it is, except in cases where the data contradicts the CEO's intuition. A company says "we promote from within" and it mostly does, except when it recruits a high-profile external hire for a senior role. These exceptions reveal the actual priority.

**The espoused value is an artifact, not a belief.** The organization says it values X because it knows that's what's expected of a modern company, but the organization doesn't actually live it. This shows up when the leadership team says the right things in public and does something different in private.

**Different parts of the organization espouse different values.** Sales says "we're aggressive and we win." Engineering says "we're thoughtful and we're right." Marketing says "we're creative and we're bold." These different espoused values coexist in the same organization because they operate in different subcultures.

---

## Layer 3: Basic Assumptions

Basic assumptions are the beliefs about how the world works that people don't even articulate because they seem obviously true. They operate outside conscious awareness. They are the deepest level of culture and the hardest to change.

Examples of basic assumptions:

**About human nature:**
- People are inherently lazy and need to be controlled and supervised.
- People want to do good work and will do it if obstacles are removed.
- People are motivated primarily by money.
- People are motivated by meaning and purpose.

**About authority and hierarchy:**
- Authority flows from position and title. What the boss says is right.
- Authority flows from competence and expertise. The best idea wins regardless of who says it.
- Hierarchy is natural and necessary.
- Hierarchy is an obstacle and should be flattened.

**About conflict:**
- Conflict is destructive and should be suppressed.
- Conflict surfaces important information and should be encouraged.
- Conflict is about winning and losing.
- Conflict is about understanding and finding common ground.

**About the environment:**
- The world is stable and predictable; change is slow.
- The world is turbulent and unpredictable; change is constant and fast.
- The world is a competitive landscape; we win when others lose.
- The world is an ecosystem; we thrive when others thrive.

**About the organization's purpose:**
- We exist to make money for shareholders.
- We exist to serve customers.
- We exist to create products that matter.
- We exist to provide good jobs for our employees.

The power of basic assumptions: **they shape behavior without conscious deliberation.** If the basic assumption is "people need to be controlled," then the organization will create control mechanisms (supervision, detailed procedures, approval requirements). If the basic assumption is "people want to do good work," then the organization will remove obstacles and give autonomy. Neither organization will think they're making a choice about human nature; they'll think they're just "how we work."

---

## How the three layers interact

The flow is typically downward:

**Basic assumptions → Espoused values → Artifacts**

The basic assumptions about how the world works shape what the organization believes it should value. The espoused values shape what symbols and rituals the organization creates. When all three layers align, the culture feels coherent. When they misalign, people notice the gap.

But the flow is also upward. If the basic assumptions haven't shifted yet but leadership installs new artifacts and espoused values, people will resist or dismiss the change as "just what leadership is saying this quarter." The visible symbols can eventually help shift basic assumptions, but only if they're sustained long enough and backed by structural and incentive changes.

---

## Examples of three-layer alignment and misalignment

### Example 1: A startup with aligned layers

**Basic assumption:** People want to build something meaningful; hierarchy is an obstacle.

**Espoused values:** We're a team of peers. Everyone's voice matters. We move fast and learn.

**Artifacts:** No job titles (or generic ones like "team member"). All-hands meetings weekly. Decision-making is participatory. Office is casual, open, no assigned seating. Failures are celebrated as learning.

All three layers point the same direction. People understand the culture and can read what it values. It's coherent.

---

### Example 2: A traditional corporation with misaligned layers

**Basic assumption:** Authority comes from position. People need to be managed and directed. The hierarchy is how we get things done.

**Espoused values:** We empower our people. We value innovation and bold ideas. We're a meritocracy.

**Artifacts:** Formal titles (Director, Senior Manager, VP). Chain-of-command decision-making. Performance reviews determine promotion. Casual dress code (recent change to seem modern). Innovation workshop once a year.

What's misaligned? The espoused values sound modern and empowering. But the basic assumption and most of the artifacts are hierarchical. The casual dress code and innovation workshop are artifacts attempting to signal that the organization is modern and flat, but the real structure and incentives are still hierarchical. People see the mismatch. The organization sounds empowering but feels controlled.

The innovation workshop is particularly telling. It's an artifact — a ritual designed to signal that the company values bold ideas. But if the basic assumption is "authority comes from position," then people will understand that innovation is allowed once a year, in the workshop, but not in their daily work. The real decision-making still flows up the hierarchy. The artifact contradicts the behavior.

---

### Example 3: A nonprofit with unexamined assumptions

**Basic assumption:** Money is scarce and we have to control costs.

**Espoused values:** We exist to serve the mission.

**Artifacts:** Lean budgets, everyone has to justify expenses, limited meetings with the leadership, centralizing decision-making around budget.

What's the problem? The basic assumption (money is scarce) might not match reality. Many nonprofits have more money than they think; they're just not spending it because the basic assumption about scarcity was embedded in the founding era when money actually was scarce. Decades later, the funding landscape has changed, but the assumption hasn't. The organization is under-investing in staff, technology, and capacity because of a basic assumption that no longer matches reality.

The gap is between the espoused value (serve the mission) and the basic assumption (money is scarce). If the organization actually believed the mission was paramount, it would spend money to enable it. But the basic assumption creates behavior that constrains the mission.

## Mapping the misalignment

A useful diagnostic: **when someone says "we say we believe X, but we actually do Y, which suggests we believe Z," they're naming the layers.**

Examples:

- "We say we value work-life balance, but we expect people to answer emails at 11 p.m. This suggests we actually believe profit matters more than people."
- "We say we're a meritocracy, but promotions go to people with connections. This suggests we actually believe politics matter more than merit."
- "We say we value innovation, but we punish failures. This suggests we actually believe control matters more than learning."
- "We say we're customer-focused, but we make decisions based on internal metrics. This suggests we actually believe efficiency matters more than serving customers."

When you see a mismatch like that, you're seeing the layers come apart.

## Why basic assumptions are hard to change

Basic assumptions are hard to change for several reasons:

1. **They operate outside awareness.** You can't easily change a belief you don't know you have. It seems like the way things are, not a choice.

2. **They've been reinforced by the organization's entire history.** Every hiring decision that favored certain types of people, every person who got promoted, every story that got told — all of it has reinforced the basic assumptions.

3. **They're socially validated within the group.** If you're surrounded by people who share the assumption, the assumption seems obviously true. Questioning it feels like questioning common sense.

4. **They're embedded in structures and incentives.** If the organization is hierarchical and the basic assumption is "authority flows from position," then the hierarchical structure proves the assumption is right. Changing the assumption would require changing the structure, which feels like letting go of something that works.

5. **They're often aligned with power.** The people most invested in the current basic assumptions are often the people benefiting from them. Those people won't voluntarily shift.

---

## How basic assumptions actually shift

They usually shift under duress. When the basic assumption is no longer useful — when the environment changes and the old assumption leads to poor decisions — the assumption can shift.

Examples:

- A company's basic assumption: "our industry is stable; the business model is durable." When the business model collapses, the assumption can shift. But it takes crisis, not argument.
- A team's basic assumption: "we need a strong leader to tell us what to do." When a strong leader makes terrible decisions, the assumption can shift to "we need to think for ourselves."
- An organization's basic assumption: "we're only accountable to shareholders." When social pressure, regulatory pressure, or employee defection creates consequences, the assumption can shift to "we're accountable to multiple stakeholders."

The research on culture change (see Schein, Kotter, Gerstner) suggests that basic assumptions shift when three things happen simultaneously:

1. **The current assumption leads to visible failure.** Not theoretical failure, but actual consequences. The stock price drops. Customers leave. Employees quit.

2. **New leadership with authority shifts the structures, incentives, and stories.** The CEO doesn't try to argue people out of the old assumption. The CEO changes what gets rewarded, who gets hired, what the visible symbols are, and what the leadership spends time on. Over time, people absorb the new assumptions by observing what actually matters.

3. **The new approach succeeds.** If the new strategy works, it provides evidence that the new assumptions are valid. If it fails, the organization reverts.

## Schein's model as a diagnostic tool

For leaders and students, the model is useful as a diagnostic:

1. **Identify the artifacts.** What do you see? What are the visible symbols, rituals, language?

2. **Identify the espoused values.** What does the organization say it believes?

3. **Identify the basic assumptions.** What do people actually do? What seems to matter when push comes to shove?

4. **Diagnose the alignment.** Do the three layers point the same direction?

5. **Predict the consequences.** If there's misalignment, people will be confused, disengaged, or cynical. If there's alignment, the culture will feel coherent.

6. **Identify the pressure point.** If change is needed, what needs to shift? The artifacts (easiest, least consequential)? The espoused values (medium, requires some honesty)? The basic assumptions (hardest, requires duress and new leadership)?

## Common mistakes in applying the model

**Mistake 1: Trying to change culture by changing artifacts alone.** Painting the office, writing a new mission statement, rebranding. If the basic assumptions don't change, the new artifacts are just performance art.

**Mistake 2: Assuming espoused values are what the organization actually believes.** Espoused values are what the organization aspires to be or knows it should claim. Basic assumptions are what it actually believes.

**Mistake 3: Underestimating how slowly basic assumptions change.** Organizations change culture very slowly. Schein's estimate is 5–10 years for a deep shift.

**Mistake 4: Not recognizing that different subgroups have different cultures.** The sales team, the engineering team, the finance team — they often have quite different cultures. The organization's overall culture is a mix of these subcultures.

**Mistake 5: Treating culture as deterministic.** Culture is powerful, but it's not destiny. Human agency and structural change can shift culture. Leaders matter. Crisis matters. It takes work, but it's not impossible.
