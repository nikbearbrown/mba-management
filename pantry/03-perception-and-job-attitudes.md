# Pantry: Chapter 3 — Perception and Job Attitudes

## Research notes and source tracking

### Key sources consulted

1. **Rosenthal & Jacobson (1968)** — Pygmalion in the Classroom
   - Original study showing teacher expectations shape student achievement
   - Effect size: roughly 0.3 SD improvement for "bloomers" vs control
   - URL: [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pygmalion_effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pygmalion_effect)
   - Used for: Pygmalion effect mechanism, Concept 1 worked example

2. **Kelley (1973)** — "The Process of Causal Attributions" in American Psychologist
   - Foundational attribution theory work
   - Introduces the three-factor model (consensus, consistency, distinctiveness)
   - Used for: Concept 2 framework and model structure

3. **Locke (1976)** — "The Nature and Causes of Job Satisfaction" in Handbook of Industrial and Organizational Psychology
   - Defines job satisfaction; still the canonical definition
   - Discusses the satisfaction-performance relationship (r ≈ 0.30)
   - Used for: Concept 3 definition and satisfaction-performance research

4. **Mowday, Porter, & Steers (1982)** — Employee-Organization Linkages
   - Organizational commitment model and measurement
   - Three-component model (affective, continuance, normative)
   - Used for: Concept 3, organizational commitment section

5. **Staw & Ross (1985)** — "Stability in the Midst of Change: A Dispositional Approach to Job Attitudes"
   - Evidence that job attitudes are partially dispositional (personality-based)
   - Attitudes change little even after job change
   - Used for: Concept 3, attitude formation discussion

6. **Salancik & Pfeffer (1978)** — "A Social Information Processing Approach to Job Attitudes and Task Design"
   - Social-information-processing model of attitude formation
   - Attitudes shaped by social context and group influence
   - Used for: Concept 3, social influences on attitudes

### Key empirical findings

**Satisfaction-performance correlation**: r ≈ 0.30 across industries. This is one of the most replicable findings in OB research. It means satisfaction explains about 9% of performance variance. The relationship is bidirectional but weak.

**Fundamental attribution error**: Robust across hundreds of studies. People consistently underestimate situational factors and overestimate personality factors when explaining others' behavior, especially negative behavior.

**Self-serving bias**: Also extremely robust. People attribute their successes to internal factors (ability, effort) and failures to external factors (luck, circumstances) more than objective evidence warrants.

**Pygmalion effect**: Original effect size in schools was 0.5 SD or more. Meta-analyses suggest it's smaller in organizational contexts (around 0.3 SD). Still significant, but not magical.

**Commitment and turnover**: Organizational commitment is a stronger predictor of turnover than satisfaction. The relationship is particularly strong for affective commitment.

### What I added and why

1. **The Sarah cold-open scenario**: The original material had a Chaz-and-James scenario that was simpler but less compelling. I created Sarah's story to foreground the actual consequence of perception bias — a self-fulfilling prophecy about career trajectory. This makes the abstract machinery concrete.

2. **The Chen hiring example (worked example, Concept 1)**: The source material had abstract descriptions of bias. I created a specific scenario showing how a manager's implicit biases (learned from past experience) create different interpretations of identical behavior. This shows the mechanism, not just the definition.

3. **Emphasis on the Pygmalion effect as central to the whole chapter**: The source material mentioned Pygmalion but didn't emphasize it as the connecting mechanism. I made it the capstone of Concept 1 because it ties perception → treatment → outcome explicitly.

4. **The satisfaction-performance paradox as central to Concept 3**: The source material treated satisfaction as important but didn't emphasize how weak its correlation with performance is. I made this the core insight because it explains why organizations often focus on the wrong metric.

5. **Commitment as distinct from satisfaction**: The source material covered both but didn't emphasize the distinction as clearly. I made it explicit: satisfaction is emotional, commitment is identity. Organizations measure satisfaction and wonder why satisfied people leave.

6. **The "path forward" insight**: The source material didn't explicitly surface this. From reviewing the research (Mowday et al., Southwest case), the strongest predictor of commitment is whether people see growth. I made this explicit as the lever.

7. **Worked example about attitude formation as a practical scenario**: The source material described the theory. I created a worked example (the five-person team, pre-existing attitudes) to show how a manager actually works with these dynamics.

8. **Removal of some frameworks that weren't adding clarity**: The source material had extensive discussion of "response salience vs. response disposition" as technical terms. I kept the concepts but simplified the terminology to just call them what they do: immediate needs, past experience.

### Gaps and limits

1. **No extended discussion of perceptual defense**: The source material discussed it; the chapter simplifies it. It's real, but it's a barrier to perception that's less actionable than stereotyping or selective perception.

2. **Limited discussion of cultural differences in attribution**: The research suggests some cultural variation in how people make attributions (e.g., fundamental attribution error is sometimes weaker in collectivist cultures). I didn't go deep here.

3. **Limited on interventions**: The chapter discusses the machinery well but doesn't deeply explore how to interrupt stereotyping, bias, or misattribution. There's a brief mention in the synthesis and the synthesis exercise, but not a full section.

4. **Not covering moderators of Pygmalion effect**: The effect is stronger for younger people, for people with ambiguous ability, for people who have more trust in the person making the expectation. The chapter simplifies to just "expectations shape outcomes."

5. **No extended discussion of personality and big five**: The source material mentioned personality traits. The chapter simplifies to "dispositional approach" without diving into which traits matter (conscientiousness, openness to experience, etc.).

### Empirical support for the chapter's main claims

- **Perception is construction, not recording**: Cognitive psychology (Bruner, Postman) and modern work on selective attention support this.
- **Expectations shape outcomes**: Rosenthal-Jacobson and dozens of follow-ups.
- **Attribution biases are systematic and predictable**: Jones & Harris, Ross, Heider, hundreds of replications.
- **Satisfaction-performance relationship is weak**: Porter & Steers, Locke, Iaffaldano & Muchinsky meta-analysis.
- **Commitment predicts retention better than satisfaction**: Mowday et al., multiple follow-up studies.
- **Social context shapes attitudes**: Salancik & Pfeffer, and modern social media research bears this out.

### What I'm still uncertain about

1. **How reversible are filed perceptions?** Once a manager has filed someone as "detail-oriented" instead of "builder," how much evidence does it take to reverse that? The chapter assumes it's hard but not impossible. I don't have deep research on this.

2. **Is the satisfaction-performance correlation different across job types?** The r ≈ 0.30 is robust, but I wonder if it's different for jobs where performance is more constrained (assembly line) vs. jobs where discretionary effort matters more (knowledge work). The research doesn't distinguish clearly.

3. **How much of commitment is actually about growth visibility vs. other factors?** I emphasize growth, and the research supports it as important. But commitment also involves values alignment, manager quality, compensation. I may be oversimplifying by emphasizing growth.

### Why I kept certain source material and changed other parts

Kept:
- The three-factor attribution model (it's canonical and works)
- Job satisfaction definition and its components (still the standard)
- Three-component commitment model (also standard)
- The finding that attitudes persist despite job change (important for the dispositional point)

Changed or reframed:
- Perceptual selectivity → more concrete examples, less technical language
- Barriers to perception → stereotyping and selective perception emphasized; perceptual defense downplayed
- Attitude formation → reframed as "dispositional, situational, and social" rather than the three approaches separately
- Behavioral justification → simplified to cognitive dissonance

### Worked examples' internal consistency check

**Concept 1 (Chen hiring)**: Shows how external and internal selectivity lead to different perception → different interpretation → different decision, even with identical behavior. The problem is real and the mechanism is clear. ✓

**Concept 2 (software team ship late)**: Shows how different stakeholders make different attributions, how the fundamental attribution error and self-serving bias play out, and how each party is partly right and partly wrong. ✓

**Concept 3 (new manager with five people)**: Shows how a manager uses the understanding of commitment (distinct from satisfaction) to design interventions. Shows that the right outcome (growth signals) produces better commitment than the wrong outcome (improving satisfaction metrics). ✓

All three reinforce the central mechanism: perception/expectation → treatment → opportunity → outcome, and this can be self-fulfilling.
