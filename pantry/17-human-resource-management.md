# 9-Box Talent Review Template

## Instructions

The 9-box (Performance-Potential Matrix) is used in talent review meetings where leadership discusses employees' current performance and readiness for advancement.

### Axes

**X-Axis: Potential for Advancement**
- **Low:** Not ready to advance; not interested in advancement; specialized role
- **Medium:** Could advance in 1–3 years with development
- **High:** Ready to advance now, or would be within 1 year with targeted development

**Y-Axis: Current Performance**
- **Low:** Below expectations for current role; performance improvement needed
- **Medium:** Meeting expectations; performing at role level
- **High:** Exceeding expectations; top performer in role

## Development Actions by Cell

### HIGH PERFORMANCE, HIGH POTENTIAL

**Development Actions:**
- Executive coaching focused on strategic leadership
- Exposure to C-suite; invite to strategic planning
- Formal succession planning for their current role
- High-visibility project leadership
- Mentoring relationships with senior leaders
- Accelerated promotion timeline (1–2 years)

**Manager Responsibilities:**
- Monthly check-in on career aspirations and development progress
- Advocate for opportunities at senior leadership level
- Ensure retention strategy in place (risk of losing to competitor)

---

### HIGH PERFORMANCE, MEDIUM POTENTIAL

**Development Actions:**
- Recognize exceptional contribution in current role
- Develop deep expertise/specialty (CRM expert, process expert, industry expert)
- Mentoring/coaching responsibilities for junior staff
- Cross-functional project work if interested in transition
- Clear communication that advancement is possible but not certain

**Manager Responsibilities:**
- Clarify whether medium potential is due to lack of interest or lack of readiness
- If lack of interest: value the expert/specialist contribution
- If lack of readiness: create development plan for specific gaps

---

### MEDIUM PERFORMANCE, HIGH POTENTIAL

**Development Actions:**
- Identify specific performance gaps and coaching plan
- Structured mentoring from high performer in role
- Feedback on what success looks like in current role
- Targeted skill development (presentation skills, technical depth, leadership fundamentals)
- 360-degree feedback or coaching to accelerate development
- Clear timeline for advancement (e.g., "If we resolve X and Y in next 12 months, promotion is on the table")

**Manager Responsibilities:**
- Monthly development conversations
- Clear performance expectations and feedback
- Provide support and accountability
- Risk: high-potential employee leaving if they perceive lack of opportunity

---

### MEDIUM PERFORMANCE, MEDIUM POTENTIAL

**Development Actions:**
- Evaluate fit and engagement
- Clarify career interests and growth areas
- Development plan if growth is desired
- Possible role change if current role not ideal fit
- Career conversation with clear expectations

**Manager Responsibilities:**
- Understand whether this is a performance dip or sustainable capability
- Determine if person is engaged or disengaged
- Create development plan if growth desired

---

### LOW PERFORMANCE, HIGH POTENTIAL

**Development Actions:**
- Immediate, structured coaching/mentoring
- Clear 90-day improvement plan with specific metrics
- Weekly check-ins with manager
- Address root cause: skill gap, engagement, fit, or environment?
- If skill gap: targeted training or external coaching
- If engagement gap: career conversation and role discussion
- If environment gap: different manager, team, or role

**Manager Responsibilities:**
- Frequent feedback and support
- High accountability
- Clear messaging: "We see potential, but current performance must improve"
- Risk of exit if person feels unsupported or unfairly managed

---

### LOW PERFORMANCE, MEDIUM POTENTIAL

**Development Actions:**
- Performance improvement plan (PIP), typically 90 days
- Clear, measurable expectations
- Weekly feedback and coaching
- Possible role change or team change if fit is issue
- Legal/HR involvement if likelihood of termination exists

**Manager Responsibilities:**
- Frequent feedback (weekly minimum)
- Clear documentation
- Determine if performance issue is solvable or if person should exit
- Follow company process for performance management

---

### LOW PERFORMANCE, LOW POTENTIAL

**Development Actions:**
- Immediate performance improvement plan (PIP)
- Clear message: improvement required or exit process will begin
- Documentation of performance issues
- Possible role change as alternative (if engagement is the issue, not capability)
- Exit process if improvement does not occur

**Manager Responsibilities:**
- Clear, documented feedback
- Follow legal/HR guidance
- Fairness in process
- Prepare team for exit

---

### HIGH PERFORMANCE, LOW POTENTIAL

**Development Actions:**
- Recognize and value expertise and specialist contribution
- Mentor junior staff
- Increase scope within current role (not vertical advancement)
- Possible title/recognition (Principal, Expert, Staff Engineer, etc.)
- Create development plan if person wants to advance despite low potential
- Communicate that advancement is not typical trajectory but may be possible with development

**Manager Responsibilities:**
- Regular recognition of contribution
- Prevent resentment (offer advancement path or clear, valued specialist role)
- Ensure compensation reflects value and expertise
- Retention strategy (external opportunities may be attractive)

## Conducting the Talent Review Meeting

### Pre-Meeting (HR Preparation)
1. Collect performance management data from past 12 months
2. Prepare draft 9-box (manager has already positioned people)
3. Prepare development history and current development plan for each person
4. Document any significant events (promotion, lateral move, performance issue, etc.)

### Meeting Structure
**Duration:** 1–2 hours for team of 12 people

1. **Review the grid** (5 min): HR presents the 9-box framework and reminds leaders of calibration goals

2. **Walk through each person** (5–7 min per person):
   - Manager describes person: performance, potential, context
   - Others share observations from cross-functional interactions
   - Group discusses and may adjust position
   - If position differs from draft, note reason for calibration

3. **Development planning** (2–3 min per person if priority):
   - For HIGH-HIGH: succession plan, stretch assignments, sponsorship
   - For MEDIUM-HIGH: development plan, timeline to advancement
   - For LOW-HIGH: improvement plan, coaching, root-cause analysis
   - For others: standard development or stabilization plan

### Post-Meeting (Manager Responsibility)
1. Manager conducts 1-on-1 with each employee
2. Communicate their position (non-negotiable: do NOT use exact numbers like "you scored 3.5")
3. Communicate development expectations
4. If high potential: communicate advancement timeline and growth opportunities
5. If performance issue: communicate clear improvement expectations
6. Document conversation and development plan

## Common Errors to Avoid

**Inflation:** Rating everyone as high potential. This defeats the purpose. A realistic distribution is 20-30% high potential, 50-60% medium, 10-20% low potential. If everyone is high potential, the scale is not meaningful.

**Recency bias:** Rating based on most recent performance (last month), not full year. The 9-box is annual review; use full-year data.

**Halo effect:** Liking someone and rating them higher. Separate likability from performance and potential.

**Confusion of potential with performance:** High performance does not automatically mean high potential (or vice versa). Performance is current; potential is future capability.

**Lack of candor:** Being nice instead of honest about low potential or low performance. Kindness that prevents honest feedback hurts the person (no development) and the organization (retention of underperformers).

---

## Maintaining Confidentiality

- Do not share 9-box positions with broad audience
- Do not tell employee they are in a specific cell ("You are high-high" or "You are medium-medium")
- Do communicate development expectations and career opportunities based on their position
- Do communicate performance issues clearly
- Do allow people to know that their growth is valued and development is planned

# Chapter 17: Human Resource Management — Research Notes

## Sources and Primary References

### 1. Hiring and Selection Validity (Schmidt & Hunter, 1998)

**Source:** Frank Schmidt and John Hunter, "The Validity and Utility of Selection Methods in Personnel Psychology: Practical and Theoretical Implications of 85 Years of Research Findings," *Psychological Bulletin*, Vol. 124, No. 2 (1998): 262–274.

**Key Findings:**
- Unstructured interviews: r = 0.20
- Structured interviews: r = 0.51
- Work samples: r = 0.54
- Cognitive ability tests: r = 0.51
- Assessment centers: r = 0.53

This meta-analysis covers 85 years of research across hundreds of studies. Validity coefficients reported here are stable across industries and job types.

**Application to chapter:** Used to establish the gap between current practice (unstructured) and predictive practice (structured, work samples).

---

### 2. Performance Management Evolution and Challenges

**Source:** Peter Cappelli and Anna Tavis, "The Performance Management Revolution," *Harvard Business Review* (2016).

**Key Concepts:**
- Original military merit-rating system (WWI)
- GE forced-ranking system (1981+) and its evolution away from forced ranking
- Challenges: aligning individual and company goals, rewarding performance, identifying poor performers, managing feedback at scale
- Deloitte redesign and shift toward real-time feedback

**Application to chapter:** Used to frame performance management as a historical evolution toward more adaptive systems, with specific focus on why forced ranking worked temporarily then failed.

---

### 3. Kirkpatrick's Four Levels of Training Evaluation

**Source:** Donald Kirkpatrick, "Evaluating Training Programs" (original 1959, revised editions through 2016).

**Four Levels:**
1. **Reactions:** Did trainees like the program?
2. **Learning:** Did they acquire the intended knowledge or skills?
3. **Behavior:** Are they using the skills on the job?
4. **Results:** Did using the skills produce business outcomes?

**Application to chapter:** Used to diagnose why most training programs fail—they stop at level 1 or 2, never measuring behavior change or business impact.

### 4. Pay-for-Performance Effectiveness

**Source:** Stephen Miller, "Study: Pay for Performance Pays Off," *SHRM.org* (2011).

**Findings:**
- 46.9% of companies cite recognizing and rewarding high performers as primary driver
- 32.5% cite achieving corporate goals
- Differentiation improves engagement when visible
- Top performers who see clear differentiation have higher retention

**Source:** World at Work "Compensation Programs and Practices Report" (2015)

**Findings:**
- 72% of companies tie pay increases to performance
- 67% of companies report increases for top performers are 1.5x–2x average performer increases
- Visible differentiation improves employee understanding of pay philosophy

**Application to chapter:** Used to establish the behavioral effect of transparent, differentiated pay systems.

**Five-Step Process:**

2. Specify the job (define competencies)
3. Develop pool (build candidate relationships before opening)
4. Assess candidates (behavioral interviews, work samples)
5. Integrate newcomers (structured onboarding)

**Application to chapter:** Used to structure Mechanism 4 (onboarding) and provide frameworks for leadership hiring.

**Source:** World at Work, *The World at Work Handbook of Compensation, Benefits & Total Rewards*.

**Six Elements of Total Rewards:**
1. Compensation (base and variable pay)
2. Benefits (health, retirement, income protection)
3. Work-life effectiveness (flexibility, wellness, time off)
4. Recognition (awards, bonuses, acknowledgment)
5. Performance management (expectations, feedback, evaluation)
6. Talent development (training, mentoring, career paths)

**Application to chapter:** Used to broaden the discussion beyond salary to a systems view of how organizations motivate and retain.

### 7. Motivation and Organizational Drives

**Source:** Nitin Nohria, Boris Groysberg, and Linda-Eling Lee, "Employee Motivation: A Powerful New Model," *Harvard Business Review* (August 2008).

**Four Drives:**
1. **Drive to acquire:** scarcity-driven, competitive, relative to peers
2. **Drive to bond:** connection to organization and team
3. **Drive to comprehend:** making sense of work, understanding
4. **Drive to defend:** fairness, protection, stability

**Application to chapter:** Used to explain why total rewards addressing multiple drives are more effective than cash-only systems.

**Concept:** 9-box (performance vs. potential) as calibration tool for leadership teams to discuss, align on, and develop talent.

**Source:** Yves Morieux and Peter Tollman, *Six Simple Rules: How to Manage Complexity Without Getting Complicated* (Harvard Business Review Press, 2014). Also: Public reporting on Zappos' Holacracy implementation (2013–2015).

**Application to chapter:** Used as opening example of what happens when HR systems are dismantled without replacement structure—shows why the machinery matters.

## Concepts Specified vs. Clarified

### Hiring Predictive Validity

**What it is:** The correlation between how someone performs in a hiring process (interview, test, assessment) and how they perform on the actual job.

**Why it matters:** Higher validity means better hiring decisions. A validity of 0.51 vs. 0.20 is 2.5x better prediction. Over 100 hires a year, this compounds.

**Limitation:** Validity is measured in controlled studies; real-world factors (manager quality, role change, market shifts) introduce additional variance.

---

### Performance Management vs. Performance Appraisal

**Performance appraisal** = evaluation. A judgment made once or twice a year about how well someone performed.

**Performance management** = a system. Ongoing goal setting, feedback, alignment, development. Appraisal is one component, not the whole system.

**Why the distinction matters:** Many companies have appraisal but no management. Appraisal without ongoing management is ceremonial.

---

### Transfer of Training

**What it is:** Whether skills learned in training are actually used on the job.

**The problem:** Training happens in controlled environments. The job is messy. Transfer failure is endemic—estimated at 60-70% of formal training.

**Why it matters:** Training budgets are wasted if transfer doesn't happen. The fix is not better training; it's environmental support for using the skill.

---

### Job Analysis vs. Job Description

**Job description** = what you publish. Usually accumulated over years, often generic.

**Job analysis** = what you actually figure out. Structured investigation of what distinguishes good performers from poor ones.

**Why the distinction matters:** Job analysis changes who you hire. You filter for behaviors that matter, not keywords.

---

### Performance vs. Potential (9-Box)

**Performance** = how well someone is doing now, in their current role. Based on performance management data. Relatively objective.

**Potential** = whether they could succeed at the next level. Based on behavioral evidence. More subjective, more predictive of future capability.

**Critical distinction:** High potential does NOT mean high value in current role. Some people are expert specialists who don't want to advance—high performance, low potential. Both are valuable.

---

## Myths Corrected by Name

### Myth 1: Unstructured interviews work fine because managers have good judgment

**Reality:** Unstructured interviews correlate 0.20 with job performance. Managers' intuition is not accurate at predicting performance.

**Why this matters:** Most companies still use unstructured interviews despite 85 years of research showing they do not predict well.

---

### Myth 2: Training automatically transfers to the job

**Reality:** Without environmental support, most training effects fade within weeks.

**Why this matters:** Companies invest in training expecting behavior change; they get initial engagement and then regression to baseline.

---

### Myth 3: Forced ranking improves performance management

**Reality:** Forced ranking improves accountability temporarily, then degrades culture and collaboration. Companies have backed away from it.

**Why this matters:** A system that works in the short term can degrade long-term performance if it changes culture.

---

### Myth 4: Personality typing (MBTI, etc.) predicts job success

**Reality:** Big Five personality traits show modest correlation with job performance in specific roles. MBTI shows no predictive validity for job performance.

**Why this matters:** Companies spend money on personality-based selection and coaching when the research support is weak or absent.

---

### Myth 5: Annual performance reviews are necessary for legal protection

**Reality:** Formal systems do not protect against legal challenges if ratings are subjective or reveal bias. Real-time documentation and fair process matter more.

**Why this matters:** Companies maintain burdensome annual review processes for legal reasons that turn out to be unnecessary.

## Deep Dive Mechanism: Structured Interview Design

### Why This Earned the Deep Dive

Structured interviewing is a technique that:
- Shows clear, measurable advantage over current practice (2.5x more predictive)
- Is underutilized despite research clarity
- Is practically implementable by any organization
- Has trade-offs worth understanding (feels mechanical, reduces rapport)

### The Mechanism

**Step 1: Question Design**
- Behavioral questions, not hypotheticals
- Same questions asked to all candidates
- Questions map to job analysis (the specification of what matters)

**Step 2: Scoring Rubric**
- Developed in advance, before first interview
- Defines what a 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 answer looks like
- Removes scorer variability

**Step 3: Multiple Raters**
- Different people conduct different questions
- Each rates independently
- Scores averaged
- Reduces weight of individual bias

**Step 4: Consensus**
- Disagreements between raters become data
- If two raters disagree substantially, the answer was ambiguous
- Further conversation may be warranted

### Why This Works

Standardization removes variability. It makes patterns visible that individual judgment misses. A hiring manager's intuition about rapport might rate a personable candidate highly despite weak technical answer. A scoring rubric makes the technical content explicit.

### Trade-offs

**Con:** Structured interviews feel mechanical. You cannot follow interesting tangents. You lose the sense of "fit."

**Pro:** You get predictive validity. Over 100 hires a year, that compounds to millions in productivity.

The trade-off is between feeling good about the choice and making a better choice.

One company's experience: 500-person software company, annual hiring of 50 people.

Baseline: 40% of new hires perform in top quartile within first year (considered good performance).

Change: Implement structured interviews with behavioral scoring.

Result: 65% of new hires in top quartile within first year.

- 12.5 × $80K = $1M/year in productivity gain

## Grounded Examples

### Google's Project Oxygen

Google's research team analyzed what makes good managers. They analyzed 10,000+ performance reviews, 360-degree feedback, and tenure data.

**Finding:** The best predictor of whether a manager retains and develops talent is whether they hold regular one-on-one meetings with direct reports.

**The mechanism:** Regular 1-on-1s create an environment where feedback flows, goals are clarified, and development needs are identified.

This informed Google's manager training curriculum and expectations for management at Google.

**Why this matters for chapter:** It's an example of HR-adjacent research that identifies specific behaviors that matter, not personality types or credentials.

---

### Best Buy's ROWE Experiment

Best Buy's corporate headquarters implemented Results-Only Work Environment (ROWE) in one department.

**The change:** Employees were evaluated solely on outcomes, not hours worked. No schedules, no meetings required, no presence expectations.

**Result:** Productivity increased 35%. Turnover decreased to near-zero.

**What it reveals:** The mechanism is not the flexibility itself. It's clarity: when people are evaluated only on results, they optimize for results and organize their time to achieve them.

**Limitation:** ROWE works in roles where outcomes are measurable and individual contribution is clear. It does not generalize to all work.

## Remaining Uncertainties

### Why Is Sustained Performance Management So Difficult?

The research is clear: regular feedback on aligned goals improves performance. Yet most organizations struggle to sustain this practice. Possible factors:

1. **Manager discomfort with difficult conversations.** Giving feedback about performance gaps requires interpersonal skill and willingness to create discomfort.

2. **Short-term pressure vs. long-term benefit.** Performance management pays off over months and years. Quarterly earnings pressure makes near-term demands feel more urgent.

3. **Measurement difficulty.** It's hard to prove that performance management caused the performance improvement (vs. market conditions, individual maturation, etc.).

4. **Cultural resistance.** In some organizations, the norm is to avoid conflict. Regular feedback feels like conflict.

This is where I stop being confident about the answer.

# Structured Interview Rubric: Example for Software Engineering Role

## Role: Senior Software Engineer

### Behavioral Question 1: Problem-Solving Under Complexity

**Question:**
"Tell me about a project where you had to deliver a significant feature on a tight timeline, but the requirements were unclear or changed during the project. What was the situation, what did you do, and how did it turn out?"

**Scoring Rubric (1-5):**

- **1:** Declined to answer or answered hypothetically
- **2:** Described a project with tight deadline but minimal evidence of problem-solving approach
- **3:** Described a project, explained the requirement change or ambiguity, and explained their approach; unclear whether approach mattered to outcome
- **4:** Described a project, explained the specific ambiguity, described their approach (e.g., clarified with stakeholders, broke work into increments, got feedback early), and connected approach to positive outcome
- **5:** Same as 4, plus: discussed trade-offs in their approach, acknowledged what they would do differently in hindsight, showed learning from the experience

---

### Behavioral Question 2: Technical Depth and Judgment

**Question:**
"Tell me about a time when you had to choose between a solution that was technically elegant but risky, and one that was more conservative but less elegant. What factors influenced your decision, and what happened?"

**Scoring Rubric (1-5):**

- **1:** Declined to answer or claimed they always go with elegant solutions
- **2:** Described a choice but minimal discussion of trade-offs
- **3:** Described a choice, explained some trade-offs (technical vs. time, or quality vs. speed), but reasoning unclear
- **4:** Described a choice, explained trade-offs with context (team capability, timeline, business impact), showed reasoning for decision, described outcome
- **5:** Same as 4, plus: discussed how they communicated the trade-off to non-technical stakeholders, showed awareness of risk, discussed how they validated their choice was right

---

### Behavioral Question 3: Collaboration and Feedback Reception

**Question:**
"Tell me about a time when a peer or manager gave you critical feedback about your work. What was the feedback, how did you react, and what did you do with it?"

**Scoring Rubric (1-5):**

- **1:** Declined to answer or gave an example of feedback they disagreed with
- **2:** Described feedback and accepted it, but no evidence of acting on it or learning
- **3:** Described feedback, acknowledged its validity, and described some attempt to change behavior
- **4:** Described feedback, explained why it was valid (even if initially defensive), made concrete changes to behavior, and showed improved outcome
- **5:** Same as 4, plus: followed up with the person who gave feedback to verify improvement, sought similar feedback from others to ensure learning was generalized, showed humility about growth areas

---

### Scoring Instructions

1. **Independent rating.** Each interviewer rates independently, without discussion, immediately after the interview.

2. **Consistency across questions.** A candidate might score 4 on one question and 2 on another—this is data. It suggests they have strength in one area and growth needed in another.

3. **Discussion protocol.** After both interviews complete, interviewers compare scores. If scores differ by more than one point on any question, discussion is warranted: "What prompted you to score this 4?" "I heard different evidence on that point."

4. **Averaging.** Final score is average of the two interviewers' ratings across all three questions.

---

## Sample Scoring

**Candidate A:**
- Interviewer 1: Q1=4, Q2=4, Q3=3 → Average 3.67
- Interviewer 2: Q1=4, Q2=3, Q3=3 → Average 3.33
- **Final score: 3.5**

**Candidate B:**
- Interviewer 1: Q1=5, Q2=4, Q3=4 → Average 4.33
- Interviewer 2: Q1=4, Q2=4, Q3=4 → Average 4.0
- **Final score: 4.17**

**Candidate C:**
- Interviewer 1: Q1=2, Q2=3, Q3=2 → Average 2.33
- Interviewer 2: Q1=3, Q2=2, Q3=2 → Average 2.33
- **Final score: 2.33**

---

## Notes

- This rubric is specific to Senior Software Engineer role. Other roles (Product Manager, Data Analyst, Customer Success Manager) would have different questions mapping to different competencies.
- The same questions should be asked to all candidates for the role, in the same order.
- Interviewers should avoid: follow-up probing if candidate initially gives weak answer, sharing their score with the other interviewer before both have submitted independent scores, and discussing candidate personality or culture fit in the same conversation as scoring (culture fit assessment is separate).
