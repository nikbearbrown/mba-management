# Chapter 14 — Conflict and Negotiation | Discussion Prompts & Reflection

## In-class discussion prompts

### On sources and types of conflict (Concept 1)

**Prompt 1: Recognizing structural vs. personal conflict**
"You're managing a marketing team and a product team. They constantly clash. Marketing says the product team is slow and unresponsive to customer feedback. Product says marketing makes unrealistic promises and doesn't understand technical constraints. Are these personality conflicts or structural conflicts? What's the difference, and why does it matter?"

**Expected responses:** Students may initially blame personalities. Guide them to see that the conflict is structural: different incentives (marketing is measured on revenue, product on quality), different time horizons (marketing cares about this quarter, product about long-term architecture), and task interdependence (they have to work together but their goals are misaligned). This matters because you can change incentives (structural fix) more easily than you can change personalities.

**Follow-up:** "If you were redesigning the organization, what would you change to prevent this conflict from erupting?"

---

**Prompt 2: When conflict is actually helpful**
"A team is working on a major decision—whether to enter a new market. During the meeting, someone points out that the market research the team is relying on is three years old and probably doesn't capture the current landscape. The team leader thinks this is a distraction. But the person who raised it is persistent. By the end of the meeting, the team agrees to commission new research before deciding. Was this conflict productive or destructive?"

**Expected responses:** Students may think it's destructive because there was disagreement. Guide them to see it as productive task conflict—it surfaced a real risk (stale data) that the team might have missed. The team is better off for the disagreement.

**Follow-up:** "Under what conditions would this same disagreement have become destructive?"
(Expected: if it became personal ("you don't understand market research"), if the person who raised it was shut down and stewed about it, if the team delayed a urgent decision for months.)

---

### On conflict-handling styles (Concept 2)

**Prompt 3: Choosing the right mode**
"You're a manager. Two of your direct reports are in conflict. Report A says Report B doesn't include her in project decisions even though her work depends on those decisions. Report B says Report A is micromanaging and wants to slow everything down with meetings. Both have points. Both are good people. You need to resolve this fast because the project is off track. What's your approach, and why?"

**Expected responses:** Listen for whether students want to compete (make a decision and impose it), collaborate (bring both parties together to understand interests), or avoid (hope it blows over). None of these is wrong on its face; the context determines which is right.

**Follow-up: "Now imagine you find out that Report A has been looking for another job because she feels excluded, and Report B is stressed about the timeline and worried about his reputation. Does this change your approach? How?"**
(Expected: This now has relationship-conflict elements. Just imposing a decision or avoiding it won't work. Collaboration that addresses the underlying anxiety is needed.)

---

**Prompt 4: The self-assessment trap**
"When asked how they handle conflict, most managers say they're collaborative or compromising. When asked how their opponents handle conflict, they say the opponents are competitive. Why do you think we have this blind spot?"

**Expected responses:** Some students will guess self-serving bias (I'm good, they're bad), but dig deeper. The real answer is fundamental attribution error: we explain our own behavior by the situation ("I had to be tough because they were being difficult"), but we explain others' behavior by their personality ("they're just competitive"). Getting students to see this in themselves is powerful.

**Follow-up:** "How would negotiation be different if both sides accurately understood that they were both responding to the situation, not to personality?"
(Expected: Less arms-race escalation, more willingness to reset, more possibility of collaborative solutions.)

---

### On negotiation and BATNA (Concept 3)

**Prompt 5: Discovering hidden interests**
"Union and management are negotiating a labor contract. Union opens demanding a $2/hour raise. Management opens at 50 cents. They seem locked. But when they actually talk—not about the money, but about why the money matters—the union says, 'Honestly, our people are worried about job security with automation coming. We need to know we can't just be laid off.' Management says, 'Actually, we're not looking to cut jobs, but we need flexibility in scheduling because demand is unpredictable.' Suddenly, the negotiation changes. Why?"

**Expected responses:** Students should see that the issue was never really the hourly rate. Union interest: security. Management interest: flexibility. These can be traded. Maybe the union gets a severance clause or a guarantee that automation means retraining, not layoff. Management gets flexible scheduling. Both get something that matters more than the original positions.

**Follow-up:** "What would have happened if both sides had stayed focused on the hourly-rate position?"
(Expected: They would have split the difference at $1.25/hour and both would feel they lost. The underlying interests would still be unmet.)

---

**Prompt 6: BATNA and power**
"You're job hunting. You have one offer on the table from Company A for $100K. You like the company and the role, but you feel the offer is low. You're about to negotiate. Unknown to you, Company A has three other qualified candidates who will accept $95K. Your BATNA is: take the $100K offer, or stay in your current job which pays $90K. Does Company A know your BATNA? And if they did, would they offer more?"

**Expected responses:** Students should realize that Company A probably does not know your BATNA, but they can guess. They know you're currently employed (probably), so they know you have *some* alternative. But if they know you have other offers or attractive options, they have less leverage. Conversely, if they know you're desperate, they have more.

**Follow-up:** "If you had told Company A honestly, 'I've got an offer from Company B at $110K, but I prefer your company,' how would the negotiation change?"
(Expected: Company A's leverage drops—you have an attractive alternative. They might match or beat $110K to keep you. Or they might realize you're not their person and walk away.)

---

**Prompt 7: Cultural differences—is it negotiation style or personality?**
"You're negotiating with a supplier from Japan. They've been in the meeting for two hours. They've said very little. When you present an offer, they don't respond directly—they say, 'We will consider this and discuss among our team.' You interpret this as: 'They're not interested,' or 'They don't respect our offer.' They interpret your directness and quick offers as: 'You don't value the relationship.' What's actually happening?"

**Expected responses:** This is not personality or disrespect. It's cultural. Japanese negotiators process information internally before responding. They consult with their team (consensus is valued). Directness can signal efficiency, but it can also signal that you don't care about the relationship. Silence is thinking, not rejection.

**Follow-up:** "If you had to negotiate with this Japanese supplier, what would you do differently?"
(Expected: Spend more time on relationship and context upfront. Be patient with silence. Understand that they might revisit issues multiple times—it's not inconsistency, it's thoroughness. Recognize that the person and relationship matter as much as the contract.)

## Reflection prompts (for individual journaling or online discussion)

**Reflection 1: Conflict in your own work or school life**
"Describe a recent conflict you were part of. Was it task conflict, process conflict, or relationship conflict (or a mix)? At which stage of Pondy's model did you notice it (latent, perceived, felt, manifest, aftermath)? How was it handled? In retrospect, what would you have done differently?"

**Reflection 2: Your conflict style**
"Think of how you typically handle conflict. Do you tend to compete, collaborate, compromise, avoid, or accommodate? Is this consistent across situations, or does it change? When do you use each style? Is there a style you overuse or underuse?"

**Reflection 3: A negotiation you've been part of**
"Describe a negotiation—a job interview, a salary discussion, a purchase, a team disagreement about how to do something. Looking back, do you think you were doing distributive or integrative bargaining? Were you focused on positions or interests? What was your BATNA, and did you understand the other side's? Would reframing the negotiation from positions to interests have changed the outcome?"

**Reflection 4: Cultural negotiation**
"Have you negotiated across cultural lines—with someone from a different country, region, or cultural background? Were there moments where you misunderstood each other's style? Were you adapting your approach, or staying in your default mode? What would you do differently?"

**Reflection 5: The relationship after conflict**
"After a conflict, does your relationship with the person change? For better or worse? Why do you think unresolved conflicts leave residue, and resolved ones sometimes strengthen relationships?"

## Small-group scenarios (20-30 minutes, 3-4 people per group)

### Scenario 1: The Engineering-Design Standoff

**Setup:** Your team is building a mobile app. The design team has created beautiful, intuitive mockups. The engineering lead says the design requires features that are technically impossible given the current backend. The project is already behind schedule. You are the product manager.

**Your role:** Solve this without escalating above you.

**What to do:**
1. (5 min) Each person states their position (design: "beauty matters for adoption"; engineering: "impossible with current constraints").
2. (10 min) Dig into interests. Ask: "What problem are you trying to solve? Why does that matter?" (Design interest: user experience; engineering interest: shipping a product that doesn't crash).
3. (10 min) Brainstorm integrative solutions. Could you ship a simpler version first and enhance? Could you prototype the design to test if users actually care about that feature? Could you add resources to engineering to expand capacity?
4. (5 min) Debrief: What interests were underneath the positions? Did the original conflict have to happen, or could it have been prevented?

---

### Scenario 2: The Salary Negotiation

**Setup:** You are a software engineer being offered a role at a new company. The offer is $110K. You've done market research and know the role should pay $125K. The hiring manager says the budget is $110-115K. You have another offer at $120K from a competitor, but you prefer this company.

**Your role:** Negotiate without losing the offer or selling yourself short.

**What to do:**
1. (3 min) Both parties state their positions (candidate wants $125K, company offers $110-115K).
2. (5 min) Candidate reveals BATNA: "I have another offer at $120K, but I prefer your company for these reasons [insert reasons]."
3. (5 min) Hiring manager explains their constraints: "Our budget is this. But I can offer other things. What matters to you beyond base salary?" (Could be: equity, signing bonus, flexible schedule, professional development budget, timeline for next raise, title, etc.)
4. (10 min) Problem-solve together. Maybe the company can't go to $125K in base, but they can offer: $118K base + $5K signing bonus + additional equity + a committed raise review at 6 months + $2K annual learning budget. That might beat the $120K offer depending on what matters to you.
5. (3 min) Debrief: Were you collaborating or competing? Did you focus on positions (the number) or interests (what you actually need)?

---

### Scenario 3: Cross-Cultural Negotiation

**Setup:** Your company wants to establish a partnership with a manufacturer in South Korea. You're flying there for the first in-person meeting. You have two days. Your Korean counterpart has blocked the schedule: Day 1 is relationship-building and general conversation. No contract discussion. Day 2 is information exchange and initial negotiations. You're frustrated—you have a lot to discuss and a tight timeline.

**Your role:** Navigate this without insulting the culture or losing the deal.

**What to do:**
1. (3 min) American negotiator states the concern: "We have limited time. I want to make the most of it."
2. (5 min) Korean negotiator explains: "Relationship is foundation. If we don't trust each other, contract is worthless. We need to know who you are."
3. (5 min) American negotiator learns: Why does relationship matter in Korean business culture? What does trust look like? What are you trying to understand about me?
4. (10 min) Plan Day 1 together. Ask good questions. Listen. Share about yourself. Understand the Korean side's business, family, values, approach to work. This is not wasted time—it's foundation-building.
5. (3 min) Debrief: How does understanding the *why* behind a negotiation style change your respect for it?

## Assessment question bank (for quizzes or exams)

### Conceptual understanding

1. **Explain the difference between task conflict and relationship conflict. Why is this distinction important for managers?**
   *Expected answer includes: Task conflict is about ideas/approaches and can be productive. Relationship conflict is personal and almost always destructive. The distinction matters because the resolution strategy differs.*

2. **You notice two of your team members are in conflict. At what stage of Pondy's model is it easiest to intervene, and why?**
   *Expected answer: Stage 1 (latent), because the structural conditions can be fixed before emotion attaches. By Stage 4 (manifest), positions are entrenched.*

3. **Describe a situation where compromising is the appropriate conflict-handling mode. What makes it appropriate?**
   *Expected answer: Time is pressing, power is equal, and the issue is not critical to either side. Example: Two managers competing for the same headcount. You can't give both, but you can negotiate a trade (one gets headcount, the other gets budget).*

4. **What is a BATNA, and why is understanding your BATNA important in negotiation?**
   *Expected answer: Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement. Your BATNA determines your walk-away threshold and your leverage. If your BATNA is attractive, you have more power. If it's weak, the other side has more leverage.*

### Application

5. **You're negotiating a vendor contract. You open by saying, "We need better prices than the last contract." The vendor says, "We can't go lower—our costs have gone up." You're stuck. What would you do, and why?**
   *Expected answer: Stop focusing on price (position) and ask about interests. "What has changed in your costs? Are there things we can do together to reduce overall costs—like guaranteeing volume, simplifying orders, extending payment terms?" Reframing as integrative often finds solutions.*

6. **A team you lead is debating whether to use Technology A or Technology B for a major project. The debate is getting heated. How do you manage this so the conflict is productive, not destructive?**
   *Expected answer includes: Encourage task conflict (keep it about the merits), separate it from person (avoid "You don't understand engineering"), ensure both sides feel heard, use criteria to decide (performance, cost, maintainability), and debrief after the decision to ensure no relationship damage.*

## Rubric for assessing conflict-resolution skills (for group projects or in-class negotiations)

### Criteria for evaluating a conflict resolution or negotiation

| Criterion | Excellent | Good | Needs work |
|-----------|-----------|------|-----------|
| **Understanding the type of conflict** | Correctly identifies task/process/relationship conflict and explains why the distinction matters | Identifies the type with minor errors in explanation | Misidentifies the conflict type |
| **Choosing appropriate handling mode** | Selects mode that fits the situation and explains the reasoning | Chooses a reasonable mode; reasoning is weak or incomplete | Chooses a mode without justification or chooses a clearly wrong mode |
| **Discovering interests, not just positions** | Asks good questions to uncover what the other side actually cares about (not just what they're asking for) | Mentions interests but doesn't dig deep | Stays focused on positions throughout |
| **Reframing to create options** | Proposes solutions that satisfy both sides' interests, not just split the difference | Proposes compromises (half-wins) | Proposes one-sided solutions |
| **Cultural awareness** | Recognizes that negotiation/conflict styles vary by culture and adapts approach | Acknowledges cultural differences but doesn't adjust behavior | Assumes own style is universal |
| **Communication during conflict** | Listens actively, separates the person from the problem, keeps language respectful | Generally communicates well with occasional dismissiveness | Uses inflammatory language, interrupts, doesn't listen |
| **Follow-up and relationship** | Checks that both sides feel heard; acknowledges the relationship matters | Concludes once agreement is reached | Doesn't consider relationship effects |

## Extension questions for advanced students

1. **Game theory framing:** Conflict and negotiation are often framed as games (zero-sum, win-win, etc.). When is this framing useful, and when does it break down? What is lost by thinking of negotiation as a game?

2. **Power and BATNA:** If one side has a much more attractive BATNA, does the negotiation become unfair? What ethical obligations, if any, does the stronger party have?

3. **Conflict and innovation:** Some research suggests that moderate conflict drives innovation. Can you design an organization that structures conflict in a way that's productive without being destructive?

4. **Cultural hybridity:** If you're negotiating with someone from a different culture, should you adopt their style, stick with yours, or blend? What are the trade-offs of each approach?

5. **Unresolved conflict as information:** You proposed that conflict is diagnostic. Can an organization systematically collect information from unresolved conflicts to improve its structure?

## Figure references (placeholders for Nik's visual review)

### Figure 1: Pondy's Five Stages of Conflict
- **Latent conflict:** Structural conditions exist (misaligned incentives, scarce resources, unclear authority). Nothing visible yet. Example: Sales department rewarded for closing deals; production department rewarded for efficiency. The conflict is baked into the org chart.
- **Perceived conflict:** Someone notices the misalignment. Awareness without emotion. Example: A production manager reads a sales email promising custom delivery and thinks, "We can't do that in the timeline we have."
- **Felt conflict:** Emotion arrives—frustration, anger, anxiety. The person is no longer observing coolly. Example: The same production manager gets angry, feels their expertise is being dismissed, starts to resent sales.
- **Manifest conflict:** People act visibly. They send sharp emails, refuse cooperation, build alliances. Example: Production slows down custom orders. Sales complains to the VP. Sides form.
- **Aftermath:** Conflict ends, but relationship changed. Residue for next episode. Example: Even if a new process resolves the immediate issue, resentment lingers. The next disagreement starts from a place of mistrust.

**Teaching note:** Managers who recognize conflict at Stage 1 or 2 can prevent escalation cheaply. Waiting until Stage 4 (manifest) to act is expensive—relationships are already strained and positions are entrenched.

---

### Figure 2: Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Modes Matrix
Two dimensions: Assertiveness (pushing for own concerns) and Cooperativeness (caring about other side's concerns).

```
High Cooperativeness, High Assertiveness: COLLABORATING
- Both parties' concerns are important
- Seeking integrative solutions
- Long-term relationship matters
- Takes time and trust

High Cooperativeness, Low Assertiveness: ACCOMMODATING
- Other side's concerns are more important
- You are wrong or it matters more to them
- Building goodwill / learning opportunity
- Risk: accumulating resentment if overused

Low Assertiveness, Low Cooperativeness: AVOIDING
- Issue is trivial or energy cost exceeds benefit
- Cooling-off period needed
- Let others solve it
- Risk: conflict festers if real issue

Low Cooperativeness, High Assertiveness: COMPETING
- Speed and decisiveness needed
- High stakes (survival decision)
- Other side is exploiting goodwill
- Risk: damages relationship

Moderate on both: COMPROMISING
- Time is pressing
- Equal power
- Issue not critical to either side
- Risk: both sides partially dissatisfied
```

**Teaching note:** Each mode is situationally appropriate. The trap is using the same mode every time or assuming one mode is "best."

---

### Figure 3: Distributive vs. Integrative Bargaining

| Feature | Distributive | Integrative |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Pie structure | Fixed (one wins, one loses) | Variable (can grow) |
| Primary aim | Get as much as possible | Find mutual benefit |
| Time horizon | Short-term, one-off | Long-term, ongoing |
| Strategy | Find resistance point; push to it | Discover interests; trade differently |
| Risk | Resentment; damaged relationship | Requires trust and openness |
| Example | Buying a house, one-time supplier | Long-term partnerships, ongoing contracts |

**Teaching note:** Most manager-to-manager negotiations are integrative even if they look distributive. The key is reframing from "split the difference on price" to "what does value look like to both sides?"

---

### Figure 4: The Four Stages of Negotiation
1. **Relationship building (non-task time):** 5 minutes (US) to several hours (Japan, Middle East). Establish trust in the person.
2. **Information exchange:** Each side presents background, constraints, priorities. In Japan, essential for context-setting. In US, often rushed.
3. **Influence and persuasion:** Make the case. Present positions, concessions, counteroffers. Cultural differences here: facts (US), emotion (Arab), ideals (Russia).
4. **Closing:** Reach agreement or walk away. Closure signals vary: signed contract (US = done), signed contract (China = beginning of final negotiations).

**Teaching note:** Different cultures spend different amounts of time on each stage. American speed through Stage 1 can signal disrespect in cultures where relationship is the foundation for trust.

---

### Figure 5: Cultural Differences in Negotiation Style (from Graham 1985)

| Dimension | Americans | Japanese | Brazilians |
|-----------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| **Primary appeal** | Facts/logic | Ideals/norms | Emotion/personal |
| **Concession pattern** | Small, early | Few, if any | Many, throughout |
| **Response to other's concession** | Reciprocate; interpret as movement | Interpret as strength; reciprocate | Interpret as weakness; push harder |
| **Deadline consciousness** | Very high (deal must close Friday) | Low (relationship takes time) | Moderate (paced with relationship) |
| **Trust basis** | Written contract | The person | Personal relationship |
| **Communication style** | Direct, verbose, logical | Indirect, measured, contextual | Expressive, frequent "no," high touch |
| **Frequency of "no" in 30 min** | 9 times | 5 times | 83 times |
| **Periods of silence** | 3 | 6 | 0 |
| **Interruptions** | 10 | 12 | 29 |

**Teaching note:** These are patterns, not stereotypes. But they matter. An American negotiator who interprets a Japanese side's few concessions as intransigence will misread the negotiation. Japanese silence is thinking, not rejection. Brazilian "no" is a tactic, not a final answer.

## Worked example deep-dives

### WE 1: Manufacturing Plant Closure Threat (Concept 1)

**Scenario:** General Concrete, Inc., a mid-sized concrete products company, announced it would close one of its plants. The plant had one truck driver who was the sole member of the Teamsters Union at that location. The company tried to lay him off and move his routes to drivers at another plant. The driver began picketing.

**What happened:** No other General Concrete employee crossed the picket line. The entire plant shut down. For three weeks, the company produced nothing. The financial loss was substantial.

**Conflict analysis:**
- **Trigger:** Layoff and perceived unfair treatment (the driver felt singled out).
- **Type:** Mixed. Task conflict (is this decision right?) + relationship conflict (do they respect me?) + process conflict (did I have a voice in this decision?).
- **Stage at escalation:** The conflict went from latent (company planning a closure) to manifest (picket line) quickly because the driver had union backing and the driver had something the company needed (his willingness to cross the line). The other employees sided with the driver not because of personal alliance but because of a shared sense of injustice.
- **Why it happened:** The company failed at Stage 1 and Stage 2. The structure (one union member in a plant of many non-union workers) created latent conflict. The communication (announcing the layoff without notice or discussion) triggered perception and escalation quickly.

**How to prevent it:**
- Stage 1 prevention: If the company wanted to consolidate operations, redesign incentives to make consolidation feel fair. Maybe offer the driver a job at the other plant with guaranteed tenure. Maybe use it as a buyout opportunity.
- Stage 2 prevention: Before the announcement, communicate with the driver and the union. Explain the rationale. Ask what the driver needs to make this work. Problem-solve together rather than impose a decision.
- Stage 3 prevention: If escalation still seems likely, have leadership sit down with the driver *before* the formal layoff. Treat him as a person, not a line item.

**What actually happened:** General Concrete eventually negotiated with the union. The driver got a package and left. But the plant's reputation took a hit—the non-union employees felt the company had not treated the union driver fairly, which made them question whether the company would treat them fairly in the future. Latent conflict between company and non-union workers increased.

### WE 2: Competing vs. Collaborating in a Product Launch (Concept 2)

**Scenario:** A software company is launching a major new feature. The release date is set in stone—customers are expecting it. Two days before launch, the QA (quality assurance) lead flags a critical bug in the payment processing. The bug doesn't crash the system, but in rare edge cases (about 0.3% of transactions), users might be double-charged and not realize it for days.

The product manager says: "We ship as planned. We fix it in the next release two weeks from now. The reputational risk of delaying launch is worse than the risk of the bug." The QA lead says: "We can't release code we know is broken. It's a financial bug. Users will lose money."

**Who's right?** Both. The product manager is not wrong about launch pressure. The QA lead is not wrong about quality. The initial reaction is to compete: "We make the decision and you follow orders."

**Why competing fails here:** 
- The relationship matters (they will work together on the next launch).
- The stakes are high (user trust is at risk).
- Neither person's expertise should be overridden (product timing AND code quality both matter).
- The problem might have integrative solutions neither person saw alone.

**How to reframe as collaboration:**
Manager says: "Let's stop fighting about the launch date or the bug severity. Both are real. What would it take to fix the bug *before* launch?" 

Possibilities emerge:
- Can we fix it in 24 hours? (Maybe. QA estimates: 12 hours to fix, 8 hours to test. Possible.)
- Can we roll out to 10% of users first (canary deployment) to catch the bug before it affects everyone? (Yes. That's actually better than waiting two weeks.)
- Can we temporarily disable the edge case that triggers the bug, ship, then re-enable it in release 2? (Maybe. Trades off some functionality for correctness.)

By collaborating, the team finds a path that honors both the deadline (launch basically on time, with a canary rollout) and the quality concern (find the bug at scale, catch it fast). Neither person "won," but the outcome is better than either person's initial position.

**This is integrative bargaining in disguise.** The "pie" was not actually fixed. The options expanded once the underlying interests (time-to-market AND user trust) were on the table.

### WE 3: International Negotiation—Malaysia and America (Concept 3)

**Scenario:** An American manufacturing company wants to establish a partnership with a Malaysian parts supplier. The American team wants to move fast—they have a board deadline. The Malaysian team seems slow, focused on building relationship, reluctant to get down to business.

**American interpretation:** "These guys are not serious. They're wasting time with small talk. We need to get to numbers."

**What's actually happening:** In Malaysian business culture, the relationship *is* the contract. If the Malaysian side doesn't trust the American side as people, a signed piece of paper means little. They are following a different negotiation protocol.

**Stage 1 (Relationship building) misalignment:**
- American: 15 minutes of small talk, then "let's get down to business."
- Malaysian: Several hours (possibly across multiple meetings) of getting to know the person, understanding their values, building trust.
- American misreads this as: "They're not organized."
- Malaysian misreads American directness as: "They don't respect us as people."

**How to navigate:**
The American team should:
1. Recognize that this is not wasted time. It is foundation-building.
2. Adjust their timeline. A Malaysian negotiation will take longer.
3. Send the same person to multiple meetings. Consistency builds relationship.
4. Listen more than talk. Show interest in the Malaysian side's business, family, values.
5. Be patient with indirect communication. The Malaysian negotiator may not say "no" directly; they may say "that would be difficult" or "we would need to think about that." Listen for the meaning, not just the words.

**Stage 2-4 (Information exchange, persuasion, closing) alignment:**
- Present information in context. Don't just give numbers; explain the business rationale.
- Understand that a signed contract in Malaysia is less binding than the relationship. Keep the relationship alive after signing.
- Be prepared for the signed contract to be revisited. In American culture, "a deal is a deal." In Malaysian culture, the deal can be reopened if circumstances change and the relationship supports it.

**Outcome:** If the American team invests in relationship-building, the Malaysian supplier becomes a long-term partner. If they rush the relationship stage, they get a supplier who works minimally and switches to other buyers at the first better offer. The investment in time pays off in loyalty.

### WE 4: Finding the BATNA in a Salary Negotiation (Concept 3)

**Scenario:** You are a software engineer at a tech company. Your manager offers you a promotion with a $10K raise (from $110K to $120K). You think the role deserves more given the market rate and your qualifications. You want to negotiate.

**Your homework before negotiating:**

1. **Your BATNA:** You know you have two job offers from other companies: one at $125K, one at $130K. You also know that your current company values you (they're promoting you) and you like the work. If this negotiation fails, your best alternative is to take the $130K offer. That is your BATNA.

2. **Your walk-away threshold:** Based on your BATNA, you will not accept less than $128K (you'd take the outside offer instead). But ideally you want $132K because that's what your market research says the role should pay.

3. **Their likely BATNA:** Your manager's department has budget constraints. She wants to promote you because you're good and you might leave otherwise. If she can't promote you, she'll hire someone external. She probably estimates that hiring external costs $5K-$10K in recruitment fees and 1-2 months of vacancy. So their walk-away threshold is probably "don't let this person leave," which means they might go higher than the initial $120K offer.

4. **The negotiation:**
   - You open the conversation: "I'm excited about the role. I've done research on market rates for this position. The range is $128-$135K. Given my performance here and the internal knowledge I bring, I'd like to be at $132K."
   - Her response: "That's higher than we budgeted. We can do $123K."
   - Your response: "I appreciate that. Can you help me understand the constraint? Is it a total budget limit, or is there flexibility?" (You're trying to understand her BATNA and constraints.)
   - If she says budget is absolutely fixed at $120K, you have a problem. Your BATNA ($130K) is better than her offer ($120K). You might take the outside offer.
   - But often, when you show market data and make a case, there is flexibility. She might say: "We can go to $126K, but that's the ceiling."
   - You negotiate: "What if we did $126K now, with a review in 6 months with the possibility of adjustment?" (You're trading different things—less money now, but upside later. That might be worth more than the extra $4K.)

**The BATNA made this negotiation work:** Because you had attractive alternatives, you could negotiate from a position of strength. If you had no other options, you would be forced to accept whatever the company offered.

## Teaching tips for the worked examples

1. **WE 1 (General Concrete):** Use this to show how early intervention is cheaper. Ask: "At which stage could this have been prevented most easily?" (Answer: Stage 1, before announcement.)

2. **WE 2 (Product launch):** Use this to show how reframing from positions to interests often finds solutions neither party initially saw. Ask: "What would happen if the QA lead and product manager had competed instead?" (Answer: Either the launch is delayed and customers are upset, or the bug ships and users lose money. Both are bad.)

3. **WE 3 (Malaysia):** Use this to challenge the assumption that negotiation has one right speed. Ask students to identify the assumptions each side is making about the other. (American: "efficiency is respect." Malaysian: "relationship is the contract." Both are wrong about the other side.)

4. **WE 4 (BATNA):** Use this to show how knowing your alternatives empowers you. Ask: "What if you had no other job offers?" (Answer: You would probably accept the $120K because the alternative—staying in your current role—is worse.) "What if you had a $140K offer?" (Answer: You might walk away from this negotiation entirely.)

## Discussion prompts for classroom or group settings

1. **On functional conflict:** "Think of a time you were in a team where disagreement made the team better. What made that disagreement productive instead of destructive?" (Listen for: psychological safety, respect for ideas, clear separation of task disagreement from personal criticism.)

2. **On your own conflict style:** "Most people think they collaborate more than they actually do. Why do you think we have blind spots about our own conflict style?" (Listen for: self-serving bias, tendency to attribute our behavior to situation, tendency to attribute others' behavior to personality.)

3. **On cultural negotiation:** "If you were negotiating with someone from a different culture, what assumptions might you make that could get you in trouble?" (Listen for: assuming your pace is universal, assuming directness is always good, assuming the other side doesn't care about relationship if they don't build relationship *your* way.)

4. **On integrative bargaining:** "Think of a conflict in your life—work, family, or school—where you've been stuck on positions. What would happen if you shifted to interests?" (Invite students to brainstorm what hidden interests might be underneath the stated positions.)

5. **On BATNA:** "Why is knowing your BATNA important? What happens if you're uncertain about your BATNA?" (Listen for: confidence in negotiation, willingness to walk away, ability to read when the other side has a strong BATNA.)

## Common points of confusion (and clarifications)

**Confusion 1:** "Collaborating is the best style, right?"
- **Clarification:** No. Collaborating is best when both sides' concerns are important and you have time. In emergencies (competing), trivial disputes (avoiding), one-off transactions (compromising), or situations where the other side is exploiting goodwill (competing), other modes are correct.

**Confusion 2:** "Distributive and integrative bargaining—how do I know which one I'm in?"
- **Clarification:** Ask: "Will I see this person again? Do we have ongoing relationship?" If yes, assume integrative and look for interests beneath positions. If no (one-off transaction), assume distributive and focus on finding the resistance point. Sometimes what looks distributive is actually integrative once you dig into interests.

**Confusion 3:** "If I give ground in negotiation, isn't that weakness?"
- **Clarification:** No. A concession is movement toward agreement. It signals you're serious about reaching a deal. How the other side responds matters: if they reciprocate (collaboration), you're building agreement. If they push harder (exploitation), you've found someone who needs competing or avoiding, not collaborating.

**Confusion 4:** "Why would a company spend hours on relationship-building with a Malaysian supplier instead of just talking about the numbers?"
- **Clarification:** Because in Malaysian culture, the person is the contract. If the Malaysian side doesn't trust you as a person, they won't be a reliable long-term partner. The time spent on relationship-building *prevents* problems later.

**Confusion 5:** "If I have a great BATNA, why would I ever agree to less than my BATNA is worth?"
- **Clarification:** You might if the negotiated agreement is worth *more* than your BATNA—not just the monetary value, but the whole package. A job that pays less but has great learning, flexibility, or stability might beat a higher-paying alternative. And sometimes, accepting less than your BATNA signals goodwill that builds a relationship worth more than the difference.

## Key empirical claims and where they come from

### Claim: Managers spend ~20% of their time on conflict
**Source:** Thomas & Schmidt (1976). This is the empirical grounding for "why this chapter matters."

### Claim: Moderate task conflict improves decision quality
**Source:** Jehn (1995) meta-analysis. The meta-analyses cited are consistent across studies that moderate task conflict in teams doing complex cognitive work correlates with better decisions. Too little conflict = groupthink or suppression. Too much conflict = relationship damage and productivity loss. There is an optimal zone.

### Claim: Relationship conflict is almost always destructive
**Source:** Jehn (1995). The research is clear: relationship conflict (personal animosity) correlates with worse team performance, less information sharing, higher turnover. Unlike task conflict, there is no research showing an optimal level of relationship conflict. More is always worse.

### Claim: Task conflict and relationship conflict are distinct types
**Source:** Jehn (1995). She distinguishes task conflict (disagreement about ideas/approach), process conflict (disagreement about procedures/how to do the work), and relationship conflict (personal animosity). The distinction is load-bearing for the chapter's argument.

### Claim: Distributive vs. integrative bargaining are two distinct approaches
**Source:** Fisher & Ury (1981), Lewicki et al. (2016). Distributive = zero-sum, fixed pie, short-term transaction. Integrative = variable pie, interests-based, long-term relationship. The distinction drives Concept 3's practical framework.

### Claim: BATNA determines bargaining power
**Source:** Fisher & Ury (1981). The party with the more attractive best alternative to a negotiated agreement has stronger leverage. This is theoretical but has strong face validity.

### Claim: Cultural differences in negotiation styles
**Source:** Graham (1985). Data on persuasion techniques (facts vs. emotion vs. ideals), concession patterns, deadline consciousness, and communication frequencies across cultures. The tables showing Americans' vs. Japanese vs. Brazilian communication patterns come directly from Graham (1985).

### Claim: Americans misattribute their own conflict behavior as situational and others' as dispositional
**Source:** Bazerman & Neale (1992); also Thomas & Pondy (1967) in the original source material. The fundamental attribution error applied to negotiation contexts.

## Concepts that needed deepening vs. those that simplified for clarity

### Deepened
- **Task vs. relationship vs. process conflict:** The original source material used "goal, cognitive, affective, behavioral" conflict types. I reframed to task/process/relationship because Jehn's framework is more useful for managers and more aligned with current research.
- **Cultural differences:** The original condensed this to a brief note. I expanded it because cultural competence in negotiation is critical for global managers and the differences are often misunderstood as personality rather than cultural conditioning.
- **BATNA and interests vs. positions:** The original mentioned Getting to Yes but did not really unpack it. I elevated this to a core principle of Concept 3 because it is the mechanistic heart of why integrative bargaining works.

### Simplified
- **Conflict escalation stages:** Pondy's full model is more technical. I simplified to five stages that managers can remember and use.
- **Negotiation stages:** Graham's research identifies more nuance by culture. I condensed to four universal stages because the chapter already has cultural complexity in Concept 3.
- **Bargaining tactics:** Lewicki et al. discuss many tactical moves (anchoring, concession patterns, BATNA revelation, etc.). I focused on the most fundamental (finding resistance point, discovering interests) to keep Concept 3 actionable.

## Points of uncertainty and what I would need to verify

1. **The 20% time claim.** Thomas & Schmidt (1976) is now 50 years old. This finding is frequently cited, but I should verify whether newer research confirms it or shows drift. The original study was of 150 managers in one organization.

2. **The causal direction of task conflict and decision quality.** Jehn's research shows *correlation* between moderate task conflict and better decisions. The causal chain could be: (a) good teams naturally surface disagreement, or (b) disagreement forces better analysis. Both interpretations are reasonable; the data does not clearly distinguish.

3. **Cultural negotiation patterns across updated contexts.** Graham (1985) is a classic but old. Modern global business, cross-cultural fluency, and generational shifts might have changed how Americans and Japanese negotiators actually behave. I would want to see if studies post-2000 confirm or complicate the patterns.

4. **The relationship between conflict prevention and negotiation success.** I claim that managers who prevent unnecessary conflict through good structural design also make negotiation simpler. This is logical but I have not seen an empirical study that directly measures this.
