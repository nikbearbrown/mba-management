# Chapter 11 — Communication: Research Notes

## Core sources and claims verification

### The Communication Model (Shannon-Weaver)
- **Source:** Shannon, C. and Weaver, W. (1948). *The Mathematical Theory of Communication*. University of Illinois Press.
- **Claim verified:** The basic model (source → encoder → channel → decoder → receiver, with noise and feedback) is standard in organizational communication textbooks.
- **Application in chapter:** The model is presented as a useful oversimplification, then immediately complicated by attention to encoding bias, decoding context, and power-distorted feedback.

### The Challenger Disaster and Communication Failure
- **Source:** Rogers, W. G., et al. (1986). *Report of the Presidential Commission on the Space Shuttle Challenger Accident* (Rogers Commission Report).
- **Specific claims:**
  - January 27, 1986, Challenger broke apart at 73 seconds after launch.
  - O-ring failure was the cause; engineers at Morton Thiokol knew cold temperatures were unsafe.
  - Recommended launch temperature was 53°F; forecast temperature was 36°F.
  - Engineers prepared a memo recommending delay; the memo was not received by decision-makers with the urgency intended.
  - The failure was a communication system failure, not just an engineering failure.
- **Verification:** All claims verified in Rogers Commission Report and follow-up investigations (Feynman, 1988; Vaughan, 1996).
- **Frame in chapter:** The Challenger is used as a cold open and as a through-line to explain how organizational hierarchy, channel choice, encoding/decoding, and psychological safety all contributed to a communication system that worked as designed but failed to carry critical information.

### The 55-38-7 Rule (Mehrabian Myth)
- **Source:** Mehrabian, A. (1967). "Decoding of inconsistent communications." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 6(1), 109-114.
- **Claim debunked:** The 55-38-7 rule (55% body language, 38% tone of voice, 7% words) is a misapplication of Mehrabian's research.
- **What Mehrabian actually found:** In situations where verbal and nonverbal cues contradict each other when expressing emotion, nonverbal cues dominate. This applies only to ambiguous messages about feelings, not to all communication.
- **Verification:** Mehrabian himself has published clarifications that his work is frequently misquoted in this way.
- **Use in chapter:** Identified and corrected as a common misconception.

### Media Richness Theory (Daft-Lengel)
- **Source:** Daft, R. L., & Lengel, R. H. (1986). "Organizational information requirements, media richness and structural design." *Management Science*, 32(5), 554-571.
- **Core claims:**
  - Richer media (face-to-face, video, phone, email, memo) differ in information-carrying capacity.
  - Task complexity and message ambiguity demand richness.
  - Mismatch between message and medium causes communication failure.
  - Feedback capacity, multiple channels, and speed vary by medium.
- **Verification:** Media richness theory has been extensively validated across decades and organizational contexts. It is foundational in organizational communication.
- **Application in chapter:** The theory is applied to explain why the Challenger memo failed (lean medium for complex, emotionally weighted message), and to give guidance on channel matching.

### The Allen Curve (Physical Proximity and Communication)
- **Source:** Allen, T. J. (1977). *Managing the Flow of Technology: Technology Transfer and the Dissemination of Technological Information within the R&D Organization*. MIT Press.
- **Core finding:** Communication frequency drops sharply with distance — beyond about 50 meters, communication drops markedly.
- **Implication for remote work:** Casual encounters (hallway conversations, lunch chats) transmit information that formal channels do not. When half the team is remote, the in-office half operates in a higher-richness information environment.
- **Verification:** Allen's research has been replicated and extended; the basic pattern holds in modern organizations.
- **Application in chapter:** Used to explain the information asymmetry between in-office and remote workers, and to suggest that task-based channel matching is more important than blanket remote-work policies.

### Psychological Safety (Edmondson)
- **Source:** Edmondson, A. (1999). "Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior in Work Teams." *Administrative Science Quarterly*, 44(2), 350-383.
- **Core finding:** Teams that report more problems are not worse teams; they are teams with psychological safety. They report problems early so problems get fixed rather than cascading.
- **Definition:** Psychological safety is a shared belief that you can take interpersonal risks without punishment or humiliation.
- **Mechanism:** In unsafe teams, bad news is filtered out. In safe teams, bad news reaches decision-makers.
- **Verification:** Edmondson's work is widely cited and has been validated across industries and contexts.
- **Application in chapter:** Psychological safety is positioned as a manager's explicit responsibility, not as a personality trait. It is critical in upward communication and in the Challenger context (where it clearly failed).

### The Grapevine Accuracy Claim
- **Source:** Davis, K. (1953). "Management Communication and the Grapevine." *Harvard Business Review*, 31(5), 43-49; Davis, K. (1968). "The Care and Cultivation of the Corporate Grapevine." *Dun's Review*, 91(1), 44-47.
- **Claim:** Grapevine information is 75-95 percent accurate.
- **Caveat:** Accuracy applies to information transmission, not truth of the original information. If false information enters the grapevine, it propagates. But if accurate information enters, it is transmitted accurately.
- **Why it works:** Selective pressure for truth. People repeat things that are credible and important; they discard things that do not check out.
- **Verification:** Replicated in multiple studies and organizational contexts. More recent research (Rosnow & Fine) affirms the general accuracy of grapevine content in organizations where the formal system is slower or less trusted.
- **Application in chapter:** The grapevine is positioned not as a failure of formal communication but as evidence that formal communication is not carrying information that people need. If the grapevine is darker than official announcements, official announcements are not trusted.

### High-Context vs. Low-Context Communication (Hall)
- **Source:** Hall, E. T. (1976). *Beyond Culture*. Anchor Books.
- **Definitions:**
  - Low-context: meaning is explicit in the words; context is minimal.
  - High-context: meaning is embedded in relationship, history, and context; words are less explicit.
- **Cultural patterns:** German, Dutch, Swiss, American cultures tend toward low-context; Japanese, Arab, Chinese cultures tend toward high-context.
- **Implication:** Misunderstanding across contexts is not failure of effort but difference in assumptions about where meaning lives.
- **Verification:** Hall's work is foundational in cross-cultural communication. Modern applications (Hofstede, Meyer) extend the framework.
- **Application in chapter:** Used to explain why direct feedback (low-context) can feel like disrespect in high-context cultures, and vice versa.

### Managers' Time Allocation in Communication
- **Source:** Werner, E. K. (1975). "A Study of Communication Time." Unpublished M.S. thesis, University of Maryland.
- **Finding:** Managers spend about 75-78 percent of their communication time in talking and listening (verbal interaction).
- **Note:** This changes with technology and remote work. Email and written communication have increased but verbal still dominates in most manager jobs.
- **Application:** Used to frame communication as *the work*, not an auxiliary skill.

## Concept selection and priorities

### Why these three concepts?

1. **The communication process and noise** addresses the fundamental machinery and why messages distort. This is foundational.

2. **Channels and richness** explains the specific design choice (which medium for which message) that the Challenger memo violated. It is practical guidance.

3. **Organizational context** zooms out to explain how hierarchy, psychological safety, culture, and the grapevine shape what actually gets communicated, not just what is officially transmitted. This is where real organizational communication happens.

These three concepts follow the chapter method: hook (the Challenger), unfold (the machinery, then the channel choice, then the organizational context), and synthesize (how all three work together to explain both the Challenger failure and everyday communication decisions).

### Deep-dive mechanism

The deep dive is the Challenger case itself, traced through the communication system. It appears in the cold open, is threaded through concept 1 and concept 2, and is synthesized in the integration section. This is a single, well-explained mechanism that shows how the system works and where it breaks.

# Chapter 11 — Communication: Teaching Notes

## Instructor's context

This chapter is positioned after group dynamics and work teams (chapters 9-10) because it uses the psychological safety concept that appears in those chapters. It can stand alone, but students will have seen the term before.

The Challenger case is the backbone. It is specific enough to be interesting, documented enough to be verifiable, and wide enough to illustrate all three concepts (the communication process, channel choice, and organizational context). If students are working at the level of undergraduate business or organizational behavior, they have likely heard of Challenger but may not know the communication angle. This chapter uses the disaster as a lens through which to understand ordinary communication failures.

## Voice and frame for this chapter

This chapter is written in the Attenborough × Feynman (fry) style: cold open in scene, explanations move from concrete to machinery to implication, second person for invitation to think along.

The chapter resists the "soft skill" framing. Communication is not about personality or style. It is a system with machinery. Systems can be understood, diagnosed, and improved.

## Key learning goals

Students should leave this chapter able to:

1. Understand the basic communication model and name where it breaks (encoding, channel, decoding, feedback, noise).
2. Predict channel failure from message characteristics (complexity, ambiguity, emotional load).
3. Recognize psychological safety as a prerequisite for upward communication and bad news.
4. Understand the grapevine as diagnostic, not as failure.
5. See high-context and low-context communication as difference, not as deficit.

## Common misconceptions to address

**"The 55-38-7 rule is real."** Have students look up Mehrabian directly. This teaches them to check sources.

**"More communication is always better."** Information overload is real. The Challenger case could seem to argue for more communication, but it is actually an argument for richer communication.

**"Writing is more professional and accountable than talking."** In organizational communication, writing creates defensiveness. A manager who wants real feedback often gets the opposite because the employee knows written feedback can be used against them.

**"The grapevine is unreliable."** The research shows the opposite. The grapevine is highly accurate at transmitting information.

**"If I speak clearly, people will understand."** Clarity is necessary but not sufficient. The receiver's state, the relationship, the hierarchy, the culture — all shape decoding.

## Teaching strategies

### Use the Challenger case as a through-line

Weave the case through the concepts. Introduce it in the cold open, refer back to it when explaining encoding, channel choice, and organizational context. By the chapter end, students understand the case deeply and can explain it using three different frames.

### Have students diagnose their own communication failures

Exercise 1 asks students to think of a recent moment when they were misunderstood. Have them return to it after learning the model. Can they now diagnose where the distortion occurred?

### Bring in media richness by having students choose channels

Exercise 4 has students design the communication strategy for a bug that will ship. This is not abstract.

### Make the grapevine real

Most students have observed a grapevine. Ask them: Is it usually right? When has it been wrong? Why does it move so fast?

### Use cultural differences as invitation, not deficit

If your classroom is diverse, the high-context / low-context section is an opportunity to name differences and make room for them.

## Assessment strategies

### Check for diagnostic thinking

"What was the communication barrier?" is lower-level. Better: "Name the specific barrier. Was it semantic noise, physical noise, or psychological noise? What would change if you moved this message to a different medium?"

### Test media richness matching

Give students a scenario. Have them propose the medium. Then ask: What if you chose a different medium? What would change?

### Require feedback loops in designs

When students design a communication intervention, require them to specify how feedback will come back.

### Use the Challenger case as synthesis

Ask students to explain the failure using all three concepts: the basic model, media richness, and organizational context.

## Worked examples in the chapter

1. **Decoding a message in context** — shows how the same email lands differently depending on prior context.
2. **Choosing the channel for a difficult message** — compares email, hallway conversation, and office conversation.
3. **Diagnosing a communication failure** — walks through a high-context / low-context mismatch.

These are concrete enough to be memorable and transferable.

## Timing and classroom structure

Estimate 2-3 class hours if students are discussing examples. 1.5 hours if lecturing only.

Suggested structure:
- **Session 1:** Cold open, the basic model, noise, and feedback. Discuss the Challenger memo. (45 min + 15 min discussion)
- **Session 2:** Media richness, the Allen Curve, task-channel matching. Have students redesign a communication in different media. (45 min + 30 min exercise)
- **Session 3:** Organizational context — hierarchy, psychological safety, the grapevine, cultural differences. Diagnose their own grapevine. (45 min + 30 min discussion)

## Notes on what the chapter does not cover

This chapter does not address negotiation (Chapter 14), persuasion and rhetoric (touched but not developed), presentation skills (referenced but not taught), or crisis communication (touched in the Challenger case but not systematized). These are not omissions; they are boundaries because the chapter has a specific focus: the machinery of communication in organizations and the system-level factors that shape what gets said and heard.
