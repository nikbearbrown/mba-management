# Research Notes — Management and Organizational Behavior (Chapter 01)

## Concept Specification

**Management:** the process of planning, organizing, directing, and controlling activities of people in combination with other resources to accomplish organizational objectives. *Contrast with* leadership (influence without formal authority), *distinguish from* administration (managing systems rather than people).

**Organizational Behavior (OB):** the study of how individuals, groups, and organizations function, with particular focus on behavior patterns, motivation, and performance. *Applies* psychology, sociology, economics, and cultural anthropology. *Differs from* general psychology by focusing on the organizational context specifically.

**Hawthorne effect:** the phenomenon that *being observed changes behavior*. Workers increase effort, improve quality, cooperate more visibly when they know they are being studied. The effect is real but smaller than originally claimed (Mayo 1933 overstated magnitude; reanalyses show other variables in the study design played larger roles — wage changes, selection of participants, changes in supervision).

**Work:** activity producing something of value for others, done in exchange for compensation. *Contrast with* labor (physical effort), employment (paid work), *distinguish from* volunteering (unpaid work for others' benefit).

**Alienation (Durkheim/Marx context in organizational use):** sense of isolation from activity to which one should belong, or in which one should be involved; feeling that one's effort is meaningless or that one has little to contribute to the collective enterprise.

## Primary Sources and Evidence

### Hawthorne Studies
- **Mayo, E.** (1933). *The Human Problems of an Industrial Civilization.* Harvard University Press.
  - Original claim: observation (the independent variable being studied) had massive effect on productivity.
  - Reanalysis: **Levitt & List (2009)** and **Fernandez-Cornejo et al. (2016)** showed that wage increases, piece-rate incentives, and changes in group composition explained more variance than the "observation" effect alone.
  - Practical implication: being studied does change behavior (effect is real and significant), but the original study made it seem like the *primary* driver. Actually, multiple factors interact.
  - **Status:** seminal and cited correctly as foundational to organizational behavior as field. The myth is the magnitude, not the existence.

### Work Functions and Meaning
- **Terkel, S.** (1974). *Working: People Talk About What They Do All Day and How They Feel About What They Do.* Pantheon Books.
  - Qualitative source: real interviews with workers across occupations. Shows that satisfaction/meaning in work is multidimensional (not reducible to pay).
  - Used in chapter: excerpts from steelworker, receptionist, sanitation worker, policeman, accountant, process clerk to illustrate the non-economic functions of work.

- **Freud, S.** (1933). *New Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis.* Lecture XXXIII. Norton.
  - Quote on work binding individual to reality: "Work has a greater effect than any other technique of living in binding the individual more closely to reality; in his work he is at least securely attached to a part of reality, the human community."
  - Context: Freud was discussing work's psychological function (binding person to objective reality, providing structure, enabling self-regard through competence).

### Management Definition and Levels
- **Fayol, H.** (1916). *General and Industrial Management.* (English translation 1949)
  - Classical definition: management as planning, organizing, commanding, coordinating, controlling.
  - Source document: foundational to how management is still taught.
  - Comparison: Follett's definition ("art of getting things done through people") is more person-focused; Fayol's is more systems-focused. Both are still used.

- **Katz, R.** (1974). "Skills of an Effective Administrator." *Harvard Business Review*, 52(5), 34–56.
  - Primary source for three-skill model: technical, human, conceptual.
  - Finding: skill emphasis shifts by level (executives need more conceptual; first-line managers need more technical).
  - Method: survey of 404 managers in a large corporation; rated on effectiveness by superiors.
  - Limitation: single organization, 1970s data. Likely still valid but dated.

### Schools of Management Thought

**Scientific Management:**
- **Taylor, F.W.** (1911). *The Principles of Scientific Management.* Harper & Brothers.
  - Method: time-motion studies to find "one best way" to do a job.
  - Effect: did increase productivity; also created monotonous, deskilled work.
  - Critique in chapter: incomplete model of motivation (assumes people respond only to pay and threat).

**Human Relations School:**
- **Mayo, E.** (1933). *The Human Problems of an Industrial Civilization.* (See above.)
  - Advance: recognized that social and psychological factors affect productivity.
  - Limitation: assumed interests of workers and management aligned (they often do not).

**Contingency Theory:**
- **Burns, T., & Stalker, G.M.** (1961). *The Management of Innovation.* Tavistock.
  - Core finding: organizational structure should fit the environment (stable environments suit bureaucratic structures; turbulent environments suit organic structures).
  - Status: still used; foundational to contingency thinking.

**Systems Thinking:**
- **Nadler, D.A., & Tushman, M.L.** (1980). "A Model for Diagnosing Organizational Behavior." *Organizational Dynamics*, 9(2), 35–51.
  - Used in source material; directly quoted in section on complexity of organizational behavior.
  - Framework: inputs → transformation → outputs, with attention to individual, group, and organizational levels.

**Evidence-Based Management:**
- **Pfeffer, J., & Sutton, R.I.** (2006). *Hard Facts, Dangerous Half-Truths, and Total Nonsense.* Harvard Business School Press.
  - Argument: management practice should be grounded in research, not intuition or fad.
  - Status: emerging field; this book is anchored in this school's epistemology.

## Personality and Hiring — Myth Correction
- **MBTI:** No predictive validity for job performance. Widely used despite this.
  - Source: **Roberts, B.W., Kuncel, N.R., Shiner, R., Caspi, A., & Goldberg, L.R.** (2007). "The Power of Personality: The Comparative Validity of Personality Traits, Socioeconomic Status, and Cognitive Ability for Predicting Important Life Outcomes." *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 2(4), 313–345.
  - Finding: Big Five traits show modest predictive validity for job performance (r ~0.25-0.40); MBTI types show essentially zero.
  - Myth correction applied: chapter notes MBTI is popular but lacks evidence; Big Five is evidence-based but less intuitive.

- **Cognitive Ability / IQ testing:** Moderate to strong predictive validity for job performance, especially complex jobs.
  - Meta-analysis: Schmitt, N. (2014). "Personality and cognitive ability as predictors of effective performance at work." *Annual Review of Organizational Psychology and Organizational Behavior*, 1, 45–65.
  - Range: r ~0.30-0.50, varies by job complexity.

## Deep Mechanism Selected for Chapter

The **Hawthorne effect as a turning point in management thinking**: from efficiency-only to understanding that social/psychological conditions are inseparable from work itself.

Why this mechanism:
- It is historically important (marks the shift from scientific management to OB).
- It is teachable (experiment is simple; finding is counterintuitive).
- It is replicable (reader can imagine themselves as worker and recognize the effect).
- It generalizes (observation changing behavior is relevant to modern remote work, surveillance, async communication).
- It includes the correction (reanalysis shows original magnitude was overstated).
