## Cold opening selection and justification

Chose: **Kahneman's coin-flip experiment** over "CEO making acquisition bet" or "Challenger launch decision"

**Why:** The coin-flip is a clean, immediately comprehensible paradox. The investment professionals rejecting a +EV bet creates cognitive dissonance: these are smart people who should know the math. ## Structure and deep-dive selection

### Five concepts identified

1. System 1 / System 2 decision-making (reflective vs. reactive)
2. Bounded rationality and satisficing (Simon's contribution)
3. Seven barriers to effective decision-making (bounded rationality, escalation, time pressure, uncertainty, confirmation bias, relationship conflict, "similar to me" bias)
4. The six-step decision-making process (for nonprogrammed decisions)
5. Group decision-making and groupthink avoidance

### Deep-dive concept selected

**Kahneman's two-systems model + bounded rationality together as integrated mechanism.**

Why: The two systems explain *how* your brain decides. Bounded rationality explains *why* you cannot optimize. Together they explain the architecture of real-world managerial decision-making. The barriers (confirmation bias, anchoring, etc.) are specific failure modes of this architecture. The six-step process is a structured workaround. This creates a coherent theory of how decisions actually happen.

**Deep-dive depth:** Explain both systems from first principles. Show when each is appropriate. Show how bounded rationality creates the conditions for bias. Show how the six-step process compensates.

## Central analogy

**System 1 as "the brain's overnight security guard; System 2 as the building's architect."**

The security guard (System 1) runs on experience and pattern-matching. Most decisions can safely be delegated to them. They are fast and they know the building.

The architect (System 2) is deliberate, calculates carefully, draws blueprints. They are slow and expensive to consult, but they are necessary for novel problems.

The trouble: under stress, the architect gets tired. The security guard takes over. And the security guard is not equipped to design new buildings.

**Testing:** Does this break down? Yes. In novel situations (not just architectural problems, but novel types of problems the security guard has never seen), the metaphor holds. In well-understood, routine situations, it holds. The metaphor is not meant to capture the full complexity of neural architecture, just the functional difference.

## Specification moves needed

- **"Rational decision-making"** — what does it mean? (Perfect information + unlimited time + complete calculation of all outcomes. Never happens in reality.)

- **"Satisficing"** — not a failure mode. A recognition that "good enough" given your constraints is better than the impossible hunt for perfect.

- **"Heuristic"** — mental shortcut that is usually right. (Not "bias" — heuristics are useful. Biases are systematic errors.)

- **"Bounded rationality"** — not irrationality. The recognition that you are bounded by information, time, and cognitive capacity.

- **"Confirmation bias"** — not conscious cherry-picking. It is automatic filtering of what you notice.

- **"Escalation of commitment"** — not stubbornness. It is a failure to separate the sunk cost (emotionally heavy) from the forward-looking decision (what matters).

---

## Trade-offs to be named

1. **Fast decision-making vs. quality decision-making.** System 1 is fast but error-prone in novel situations. System 2 is accurate but slow and exhausting. You are always trading off.

2. **Satisficing vs. optimal.** You can hunt for the perfect solution (slow, expensive, might miss the opportunity), or you can find a good-enough solution and move on (fast, practical, might miss a genuinely better option).

3. **Group diversity vs. group cohesion.** Diverse groups make better decisions but are harder to manage (more conflict, more time spent negotiating). Homogeneous groups are harmonious but make weaker decisions.

4. **Information gathering vs. decision time.** More information helps, but gathering it takes time. At some point the cost of waiting exceeds the value of additional information.

## Common misconceptions to correct

1. **"Trusting your gut is good decision-making."** It is IF you have thousands of feedback-rich repetitions. Otherwise it is just confidence.

2. **"More information always helps."** False. After a point, more information is noise. The cost of gathering exceeds the value.

3. **"Rational actors always optimize."** False. Humans satisfy because optimization is impossible.

4. **"Emotions are the enemy of good decisions."** False. Emotions are data. But they are not all the data.

5. **"Group decisions are better than individual decisions."** True IF the group is diverse and genuinely permitted to disagree. False if the group is trying to harmonize.

# Teaching Notes — Chapter 6: Perception and Managerial Decision Making

*For instructors using this chapter with students. These notes cover learning outcomes, class activities, common student misconceptions, and discussion prompts.*

---

## Learning outcomes checklist

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

**[Identify]** the two systems their brain uses to make decisions and diagnose when each is appropriate.
- *Look for:* Student can name System 1 (fast, automatic, emotional) and System 2 (slow, deliberate, analytical) and explain why System 2 exhausts glucose and why this matters under time pressure.

**[Distinguish]** between programmed and nonprogrammed decisions and apply the correct decision-making structure to each.
- *Look for:* Student correctly identifies routine decisions (schedule, reorder supplies) vs. novel decisions (new market, merger). Can explain why nonprogrammed decisions need the six-step process.

**[Name]** the seven major barriers and build defenses against each.
- *Look for:* Student can identify confirmation bias, escalation of commitment, overconfidence, anchoring, time pressure, the "similar to me" bias, and relationship conflict in a case study or real scenario. Proposes structural or procedural defenses (diverse hiring committees, devil's advocates, etc.).

**[Execute]** the six-step decision-making process.
- *Look for:* Student can walk through a hypothetical decision, explicitly completing each step. Shows that they understand which steps people usually skip.

**[Recognize]** when emotions are signals worth listening to vs. when they are static.
- *Look for:* Student gives examples of emotions providing useful pattern-recognition data (gut discomfort about a partner that turns out to be warranted) vs. emotions that mislead (fear of change, preference for the familiar).

**[Conduct]** group decision-making without slipping into groupthink.
- *Look for:* Student can propose structures (devil's advocate, diverse teams, explicit permission to dissent) that prevent groupthink while capturing group benefits.

## Common student misconceptions and how to address them

### "Smart people don't have biases"

**The misconception:** Intelligence is a vaccine against cognitive bias. Successful people must not fall into these traps.

**Reality:** Intelligence correlates with *ability to rationalize* biases, not with absence of them. Smart people are actually more sophisticated at defending bad decisions. Kahneman and Tversky tested their theories on themselves and fell into the same traps.

**In class:** Have students complete the "Steve is a librarian or farmer" task themselves before revealing the answer. Most smart people get it wrong. Then ask: why? (Because they relied on representativeness heuristic and neglected base rates.) This shows that getting the right answer requires *structure*, not just smart thinking.

### "Trusting your gut is what successful people do"

**The misconception:** Intuition is the mark of a good leader. The best decisions come from instinct.

**Reality:** Intuition is reliable *only* in domains with regular, unambiguous feedback. A firefighter's gut about when a building will collapse is calibrated by thousands of close calls. A CEO's gut about which startup to fund is not (they might only see one outcome per decision). Successful people who "trust their gut" usually have domain expertise that calibrated that gut.

**In class:** Have students name someone they admire who talks about "following their intuition." Then ask: how much experience does this person have in the domain where they are intuiting? (Is it an experienced surgeon making a diagnosis? Or a first-time entrepreneur making a market prediction?) Then discuss: when is gut feeling trustworthy?

### "Analysis paralysis — overthinking kills decisions"

**The misconception:** Too much deliberation prevents action. The best managers are decisive, not analytical.

**Reality:** The bias is usually not toward too much analysis but toward too little. Research shows that managers usually spend 30 seconds generating alternatives (thinking of two options) when higher quality comes from generating five or six. The six-step process is not about slowness. It is about not skipping steps.

**In class:** Present a scenario: "A manager spent two weeks analyzing whether to hire candidate A or B. Was this too much analysis?" Ask students to debate. Then reveal: what did the manager actually do during those two weeks? (If they only gathered the same information over and over, yes, that is wasteful. If they systematically worked through the six steps, talked to the people who would supervise this person, checked references carefully, and structured a decision, that is good process.)

### "Groups make better decisions than individuals, so always use groups"

**The misconception:** More people → better thinking.

**Reality:** Groups can make better decisions OR worse decisions. It depends entirely on group dynamics. A diverse group with genuine permission to disagree outperforms individuals. A homogeneous group trying to be harmonious makes worse decisions. The structure matters as much as the group size.

**In class:** Show a groupthink case (Irving Janis's Bay of Pigs analysis works well). Ask students: what went wrong? Not "the group was stupid" but "what group dynamics prevented good decision-making?" Then ask: if you were redesigning this group, what would you change? (Answer: would have included someone not invested in the plan, devil's advocate, explicit permission to say "this will fail," etc.)

### "Emotions are the enemy — good decisions are logical"

**The misconception:** Feelings muddy thinking. Effective decision-making is purely rational.

**Reality:** People who have damage to emotional systems and cannot feel emotions make worse decisions, not better ones. Emotions are pattern recognition. They are data. The job is to distinguish between emotions that are signal (your gut discomfort about a partner comes from pattern recognition) vs. emotions that are noise (you prefer Supplier A because you know them, not because they are better).

**In class:** Ask students about a time they felt something was wrong and it turned out to be right. What was their system detecting? Then ask: a time they felt something was wrong and it turned out to be fine. What was that feeling? (Usually fear of change, preference for the familiar.) Then discuss: how do you learn the difference?

## In-class activities

### Activity 1: The Kahneman Coin-Flip (15 minutes)

**Setup:** Present the scenario from the chapter opening.

"A company faces two options:
- Option A: Guaranteed profit of $240,000
- Option B: 50% chance of $500,000, 50% chance of $0"

"Which would you choose?"

Have students vote and tally.

**Reveal:** "Most people choose A, the safe option. The expected value of B is $250,000, which is higher. But we hate the idea of coming away with nothing, so we choose the sure thing."

"Now imagine the problem as losses:
- Option A: Guaranteed loss of $240,000
- Option B: 50% chance of $500,000 loss, 50% chance of $0 loss"

"Which would you choose?"

Vote again. (Usually reverses — people choose the gamble.)

**Debrief:** This is prospect theory. Loss aversion. Your brain feels loss more sharply than gain, so the decision-making logic flips depending on how the problem is framed. This is not irrationality. It is how your wiring works. But it means the way a decision is *presented* can shift what you choose, even if the math is the same.

**Discussion:** When have you seen this in real organizations? (Accepting a sure cost to avoid the possibility of larger loss. Or gambling on a risky move because accepting the safe loss feels terrible.)

### Activity 2: Building a Hiring Committee (30 minutes)

**Setup:** You need to hire a new VP of Operations. The hiring team will be you and four other people. Who do you choose?

Have students propose the team composition: "I would choose the CFO, the current VP of Operations (retiring), two senior managers from my department, and the head of HR."

**Debrief:** This is a reasonable team, but who is NOT on the team? (The VP of Sales, who depends on operations. The head of the manufacturing plant, who deals with operations most directly. Someone from a different industry who might bring a fresh perspective on operations.) "Your team is all Operations-focused. They will all evaluate the candidate against 'will they run operations the way we currently do?' What are you missing? (Diverse perspectives. Someone who would ask 'what if we changed our approach?')"

Then redesign: bring in someone from outside the function. Someone from a different department. Someone from a competitor or different industry if possible. Require that the team include different genders, ages, career paths.

**Discussion:** What changes when you diversify the team? (More arguments. Longer decision process. But they notice things the homogeneous team missed. They question assumptions.) Is it worth the friction? (Yes, if the decision is important.)

### Activity 3: Spot the Bias (20 minutes)

**Setup:** Present a mini case study. Example:

"A manager has a direct report, Jane, whom they really like. Jane reminds them of themselves — same alma mater, same humor, same career path. Jane has been in the role for one year. The manager gives her very positive performance reviews. When Jane makes an error, the manager attributes it to external factors ('she was dealing with that difficult client'). When Jane has a success, the manager attributes it to Jane's competence.

Compare this to another report, Tom. The manager finds Tom's style a bit abrasive. When Tom makes an error, the manager attributes it to Tom's lack of attention to detail. When Tom has a success, the manager attributes it to luck or external help."

**Task:** What bias is at play? (Confirmation bias, similar-to-me bias, and attribution bias all together.) What is the defense? (Structured performance evaluation using the same criteria for everyone, requiring specific examples, getting 360 feedback.)

**Variation:** Give students different scenarios and have them identify the biases and propose structural defenses.

### Activity 4: The Six-Step Process in Action (45 minutes—good for a group project)

**Setup:** Present a real decision facing the organization (fictional or actual).

Example: "The company has been using the same email system for five years. The vendor announces they are discontinuing support. You need to decide: stay with a legacy system and hire a vendor to maintain it, migrate to Microsoft 365, or adopt a new alternative like Google Workspace."

**Task:** Students work in groups (3–4 people). Each group works through the six steps:

1. **Recognize the decision.** (Not hard — vendor is discontinuing support, we must act.)

2. **Generate alternatives.** Have them propose at least five options. This is the step most people skip, so push them.

3. **Analyze alternatives.** What information would they gather? Cost? Implementation time? Risk to email continuity? Integration with existing systems?

4. **Select an alternative.** Based on the information they gathered, which would they choose? Why?

5. **Implement.** How would they roll this out? How would they communicate it to staff?

6. **Evaluate.** Six months from now, how would they know if they made the right decision? What would success look like?

**Debrief:** Present each group's answer. Discuss where they diverged. "Group A chose to stay with the legacy system because the cost is lowest. Group B chose Microsoft 365 because of integration with other systems. Both are reasonable, given different priorities. What assumptions did each group make?" (About integration, about staff retraining, about long-term sustainability.)

## Discussion prompts

### On System 1 vs. System 2

"Describe a decision you made quickly (System 1) that turned out well. Why did it work? Do you have thousands of hours of experience in that domain? Now describe a quick decision that went badly. What was different?"

"When you are tired or hungry, your System 2 performance drops. How does this show up at work? Have you noticed people making worse decisions at the end of a long day? When do you make your own important decisions?"

### On bounded rationality

"Herbert Simon showed that managers do not optimize — they *satisfice*. They find a good-enough solution and move on. Is satisficing a bug or a feature? When is it better to satisfice than to optimize? When would optimizing be worth the time?"

"If you had perfect information, would you make better decisions? Or would you just have more information to get confused by?"

### On the seven barriers

"Which barrier do you think you are most vulnerable to? (Confirmation bias? Escalation of commitment? Overconfidence?) Why? What structural check could you build into your own decision-making process to guard against it?"

"Think about a company or organization that made a bad decision. Which barrier was at play? Was it a decision-making structure problem, or a people problem, or both?"

### On the six-step process

"The six-step process is simple. So why do managers skip steps? What pressures create that? When you skip steps, what usually goes wrong?"

"Which step is most often skipped? (Usually Step 2 — generating alternatives. Or Step 6 — evaluating effectiveness.) Why do you think?"

### On group dynamics

"Groupthink sounds like it is about the group being too agreeable. But it can also show up as a group that is very combative. How? (If the conflict is personal instead of process-focused, it can be as damaging as harmony. People stop listening to arguments and just fight.)"

"You are running a meeting and someone disagrees with the direction you are leaning. Your gut reaction is to defend your position. What is the better response? (Listen. Ask them what they are seeing that you are not. Assume they have useful information, even if it contradicts your intuition.)"

"How do you create a team culture where dissent is actually safe? Not just tolerated, but valued?"

## Assessment strategies

### Knowledge-level questions

1. Name the two systems your brain uses to make decisions and explain when each is appropriate.
2. What is satisficing, and how does it relate to bounded rationality?
3. List five barriers to effective decision-making and propose a structural defense for each.
4. Walk through the six-step decision-making process for a nonprogrammed decision of your choice.

### Application-level questions

1. You are a manager. A colleague is recommending a hiring candidate who went to the same university as you and reminds you of yourself. Your System 1 likes them. What questions would you ask to use System 2 to check this judgment?

2. Analyze the group decision-making in a real case (Bay of Pigs, Challenger, a corporate acquisition you know about). What group dynamics led to the poor decision? How would you have restructured the group?

3. You have invested six months and $500K in a project that is not working. New evidence suggests you should pivot. Walk through the barriers you would face (escalation, sunk-cost thinking, relationship conflict with people invested in the original plan) and how you would overcome them.

### Synthesis-level questions

1. Describe a decision-making process you observe in your current or former organization. What are the strengths? What are the weaknesses? What would you change to make it better? (What biases does it fail to guard against? What structural changes would help?)

2. Reflect on your own decision-making style. When do you rely on System 1? When do you switch to System 2? What patterns do you notice? What would make you more effective?

3. Design a decision-making process for a real high-stakes decision in an organization you know. Walk through each of the six steps. Where would you need to involve other people? How would you guard against groupthink while capturing the benefits of group input?

## Common teaching challenges

### "Students think biases only happen to other people"

**Challenge:** Students understand confirmation bias intellectually but do not see it in themselves. They think of it as something weak people do.

**Solution:** Have them complete a biased task (like the Steve/librarian problem) *before* explaining the bias, so they experience getting it wrong. Then discuss: "You are smart. You still got it wrong. That is the point of understanding biases — not because smart people are immune, but because understanding the mechanism helps you compensate."

### "The six-step process feels too rigid"

**Challenge:** Students worry that following a rigid process will slow them down and prevent bold action.

**Solution:** Reframe. "The process is not the opposite of speed. It is the difference between fast thinking and fast movement. You can work through the six steps quickly (in a meeting) or slowly (over weeks). The question is not fast vs. slow, but thoughtful vs. careless. The process is a checklist, not a prison."

### "Students default to individual decision-making examples"

**Challenge:** When discussing decisions, students mostly bring examples of their own individual choices.

**Solution:** Explicitly ask for organizational examples. "Think about a decision made by a group, a team, or an organization you know. How did the group dynamics shape the decision?" This shifts them to think about the collective dimensions of decision-making.

### "Students want a formula for 'the right answer'"

**Challenge:** Some students are frustrated that the chapter does not give them a simple rule to follow. They want to know: how do I make sure my decision is correct?

**Solution:** Name the reality. "This chapter teaches you how to make *good* decisions, not *perfect* ones. Perfect decisions are impossible. Your job as a manager is to make a defensible decision — one where you can explain your reasoning, where you have gathered relevant evidence, where you have considered alternatives, and where you have accounted for your own biases. Some will still go wrong. That is okay. That is the work."

## Extensions and deeper dives

### For students interested in behavioral economics

Point them to Daniel Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, which goes deep on prospect theory, anchoring, availability heuristic, and other biases. The book is accessible and filled with examples.

### For students interested in organizational culture and group decision-making

Irving Janis's *Victims of Groupthink* is the classic. Also recommend Sheryl Sandberg and Adam Grant's research on psychological safety in teams (Amy Edmondson's work on psychological safety is relevant here).

### For students interested in ethics and decision-making

Linda Trevino's work on ethical decision-making, and the specific discussion of moral sensitivity (recognizing that a decision has an ethical dimension) as the first step.

### For students interested in expertise and intuition

Gary Klein's *Sources of Power* explores when intuition is reliable and when it is not. The distinction between high-feedback-loop domains and low-feedback-loop domains is important.

- **Chapter 9 (Group dynamics):** Group decision-making builds on group cohesion, shared identity, intergroup conflict, etc.
- **Chapter 12 (Leadership):** Leaders set the decision-making culture. Openness to dissent, psychological safety, etc., are leader responsibilities.
- **Chapter 14 (Conflict and negotiation):** Many managerial decisions involve conflict. This chapter covers decision-making. Chapter 14 covers what to do when people disagree.

## Final thoughts for instructors

The research on decision-making is clear: process beats intelligence. A structured approach used consistently will outperform genius intuition used ad hoc. Students often resist this because they want to believe in their own judgment. Part of your job is to help them see that structure is not a cage. It is a tool that makes good judgment possible in high-stakes situations where intuition alone is not enough.

# Worked Example — Full Decision-Making in Practice: Supplier Selection Under Uncertainty

**Context:** You are the head of procurement for a mid-sized manufacturing company. Your primary supplier of a critical component has indicated they will raise prices 15% next year due to material costs. You need to decide whether to accept the increase, switch suppliers, or pursue other options.

**Timeline:** You have six weeks to decide. Implementation would take 8–12 weeks if you choose to switch.

---

## Step 1: Recognize that a decision needs to be made

**What happens:** Your supplier announces the price increase. You feel a mix of frustration (they are raising prices without advance notice) and anxiety (you do not know how to respond). Some team members say "we have to switch." Others say "let's just absorb the increase." No one is clear on what the options actually are.

**The trap:** Delaying the decision hoping the supplier will back down. Or making a quick decision to switch without understanding the true cost of switching.

**What you do:** Call a meeting. Say out loud: "This is a real decision. We have six weeks. By [date], we will have made a choice." You set a decision deadline. You assign someone to lead the analysis. You make it clear this is not a problem to be ignored or wished away.

---

## Step 2: Generate multiple alternatives

**What emerges:**

1. **Accept the increase.** Pass the cost on to customers through price increases. Risk: customers might leave.

2. **Negotiate with current supplier.** Accept a smaller increase in exchange for a longer contract. Risk: leaves you dependent on them.

3. **Switch to supplier B.** You know them. They can match the current price. Risk: they will probably raise prices too once you are locked in.

4. **Develop a second source.** Use them for 30% of volume while staying with current supplier for 70%. Risk: neither supplier is happy about this arrangement. Both might raise prices.

5. **Vertical integration.** Bring manufacturing in-house. Risk: huge capital investment. Might never make sense financially.

6. **Redesign the product to use a different component.** Completely different supply chain. Risk: the redesign takes time. Customers might not like the change. The new component might have its own supply issues.

7. **Move manufacturing to a different country where this component is cheaper.** Risk: currency risk, regulatory risk, quality risk, logistical complexity.

**The work:** Do not stop here. Your team generates five more. The worst ones — like "move manufacturing" — will eventually be eliminated. But by generating them, you have surfaced the strategic question: how much are we willing to change our business model?

---

## Step 3: Analyze the alternatives

You now have ten options. You need to evaluate each one.

**For "accept the increase":**
- Current volume: 100,000 units/year at $10/unit = $1M/year
- After 15% increase: $1.15M/year = $150K additional cost
- Can you raise customer prices 5–10%? That would offset most of the increase. But do customers have alternatives? Survey three major customers. They say they can tolerate a 3% price increase but not more.
- That covers $30K of the $150K increase. You would need to find $120K of cost savings elsewhere, or accept a margin reduction.
- Implication: this is viable if you can find offsetting savings. Probably not viable otherwise.

**For "negotiate with current supplier":**
- Call them. What is the most favorable deal you can negotiate? They are willing to do 8% increase instead of 15% if you sign a three-year contract.
- That is $80K additional cost per year for three years = $240K total.
- Risk: you are locked in. If material costs drop, you still pay 8%. If a better supplier emerges, you cannot switch.
- Implication: mathematically less expensive than accepting the full increase, but you lose flexibility. Only makes sense if you believe material costs will stay high.

**For "switch to supplier B":**
- You call them. Can they handle your volume at current prices? They say yes, but their lead time is 16 weeks, not your current 8 weeks.
- What does that mean? You would need to increase your inventory to handle the extra lead time. Current inventory carrying cost is 15% of component cost.
- Extra inventory for 8 weeks × $10/unit × 100,000 units/year ÷ 52 weeks × 15% = $23K/year
- You also have switching costs: testing the new supplier's quality, retraining your line workers, potential scrap as they ramp up. Estimate: $80K one-time.
- Implication: switching saves you the $150K/year but costs $23K/year in extra inventory plus $80K upfront. Payback in six months. This is starting to look economical.

**For "second source":**
- Supplier B takes 30% volume. Supplier A keeps 70% but now agrees to a smaller increase: 10% instead of 15% to keep the remaining volume (because losing 30% of volume is painful for them).
- Supplier A: 70% × $1M × 1.10 = $770K
- Supplier B: 30% × $1M = $300K
- Total: $1.07M (vs. $1.15M with accepting the full increase, or $1.15M with Supplier B alone)
- But now you have two supply chains to manage. Quality variance. Two sets of relationships. Operational complexity.
- Implication: this is a middle path. You save $80K but you gain complexity. Probably makes sense only if you believe supplier B might fail or prices might increase.

Now you are building a real picture. You have numbers. You have trade-offs. The analysis is not complete, but it is good enough to decide.

---

## Step 4: Select an alternative

Look at the evidence:

- **Accept the increase:** $150K/year cost. Feasible only if you can pass it to customers (unlikely in full).
- **Negotiate:** $80K/year cost. Locked in for three years.
- **Switch:** $150K/year cost savings, but $80K upfront cost and $23K/year inventory carrying cost. Net: $127K/year savings after two years.
- **Second source:** $80K/year cost savings plus operational complexity.

You also consider strategic factors:
- Supplier A has been with you for 10 years. There is value in that stability.
- Supplier B has good quality but is less experienced with your business.
- Material costs are rising globally. This suggests costs will stay high. A fixed price is attractive. A flexibility is also attractive.

Here is what you decide: **Pursue a two-step approach.** First, negotiate with Supplier A for a smaller increase (target: 8–10% instead of 15%) in exchange for a two-year contract. Simultaneously, qualify Supplier B. If negotiations with Supplier A fail, you have a backup.

Why this approach? You get the stability and relationship benefit of staying primarily with Supplier A, but you reduce the risk by having an alternative ready. You are not fully switching (which would be disruptive) but you are not passively accepting a large increase (which would hurt margins).

---

## Step 5: Implement the decision

This is where things get real.

**Call Supplier A.** Explain your analysis. You have done the work. You know their increase would cost you $150K/year. You have evaluated alternatives. You are willing to stay with them if they move closer to 8%. Be honest. Say: "I prefer to work with you, but I need you to work with me on price." Give them 10 days to respond.

**Simultaneously, begin qualification of Supplier B.** Get samples. Test quality. Confirm lead times. Talk to their other customers. This is not just a fallback. You are genuinely evaluating them as a potential long-term supplier.

**Get input from your team.** The logistics people might see issues with Supplier B that you do not. The quality people have experience with both suppliers. The sales people know whether customers will tolerate a change in supplier (it sometimes matters where a component comes from).

**Communicate with leadership.** If the increase is material to the business, senior leadership should know that you are handling it. They should know the options you considered and the approach you chose.

---

## Step 6: Evaluate the effectiveness

Two months later, Supplier A comes back with an offer of 10% increase (not 8%, but better than 15%). You accept, but you maintain the Supplier B qualification. The new cost is $1.1M/year (up $100K from the current baseline).

**Did you decide well?**

In the moment, you made a reasonable decision based on the information you had. But the real evaluation comes over time.

- **Six months later:** Material costs globally drop 5%. Supplier A's costs drop with them. Do they volunteer to reduce their prices? Probably not. You are locked in. If you had switched, Supplier B might have passed through the savings. Or they might not have. You will never know. This is the trade-off of choosing stability: you miss some of the upside.

- **One year later:** Supplier B has a quality issue with another customer. Their capacity gets constrained. If you had switched fully, you would have been in trouble. The qualification process you ran is looking smart.

- **Two years later:** Supplier A wants to renegotiate again. You have evidence that they did not actually face cost pressure because material costs are now stable. You push back harder. The relationship is still good because you chose negotiation over switching.

The evaluation is not "did we make the perfect decision?" The evaluation is "did we make a reasonable decision, did we execute it well, and did we learn from it?"

---

## The decision-making skill this demonstrates

This example shows the decision-making process working as intended:

1. **Recognized** the decision needed to be made (did not delay or hope it would go away).
2. **Generated** multiple alternatives (not just "switch or stay").
3. **Analyzed** them with data (not just gut feeling).
4. **Chose** based on explicit trade-offs (stability + negotiating power vs. lowest cost).
5. **Implemented** with communication and buy-in.
6. **Evaluated** over time to learn.

The decision was not perfect. But it was defensible. And that is what skill looks like.
