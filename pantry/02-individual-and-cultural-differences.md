# Pantry: Chapter 2 — Individual and Cultural Differences

## Research notes and source tracking

### Sources consulted

1. **Big Five Personality Framework**
   - Costa, P. T., & McCrae, R. R. (1992). Four ways five factors are basic. *Personality and Individual Differences*, 13(6), 653-665.
   - McCrae, R. R., & Costa, P. T., Jr. (2008). The five-factor theory of personality. In O. P. John, R. W. Robins, & L. A. Pervin (Eds.), *Handbook of personality: Theory and research* (3rd ed., pp. 159-181). Guilford Press.
   - Barrick, M. R., & Mount, M. K. (1991). The Big Five personality dimensions and job performance: A meta-analysis. *Personnel Psychology*, 44(1), 1-26.
   - Evidence: Meta-analyses confirm conscientiousness predicts job performance across fields with effect sizes r = 0.30-0.40. Generalizability across job types is strong.

2. **Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) Validity**
   - Pittenger, D. J. (1993). The utility of the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator. *Review of Educational Research*, 63(4), 467-488.
   - Capraro, R. M., & Capraro, M. M. (2002). Myers-Briggs Type Indicator score reliability across studies: A meta-analytic reliability generalization study. *Educational and Psychological Measurement*, 62(4), 590-602.
   - Boyle, G. J. (1995). Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI): Some psychometric limitations. *Australian Psychologist*, 30(1), 71-74.
   - Evidence: MBTI test-retest reliability is moderate (r = 0.59-0.82 over 4-5 weeks, per Capraro & Capraro). When retested, approximately 50% of respondents receive a different four-letter type. No predictive validity for job performance demonstrated. Pittenger's review found limited empirical support.

3. **Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions**
   - Hofstede, G. (1980). *Culture's consequences: International differences in work-related values*. Sage.
   - Hofstede, G., Hofstede, G. J., & Minkov, M. (2010). *Cultures and organizations: Software of the mind* (3rd ed.). McGraw-Hill.
   - Original IBM data: surveyed employees across 50+ countries in 1966-1973. Sample sizes and methodology notes below.
   - Six dimensions (original four, then added two later): Power Distance, Individualism-Collectivism, Masculinity-Femininity, Uncertainty Avoidance, Long-Term Orientation, Indulgence-Restraint.

4. **GLOBE Study (Global Leadership and Organizational Behavior Effectiveness)**
   - House, R. J., Hanges, P. J., Javidan, M., Dorfman, P. W., & Gupta, V. (Eds.). (2004). *Culture, leadership, and organizations: The GLOBE study of 62 societies*. Sage.
   - Surveyed 17,300+ managers in 62 cultures. Nine dimensions (overlapping but not identical to Hofstede).
   - Key finding: practices and values diverge. Nations practice high power distance while valuing lower power distance (espoused vs. enacted values).

5. **Mental Abilities in Organizational Performance**
   - Hunter, J. E., & Hunter, R. F. (1984). Validity and utility of alternative predictors of job performance. *Psychological Bulletin*, 96(1), 72-98.
   - Schmidt, F. L., & Hunter, J. E. (1998). The validity and utility of selection methods in personnel psychology: Practical and theoretical implications of 85 years of research findings. *Psychological Bulletin*, 124(2), 262-274.
   - Finding: Cognitive ability (general mental ability) is the best single predictor of job performance across job types. Effect sizes r = 0.50-0.60 uncorrected. Combined with conscientiousness, predictive validity improves.

6. **Cognitive Complexity in Decision-Making and Leadership**
   - Schroder, H. M., Driver, M. J., & Streufert, S. (1967). *Human information processing*. Holt, Rinehart and Winston.
   - Mitchell, T. R. (1970). Cognitive complexity and leadership style. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 16(2), 166-174.
   - Finding: Managers high in cognitive complexity seek more information, process more information, integrate discrepant information, generate more decision alternatives, and use more complex decision strategies.

7. **Physical and Psychomotor Abilities**
   - McCormick, E. J., & Tiffin, J. (1976). *Industrial psychology* (6th ed.). Prentice-Hall.
   - Florida Power case: Feuer, D., & Lee, C. (1988). The Kaizen Connection: How companies pick tomorrow's workers. *Training*, 25(5), 23-35.
   - Finding: Florida Power's 16-hour, 12-test selection process for lineperson jobs reduced turnover from 43% to 4.5% and saved $1 million net.

8. **Generational Differences in Work Values**
   - Twenge, J. M., Campbell, S. M., Hoffman, B. J., & Lance, C. E. (2010). Generational differences in work values: A meta-analysis. *Journal of Organizational Behavior*, 31(2-3), 279-290.
   - Finding: Generational differences in work values exist but are modest (effect sizes typically d = 0.2-0.3, small to medium). Effect sizes for birth year within generations often exceed those between generations. The evidence does not support strong "generational cohorts want different things" claims.

9. **Personality Development and Stability**
   - Roberts, B. W., Kuncel, N. R., Shiner, R., Caspi, A., & Goldberg, L. R. (2007). The power of personality: The comparative validity of personality traits, socioeconomic status, and cognitive ability for predicting important life outcomes. *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 2(4), 313-345.
   - Jackson, J. J., Hill, P. L., Payne, B. R., Stine-Morrow, E. A., & Roberts, B. W. (2012). Can an old dog learn (and want to experience) new tricks? Cognitive training increases openness to experience in older adults. *Psychology and Aging*, 27(2), 286-292.
   - Finding: Big Five factors show moderate stability (test-retest correlations r = 0.70-0.80 year to year), but also show meaningful change across the lifespan, particularly conscientiousness increasing with age.

10. **Personality Trait Predictions**
    - Boyle, G. J., Matthews, G., & Saklofske, D. H. (Eds.). (2015). *Measures of personality and social psychological constructs*. Academic Press.
    - Judge, T. A., Rodell, J. B., Klinger, R. L., Simon, L. S., & Crawford, E. R. (2013). Hierarchical representations of job characteristics and the personality relationships with job satisfaction and engagement. *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 98(4), 565-579.
    - Summary: Conscientiousness predicts job performance; agreeableness predicts cooperation and coworker satisfaction; extraversion predicts social engagement and leadership nomination; neuroticism predicts stress-related distress; openness predicts creativity and adaptation.

11. **Hofstede Power Distance and Managerial Behavior**
    - Laurent, A. (1983). The cultural diversity of western conceptions of management. *International Studies of Management and Organization*, 12(1-2), 75-96.
    - Key study: Asked managers from 10 countries whether they should have precise answers to subordinate questions. USA: 18% said yes. France: 40%. Japan: 78%. Germany: 44%.
    - Interpretation: High power-distance cultures expect managers to have answers. Low power-distance cultures are comfortable with "let's figure this out together."

12. **Personality and Hiring Validity**
    - Dilchert, S., Ones, D. S., Davis, R. D., & Rostow, A. B. (2007). Cognitive ability predicts objectively measured physical fitness. *Journal of Individual Differences*, 28(1), 10-19.
    - Morgeson, F. P., Campion, M. A., Dipboye, R. L., Hollenbeck, J. R., Murphy, K., & Schmit, M. J. (2007). Reconsidering the use of personality tests in personnel selection contexts. *Personnel Psychology*, 60(3), 683-729.
    - Finding: Personality tests can improve hiring decisions if used to predict traits that matter for the job. MBTI does not improve decisions. Big Five with job-relevant focus does.

### Core concepts identified

1. **Ability vs. motivation distinction.** Job performance = f(ability, motivation, opportunity). A person can be highly motivated but unable. A person can be able but unmotivated. These require different interventions.

2. **Mental ability and cognitive complexity.** Cognitive complexity in particular predicts how much information is sought, how many options considered, integration of discrepant information. This is the mechanism underlying decision-making quality.

3. **Big Five as empirically convergent.** Five factors kept emerging from independent studies. This convergence is unusual in personality psychology and suggests something real. The Big Five are spectra, not types.

4. **MBTI as popular but invalid.** Its popularity stems from face validity (it feels true) and the intuitive appeal of types. Its problems: weak test-retest reliability, lack of convergent validity with Big Five, no predictive validity for important outcomes.

5. **Hofstede as descriptive of averages, not individuals.** Japan scores high on uncertainty avoidance (as a culture). Japan contains millions of people, some highly ambiguity-averse and some comfortable with it. The average masks individual variation.

6. **Cross-cultural management as recognizing different starting points.** A low power-distance manager in a high power-distance organization is not wrong; they are working from a different cultural script. Adapting means understanding the script, not abandoning their values.

7. **Personality change is real but modest.** Conscientiousness increases with age and responsibility. Neuroticism can decrease with therapy. But these changes are modest relative to individual differences at any point in time.

### Trade-offs documented in chapter

- **Ability-based hiring:** Upfront cost of testing vs. better fit and lower turnover.
- **Big Five vs. MBTI:** Big Five has validity but is more neutral (not tied to "types"). MBTI is appealing but unfounded.
- **Culture as category:** Hofstede's dimensions are efficient for cross-cultural briefing. But they obscure individual variation and practice-values gaps.
- **Conscientiousness:** Predicts job performance but might be suboptimal in some contexts (a conscientious person might overthink rapid-decision roles).

### Myths corrected by name

1. **"MBTI is scientifically valid"** — No. Test-retest reliability is moderate. No predictive validity for job outcomes. Popular ≠ valid.

2. **"Different generations want fundamentally different things from work"** — Weak support. Variation within generations exceeds variation between generations.

3. **"Personality determines behavior"** — Personality influences behavior, but context is powerful. Same trait, different behaviors in different contexts.

4. **"Cultural dimensions determine individual behavior"** — They describe average patterns. Within-culture variation is large. Individual factors (ability, personality, values) often outweigh cultural background.

5. **"Personality is fixed"** — Relatively stable, but changeable. Conscientiousness increases with maturity. Neuroticism responds to skill development and coping.
