# Chapter 13 — Case Studies: Power and Politics in Action

## Case 1: The Challenger Disaster — Unethical Use of Coercive Power

**Context:** January 1986. Morton Thiokol, a major defense contractor, manufactured booster rockets for the Space Shuttle Challenger.

**The Power Dynamic:** Morton Thiokol engineers had warned repeatedly during the design phase and during production that the booster seals were unsafe in cold weather. These warnings were not heeded. The organization — through management — exercised coercive power to suppress the warnings.

**What Happened:** After the shuttle exploded at a cost of seven lives, congressional investigators found that Morton Thiokol engineers who had testified before Congress about the safety problems were abruptly transferred to undesirable assignments. The transfers were retaliation for whistleblowing — an explicit use of coercive power to punish dissent.

**The Mechanism:** The organization used coercive power (threat of transfer, removal from valued work) to enforce silence. This is an example of power used unethically — not toward organizational goals but toward suppressing information that the organization should have heard.

**Lesson:** Coercive power is effective in the short term (it silences dissent), but it is corrosive long-term (it prevents the organization from learning about real problems). Organizations that rely on coercive power to maintain control often fail catastrophically because they cannot hear bad news.

## Case 2: Adobe's Check-In Performance Review — Managing Politics Through System Design

**Context:** 2012. Adobe, a major software company, was struggling with its performance management system. Traditional annual reviews were creating several problems: politics (managers dreading difficult conversations and avoiding them), bias (annual reviews creating opportunity for political maneuvering and favoritism), and misalignment (feedback delayed so long it was not actionable).

**The Political Problem:** The annual review cycle created conditions for political behavior: ambiguous criteria, concentrated decision-making at the end of year, opportunity for managers to rewrite history or play favorites. Employees were frustrated because surprises in reviews suggested the appraisal was political rather than merit-based.

**What They Did:** Adobe replaced the annual review with "Check-in" — frequent, brief check-in conversations between manager and employee. Feedback was immediate, bidirectional, and not tied to a single high-stakes judgment. The review criteria became: what did we agree on, how is it going, what needs to adjust?

**The Mechanism:** By reducing ambiguity (clear criteria, immediate feedback), reducing competition (no zero-sum ranking system), and making decisions transparent and frequent (not annual and concentrated), Adobe reduced the conditions that trigger political behavior.

**Results:** Higher engagement, higher satisfaction with feedback, and fewer negative surprises. The system did not eliminate political behavior (some always exists), but it made political behavior more costly and made meritocratic evaluation more possible.

**Lesson:** You cannot eliminate politics, but you can manage the structural conditions that spawn it. Transparency, frequent feedback, and clear criteria are more powerful than exhortations to "be less political."

---

## Case 3: General Electric's Leadership Crisis — Strategic Contingencies Shift

**Context:** 2017-2018. General Electric, long a pillar of American manufacturing, faced a period of turbulent leadership change.

**The Power Dynamic:** Jeff Immelt, the long-time CEO, had built his reputation and power on operational discipline and control. This worked when GE's strategic contingency was stable manufacturing and gradual growth. But the firm was struggling in a tech-dominated economy. The board decided change was needed.

**What Happened:** John Flannery was appointed CEO to drive transformation. His strategy: cut dividends, restructure unprofitable business lines, prepare for a smaller but more focused company. This was the right strategy given the changed environment. But Flannery had limited political capital (he was new, from outside). After one year, with the market punishing the stock, the board lost patience and removed him, absorbing a $23 billion loss in the process.

**The Strategic Contingency Shift:** The environment had shifted. The old contingency (manufacturing excellence) no longer determined survival. The new contingency (agility in a tech-driven market) did. But the organization's power structure had not adapted to the new contingency. People still believed in the old way.

**What Actually Worked:** Lawrence Culp, an outsider with a track record of transformation at Danaher, took over as the first external CEO. His power came not from long internal relationships but from demonstrated success at the exact transformation GE needed. The stock soared because Culp's expertise aligned with the new strategic contingency.

**Lesson:** When strategic contingencies shift, power must redistribute. Organizations that fail to redistribute power to the people who understand the new contingency often fail. Culp succeeded because he understood the new contingency (how to operate in a tech-dominated market) better than anyone inside GE.

## Case 4: The VP with Authority but No Power — Power Dependencies in Practice

**Context:** A large financial-services firm hires a new VP of Human Resources from a well-known tech company. The VP's mandate: streamline hiring from 6 weeks to 3 weeks.

**The Authority Problem:** The VP has authority (title, budget, org-chart power). But to accomplish the goal, the VP must change the behavior of background-check companies, recruiting teams, hiring managers, and candidates. The VP controls none of these directly.

**What Happened:** The VP proposed solutions: new background-check vendor, tighter timelines, candidates pre-screened. Each proposed change hit resistance. The recruiting team said background checks are not their responsibility. The hiring managers said they could not move faster without compromising quality. The background-check company said four weeks is the legal minimum.

**The Root Cause:** The VP was highly dependent on people and systems the VP did not control. In power-dependency terms, the VP had modest power but the targets of influence (recruiting team, hiring managers, background-check vendors) were not highly dependent on the VP. They could comply partially or ignore the VP entirely.

**What Actually Worked:** A VP who succeeded in the same role did three things. First, she made the hiring-speed improvement a personal priority for herself and published results weekly. (Commitment rather than compliance.) Second, she worked with IT to build tools that eliminated bottlenecks rather than asking people to work faster. (Removing the obstacle rather than demanding different behavior.) Third, she convinced senior leadership to hold hiring managers accountable for time-to-hire as a key metric. (Shifting the power structure so that compliance was rewarded and resistance was costly.)

**Lesson:** Authority without power is ineffective. To move an organization, you must either have genuine power (control over resources people depend on) or must build it through commitment, by making people want to help you succeed.

## Case 5: The Politics of Innovation Inside Large Corporations

**Context:** A product team inside a large corporation has an idea for a new product that could cannibalize existing product lines. The team believes the new product is strategically important for the company's long-term survival. But the existing business unit that owns the affected products sees it as a threat.

**The Political Dynamic:** Innovation often triggers political behavior because it creates scarce resources (development budget, engineering time), because goals conflict (grow the new product vs. protect the existing one), and because it threatens existing power bases (the existing business unit's budget and influence).

**What Happens:** The innovation team faces political resistance:
- The existing business unit emphasizes the risk to current revenue
- Subject-matter experts highlight technical problems that seem insurmountable
- The innovation idea is tabled "pending further study"
- Budget is allocated but to the existing business unit, not the innovation team

**Why It Happens:** From the existing business unit's perspective, they are protecting the organization's core business. From the innovation team's perspective, they are pursuing the future. Both are rational positions. But the resource-allocation politics favors the existing unit because it controls current revenue and has established power.

**Common Outcomes:** Most innovations proposed inside large organizations never make it to market. Not because they are bad ideas, but because they trigger political resistance from the units they threaten. This is why so much innovation comes from startups and entrepreneurs rather than from large corporations with all the resources to innovate. Paradoxically, the existence of clear power structures and established budgets can actually inhibit innovation.

**Lesson:** Politics can be rational (protecting current business) and still be corrosive to long-term organizational success. Organizations that want to innovate must either create separate units insulated from the existing power structure, or must shift the power structure to make innovation a strategic contingency that gets rewarded rather than resisted.

## Case 6: Executive Assistants and Counterpower

**Context:** A large technology company. Executive assistants (EAs) to senior leaders are increasingly critical. They manage calendars, handle confidential information, serve as sounding boards, and often know how the executive actually thinks about various issues.

**The Power Dynamic:** Formally, the EA has no authority. The EA reports to the executive. The executive can fire the EA. But informally, the EA has considerable power. This is counterpower.

**Sources of the EA's Power:**
- Information control: The EA knows who talks to the executive and when
- Access control: The EA controls the executive's calendar
- Expertise: The EA knows how the executive operates and what the executive will and won't tolerate
- Scarcity: High-quality EAs are hard to find and expensive to replace

**What This Means Practically:** A skilled EA can significantly affect what an executive actually learns about (by controlling email and meetings), what an executive prioritizes (by controlling calendar), and even what an executive decides (by how they frame issues). An executive who has a bad relationship with their EA is often less effective than an executive with a strong partnership with their EA.

**The Ethical Dimension:** EAs wield this power without formal authority. The question is whether they use it toward organizational goals (helping the executive be more effective) or personal ends (protecting their own position). Most do the former, but the asymmetry is worth recognizing.

**Lesson:** Power accrues to whoever controls scarce resources and manages uncertainty, regardless of formal position. The EA controls access and information, which are scarce and valuable. The organization should recognize this power and ensure EAs are treated as professionals, not as administrative staff. The best executives build genuine partnerships with their EAs rather than thinking of them as subordinates.

# Chapter 13 — Glossary: Power and Politics

## Core Concepts

**Power** — The ability to cause another person or group to take an action they would not otherwise take. Not dependent on formal position. Two people matter: the power holder and the target of influence.

**Authority** — The right to seek compliance. Legitimate power. Backed by organizational position and group acceptance of that position's authority. Constrained by role boundaries.

**Leadership** — The ability to elicit responses from others that go beyond required or mechanical compliance. Voluntary engagement. Distinguished from headship (the position) and from power (the raw ability).

**Commitment** — The behavioral response to referent or expert power. The person is motivated to help you succeed, applies discretionary effort, and suggests improvements.

**Compliance** — The behavioral response to legitimate or reward power. The person does what is asked, either from duty or expectation of reward, but does not engage discretionary effort.

**Resistance** — The behavioral response to coercive power. The person does the minimum required to avoid punishment and looks for ways to circumvent the rule.

---

## The Five Bases of Power (French and Raven, 1959)

**Referent Power** — Power based on personal appeal, admiration, or the target's desire to be similar to the power holder. Also called charisma. Generates commitment responses. Example: Junior employees imitating senior employees they admire.

**Expert Power** — Power based on knowledge or skill that others need. Domain-specific and durable as long as expertise is scarce and relevant. Generates commitment responses. Example: A senior surgeon in the operating room.

**Legitimate Power** — Power based on the right to exercise control, granted by formal position or role. Also called authority. Bounded by role and acceptable by group consensus. Generates compliance responses. Example: A supervisor assigning work within scope.

**Reward Power** — Power based on control over things people want (pay, promotion, status, good assignments, opportunity). Generates compliance responses. Often less available than it appears on org charts. Example: A manager controlling hire decisions.

**Coercive Power** — Power based on the ability to punish, through firing, demotion, undesirable assignment, or withholding rewards. Can be physical threat or social exclusion. Generates resistance responses. Example: A manager implicitly threatening to fire someone for disagreement.

---

## Power Dependencies and Counterpower

**Power Dependency** — The state in which one person or group depends on another for outcomes they value and cannot easily obtain elsewhere. Determines susceptibility to influence.

**Counterpower** — The ability of the target of influence to exercise power in return, reducing the other's influence. Protects against one-sided dependence. Example: A union protecting members from a manager's coercive power.

---

## Strategic Contingencies Model

**Strategic Contingency** — A requirement of one unit's work that is affected by another unit's work. A source of uncertainty in the decision-making process. Becomes strategic when it affects the balance of interdepartmental power.

**Nonsubstitutability** — The degree to which a unit's contribution is difficult to replace. High nonsubstitutability = high power (few alternatives available). Low nonsubstitutability = low power (many alternatives available).

**Work Centrality** — The degree to which a unit's work is interconnected with others' work. Measured by workflow pervasiveness (how much other units depend on this unit) and workflow immediacy (how quickly this unit's work affects organizational output).

**Coping with Uncertainty** — The primary source of subunit power. One unit gains power by helping another unit reduce the uncertainty associated with its work. Three mechanisms: preventing/forestalling uncertainty, providing/collecting information, absorbing pressures.

---

## Political Behavior

**Politics** — Those activities taken within organizations to acquire, develop, and use power and other resources to obtain one's preferred outcomes in a situation in which there is uncertainty or dissensus about choices. (Pfeffer)

**Political Behavior** — Activity initiated for the purpose of overcoming opposition or resistance. Emerges predictably under specific structural conditions.

---

## Conditions That Increase Political Behavior

**Ambiguous Goals** — When organizational goals are unclear or conflict with each other, people have room to pursue personal agendas under the guise of pursuing organizational goals.

**Scarce Resources** — When resources are limited, someone wins and someone loses. Rational for people to engage in political behavior to protect their area.

**Nonprogrammed Decisions** — Decisions not governed by routine policy have more ambiguity and therefore more opportunity for political maneuvering.

**Organizational Change** — During restructures, mergers, new product launches, or strategic shifts, normal power structures are malleable. People compete for influence in the new structure.

**Complex or Uncertain Environment** — When technology is uncertain, markets shift rapidly, or regulations change, different units can argue for different strategies, each claiming legitimacy.

---

## Power Tactics

**Controlling Access to Information** — Managing what information people see, thereby shaping decisions. Example: Only personnel and senior management know actual salary information.

**Controlling Access to Persons** — Gatekeeping who can talk to decision-makers. Example: An executive assistant controlling the CEO's calendar.

**Selective Use of Objective Criteria** — Choosing the decision criteria that favor your preferred outcome. Example: One group emphasizing teaching credentials in faculty hiring while another emphasizes research output.

**Controlling the Agenda** — Ensuring unwanted proposals never come up for a vote, or are tabled until they die of neglect.

**Using Outside Experts** — Commissioning studies or hiring consultants known to support your position, then using their report as "objective evidence."

**Coalitions and Alliances** — Combining power with other units that share interests to increase collective influence. Example: Multiple unions forming a coalition to bargain collectively.

**Bureaucratic Gamesmanship** — Using the organization's own rules as weapons. Example: "Work to rule" — following every policy to the letter, which often grinds operations to a halt.

---

## Resource Dependence Model

**Resource Dependence** — When one subunit controls scarce, valued resources that another subunit needs, and there are no easy alternatives, the controlling unit acquires power.

**The Rich Get Richer** — Once a unit has power from securing resources, it uses that power to secure more resources, creating a self-reinforcing cycle.

---

## Common Symbols of Managerial Power (Rosabeth Moss Kanter)

- Ability to intercede favorably on behalf of someone in trouble
- Ability to secure desirable placements for talented subordinates
- Ability to get approval for expenditures beyond budget
- Ability to secure above-average salary increases
- Ability to get items on the agenda at policy meetings
- Quick access to top decision-makers
- Early information about decisions and policy shifts
- Being asked for opinions on important questions
- Being invited to important meetings
- Being greeted by senior leaders

# Chapter 13 — Research Notes: Power and Politics

## Primary Sources and Key Studies

### French and Raven (1959) — The Five Bases of Power

**Citation:** French, J. R. P., & Raven, B. (1959). The bases of social power. In D. Cartwright (Ed.), Studies in social power (pp. 150–167). Institute for Social Research.

**Why It Matters:** This is the foundational framework. 60+ years later, it remains the standard model for understanding bases of power. The distinction between referent, expert, legitimate, reward, and coercive power has held up remarkably well.

**What the Research Shows:**
- Each base of power generates different behavioral responses in targets
- Expert and referent power tend to generate commitment
- Legitimate and reward power tend to generate compliance
- Coercive power tends to generate resistance
- The same person can use multiple bases simultaneously

**Limitations and Revisions:**
- The model was tested primarily in laboratory settings; field validation has been strong but not universal
- Later research (Podsakoff & Schriesheim, 1985) suggested two additional bases that gained traction: information power and connection power
- The model does not account for how bases of power interact or trade off against each other

---

### Salancik and Pfeffer (1974) — Resource Dependence Model

**Citation:** Pfeffer, J., & Salancik, G. R. (1978). The external control of organizations: A resource dependence perspective. Harper & Row.

**Why It Matters:** This book established the resource dependence model, which explains power distribution across organizational units. The core insight: power goes to units that control scarce, valued resources.

**Key Study — University Budgets:** Salancik and Pfeffer studied budget allocation in 42 departments across a large university. They found that departments with higher external grant funding received larger university budget allocations, even controlling for department size. Conclusion: power from securing external resources is used to secure internal resources.

**What This Predicts:**
- Departments that bring in money have power
- Departments that control critical materials have power
- Units with information others need have power
- Power is used to secure more power (the rich get richer)

**Limitations:**
- The model assumes organizations are rational in resource allocation; they are not always
- The model may overstate the importance of resources relative to other factors like political skill or relationships
- The model may apply better to resource-scarce organizations than to abundantly funded ones

---

### Hickson et al. (1971) — Strategic Contingencies Model

**Citation:** Hickson, D. J., Hinings, C. R., Lee, C. A., Schneck, R. E., & Pennings, J. M. (1971). A strategic contingencies theory of intraorganizational power. Administrative Science Quarterly, 16(2), 216–229.

**Why It Matters:** This model adds something crucial to resource dependence: it is not just what resources you control, but whether you help others manage uncertainty. Coping with uncertainty is what generates power.

**Core Proposition:** Subunit power is influenced by:
1. Ability to reduce uncertainty for other units
2. Nonsubstitutability (whether your contribution can be replaced)
3. Centrality (how interconnected you are with other units)

**Key Evidence:** Lawrence and Lorsch studied power distribution in three industries. In successful firms, power resided in the departments that managed the firm's strategic contingencies. In unsuccessful firms, power and strategic contingency did not align.

**What This Predicts:**
- In manufacturing, power goes to the units that manage demand or supply uncertainty
- In utilities facing regulation, power goes to legal (manages regulatory uncertainty)
- In tech companies, power goes to the units that manage market or technical uncertainty
- When strategic contingencies shift, power redistributes

**Limitations:**
- The model was tested primarily in manufacturing and utilities; generalization to services and digital companies unclear
- The model assumes organizations rationally identify strategic contingencies; they often get it wrong
- The model does not account for historical power bases or resistance to redistribution

---

### Pfeffer (1981, 2010) — Organizational Politics

**Citation:** Pfeffer, J. (2010). Power: Why some people have it and others don't. Harper Business.

**Why It Matters:** Pfeffer is the leading researcher on organizational politics. He has spent decades documenting that power and politics are not aberrations but central to how organizations work.

**Key Arguments:**
- Politics is not bad; it is how organizations make decisions under uncertainty
- Political behavior is predictable, not random
- Organizations that try to eliminate politics often do so by making decisions so rigid they cannot adapt
- The best managers are politically skilled

**What the Research Shows:**
- Political behavior increases when goals are ambiguous, resources are scarce, decisions are nonprogrammed, and the environment is uncertain
- Political behavior decreases when goals are clear, decisions are programmed, resources are abundant, and the environment is stable
- Organizations with the most political behavior often have the poorest performance, but this is correlation, not causation (causation may run the other way: poor performance causes political infighting)

**Limitations:**
- Pfeffer's work is sometimes criticized as cynical, suggesting all behavior is political
- The research is primarily qualitative/observational; quantitative measures of "political behavior" are harder to develop
- The model may apply better to large, complex organizations than to small ones

---

### Kanter (1979, 2004) — Power Symbols and Effectiveness

**Citation:** Kanter, R. M. (2004). On the frontiers of management. Harvard Business School Press.

**Why It Matters:** Kanter identified concrete behavioral indicators of managerial power. These observable actions signal who has power and who does not.

**Symbols of Power Include:**
- Ability to intercede on behalf of others
- Access to top decision-makers
- Early information about decisions
- Ability to get budget exceptions approved
- Being invited to important meetings
- Being sought for opinions on important questions

**Why This Matters:** These visible signals reinforce power structures. The organization sends clear messages about who is powerful through who gets invited where, who gets greeted, who gets listened to.

**Application:** You can assess a manager's actual power (as opposed to formal position) by observing these behaviors.

---

## Secondary Sources and Related Literature

### Kipnis and Others on the Psychology of Power

Kipnis, D. (1976). The powerholders. University of Chicago Press.

Key finding: People who exercise coercive power tend to devalue the targets of that power and attribute their compliance to fear rather than to any personal qualities. This is a mechanism by which power corrupts.

### Mintzberg on Organizational Politics

Mintzberg, H. (1983). Power in and around organizations. Prentice Hall.

Mintzberg's framework for understanding power holders: operators (people with formal authority), technicians (experts), administrators (those controlling information flow), and politicians (those exercising power outside formal channels).

### Miles on Conditions for Political Behavior

Miles, R. H. (1980). Macro organizational behavior. Scott, Foresman.

Miles documented the conditions under which political behavior emerges: ambiguous goals, scarce resources, nonroutine technology, complex environment, nonprogrammed decisions, organizational change.

---

## Methodological Notes

### Strength of Evidence

**Very Strong:**
- The five bases of power (French & Raven) have held up across 60+ years
- Strategic contingencies do influence power distribution (multiple replications)
- Political behavior correlates with ambiguous goals and scarce resources

**Strong but Context-Dependent:**
- Resource dependence model works well in manufacturing/utilities; less clear in service/digital organizations
- The symbolic indicators of power (Kanter) are observable and reliable but may vary across organizations
- Political behavior is more intense in some organizational cultures than others

**Moderate:**
- The prediction that eliminating politics requires making decisions so rigid they cannot adapt (this is logical but hard to test quantitatively)
- The idea that power must redistribute when strategic contingencies shift (this is true, but organizations often resist the redistribution, creating lag and conflict)

### Gaps and Unknowns

**What We Do Not Know Well:**
- Why some managers become trapped by their own power bases (psychological mechanism unclear)
- How political behavior differs across national cultures with different norms about power
- Whether virtual/remote organizations have different patterns of power and politics
- How to predict which power tactics will work in which organizational contexts

**Why It Matters:**
- If we understood why managers become trapped in outdated power bases, we might help them adapt
- Different cultures may have genuinely different politics (not just the same dynamics expressed differently)
- Virtual work may create new power dynamics around information and access
- Better prediction of political behavior would help organizations manage it better

---

## Critical Questions for Further Study

1. **Power and Organizational Adaptation:** Do organizations with distributed, fluid power structures adapt faster to environmental change than organizations with concentrated, stable power structures? The strategic contingencies model suggests yes, but the evidence is mixed.

2. **Political Skill as Competency:** Can organizations train managers in political skill without encouraging unethical behavior? Or does teaching people how to exercise power necessarily teach them how to use it for personal ends?

3. **Virtual Work and Power:** Does remote work change the bases of power? Is expert power more important when you cannot rely on physical presence or personal appeal? Is legitimate power weaker when the supervisor is not present?

4. **Power and Decision Quality:** When political behavior determines organizational decisions, does it produce worse decisions than when decisions are made by pure expertise or data? The evidence is mixed; sometimes political decisions are actually better because they incorporate concerns that pure logic would miss.

5. **Cultural Variations in Politics:** Does the strategic contingencies model apply the same way in collectivist vs. individualist cultures? In hierarchical vs. egalitarian organizations?

# Chapter 13 — Teaching Notes for Instructors

## Chapter Purpose and Positioning

Chapter 13 serves as a bridge between interpersonal dynamics (Chapters 1-12) and organizational systems (Chapters 14-19). Understanding power and politics is foundational to understanding how organizations actually make decisions, how resources get allocated, how conflicts emerge, and how organizations succeed or fail.

This chapter deliberately resists the common management textbook temptation to treat politics as aberration or failure. Politics is normal. The question is not how to eliminate it but how to shape it toward legitimate organizational ends.

---

## Learning Objectives — What Students Should Be Able to Do

### By Concept 1 (What Power Is)

Students should be able to:
- Define power, distinguish it from authority and leadership
- Identify which base of power a person is using in a given scenario
- Predict the likely employee response (commitment, compliance, resistance) given the base of power
- Recognize when they themselves are exercising each base of power

**Common Student Mistakes:**
- Confusing authority with power (assuming title = influence)
- Thinking all power is bad (it is not; some is necessary)
- Assuming coercive power is always ineffective (it works in the short term; long-term effects are negative)

### By Concept 2 (Organizational Unit Power)

Students should be able to:
- Distinguish resource dependence from strategic contingencies
- Use the strategic contingencies model to predict which units will have power
- Predict how power will redistribute when an organization's environment changes
- Recognize when a department is losing power and why

**Common Student Mistakes:**
- Assuming the largest or oldest department has the most power (not necessarily true)
- Not recognizing that power follows need, not preference
- Overestimating how quickly organizations redistribute power as contingencies shift

### By Concept 3 (Political Behavior)

Students should be able to:
- Identify the structural conditions that trigger political behavior
- Recognize specific political tactics being used (or being used on them)
- Propose organizational changes that would reduce destructive political behavior
- Distinguish between politics that serves organizational goals and politics that serves only personal ends

**Common Student Mistakes:**
- Treating all politics as bad and all politicians as untrustworthy
- Underestimating how much political behavior is happening in organizations they have experienced
- Assuming that being "apolitical" is a virtue (it is often a disadvantage)

---

## Recommended Teaching Approach

### Section 1: Make Power Visible

Many students grew up in educational settings where power and politics are explicitly discouraged ("we don't do politics here"). They then enter organizations where power is everywhere but unspoken. The first job is to make power visible.

**Activity:** Ask students to map the informal influence structure of an organization they have been part of. Provide a simple matrix:
- Who has formal authority?
- Who actually shapes decisions?
- Where are these the same person? Where different?
- Why the difference?

Most students will discover that the person with the title is not always the person with the influence. This is the aha moment. From there, the five bases of power become concrete explanations for why.

### Section 2: Make Politics a Tool, Not a Dirty Word

Many students associate politics with dishonesty. The chapter explicitly argues that some political behavior is healthy and necessary. The question is not whether to have politics, but how to channel it.

**Activity:** Present students with a resource-allocation scenario (e.g., a software company deciding whether to invest in a new product or mature an existing one). Have them identify:
- Who would advocate for each position?
- What power bases would they use?
- What political tactics might emerge?
- How would you design the decision process to get good outcomes?

This activity reveals that the political behavior is rational, not corrupt. Each person is protecting their area. The goal is not to shame them but to understand the dynamics.

### Section 3: Strategic Contingencies Shift

The hardest concept for students to grasp is that power is not stable. As organizations' environments change, the people and departments who matter change. This creates both opportunity and threat.

**Activity:** Have students research a real organization that went through a major transition (GE, IBM, Kodak, Blockbuster, or any company that had to reinvent itself). Ask:
- What was the strategic contingency (what did the organization need to succeed) in the old environment?
- What units had power then?
- What is the strategic contingency now?
- What units have power now?
- What happened to the people in the units that lost power?

This makes clear that power redistribution is not abstract; it has real human consequences.

---

## Discussion Questions and Follow-Ups

### Concept 1: What Power Is

**Opening Question:** "Is it possible to be a good manager without understanding power?" 

Most students will initially say yes. Push back: "But if you don't understand why your team actually does what you ask, how will you lead them effectively?"

**Follow-up:** "What is the difference between a manager who has authority but no power, and a manager who has power but no authority?"

Answer framework: Authority is about right; power is about ability. The VP in the chapter had authority but lacked power because the actions she needed others to take were outside her control. A person without formal authority but with expertise or trust can still move an organization.

### Concept 2: Organizational Unit Power

**Opening Question:** "In your organization, which department has the most power?"

Most students will name Finance or Sales (traditional power bases). Push them to think about what gives those departments power. Is it resources they control? Uncertainty they manage? Scarcity of their function?

**Follow-up:** "What if your industry fundamentally changed? How would power redistribute?"

Use a concrete example: A manufacturing company suddenly facing a sustainability crisis. Environmental compliance becomes the strategic contingency. Legal and Environmental Affairs units gain power. Traditional operations units lose influence. What happens to the operations VP?

### Concept 3: Political Behavior

**Opening Question:** "Is politics in your organization a big deal or not a big deal?"

Many students will say "not much" because politics is not openly discussed. Counter: "Then why do budget meetings take three hours instead of thirty minutes? Why do people care who reports to whom? Why do people worry about 'optics'?"

**Follow-up:** "What would happen if we actually eliminated all politics from your organization?"

Expected answers: decisions would take longer (because you would need perfect information), or decisions would be too rigid (because you would program everything), or the organization would be unable to adapt (because people would not advocate for needed change).

## Common Challenges and How to Address Them

### Challenge 1: "This Makes Organizations Sound Corrupt"

Some students react to the frank discussion of power and politics by assuming the chapter endorses corruption. Clarify:

*The chapter says:* Politics is how organizations actually work. Understanding it helps you operate effectively and ethically.

*Not:* All politics is corrupt, or corruption is necessary.

**Address by:** Showing that organizations with transparent processes, clear accountability, and strong ethics can still have politics — just more channeled toward legitimate ends.

### Challenge 2: "But I Don't Want to Be Political"

Some students have been taught that being apolitical is a virtue. They want to "just do good work" and have people recognize it. Validate the aspiration but be honest:

*The truth:* Good work is necessary but not sufficient. Without understanding and engaging with power dynamics, your good work may not get resources or recognition. You may be ineffective even though you are ethical.

**Address by:** Showing that understanding power allows you to be more effective at your core mission, not less. A researcher who understands power can allocate research resources better. A doctor who understands power can advocate better for patients.

### Challenge 3: "Doesn't This Give People Permission to Manipulate?"

Some students worry that teaching political skill teaches manipulation. Valid concern. Address by:

*Distinguishing:* Political skill (understanding how to move an organization) is not the same as manipulation (using deception for personal ends). The chapter teaches the former and explicitly condemns the latter.

**How to reinforce:** Emphasize that ethics matter. The ethical use of power is toward legitimate organizational ends. The unethical use is purely for personal gain. The best managers are politically skilled AND ethical.

## Assessment Ideas

### Knowledge-Level Assessment

**Multiple Choice Example:**
"Which of the following would most likely increase the power of a department?"
A. The department's expertise becomes more common
B. The department's work becomes more central to organizational operations  ← Correct
C. The department has fewer employees
D. The department's budget increases

**Essay Question:**
"Describe a real or hypothetical situation where a person with legitimate authority lacks power. Explain why, using concepts from the chapter."

### Application-Level Assessment

**Case Analysis:**
Provide a short case (e.g., "The IT department wants more budget for infrastructure. The CFO wants them to cut costs. How will this be resolved?"). Ask:
- What base of power will each party use?
- What political tactics might emerge?
- How could the organization redesign the process to get a better decision?

**Scenario Planning:**
"Your company is moving from product-centric to customer-centric. This will require different capabilities. Using the strategic contingencies model, predict which units will gain and lose power. What are the implications?"

### Synthesis-Level Assessment

**Position Paper:**
"Write a 2-page memo to a CEO: Given what you know about power and politics, how should our organization redesign our budget process to reduce destructive political behavior while keeping the engagement that is necessary?"

## Integration With Other Chapters

### Links to Chapter 12: Leadership

Leadership is one response to power. A leader with expert power might inspire people to improve themselves. A manager with only coercive power cannot be a leader. Understanding power helps students understand why leadership is harder than management.

### Links to Chapter 14: Conflict and Negotiation

Conflict often emerges from power dynamics (scarce resources, competing goals). Negotiation is often really an exercise in power dynamics. Understanding this chapter makes Conflict and Negotiation much clearer.

### Links to Chapter 9: Group Dynamics

Power dynamics within groups follow similar patterns to power between units. The person with information, expertise, or the ability to reduce group uncertainty often becomes the informal leader, regardless of who is the formal leader.

### Links to Chapter 6: Decision-Making

How organizations make decisions is inseparable from power. When goals are clear and information is good, politics is minimal. When they are not, politics determines outcomes.

## Red Flags to Watch For

### Student Misunderstandings

1. **"Power is bad"** — Correct by noting that organizations need power to function. The question is legitimate vs. illegitimate use.

2. **"My title means I have power"** — Correct by giving examples of people with title but no influence.

3. **"Being apolitical is ethical"** — Correct by noting that abdication from power and politics is itself a political choice with consequences.

4. **"The most powerful person in an organization is the CEO"** — Often true, but not always. Be prepared to discuss hidden power (assistants, gatekeepers, experts).

## Grading Rubric for Essays and Cases

**Excellent (A):**
- Correctly identifies the power dynamics in the scenario
- Uses specific terminology from the chapter (bases of power, strategic contingencies, political behavior conditions)
- Predicts outcomes that are grounded in theory
- Proposes solutions that address underlying structural issues, not just surface behavior

**Good (B):**
- Identifies main power dynamics
- Uses some chapter terminology correctly
- Makes reasonable predictions
- Proposes solutions that might help but may not address root causes

**Satisfactory (C):**
- Identifies some power dynamics
- Uses some terminology but may misapply it
- Makes predictions with limited grounding in theory
- Proposes solutions that are common sense but not theory-informed

**Below Satisfactory (F):**
- Misses power dynamics or treats power as bad rather than as a fact of organizational life
- Does not use chapter concepts
- Makes predictions without grounding
- Proposes solutions that would not work (e.g., "eliminate all politics")
