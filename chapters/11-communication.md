## TL;DR

Communication in organizations is not just the transfer of information from a speaker to a listener. It is a system with machinery: encoding and decoding, feedback loops, barriers that distort messages, channels with different carrying capacities. Understanding this machinery — what breaks and why — is what separates managers who get things done from managers whose words evaporate.

## Cold open: The memo that killed a launch decision

On January 27, 1986, at 73 seconds after liftoff, the Space Shuttle Challenger disintegrated in the cold Florida sky. Seven astronauts died. The investigation that followed uncovered something quieter and more haunting than a mechanical failure: a communication system that worked exactly as designed to hide the truth.

Four days before launch, engineers at Morton Thiokol, the contractor that built the shuttle's O-ring seals, knew those seals would fail in cold weather. They had tested them. They knew that the overnight temperature at the Cape was expected to drop to 36 degrees Fahrenheit — far below the minimum safe operating temperature of 53 degrees. They prepared a memo. The memo was technical, dense with engineering data. It recommended delay.

But the memo was not sent to the people making the launch decision.

Instead, Thiokol's vice president of engineering transmitted the recommendation upward through the chain of command — to NASA's engineering chain, not to the people in the Launch Control Center. The message got encoded and re-encoded at each level. By the time it reached the Flight Director, it had transformed. The data was there, but embedded in a document that was read as "Thiokol has some engineering concerns" rather than "Thiokol is saying do not launch." The recommendation was so well encoded in technical language, and so separated from the consequence — human beings strapped into a machine — that the people who made the decision did not receive the warning the way the people who sent it meant to send it.

This is not a story about human error. It is a story about a communication system. The system worked. The engineers communicated. The memo was transmitted. The data arrived. The system failed because the machinery of communication — the encoding, the channel, the decoding, the feedback — was not designed to carry the weight of a life-or-death message through a hierarchical organization where authority, status, and organizational role all distort what a listener hears.

Every manager will send and receive messages that carry weight. Understanding why the message you send is almost never the message that gets heard — and what machinery can make the hearing better — is the work of this chapter.

### Why this chapter matters

Managers spend about 75% of their time in communication. This is not a skill. This is the work. The best strategy fails if the people who need to execute it do not hear it. ## Concept 1 — The communication process and the many places where it fails

Communication sounds simple. I think a thought. I encode it into words. I send the words through a channel — speaking, writing, gesture. You decode the words. You understand what I meant. But this model leaves out almost everything.

Shannon and Weaver, two Bell Labs engineers in 1948, offered a map: an *information source* encodes a message, sends it through a *channel*, and a *receiver* decodes it. Between sender and receiver sits *noise* — anything that distorts the message. The process is circular: the receiver sends *feedback* back to the sender, which closes the loop and tells the sender whether the message landed.

This model is more honest than the simple telling, but it is still incomplete. It assumes the sender and receiver are neutral boxes. They are not. Encoding is not neutral. A manager under pressure who says "I want this completed today and I don't care what it costs" often does care what it costs — but the emotional state, the urgency, the phrasing have encoded something different from the thought the manager held. Decoding is not neutral. You receive a message in a moment of exhaustion, after a difficult morning, when you are reading an email on your phone at midnight. The same message would mean something entirely different if you read it in a conference room at 9 a.m. with the sender across the table. Context invades decoding.

Feedback is not neutral either. When a manager asks "Do you understand?" and you nod, the manager believes you understood — even if you are nodding because you are afraid to ask again. Feedback is easy to fake, especially when power is involved.

### The three types of noise

Not all distortion comes from the channel. Shannon broke distortion into types.

**Semantic noise** is distortion in meaning. The word "robust" sounds like technical competence until you realize that "robust" on a project means different things to different people — one engineer means "tested thoroughly," another means "built with redundancy," a third means "able to withstand edge cases." You are using the same word and encoding different concepts. The word arrives. The meaning does not.

**Physical noise** is simpler: an airplane flies over while you are speaking. You do not hear the instruction. The slack message arrives with a typo that inverts the meaning — "Do not approve this" becomes "Do approve this" because someone fat-fingered the word "not." The channel itself distorts.

**Psychological noise** is the most consequential. You have stereotyped the person sending the message. You have decided they are incompetent, or political, or trying to grab credit. A message from someone you have decided is untrustworthy lands differently than the same message from someone you trust. The message is the same. Your decoding filter changes whether it reaches your ears as warning or as noise to ignore.

The Challenger investigation uncovered all three types. The O-ring data was semantic — the same engineering language that meant "catastrophic failure risk" to the engineers meant "we are not sure" to the managers three levels up. It was physical — the memo format buried the recommendation in pages of technical detail. And it was psychological — the NASA culture had decided that schedule pressure and optimism bias were acceptable filters for bad news, so messages that contradicted that bias were discounted.

### Feedback closes the loop — if it is real

The only corrective is feedback. If you encode a message and send it out with no feedback mechanism, you do not know whether you have been understood or misunderstood until the moment the miscommunication becomes visible as a problem.

There are three types of feedback. **Informational feedback** is data: "We have 47 units in inventory." The receiver has heard the number and can act on it. **Corrective feedback** is pushback: "I disagree that this is my responsibility." Corrective feedback tells the sender that the message did not land as intended. **Reinforcing feedback** confirms: "I understand, and I will have this to you by Friday." Each type serves a different function. None work well if the person sending the feedback fears the sender will punish disagreement or ignore the truth.

This is where power distorts the system. If you are speaking to someone who can fire you, or grade you, or block your promotion, your feedback becomes noise. You say "yes" when you mean "I do not understand." You nod when you are confused. You smile when you are angry. The communication system accumulates false feedback, and the sender goes on believing the message landed correctly — when in fact it landed wrong, and the person on the other end is now operating on a false understanding.

The Challenger memo came back through the chain of command with the feedback "We have decided to go ahead." The engineers who sent the message received the opposite of corrective feedback. They received the message that their objection had been heard and processed and set aside. They did not receive the feedback that the people making the launch decision had not actually heard — had not decoded — the severity of the risk. By the time the truth arrived at the Launch Control Center, there was no remaining feedback loop to clarify.

### Worked example: Decoding a message in context

You receive this email from your boss: "I'd like to see what you've been working on. Come by my office at 2 PM tomorrow."

The semantic meaning is simple: the boss wants to review your work. But the decoding depends on context.

If this email arrives after you have just delivered a successful project, and your boss has been praising you in meetings, you decode it as recognition. *They want to see what you've done because it's good.*

If this email arrives after you made a mistake that had to be corrected by a colleague, and your boss has been distant all week, you decode it as criticism. *They found out about the error.*

The same message. Different context. Different decoding. The sender may have meant it as recognition. You have decoded it as threat.

This is why feedback is critical. The best sender would follow that email with a brief clarification: "I want to see how the project came together — I think there's something here we should build on." That one additional sentence prevents the recipient from decoding threat when the sender meant opportunity.

### Common misconceptions

**"More communication is always better."** Information overload is real and counterproductive. If you send 200 emails a day to someone, they stop reading at email 47. The channel becomes a firehose. The message drowns.

**"The 55-38-7 rule: only 7% of communication is verbal."** This is a misreading of Albert Mehrabian's 1960s research. His findings applied only to messages about emotions in situations where the verbal and nonverbal cues contradict each other. Someone says "I love this idea" while looking at their shoes with their arms crossed. Which do you believe — the words or the body language? Mehrabian said nonverbal cues dominate when there is contradiction. That does not mean 93% of all communication is nonverbal. If I tell you "The meeting is at 3 PM," the verbal cue carries the information; whether I am smiling or frowning while I say it is secondary.

**"If I explain it precisely, they will understand."** Clarity is necessary but not sufficient. The best-explained message can land wrong if the receiver is in the wrong state of mind, does not trust the sender, or decodes the words through a different frame of reference. Clarity is your responsibility. Understanding is a joint responsibility — it requires feedback and the willingness to decode what the other person actually means by their feedback.

## Concept 2 — Channels and the richness they carry

Not all messages should travel the same way. Some messages need the full bandwidth of human conversation. Others travel better in writing. The difference is called *media richness*.

Daft and Lengel, two organizational communication researchers, developed a theory that proved robust across decades: the richer the medium, the better it works for ambiguous, complex, or emotionally loaded messages. Face-to-face conversation is the richest channel. You get real-time feedback, nonverbal cues, the ability to sense confusion and adjust on the fly, to ask clarifying questions, to read the room. Video conference is nearly as rich, though something is lost without physical presence — you cannot read the whole body, cannot sense the room energy, cannot use physical space to build trust.

A phone call is leaner. You lose visual cues. The person can hang up more easily. You cannot see their face; you are reading only tone and word choice.

Email is much leaner. It is asynchronous — you send it and wait for response. Tone is invisible. "This is terrible" arrives on the screen and you are left guessing whether the sender means *this is catastrophically wrong* or *this is slightly less good than I hoped*. The emotional temperature of the message is not transmitted. Slack and texting are slightly richer than email because of synchronicity and brevity — you can have back-and-forth quickly — but tone is still invisible.

A memo or document is the leanest medium. It is slow, formal, creates a record that can be used against you later, and provides no feedback channel at all.

The task determines the channel. For routine information — the meeting is at 3 PM, we will ship on Thursday, the conference call is confirmed — lean media work fine. The message is unambiguous. Feedback is not needed. Async channels save everyone's time.

But for complex, ambiguous, or emotionally loaded messages — "I have concerns about your fit for this role," "We need to talk about what happened in that meeting," "I want to understand what you meant by that comment" — lean media fail. The person receiving the message cannot see your face, cannot ask for clarification, cannot sense that you are trying to help rather than harm. The message gets decoded wrong.

This is where the Challenger memo failed. O-ring failure risk is a complex, technical message, but it is also an emotionally loaded message in an organization where schedule pressure is the dominant emotion. The memo was sent through a lean medium — written technical language — up a chain of command. At each level, the message lost information. By the time it reached the person making the decision, the emotional load was gone. It arrived as engineering data, not as warning. A face-to-face conversation between Thiokol's engineers and NASA's Flight Director would have transmitted not just the data but the engineers' conviction about the risk. That conviction — encoded in voice, face, the way they held themselves — would have been much harder to decode as "we are not sure."

### Task-channel matching

The best organizations match the channel to the task. Procter & Gamble managers cannot raise an issue in a meeting unless it has first been circulated in writing — this ensures the idea is clear, well-thought, and not delivered in the heat of the moment. But P&G does not ask managers to deliver a performance review or a difficult message through writing. Those messages come in person, or video call if distance requires, where feedback can flow.

### Media richness in a remote world

The pandemic forced remote work. Many organizations discovered they could operate at lean-channel richness for tasks they had been conducting face-to-face. A 10-minute Slack exchange can accomplish what used to take a half-hour meeting. But organizations also discovered that some tasks became harder. Trust-building, relationship formation, creative collaboration, conflict resolution — all of these degraded when the channel lost richness. Some people thrived in the lean-channel environment. Others found it isolating. Across the studies, the sweet spot is not all-remote and not all-in-person, but matched to task and person.

### The Allen Curve: why physical proximity still matters

Tom Allen, an MIT researcher, studied how often engineers in a building communicated with each other. He plotted communication frequency against the distance between their desks.

The result was called the Allen Curve: beyond about 50 meters (roughly 150 feet, or a little longer than half a football field), communication frequency drops sharply. An engineer 30 feet away gets communicated with much more often than an engineer 80 feet away. The curve flattens out — someone 200 feet away gets roughly as much communication as someone 500 feet away — but the initial drop is steep.

This is not because people dislike walking. It is because the richer the medium, the more often you use it. If someone is at your desk, you have a conversation. If someone is two buildings away, you send an email instead. The channel shifts, and lower-richness channels get used less frequently. Communication does not need to happen as often through writing as it does through face-to-face, so it happens less.

This matters for remote work policy. An office where half the team is remote and half is in-building creates a two-tier communication system. The in-person people talk constantly; they pick up context from hallway conversations, lunch-table gossip, the ambient information of presence. The remote people get more formal, channel-based communication — email, video calls, Slack — all of which are leaner. Over time, the in-person people know more, build stronger relationships with managers, move faster. It is not that remote workers are less capable. It is that they are communicating through channels that carry less information.

The solution is not to force everyone back to the office. The solution is to be deliberate about which communication moments require richness, which can happen asynchronously, and which should be the same for everyone regardless of location.

### Worked example: Choosing the channel for a difficult message

You have just learned that one of your direct reports — a person who has been with you for three years and is generally solid — made a significant mistake. A customer is angry. The problem will cost the company about $50,000 to fix.

You need to deliver this message. You could:

1. Send an email detailing the problem and asking for an explanation.
2. Call them into your office for a conversation.
3. Grab them in the hallway and give them the news informally.

Email is lean, creates a record, gives the person no ability to respond with emotion, allows them to craft a written defense. The person will be alone when they read it, likely in shock.

Hallway conversation is high-richness but hasty, public, gives the person no time to compose themselves. It feels like an ambush.

Office conversation is richest and most formal. You can see their reaction, they can ask questions, you can clarify your intent, they can ask for time to gather their thoughts. If the conversation goes badly, you can pivot. The person has your attention. They know this moment matters.

For a message of this weight, the office is the right channel. This does not mean avoiding the difficult truth. It means delivering it in a channel where the person can respond, where you can decode their reaction, where you can clarify that this is about fixing a problem, not firing someone.

### Common misconceptions

**"Written communication creates a paper trail, so it is more accountable."** Writing does create a record, and that record can protect you legally. But in organization, it often creates defensiveness instead. The moment the difficult message arrives in writing, the recipient knows they can use that writing as evidence of your criticism. They are more likely to craft a defensive response rather than hear what you are saying. The most accountable approach is conversation with documentation after: "We talked about this on March 15. I wanted to follow up in writing so there is a record of what we discussed and what we agreed to do next."

**"Remote workers should use cameras so I can see their faces."** Camera-on requirement treats richness as something the remote worker needs to provide. But richness is created by mutual presence. If you are meeting with someone face-to-face, you also have a camera on (your eyes), and you can see their full body language. In a video call, you are both constrained to a narrow view of each other's face and upper body. This is somewhat richer than voice-only, but much leaner than physical presence. The assumption that "camera on = face-to-face" misses the point.

## Concept 3 — The organizational context: hierarchy, formality, and the grapevine

Communication does not happen in a vacuum. It happens in an organization with structure, hierarchy, and culture. That structure shapes what gets said, what does not get said, and what gets said in whispers instead of in the open.

### Upward, downward, and lateral

Messages travel in three directions in an organization.

**Downward communication** moves from manager to direct report. Instructions, feedback, company news, strategy, goals. The channel is usually asymmetrical — the manager can speak, the direct report is expected to listen and comply. This is why downward communication carries built-in distortion. The person receiving it is in a weaker power position. Their feedback is constrained. They cannot easily say "I disagree" or "I do not understand" without professional risk. The message lands in the context of authority, not inquiry.

**Upward communication** moves from direct report to manager. Status updates, problems, ideas, concerns. This is the rarest direction in hierarchical organizations. Research by Rosemary Stewart in the 1960s found that managers spent 12% of their time with superiors, 41% with direct reports, and 47% with peers. If managers are speaking less to their bosses than to anyone else, and if the messages that go upward are constrained by power dynamics, then the people making decisions have information from only the middle layers.

This is critical. Decisions made without good upward information are decisions made half-blind. The executive team thinks the frontline is satisfied, but frontline workers are frustrated. The C-suite thinks a project is on track, but the team knows it is behind and heading for failure. The problem is not that frontline workers will not communicate. The problem is that the channel for upward communication is so constrained by hierarchy and power that what travels upward is a sanitized version of the truth.

**Lateral communication** happens between peers. It is the least constrained by power, the fastest, the most informal, and often the most accurate. Colleagues tell each other things they would never tell a boss. They share rumors, concerns, worries, and private interpretations of what they saw in a meeting. Lateral communication is where organizations figure out what is actually happening.

### Psychological safety and the silence of bad news

Amy Edmondson, a Harvard researcher, spent years studying teams. She noticed something strange: the best teams reported more problems than the worst teams. This seems backwards. You would expect the best teams to have fewer problems.

What Edmondson found was that the best teams had no fewer problems than the worst teams. They just reported them. They created what she called "psychological safety" — a shared belief that you can take interpersonal risks in this group without getting punished or humiliated. That means you can speak up when you see a problem. You can say "I do not understand." You can disagree with the boss in a meeting. You can admit a mistake before it becomes catastrophic.

Psychological safety does not mean there are no consequences. It means the consequences for speaking up are less severe than the consequences of silence. In a psychologically safe team, telling your manager "I think we are about to ship a bug" results in a brief fix. In a psychologically unsafe team, telling your manager means you are labeling yourself as someone who let a bug slip into the system, and you pay for that later.

The Challenger launch was made by people in a psychologically unsafe system. The engineers who saw the problem did not feel safe saying "Do not launch" to the people who had decided to launch. So they encoded their message carefully, trying to make it clear without being insubordinate. They sent it through channels. They were polite. And their message was heard as information rather than as a command to stop.

Building psychological safety is an explicit act of management. It requires rewarding bad news. It requires asking "What am I missing?" instead of "Why did you let this happen?" It requires admitting your own uncertainty and mistakes. It requires listening to disagreement without defensiveness. Psychological safety is not permissiveness — it is the opposite. It creates the conditions where people will speak the truth so you can make decisions on true information rather than sanitized information.

### The grapevine: informal communication and what it reveals

In the 1950s and 1960s, researchers studied how information actually moves through organizations. What they found surprised them. Informal networks — what people called "the grapevine" — move information faster and more accurately than formal channels.

Keith Davis, the pioneer of grapevine research, found that in many organizations, 75 to 95 percent of grapevine information was accurate. This is not the grapevine as children's game, where "the telephone operator sits down" becomes "the telephone operator has sat down" after ten retellings. This is the grapevine as neural network. People gossip about things that matter. They repeat what they heard if it is important or interesting. They stop repeating things that are not true. Over time, accurate information propagates, and false information dies out.

The grapevine is usually faster than formal communication because it does not go through channels. "Did you hear they are laying people off?" travels peer-to-peer through lunch conversations, Slack DMs, hallway chats. It moves laterally, not upward or downward, so it faces no authority obstruction. It uses the richest medium available — conversation, face-to-face when possible. The grapevine is the informal communication system that emerges when the formal system is too slow, too filtered, or too much in service of management perception rather than truth.

When a company tells the formal communication system "We are not making changes" and employees hear through the grapevine that the company is exploring layoffs, the grapevine is not failing. The grapevine is compensating for the formal system's failure to communicate truth. The grapevine becomes necessary when formal channels are used for propaganda rather than information.

Wise managers do not try to kill the grapevine. They cannot. The grapevine is an emergent property of human communication. Instead, they use the grapevine as a signal. If the grapevine is moving fast and dark — people are talking behind closed doors, rumors are dark, morale is dropping — the formal communication system is failing to carry important information. The manager's job is to figure out what that information is and start addressing it through formal channels, with truth.

### High-context and low-context communication across cultures

Edward Hall, an anthropologist, noticed that people in different cultures communicate in different ways. In some cultures, meaning is carried largely in explicit words. "We will meet on Tuesday at 2 PM in the conference room." The context is minimal. The words carry the full meaning. Hall called this "low-context" communication. It is the style of German, Dutch, Swiss, and American business culture.

In other cultures, meaning is embedded in context. The relationship, the history, the unspoken assumptions, the tone, the setting — these carry as much meaning as the words. A manager in a high-context culture might say "You will understand what we need from you" without specifying what we need, because the relationship carries the understanding. Hall called this "high-context" communication. It is the style of Japanese, Arab, Chinese, and many relationship-based cultures.

This difference is not a value judgment. It is a difference in channel richness and trust. Low-context cultures compensate for the leanness of written communication by being explicit in what they write. High-context cultures assume richness in the relationship and embed meaning there. When people from high-context and low-context cultures work together, misunderstandings are inevitable.

A person from a high-context culture might feel that a low-context colleague is being rude — too direct, too explicit, treating everyone the same way rather than acknowledging the relationship. A person from a low-context culture might feel that a high-context colleague is being vague — refusing to be clear, hiding behind politeness, making assumptions rather than stating facts. Neither is wrong. They are using different channels and different assumptions about what carries meaning.

The solution is to make the difference explicit. "In my culture, we tend to be direct about disagreement. I want you to know that if I say something directly, it is because I respect you enough to be honest, not because I am angry." "In my culture, relationships come before tasks. I want to build trust with you before we dive into the details." Naming the difference makes room for both styles to work.

### Worked example: Diagnosing a communication failure

You are a manager in a multinational team. Your Tokyo office is working on a project with your Berlin office. The Tokyo team has noticed that the Berlin team is very direct in feedback — they send emails saying "This approach won't work" and "You misunderstood the requirement." The Tokyo team interprets this as hostility and is now reluctant to share ideas.

The Berlin team is confused. They think they are being helpful. They are trying to be clear and prevent wasted effort. They do not understand why the Tokyo team is withdrawing.

This is not a performance problem. This is a context mismatch. The Berlin team is operating in a low-context style: direct feedback is efficient and respectful because it saves time. The Tokyo team is operating in a high-context style: direct feedback without relationship acknowledgment feels like disrespect because it treats the relationship as secondary.

A good diagnosis would be: "The Berlin team is using direct feedback as a form of respect — they care enough to be clear. The Tokyo team is interpreting directness as a sign of disrespect — they feel the relationship should come first. Both teams are actually trying to be respectful. They are just using different channels to express it."

The solution is not to ask one team to change. It is to ask both teams to translate. Berlin could say: "I value working with you and I want us to succeed together on this. That is why I am going to be direct about where I see problems — I would rather have that directness now than have us build on a weak foundation." Tokyo could say: "I appreciate the direct feedback. It helps me improve. I also want to deepen our working relationship. Can we start our next weekly meeting with a few minutes just catching up as people?"

The communication is not failing. The translation is missing.

### Common misconceptions

**"The grapevine spreads misinformation."** The research does not support this. The grapevine is usually more accurate than formal announcements because it faces selective pressure for truth. People repeat what they heard if it is important and credible, and they stop repeating things that do not check out.

**"You can stop the grapevine by restricting information."** You cannot. Restriction makes the grapevine more necessary. If the formal channel is not carrying information, the grapevine fills the void. The more you try to suppress it, the darker and more suspicious it becomes.

**"Hierarchy ensures consistent messages."** Hierarchy ensures consistent official messages. It does not ensure consistent understanding. In fact, hierarchy distorts upward and downward communication so much that consistent understanding is rare. The person at the top often believes a message has been received and understood throughout the organization. The person at the bottom often feels they never heard it at all.

## Integration and synthesis

You have encountered the machinery of communication at three levels: the basic process (encoding, channel, decoding, feedback, noise), the choice of medium (richness matching task), and the organizational context (hierarchy, psychological safety, culture).

The Challenger memo is the through-line. Six hundred and seventy-five seconds of flight time ended because the communication system, at each level, did its job correctly by its own logic but failed at the next level. Engineers encoded a technical warning. Managers re-encoded it as a policy concern. Policy moved it upward as an engineering judgment call that had been resolved. By the time it reached the Flight Director, it was no longer a warning. It was a solved problem. The system worked. The message arrived. But the *weight* of the message — the engineers' conviction that something terrible would happen — was lost in the encoding and decoding.

This is the perpetual challenge of management communication. You cannot eliminate noise. You cannot force decoding. You cannot make people hear what you are saying. You can only create conditions where the machinery of communication has the best chance of working.

Those conditions are:

1. **Match medium to message.** Routine information can travel lean. Critical information needs richness. Ambiguous information needs feedback. Know the difference.

2. **Close the feedback loop.** Feedback that is safe to give is feedback worth getting. Create psychological safety so people will correct your understanding instead of letting your misunderstanding stand.

3. **Use hierarchy as a tool, not a constraint.** Hierarchy exists. It will distort communication. Your job is to know how it distorts and to build channels that bypass it when it matters — skip-level conversations, anonymous surveys, open forums where dissent is invited.

4. **Read the grapevine.** If the grapevine is faster than formal channels, formal channels are too slow. If the grapevine is darker than official news, official news is not trusted. Use the grapevine as a diagnostic tool.

5. **Make cultural differences explicit.** If your team spans cultures, do not assume shared communication norms. Name how your culture approaches directness, relationship, context, time, decision-making. Give people permission to be their culture while learning others' cultures.

6. **Recognize that communication is invention.** Meaning is not transmitted; it is created. The moment you send a message, it belongs to the receiver. They will interpret it through their frame of reference, their emotional state, their relationship with you, their prior experience with people like you. The best you can do is send a message carefully and ask for feedback on what they heard.

## Graduated exercises

### Warm-up

1. Think of a recent moment when you felt misunderstood. What was the message you thought you sent? What message did the other person receive? What caused the distortion — encoding, channel, decoding, or noise?

2. Watch a manager give feedback to a direct report (this could be from a TV show, a meeting you observe, or your own experience). What was the medium? Was it matched to the content? What feedback loops were available?

3. Look at your organization chart. Where are the biggest distances between sender and receiver? (Physically, hierarchically, culturally?) What communication failures would you predict? Which ones are actually happening?

### Application

4. You are a VP of engineering and you have just learned that a critical bug will ship with the next release. You need to tell the product manager, the CEO, and the customer support team. Design the communication strategy. What will you say to each? Which medium will you use for each? How will you ensure feedback comes back to you? What is your plan if the feedback contradicts your initial message?

5. Your organization has decided to move to a fully remote work model. You know that some work requires face-to-face richness. Map out which communication moments should stay synchronous and richly medium, which can move to asynchronous channels, and which should happen the same way for everyone regardless of location.

6. You are leading a team with people from low-context cultures (the United States, Germany) and high-context cultures (Japan, Saudi Arabia, Mexico). Your first all-hands meeting is next week. What is one thing you can do or say to make it safer for people from both styles to communicate authentically?

### Synthesis

7. The Challenger disaster happened because the communication system worked efficiently at each level but failed to carry the weight of the message through the levels. ## Chapter summary

Communication in organizations is not a soft skill or a personality trait. It is a system with machinery: encoding, channels, decoding, feedback, and multiple sources of noise. Messages distort at each stage — semantically, physically, psychologically. The sender can only create conditions for good communication; the receiver does the work of decoding.

Media richness theory explains why some channels carry some messages better than others. Face-to-face is richest; memos are leanest. Task complexity, ambiguity, and emotional weight demand richness. Routine information can travel lean. Mismatch between message and medium causes systematic failure.

Organizational context shapes what gets said. Hierarchy constrains upward communication; power constrains feedback. Psychological safety is the explicit work of management — it creates conditions where bad news reaches decision-makers rather than being filtered out. The grapevine is not a failure of formal communication; it is compensation for formal communication that is too slow, too filtered, or too much in service of management perception rather than truth.

High-context and low-context cultures carry meaning differently — in explicit words or in relationship context. The difference is neither good nor bad. But when cultures collide, the collision is invisible unless you make it explicit.

The machinery of communication can be understood, designed, and improved. The goal is not perfect clarity. The goal is conditions under which people are willing to say what they actually think and willing to hear what others are actually saying. That goal is achievable. It requires attention to process, not just content. It requires matching medium to message. It requires psychological safety. It requires honesty about what the hierarchy distorts and deliberate design to bypass that distortion when it matters. It requires using the grapevine as diagnostic tool rather than something to suppress.

---

## What would change my mind

I am arguing that communication is a system that can be understood and improved. Evidence that would change this reading: research showing that communication failures are primarily due to individual skill deficits rather than system design, or showing that the richness of the medium does not actually correlate with message fidelity in organizational settings, or showing that hierarchy is not the primary distortion mechanism in upward communication. So far, the research aligns with this account. But organizations are complex. Simpler models that emphasize individual factors over system design have historically underestimated the role of the system.

## Still puzzling

What I do not fully understand is why organizations so rarely act on what they know about communication failure. We have known since the 1950s that the grapevine is faster and more accurate than formal channels. We have known since Edmondson's research that psychological safety matters more than most other factors in team performance. We have known since the Challenger investigation what happens when hierarchy prevents bad news from traveling upward. Yet most organizations still operate on the assumption that louder, more frequent, and more top-down communication solves communication problems. I suspect this has to do with how power works: the people making communication policy are the people at the top of the hierarchy, and the policy that feels safest to them is policy that keeps control at the top.
