# Chapter 12 — Leadership

## TL;DR

Leadership is not a set of traits you're born with. It's a relationship between a person and a group trying to accomplish something together, shaped by what the situation demands. The evidence shows which approaches work under which conditions — and why the same move that saves one organization might sink another.

## Chapter opening — Scene and puzzle

It's August 1914, and Ernest Shackleton's ship, the *Endurance*, is locked in Antarctic pack ice. The ship is breaking apart. The boat has no engine — just sails and oars. His 28 men are stranded in one of the most hostile environments on Earth, 346 miles from the nearest land. Their goal of crossing the continent is finished. Their new goal: get everyone home alive.

What Shackleton does over the next 22 months becomes a laboratory for understanding leadership. He doesn't discover new routes or invent new technology. He doesn't motivate through bonus structures or promotion ladders. He makes thousands of small decisions — which man sleeps next to which, who gets the extra biscuit, whether a risk is worth taking — and the cumulative weight of those decisions is this: every single person came home.

In contrast, sit with a CEO in a crisis of a different kind. A product recall. A stock plunge. A reputational attack. Her job is not to get everyone home alive. Her job is to make the call that saves the company — sometimes that means laying people off. Cutting projects. Walking away from markets. The decisions are different. The constraints are different. The kind of leader the moment demands is different.

This is what we'll untangle in this chapter: what leadership actually is, which aspects of it are fixed (and which aren't), and — most importantly — why *matching the leader to the moment* matters more than finding the "best" leader.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

- **Define** leadership as a relationship, not a trait — and explain why the distinction changes everything.
- **Distinguish** between leaders and managers, formal and informal, designated and emergent.
- **Evaluate** the trait approach to leadership — what the evidence actually says about "born leaders" versus what folklore claims.
- **Explain** how the Ohio State and University of Michigan studies identified two independent dimensions of leadership behavior (task and relationship), and why their findings surprised everyone.
- **Apply** contingency theories — Fiedler's, Hersey-Blanchard's, House's path-goal theory — to diagnose which leadership style works under which conditions.
- **Analyze** transformational leadership, servant leadership, and leader-member exchange theory — and understand the evidence for when each creates value.
- **Correct** three myths: that leaders are born not made, that great leaders share specific traits, and that the Stanford Prison Experiment tells us anything reliable about workplace power.

### Prerequisites

Chapters 1 and 2 (management and organizational behavior, individual differences). Comfort with the idea that the same behavior can be brilliant in one context and disastrous in another.

### Why this chapter matters

Leadership is the most studied thing in organizational behavior — because it's the most expensive. A single bad hire at the CEO level can destroy shareholder value. A single good leader can turn an organization around. But the folklore — "leaders are born," "great leaders have X trait," "the best leadership style works everywhere" — has kept us from seeing what the research actually shows. You will meet people who cite Shackleton or Steve Jobs as evidence that leaders are made of special stuff. You will meet others who cite leadership frameworks as if they were physics laws. This chapter teaches you to read the evidence, not the story.

## Concept 1 — Leadership as a relationship, and why it's not the same as management

### The scene

Leonard Bernstein stands in front of the New York Philharmonic. He has a baton. He has authority. He has decades of musical knowledge. He could, in theory, conduct by force of personality alone — demand that the violins play louder, that the horns stay in time.

But that's not what he does. He conducts by *influence*. The orchestra members choose to follow his gestures. They could ignore him; the baton has no force. Instead, they watch him. They respond. They form a shared interpretation of the music, a coherence that wouldn't exist if each musician were playing alone.

### The mechanism: leadership as a dyadic relationship

Here's the definition that organizes this chapter:

**Leadership** is a social influence relationship between two or more people who depend on each other to attain mutual goals in a group setting.

Notice what's in that definition and what's not.

**What's in it:** The relationship is *mutual* — leader and followers influence each other. It unfolds *over time*, built through repeated interactions. It's goal-directed — the group exists to accomplish something. And crucially, it's *perceived*. The followers decide whether the person has influence; the title alone doesn't guarantee it.

**What's not in it:** A hierarchical position. An appointment. The ability to fire someone. A formal title.

This matters because it clarifies something that looks muddy on org charts: **managers and leaders are not the same thing**.

A **manager** is appointed to a position. A manager has formal authority — the right to schedule work, allocate resources, make hiring decisions. A manager motivates primarily through extrinsic mechanisms (carrots and sticks). These are powerful. They work. But they are not leadership.

A **leader** is someone whose influence others choose to accept. Leaders can be appointed (many are), but the appointment doesn't make them leaders — it just gives them access to the office and the budget. The actual relationship between a leader and followers is built on something different: perceptions of expertise, trustworthiness, and vision. Leaders motivate primarily through intrinsic processes — the follower wants to follow.

Here's the shift that changes everything: **a follower is not passive**. Edwin Hollander, who studied leadership emergence for decades, argued that the follower is the most critical factor in any leadership event. It is the follower who perceives the situation. It is the follower who decides whether to accept or reject a person's influence. It is the follower who surrenders power — willingly — to the leader.

This is why Shackleton's men didn't mutiny. Shackleton never threatened punishment. He never withheld rations as discipline. Instead, he made decisions that told them: *I see you. I am managing this carefully. I am not asking more than you can give.* And they chose to follow.

### Named trade-off: formal authority vs. earned influence

The tension between management and leadership creates a real dilemma for anyone in an organization:

**High formal authority, low influence:** You have the power to fire someone, but they don't trust you. They comply when you're watching and resist when you're not. You get compliance, not commitment. You get the letter of the work, not the spirit of it. A classic corporate middle manager can spend years in this position, running effective operations but never gaining the loyalty that would let them drive real change.

**High influence, low formal authority:** You have no hiring power, no budget authority, but people follow you anyway. You move through the organization solving problems, brokering ideas, connecting people. Your influence rests entirely on what others perceive you can do for them and how they perceive you. The moment you stop delivering on that, the moment your judgment is questioned, the influence evaporates.

**High on both:** Rare, but this is where transformation happens. You have the formal authority to make hard decisions and the earned influence to make people understand why those decisions matter.

The mistake most organizations make is assuming formal authority *creates* influence. It doesn't. It just creates the opportunity to build it.

### Worked example: Arroyo and the Sea Lions

The source material gives us John Arroyo, a general manager hired to replace a beloved predecessor at a minor-league baseball team. His opening move: "I'm the general manager — the CEO of this ball club! In time, the staff *will* respect me."

This is high formal authority with no influence yet. The staff resented him. Sales dropped. People talked about quitting.

His corrective move: "I'm going to spend a day in each of your shoes. You'll have the day off to sit in the GM's box."

He didn't use his authority. He created *asymmetrical empathy* — he was willing to experience their work (the less comfortable position) while giving them his (the more comfortable one). Then he renamed an award after his predecessor, signaling: I respect what came before. I'm not here to erase it.

That's influence-building. That's leadership.

### Common misconception: "Leadership is just management done well"

False. They are different functions. A manager who is not a leader can run an efficient operation. A leader who is not a manager can drive change without the authority to make it stick. The best organizations often separate them: the manager keeps the machine running; the leader defines what the machine should run toward.

## Concept 2 — The trait approach and the birth-vs-made question

### The scene and the mistake

For 2,000 years, people told stories about leaders as heroes — Alexander the Great, Julius Caesar, Cleopatra, Genghis Khan. These stories created a narrative: great leaders are *born*, not made. Some people arrive in the world with the stuff inside them. Others don't.

This belief was so durable that in the early 1900s, when researchers first tried to study leadership empirically, they asked the same question: *What traits do great leaders have in common?*

Ralph Stogdill, then at Ohio State, led the charge. He reviewed hundreds of studies. He created questionnaires. He tried to find the list: the traits that predict leader emergence and leader effectiveness. If we could identify the traits, we could recruit the right people and skip the ones who lacked the stuff.

The research took decades. Here's what Stogdill found in 1974: Leaders do tend to have certain traits. Drive, self-confidence, cognitive ability, knowledge of the business, honesty. These traits appear more often in leaders than in non-leaders.

But — and this is the crucial but — the relationship is weak to moderate. Extraversion correlates with leadership emergence at *r* = 0.31. That means extraversion accounts for about 10% of the variance in whether someone becomes a leader. Conscientiousness is about the same. Even when you pile multiple traits together, you explain maybe 30–50% of why someone becomes a leader.

**What explains the other 50–70%? Mostly context and effort.**

Stogdill's conclusion, stated carefully: traits do not solely determine leadership. They are *preconditions* that *suggest* leadership potential. But potential is not destiny.

Here's the modern evidence: Twin studies — which separate genetic influence from environment — show about 30% heritability for leadership. That's significant. It's not nothing. But it also means 70% of the variation comes from the environment: training, opportunity, luck, the specific moment, who needed what when.

### The mechanism: why traits matter modestly

Let's be precise about what the trait research actually shows.

People are more likely to follow someone who displays:

- **Drive** — ambition, energy, persistence, the visible hunger to get things done
- **Self-confidence** — the belief that their judgment is worth listening to
- **Honesty and integrity** — the perception that they won't lie or exploit power
- **Cognitive ability** — the sense that they can think rigorously about complex problems
- **Knowledge of the business** — expertise that solves real problems

None of these *guarantees* leadership. But they make the follower more likely to surrender influence to you.

Here's the subtlety: **the situation matters as much as the trait**. Psychologist Walter Mischel showed that personality expresses strongly in "weak" situations (situations with few rules and unclear expectations) and gets suppressed in "strong" situations (situations with clear norms and strong incentives). If you put a low-confidence person in a situation where the job is clear, the path is obvious, and success is obvious, their low confidence might barely matter. If you put a high-confidence person in chaos, their confidence might look like recklessness.

### Named trade-off: selection vs. development

Organizations face a choice: Do you hire for traits and assume you've hired a leader? Or do you hire for potential and invest in developing leadership?

**Select for traits:** You spend less on training. You assume the "right stuff" is there. You're relying on the folklore — that leaders are made, not born. The risk: you miss people who have the potential but not yet the full set of traits. You also risk confusing traits like "articulate" or "tall" or "comes from privilege" with actual leadership capacity.

**Invest in development:** You assume that most people can learn to lead under the right conditions, with the right support, in the right role. The risk: you waste time and money developing people who lack the foundational drive or integrity to be worth developing. You also run the risk of believing that training alone can create leaders — it can't.

The evidence suggests the answer is both-and, not either-or. Hire for foundational traits like honesty and drive and cognitive ability. Then invest heavily in developing the skills and judgment that turn those traits into actual influence. A brilliant person with no integrity is worse than useless. An honest person with low cognitive ability will struggle with complex decisions. But a person with both, combined with deliberate practice in how to lead, can become effective in ways they weren't naturally inclined toward.

### Worked example: twin studies and heritability

The most direct evidence on the birth-vs-made question comes from twin studies. Researchers found twins who were raised apart, and measured their leadership emergence in different contexts. The findings: about 30% of the variation in whether people emerge as leaders is explained by genetic differences. The rest is environment.

What does 30% heritability mean in practice? It means if you took two identical twins and raised them identically, one might emerge as a leader in their context and the other might not — purely because of small differences in opportunity, timing, and circumstance. It also means that a person with modest genetic predisposition toward leadership can become a very effective leader through deliberate practice, support, and the right circumstances.

### Myth to correct: "Great leaders have X trait"

The folklore: Great leaders are charismatic. Great leaders are tall. Great leaders are extraverted. Great leaders are fearless.

The evidence: Specific traits appear *slightly more often* in leaders than in non-leaders, but the effects are small and context-dependent. Charisma helps in some situations (turnarounds, times of crisis, when you need to inspire). It's a liability in others (technical work, situations that require careful deliberation, when you need people to scrutinize your ideas, not follow them blindly).

What *does* predict effectiveness once someone is in a leadership position? Honesty, cognitive ability, knowledge of the business, and the capacity to learn from feedback. But even these are not universal. In a highly structured job with clear goals and clear paths to success, these traits matter less.

## Concept 3 — Behavioral approaches: what leaders *do* vs. who they are

### The scene and the puzzle

By the 1940s, researchers had spent decades hunting for traits and found modest results. So they asked a different question: *What if we're looking at the wrong thing? What if the question isn't who the leader is, but what the leader does?*

Two independent research teams set out to find the answer: Ohio State University and the University of Michigan. They used different methods. They studied different organizations. And they found almost identical results.

### The mechanism: two independent dimensions

**At Ohio State** (led by Ralph Stogdill), researchers created detailed descriptions of leader behavior. They asked people: What does your leader actually *do*? They found two distinct clusters of behavior:

**Consideration** — "relationship-oriented" behavior. The leader is supportive and friendly. They listen to what people have to say. They represent people's interests. They recognize contributions. They express concern for people's feelings. This addresses the group's *maintenance needs* — keeping the team glued together, making sure people feel valued.

**Initiating structure** — "task-oriented" behavior. The leader schedules work. They decide what needs to be done and how and when to do it. They provide direction. They maintain performance standards. They encourage following procedures. This addresses the group's *task needs* — making progress toward the goal.

The Ohio State researchers expected these to be opposite ends of a spectrum. You could be high on consideration and low on initiating structure (a people person who doesn't get results). Or low on consideration and high on initiating structure (a taskmaster with no empathy).

Instead, they found something different: **the two dimensions are independent**. You can be high on both. You can be low on both. You can be high on one and low on the other. A leader can care deeply about people *and* maintain clear expectations about work.

**At the University of Michigan**, researchers independently found the same pattern, using slightly different terminology: "job-centered" leadership (similar to initiating structure) and "employee-member-centered" leadership (similar to consideration). The replication mattered — it meant the finding wasn't an artifact of measurement.

### Named trade-off: people vs. output

The original hope was simple: *If high consideration and high initiating structure are both good, then leaders should be high on both, and they'll always be effective.*

That didn't work out. The effect of these behaviors varied wildly by situation:

- In some organizations, high initiating structure increased performance. In others, it made little difference or made things worse.
- Most people reported satisfaction when their leader was considerate. But consideration had no consistent effect on *performance*.

The researchers were disappointed. They had hoped to identify the "ideal" profile and train leaders into it. Instead, they found: **there is no one best style of leader behavior for all situations**.

This is not a failure of the research. It's a discovery: the right leadership behavior depends on the context.

### Worked example: the assembly line vs. the research lab

An assembly-line supervisor whose job is to maintain quality and throughput benefits from high initiating structure. The work is clear. The metrics are clear. The supervisor who sets expectations and maintains standards moves the operation forward. Consideration matters too — burned-out workers make mistakes — but if the choice is between caring and clarity, clarity moves the work.

A research-lab director managing PhDs working on unsolved problems faces a different context. If the director spends all their time on initiating structure ("You must follow procedure X, complete task Y by date Z"), you suppress the exploration that discoveries require. The work needs a leader who asks good questions, supports autonomy, and maintains an intellectual standard — high consideration and high initiating structure, but deployed differently.

### Common misconception: "Behavioral leadership is universal"

False. Behavior matters, and these two dimensions are a useful framework. But whether high consideration or high initiating structure is *effective* depends on the task, the followers, the environment, and the goal. The next concept section addresses this directly.

## Concept 4 — Contingency theories: matching the leader to the moment

### The scene and the question

By the 1950s, researchers had evidence that traits mattered modestly, and that leadership behavior could be categorized into two dimensions. But they also had evidence that the *same behavior* produced wildly different results in different contexts.

Ralph Stogdill stated it plainly: "The qualities, characteristics, and skills required in a leader are determined to a large extent by the demands of the situation in which he is to function as a leader."

This raised a new question: *Under what conditions is which leadership behavior effective?*

This is the beginning of contingency theory — the insight that leadership effectiveness is contingent on (depends on) the fit between the leader, the followers, and the situation.

### The mechanism: Fiedler's contingency model

Fred Fiedler, a psychologist at the University of Washington, created the most detailed contingency model. The framework is simple: **To predict whether a leader will be effective, you need three pieces of information:** the leader's underlying orientation, the favorability of the situation, and the match between them.

**The leader's orientation** is measured by the Least-Preferred Coworker (LPC) scale. You're asked: Think of the person you least enjoy working with. How do you evaluate them? If you rate them as fairly pleasant, capable, and trustworthy even though you don't like working with them, you're high-LPC: *relationship-oriented*. You can separate your personal feelings from your professional judgment. If you rate them harshly — unpleasant, incompetent, untrustworthy — you're low-LPC: *task-oriented*. Your primary motivation is to accomplish the task, and relationships matter once the task is done.

This is a trait-like orientation — it's relatively stable over time. Fiedler argued that leaders don't change their orientation much, so the organization's job is to *engineer the situation to fit the leader* (or find a different leader).

**The situation's favorability** depends on three factors:

- **Leader-member relations** — Do people trust and like the leader? Is the relationship good?
- **Task structure** — Is the goal clear? Is there an obvious path to success? Or is the work ambiguous?
- **Position power** — Can the leader actually enforce decisions? Do they have the authority to reward or punish?

The most favorable situation is when the relationship is good, the task is clear, and the leader has power. The most unfavorable is the opposite.

**The match.** Fiedler's research showed:

- **Relationship-oriented leaders (high-LPC) are most effective in moderate-favorability situations.** When things are somewhat uncertain and require buy-in, the relationship-oriented leader's nondirective, inclusive approach builds commitment.
- **Task-oriented leaders (low-LPC) are most effective in highly favorable and highly unfavorable situations.** When the task is clear and you just need execution, the task-oriented leader's focus on standards and clear direction works. When everything is chaos and people are panicking, the task-oriented leader's calm insistence on getting the fundamentals right stabilizes things.
- **Mid-LPC leaders are most versatile.** They can shift between relationship and task focus depending on what the situation requires.

### Named trade-off: fit vs. flexibility

Fiedler's model assumes leaders have a dominant style that is hard to change. So the contingency solution is: **diagnose the situation, and either put the right leader in it or change the situation to fit the leader you have.**

This is controversial because it seems to say: *Don't develop the leader. Work around them.*

The alternative approach — represented by Hersey and Blanchard's "situational leadership" — argues the opposite: **Leaders can and should adapt their behavior to the situation.** A good leader is flexible. They can be directive when the task is clear and the follower is inexperienced. They can be supportive when the follower is experienced but unmotivated. They can be delegative when the follower is both experienced and motivated.

Both are partially right. Some leaders are genuinely rigid — their personality, their cognitive style, their core motivation doesn't shift much. Others are quite adaptive. The organizations with the most consistent leadership effectiveness are those that **do both**: invest in developing flexibility, but also thoughtfully match leaders to situations where their natural style is an asset, not a liability.

### Worked example: the startup CEO crisis

A startup founder leads through crisis. The situation is highly unfavorable: unclear path to success, little structure, founders questioning whether the company can survive. Founder is task-oriented (low-LPC). Every decision is made fast. There's no process — there's speed. This works. People follow because chaos is being managed.

The startup survives and grows. Now the company has 200 people. The situation has changed: the path is clearer, there's structure, people mostly trust the CEO. The situation is now highly favorable. The task-oriented CEO is still using the same moves — rapid decisions, little consultation, efficiency over inclusion. But now these moves feel autocratic. People feel unheard. People burn out. Effectiveness drops.

Fiedler's model predicts this. The CEO's style doesn't match the new situation. The solutions: (1) bring in a different leader for the growth phase, or (2) the CEO deliberately develops relationship-oriented behaviors to match the new context. (1) is cleaner for the organization. (2) requires real change on the CEO's part.

### Common misconception: "A good leader works in any context"

Partially true. A leader who is honest, cognitively capable, and willing to learn can be effective in many contexts. But the research is clear: **the match between the leader's default style and the situation's demands matters a lot**. A relationship-oriented leader trying to manage an emergency where quick decisions are needed will be less effective. A task-oriented leader trying to lead a creative team through ambiguity will frustrate people and suppress innovation. The best leaders have enough self-awareness to know their style and enough flexibility to bend it. But they also choose contexts where their natural style is an asset.

## Deep dive — Transformational leadership and why it works (under specific conditions)

### The mechanism: from transactional to transformational

For decades, the leadership theory said this: **Leaders lead through exchange.** You do what the leader wants, and the leader gives you what you want (money, recognition, status, time off). This is *transactional leadership*. It's powerful. It works. It's the foundation of most organizations.

But in the 1970s, James MacGregor Burns and later Bernard Bass observed something different in leaders who created large-scale change:

These leaders didn't lead through exchange. They led through **inspiration**. They articulated a vision of what *could* be. They modeled commitment to that vision. They intellectually stimulated people — challenged assumptions, encouraged dissent, made people think. They showed individualized consideration — they noticed each person's development and supported it. They used charisma not as personal magnetism but as *idealized influence* — the followers saw in the leader a commitment to something larger than themselves.

Burns called this **transformational leadership** — the leader transforms the follower's goals and values, not just their behavior. The follower comes to internalize the mission, not just comply with it.

### The evidence: when it works and when it doesn't

The evidence on transformational leadership is strong — but specific. Transformational leadership is associated with:

- Higher follower satisfaction
- Higher follower performance
- Higher follower motivation and commitment
- More citizenship behaviors (people going beyond the job description)

This is real and measurable. But it comes with conditions:

**Condition 1: Vision must be communicable.** Warren Bennis, who studied transformational leaders extensively, found that the vision is only effective to the extent the leader can communicate it in a way followers internalize as their own. A visionary leader who can't articulate the vision is just a person with abstract feelings.

**Condition 2: The environment must allow it.** Transformational leadership works in uncertain, turbulent environments where people need inspiration to persist. In highly structured, clear environments, it matters less. You don't inspire your way through a well-mapped assembly line.

**Condition 3: The trust baseline must be in place.** Transformational leadership builds on top of a foundation: followers believe the leader is honest and has their interest in mind. If that's not in place, the vision just looks like marketing.

**Condition 4: It requires real change.** Transformational leadership is not about smooth, incremental improvement. It's about genuine discontinuity. If the organization doesn't need transformation, if the task is optimization, transformational leadership can actually distract from the work.

### Worked example: Shackleton and the *Endurance*

Shackleton's situation was transformational in every sense: his original goal (crossing Antarctica) was impossible. The situation was existential (everyone dies or everyone lives). The path forward required people to commit to something not guaranteed.

What Shackleton did:

- **Articulated a new vision**: We're not crossing Antarctica. We're getting everyone home alive. That is the mission that matters now.
- **Modeled commitment**: He shared hardship with his men. He slept in the same cold tent. He rationed his own food as carefully as everyone else.
- **Maintained hope**: When the ice began to melt, he made decisions to move toward land. When people talked about survival, he joined them. He didn't pretend things were fine, but he made clear that the goal was achievable.
- **Showed individualized consideration**: He made note of which men were struggling. He rotated assignments so no one was in crisis indefinitely. He noticed who needed encouragement and who needed to be left alone.

Every man survived. This is what transformational leadership looks like at its best: the follower's commitment to the mission transcends self-interest.

### Named trade-off: transformation vs. charisma risk

Transformational leadership relies partly on charisma — the personal magnetism of the leader. The risk is that charisma can become a substitute for judgment. Followers can become so invested in the leader's vision that they stop scrutinizing whether the vision is actually sound. They can overlook bad decisions because the leader has been right before. They can follow the leader into a ditch.

The research is clear on this risk: charismatic leadership without the counterweight of honest, capable followers who ask hard questions can lead to disaster. (Enron is a well-documented example of charismatic leadership divorced from sound judgment and honest reporting.)

The best organizations have **transformational leaders who actively encourage dissent**, who welcome bad news, who want their ideas challenged. These leaders get the benefits of transformational leadership (commitment, motivation, going above and beyond) without the downside (blind loyalty leading to bad decisions).

## Synthesis — what the research teaches and what's still unsettled

### What we know

**Leadership is a relationship, not a trait.** You cannot identify a "born leader" by looking at someone's traits or personality in isolation. You have to understand the context, the follower, and the situation.

**Traits matter, but modestly.** Honesty, drive, cognitive ability, and self-confidence appear more often in leaders. But they explain maybe 30–50% of leadership effectiveness. The rest is context and learning.

**Leadership behavior can be categorized into two independent dimensions.** Task-oriented (initiating structure) and relationship-oriented (consideration) leadership are not opposites — they're independent. A good leader can be high on both. The question is not "Which should I be?" but "When should I emphasize which?"

**The right leadership behavior depends on the situation.** Contingency theories (Fiedler, Hersey-Blanchard, House's path-goal theory) show that the same behavior produces different results in different contexts. A directive, task-focused leader is effective in crisis or chaos. A relationship-focused, supportive leader is effective when people are experienced but unmotivated or confused. The most effective leaders adapt.

**Transformational leadership works, but under specific conditions.** When followers need inspiration to persist through uncertainty, when the mission is genuinely important, and when the follower base is willing to scrutinize the leader's judgment, transformational leadership produces commitment and performance beyond transactional exchange.

### What's still unclear

**The causal arrow on charisma.** Does charisma *create* transformational leadership, or does transformational leadership sometimes happen to have a charismatic person leading it? The evidence leans toward: charisma is one tool among many, and it's not necessary for transformation.

**Cross-cultural variation.** The evidence is largely from Western organizational contexts. The degree to which these theories apply in other cultures — where leadership might be understood as collective rather than individual, or where hierarchy is more or less important — is unknown. Research in this area is expanding, but the picture is incomplete.

**The development of leadership flexibility.** We know that some leaders can adapt their style and others are more rigid. We don't fully understand how much of that difference is personality, how much is learned, and how much is situational (whether the person feels safe enough to be flexible). This matters for leadership development.

### One honest limit

The most consistent finding is also the least satisfying: **The quality that matters most in leaders — the ability to make sound judgments in novel, high-stakes situations — is the hardest to predict and the hardest to teach.** Twin studies suggest it's somewhat heritable. Cognitive ability predicts it. Experience matters. But we have no algorithm that says, "This person will make the right call under pressure." This is why boards of directors spend so much time on leadership selection. They know that getting this one right is irreplaceable.

## Myths to correct

### Myth 1: Leaders are born, not made

**The folklore:** Look at Shackleton, Steve Jobs, Cleopatra. These were special people. They arrived in the world with the stuff inside them.

**The evidence:** Twin studies show about 30% heritability for leadership. That means 70% is environmental — training, opportunity, feedback, the specific moment, who needed what when. The traits that appear in leaders (drive, honesty, cognitive ability) are partially heritable, but they're also developed through feedback and practice. And the *skills* of leadership — how to communicate a vision, how to create psychological safety, how to make people feel heard — are learned.

**The implication:** Leadership is not destiny. You can develop into a leader even if you didn't start out with all the traits. You can improve as a leader even if you're already leading. Conversely, having the traits is not enough — you have to develop the skills.

### Myth 2: Great leaders share specific traits (and you can spot them)

**The folklore:** Great leaders are charismatic. Or they're decisive. Or they're visionary. Or they're empathetic. Pick your trait, and there's a business book arguing it's the key to leadership.

**The evidence:** Specific traits appear *slightly more often* in leaders than in non-leaders, but the effects are small and context-dependent. Charisma helps in some situations (times of radical change, when you need people to follow into the unknown). It's a liability in others (technical organizations, situations that require scrutiny and dissent, when people need to think rigorously rather than follow brilliantly).

What *does* matter across most contexts: honesty, cognitive ability, knowledge of the business, and willingness to learn from feedback. But these are enablers, not guarantees.

**The implication:** If you're hiring for leadership, don't hunt for a single trait. Hunt for a pattern: Is this person honest? Can they think rigorously about complex problems? Do they know the territory? Are they willing to learn? And then diagnose the situation: Does the current moment need transformation or optimization? Does it need relationship-building or directive clarity? Match the leader to those needs.

### Myth 3: The Stanford Prison Experiment shows us how leaders misuse power

**The folklore:** In Stanfords's experiment (1971), researchers assigned college students to be "guards" and "prisoners" in a mock prison. The guards, given power, quickly became abusive. The experiment is cited as evidence that situational power corrupts, that ordinary people become tyrants when given authority, and that leadership in hierarchies is inherently corrosive.

**The evidence and the problem:** A detailed reanalysis by Thibault Le Texier (2019), based on interviews with participants and archival research, showed that the experiment's procedures were deeply flawed. The experimenter explicitly coached the guards on how to be oppressive. Prisoners who tried to leave were told to stay. The conditions were set up to make oppression the expected behavior.

More fundamentally: The experiment was not about leadership. It was about compliance with experimenter demand. The participants performed a role because an authority figure (the researcher) was directing them to do so.

**The implication:** Power doesn't automatically corrupt. The situations in which people use power, the feedback they receive, the cultures they're embedded in — these matter enormously. A leader in an organization that values honesty and scrutinizes power is very different from a prison guard in a designed environment. Real leadership research looks at actual organizations, actual decisions, actual consequences — not laboratory theater.

## Chapter summary

Leadership is a relationship, not a possession. It's built between a person and a group trying to accomplish something together, shaped by what the situation demands.

Traits matter — honesty, drive, and cognitive ability predict leadership emergence and effectiveness. But they explain only part of the story. The rest depends on the followers (who choose to accept influence), the context (what the situation demands), and the fit between them.

Behavioral research shows that effective leaders can be both task-focused and relationship-focused. The question is not which to be, but when to emphasize which.

Contingency theories teach us to diagnose the situation: Is the crisis existential or developmental? Is the task clear or ambiguous? Do the followers have the skills and motivation to act independently, or do they need direction? The answer tells you which leadership approach is most likely to work.

Transformational leadership works when the followers need inspiration to persist through uncertainty and when the leader has built a foundation of trust and competence. It's powerful, but it's not universal.

The most consequential leaders are those who can match their approach to the moment, who admit what they don't understand, and who build organizations in which people feel safe enough to tell them hard truths.
