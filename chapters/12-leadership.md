# Chapter 12 — Leadership
*Why the same move that saves one organization sinks another.*

---

Here is a fact about Ernest Shackleton that most people who cite him don't mention.

In August 1914, with his ship *Endurance* locked in Antarctic pack ice and breaking apart, Shackleton had twenty-eight men stranded in one of the most hostile environments on Earth, 346 miles from the nearest land. Over the next twenty-two months he made thousands of decisions — which man slept next to which, who got the extra biscuit, whether a particular risk was worth taking. Every single person came home alive. The story gets cited, constantly, as proof that leaders are born with special qualities — vision, charisma, strength of will — that ordinary people lack.

But here is what the Shackleton story actually proves, if you look at it carefully: Shackleton's approach worked in that situation. It worked because his men were in existential crisis, because the goal had to be completely redefined, because survival required sustained commitment to something not guaranteed. The same Shackleton — the same person, using the same moves — would have been a catastrophic CEO of a stable company running quarterly optimization cycles. He would have generated urgency where none was needed, inspiration where people needed clarity, transformation where people needed consistency. The trait that made him legendary in Antarctica is not a universal virtue. It's a fit.

This chapter is about fit. Not about finding the right leader — about finding the right leader *for the moment*. The research is unambiguous: there is no single effective leadership style. There are styles that match situations and styles that don't. Understanding the matching mechanism is what distinguishes someone who has read leadership folklore from someone who understands how leadership actually works.

---

## What leadership is, and what it isn't

Start with the definition, because the word is used so loosely that it obscures the mechanism.

Leadership is a social influence relationship between two or more people who depend on each other to attain mutual goals in a group setting. Notice what's in that definition: it's *mutual*, it unfolds over time, it's goal-directed, and — critically — it's *perceived*. The followers decide whether the person has influence. The title alone doesn't grant it.

This immediately separates leadership from management. A manager is appointed to a position. A manager has formal authority — the right to schedule work, allocate resources, make hiring decisions, reward and punish. These are real and powerful. But formal authority is not leadership. It's access to the machinery. Leadership is what happens when people choose to follow, when they accept your influence because they believe in your judgment or your vision or your commitment to the shared goal.

Edwin Hollander, who studied leadership emergence for decades, made an observation that the folklore consistently underplays: the follower is the most critical factor in any leadership event. It is the follower who perceives the situation. It is the follower who decides whether to accept or reject a person's influence. It is the follower who surrenders power — willingly. The leader cannot lead without that surrender.

This matters practically because it means the transition from manager to leader is not accomplished by accumulating authority. It's accomplished by building credibility — the perception that your judgment is worth following, that you see what needs to be done, that you won't exploit the power differential. A manager who has been in their role for ten years and has never built that credibility is not a leader. They're a manager. They get compliance. They don't get commitment.

The distinction between compliance and commitment is the difference between people doing the work when you're watching and people doing it because they believe it matters. Organizations that run on compliance get the letter of the work, not the spirit. Organizations that cultivate genuine leadership get the spirit — and the spirit is where the extra effort lives.

## The trait question

For about two thousand years, people told stories about leaders as heroes — Alexander, Caesar, Cleopatra, Genghis Khan — and built from those stories a narrative: great leaders are born with special qualities. Some people arrive in the world with the stuff inside them. Others don't.

This belief was so durable that when researchers first tried to study leadership empirically in the early twentieth century, they were asking the same question: What traits do great leaders share? If we could identify the list, we could recruit the right people and skip the rest.

Ralph Stogdill spent years on this project. He reviewed hundreds of studies, created questionnaires, tried to find the pattern. Here is what he found: Leaders do tend to have certain traits. Drive, self-confidence, cognitive ability, knowledge of the domain, honesty and integrity. These traits appear more often in leaders than in non-leaders.

But the relationship is weak to moderate. Extraversion correlates with leadership emergence at *r* = 0.31. That means extraversion explains about ten percent of the variance in whether someone becomes a leader. Even when you combine multiple traits, you explain perhaps thirty to fifty percent of leadership effectiveness. The rest — the majority — is context, learning, and fit.

Twin studies — which separate genetic from environmental influence — show roughly thirty percent heritability for leadership. That's real. It's not zero. But it means seventy percent of the variation comes from training, opportunity, timing, who needed what when.

The honest summary of the trait research: certain traits function as *preconditions* that suggest leadership potential. Drive, honesty, and cognitive ability are real prerequisites. A person who lacks honesty and integrity cannot build the trust that leadership requires. A person who lacks the cognitive ability to think rigorously about complex situations will make bad calls at the moments that matter most. These aren't optional.

But preconditions are not destiny. They create the possibility. What happens with that possibility depends on the environment, the specific demands of the situation, and what the person learns through experience and feedback. The person who has the traits and never encounters the right situation never develops into a leader. The person who encounters the right situation without the foundational traits will fail when the situation gets hard.

The practical implication cuts in both directions. Organizations that assume leadership is born and cannot be developed are leaving capability on the table — they're not investing in the seventy percent that is learnable. Organizations that assume any person can be developed into a leader regardless of foundational traits are wasting resources. The evidence calls for both: hire for the traits that cannot be taught (honesty, drive, basic cognitive capacity), and then invest seriously in developing the skills and judgment that turn those traits into actual influence.

## What leaders do

By the 1940s, researchers had spent decades on traits and found modest results. So they asked a different question: What if we're looking at the wrong thing? What if the question isn't who the leader is, but what the leader does?

Two independent research programs set out to answer that question — Ohio State and the University of Michigan. They used different methods, studied different organizations, and found almost identical results.

At Ohio State, researchers asked people to describe what their leader actually did. They found two distinct clusters of behavior.

The first cluster they called *consideration*: supportive, friendly behavior. The leader listens. They represent people's interests. They recognize contributions, express concern for how people feel, make people feel valued. This addresses the group's maintenance needs — keeping the team coherent, ensuring people feel the work is worth doing.

The second cluster they called *initiating structure*: task-focused behavior. The leader schedules work, sets expectations, provides direction, maintains performance standards, holds people to their commitments. This addresses the group's task needs — making progress toward the goal.

The researchers expected these to be opposite ends of a single spectrum: either you're a people-person or a taskmaster. What they found instead was that the dimensions are independent. You can be high on both. You can be low on both. You can be high on one and low on the other. A leader can care deeply about the people in their group *and* maintain rigorous expectations about what gets done.

This seems obvious once you've heard it. But it overturned a persistent assumption: that there is a fundamental tradeoff between caring about people and caring about results. The Ohio State data said: no. The best leaders do both. The question is not which to be, but when to emphasize which.

The Michigan team found the same thing independently with slightly different labels — job-centered and employee-centered — which was confirmation that the pattern was real and not an artifact of how Ohio State had measured it.

But here is where the optimistic version of the story runs into trouble. When researchers tried to determine whether high consideration or high initiating structure produced better outcomes, the results were inconsistent across contexts. In some organizations, high initiating structure increased performance significantly. In others, it had little effect or made things worse. Consideration reliably increased follower satisfaction, but had no consistent effect on performance. The same behavior produced different results in different situations.

This was not the finding the researchers wanted. They had hoped to identify the optimal profile and train leaders into it. Instead, they discovered that there is no universally optimal profile. Whether task-focus or relationship-focus is more effective depends on the situation. This insight took another twenty years to formalize into contingency theory.

## The contingency insight

Ralph Stogdill stated it plainly in 1974: "The qualities, characteristics, and skills required in a leader are determined to a large extent by the demands of the situation in which he is to function as a leader."

This seems obvious. It's actually radical. It means the question "who is the best leader?" has no general answer. The answer is always relative to the situation. A leader who is brilliant in one context may be disastrously wrong for another.

Fred Fiedler worked out the most detailed version of this insight. His contingency model starts with two observations. First, leaders have a dominant orientation — some are primarily motivated by relationship quality, others primarily by task completion. Fiedler measured this with an instrument called the Least-Preferred Coworker scale: how do you describe the person you least enjoy working with? Someone who can still describe that person charitably — can separate their dislike of the working relationship from their evaluation of the person — is relationship-oriented. Someone who describes them harshly is task-oriented. Fiedler argued this orientation is relatively stable — it's a core motivation, not easily changed.

Second, situations vary in *favorability* — how much the situation gives the leader room to operate. Three factors determine favorability: the quality of the leader-member relationship (do people trust and like you?), the clarity of the task (is there an obvious path to success?), and the leader's position power (can you actually enforce decisions?). When all three are favorable, the situation is highly favorable. When all three are unfavorable, it's the opposite.

The matching prediction: task-oriented leaders perform best in highly favorable situations (clear path, trusted leader, real authority — just execute) and in highly unfavorable situations (chaos, crisis, unclear path — someone needs to grab the wheel and impose order). Relationship-oriented leaders perform best in moderately favorable situations — where things are somewhat uncertain, buy-in matters, and the leader needs people to commit rather than just comply.

Fiedler drew a conclusion from this that proved controversial: since leaders' orientations are hard to change, organizations should engineer situations to fit the leaders they have, not try to change the leaders to fit the situations. This seems pessimistic, and in practice most organizations don't follow it completely. But the underlying insight is sound: it is much easier to change the clarity of a task or the degree of authority someone has than it is to change the core motivation of a person who has been leading for twenty years.

A different contingency theory — Hersey and Blanchard's situational leadership model — takes the more optimistic position that leaders can and should adapt their behavior to the readiness of their followers. A new, inexperienced follower needs direction: tell them what to do and how. A follower who has developed some skill but lacks confidence needs coaching and support. A follower who is capable but unmotivated needs participation — bring them into the decision, give them ownership. A fully capable, fully motivated follower needs delegation — get out of their way.

Both Fiedler and Hersey-Blanchard are capturing something true. Some leaders genuinely cannot flex their orientation. Others have real range. The most capable leaders know their natural default, know which situations that default fits, and have enough range to behave differently when the situation calls for it — even if it's uncomfortable.

## Transformation

For decades, the dominant model of leadership said: leaders lead through exchange. You do what the leader asks, and the leader gives you what you want — money, recognition, status, opportunity. This is *transactional leadership*. It's powerful. Most organizations run on it. It works as long as people have what they want and the leader can deliver it.

James MacGregor Burns and later Bernard Bass observed something different in leaders who created genuine large-scale change. These leaders weren't running the exchange mechanism. They were doing something that changed what followers wanted. They articulated a vision of what *could be* that became the follower's own aspiration. They modeled commitment to that vision with a consistency that made the follower trust the goal was real. They challenged people intellectually — asked hard questions, invited dissent, pushed people to think beyond the obvious. They showed individualized consideration — noticed each person's development and supported it specifically.

Burns called this transformational leadership: the leader transforms the follower's goals and values, not just their behavior. The follower comes to internalize the mission rather than comply with it. The result is commitment that doesn't require continuous monitoring, initiative that goes beyond what's explicitly required, and performance that reflects genuine investment rather than contracted output.

The evidence is real. Transformational leadership is associated with higher follower satisfaction, higher follower performance, and more citizenship behaviors — the discretionary effort that shows up when people actually care about the outcome. But it comes with conditions.

Transformational leadership works in uncertain environments where people need inspiration to persist. In highly structured environments with clear goals and clear metrics, it matters less — you don't inspire your way through a well-mapped production line. It requires a trust foundation: if followers don't believe the leader is honest and has their interests in mind, the vision looks like marketing. And it requires real change to be worth attempting — if the organization needs optimization, not transformation, the transformational approach distracts rather than focuses.

The most important condition is the one most often ignored: transformational leadership can fail catastrophically when it tips into charismatic authority that suppresses scrutiny. Followers who are deeply committed to a leader's vision can stop asking whether the vision is actually sound. They overlook bad decisions because the leader has been right before. They follow into the ditch. The organizations that get the benefits of transformational leadership without the costs are those with leaders who actively invite challenge, welcome bad news, and treat dissent as evidence the organization is working correctly.

Shackleton was transformational in the right sense. He articulated a new mission — not crossing the continent but getting everyone home alive — and made that mission the thing everyone was committed to. He modeled the commitment by sharing every hardship. He maintained hope by making decisions that demonstrated escape was possible. But his men were also not passive followers. They were competent, experienced, and entirely capable of telling him when an idea was wrong. The transformation worked because it operated on top of a foundation of genuine mutual trust and real competence, not in spite of it.

## What the myths get wrong

Three pieces of leadership folklore recur so often that they're worth addressing directly.

The first: leaders are born, not made. The evidence says thirty percent heritability. The traits that matter — honesty, drive, cognitive capacity — are partially inherited but substantially developed. The skills of leadership — how to communicate a vision, how to build trust, how to create conditions where people tell you hard truths — are learned. Leadership is neither destiny nor pure construction. It is both.

The second: great leaders share specific traits, and you can spot them. Charisma, decisiveness, vision — business books pick a favorite trait and build a theory around it. The evidence says specific traits appear *slightly more often* in leaders than non-leaders, but the effects are small and context-dependent. Charisma is an asset when you need people to follow into the unknown. It is a liability in organizations that need careful deliberation, where the charismatic leader's certainty suppresses the scrutiny that would catch errors. What holds across contexts is much more modest: honesty, cognitive ability, domain knowledge, and the willingness to update in response to feedback. These are prerequisites, not sufficient conditions.

The third is specific to how leadership and power get discussed: the Stanford Prison Experiment, in which college students assigned as guards in a mock prison quickly became abusive, is cited as evidence that situational power corrupts, that ordinary people become tyrants when given authority. This is used to argue that hierarchical leadership is inherently dangerous.

The problem: detailed reanalysis of the experiment by Thibault Le Texier in 2019, based on interviews with participants and archival research, showed that the guards were explicitly coached by the experimenter to be oppressive. Prisoners who tried to leave were told to stay. The conditions were designed to produce the result, and then the result was attributed to the power of the situation rather than to experimenter direction. More fundamentally, the experiment was not about leadership — it was about compliance with authority. Its lessons for how leaders in organizations use power are essentially nil. Real leadership research studies actual organizations, actual decisions, and actual consequences over time. The Stanford experiment was laboratory theater.

## The honest limit

The most consistent finding in leadership research is also the least satisfying: the quality that matters most in leaders — the ability to make sound judgments in novel, high-stakes situations — is the hardest to predict and the hardest to teach.

Cognitive ability predicts it. Honesty is a prerequisite for it. Experience in situations with real feedback helps calibrate it. But there is no formula that says: this person will make the right call when the stakes are high and the path is unclear. This is why leadership selection is genuinely hard, why boards spend so much time on it, and why organizations that get it wrong at the senior level pay such severe costs.

The other honest limit is what isn't in the research. The evidence comes predominantly from Western organizational contexts. The degree to which contingency models, behavioral dimensions, and transformational frameworks apply in cultures where leadership is understood as collective rather than individual, or where hierarchy carries different meaning, is an open question. The literature is expanding, but the picture is incomplete.

## The loop

Here is the argument this chapter has been building.

Leadership is a relationship, not a trait and not a technique. It requires a person who has certain foundational qualities — honesty, drive, and cognitive capacity — and who has developed the skills to translate those qualities into influence. It requires followers who choose to accept that influence. And it requires a situation that the leader's approach actually fits.

Traits matter as prerequisites. Behavior matters as the mechanism through which influence is exercised. But the same behavior produces different outcomes in different situations, and the most consequential thing a leader can do is accurately read what the moment demands and bring the appropriate approach rather than the comfortable one.

The manager who imposes tight structure in a high-performing autonomous team will generate resentment and lose the best people. The leader who employs inspirational vision in a crisis requiring clear rapid decisions will generate confusion and lose the race against time. Shackleton's approach worked in Antarctica because Antarctica demanded it. What makes leadership effective is not the heroic quality of the approach — it's the match.

## Summary

Leadership is a social influence relationship, not a formal position. Managers have authority; leaders have influence. The distinction matters because authority produces compliance and influence produces commitment, and organizations that run only on compliance cannot do the things that require genuine initiative.

Traits are real prerequisites — honesty, drive, and cognitive ability appear reliably in effective leaders — but they explain only thirty to fifty percent of leadership effectiveness. Thirty percent of leadership is heritable. The rest is environment, learning, and situation.

The behavioral research from Ohio State and Michigan identified two independent dimensions of leadership behavior: initiating structure (task-focused) and consideration (relationship-focused). These are not opposites. A leader can be high on both. Whether emphasizing one or the other produces better outcomes depends on the situation, not on some universal standard.

Contingency theories formalized this insight. Fiedler showed that task-oriented leaders perform best in high-favorability and low-favorability situations; relationship-oriented leaders perform best in moderate-favorability situations. Hersey and Blanchard argued that effective leaders adapt their approach to the readiness of their followers. Both insights point to the same conclusion: match the approach to the moment.

Transformational leadership — inspiring commitment through vision, intellectual stimulation, and individualized consideration — works when followers need inspiration to persist through uncertainty and when a foundation of genuine trust exists. It fails when it tips into charismatic authority that suppresses the scrutiny that catches errors.

Three myths warrant correction: that leaders are born (thirty percent heritability, seventy percent environment), that specific traits are universal virtues (traits are context-dependent), and that the Stanford Prison Experiment tells us something reliable about leadership and power (it was experimenter-directed theater, not natural observation).

The irreducible hard problem: the quality that matters most — sound judgment under novel, high-stakes conditions — is the hardest to predict and the hardest to develop. There is no algorithm for it. There is only experience, feedback, honesty, and the willingness to keep learning.
