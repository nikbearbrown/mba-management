# Chapter 1 — Management and Organizational Behavior
*What the lights at Hawthorne revealed about every organization you will ever work in.*

---

Here is a puzzle from 1924.

Researchers at the Hawthorne Works of Western Electric — a telephone relay factory outside Chicago — wanted to know whether lighting affects productivity. The setup is as simple as experiments get. They isolate a group of women assembly workers. They record baseline output. Then they increase the light intensity. Output goes up. Fine. Expected.

Then they decrease the light. Output goes up again.

They return the light to the original level. Output goes up once more.

At every intervention — brighter, dimmer, unchanged — the workers produce more relays, more consistently, with fewer errors. The researchers have managed to find an independent variable that predicts productivity in exactly the same direction regardless of which way it moves. That is not a finding. That is a broken experiment.

Elton Mayo, the Harvard researcher overseeing the studies, eventually figured out what was actually happening. The variable was not the light. The variable was *attention*. The workers knew someone was watching. Someone was taking notes, measuring their output, caring about the result. And that — just that — changed how they worked. They moved more carefully. They helped each other. They showed up differently. The effect evaporated when the observation stopped.

This discovery is now called the **Hawthorne effect**, and it is both smaller and larger than it first appears. Smaller: later reanalyses of the original data suggest Mayo overstated it. Other factors — which workers were selected, changes in supervision practices, wage adjustments mid-study — explained more variance than Mayo credited to the observation effect alone. The effect is real. It is not as enormous as the original story implies.

Larger: it revealed something that every theory of management before it had missed entirely. A factory is not a machine with people attached. It is a social system. Changing the social conditions around work changes the work itself. The machinery of management is not primarily about levers and outputs. It is about creating the conditions under which people *choose* to do their best work.

That is the discovery this chapter is organized around. Not because it settles everything — it does not — but because it locates the central problem. Management is hard not because planning and organizing are technically complex, but because the thing being managed is people, and people respond to the whole situation they are in, not just to the incentives and procedures you design.

## What Work Actually Does

Before you can manage people effectively, you need to understand what work does for them. Not what work *is* — that is easy: activity that produces something of value for others, in exchange for compensation. The harder question is what work *provides*, and why people care about it in ways that go well beyond the paycheck.

In the 1970s, the Chicago writer Studs Terkel spent years interviewing workers across occupations and asking them what their work meant. The answers were not about money. A steelworker said he used to be able to point to a house he built, logs he stacked — something that proved his labor had landed somewhere real. A sanitation truck driver said he felt he earned his money and his wife was proud of him; that combination seemed to matter more than the wage itself. A receptionist described being dismissed at a party for her job title, and said she felt worthless — not because her income was insufficient, but because her work did not confer social standing in the rooms she wanted to enter.

Work serves at least five functions that money cannot fully substitute.

**The economic function** is obvious and real. Work produces income. For most people, income is the first reason they work. But once income exceeds the threshold required for basic stability, it becomes one variable among several — and often not the most powerful one. Research consistently finds that a person earning $45,000 in meaningful work reports higher satisfaction than someone earning $65,000 in work they find pointless. This does not mean money does not matter. It means money alone does not explain behavior as well as managers often assume.

**The social function** is one most people do not notice until it disappears. The workplace is where most adults spend the majority of their waking hours. It is where friendships form, where they become known by colleagues, where daily conversation happens. Strip work away — as the pandemic did for millions of people in 2020 — and many people find themselves not just idle but profoundly isolated, even when the work itself continues remotely. The work can be identical. The social context can be gone. The difference is not subtle.

**The status function** is one people are often reluctant to admit matters to them. But it does. In nearly every society, your occupation signals your social position. A job that confers status — or that denies status you believe you deserve — shapes how others treat you, what opportunities become visible to you, and how you see yourself. A manager who ignores this is missing a real motivational variable.

**The identity and self-esteem function** may be the deepest one. Freud observed that work binds a person more closely to reality than almost any other activity. Here is what he meant: successful work accumulates evidence that you are capable. You attempt something difficult, you complete it, and you have demonstrated — to yourself and to others — that you can do hard things. Work answers the question *what can I do?* in a way that is hard to replicate elsewhere. When work fails to provide this — when it is too easy, too meaningless, too disconnected from any sense of accomplishment — the result is what Emile Durkheim called **alienation**: not just dissatisfaction, but a sense of being separated from something you should belong to.

**The actualization function** is the rarest, and when present, transforms work from obligation into something closer to calling. Some jobs offer genuine challenge — the kind where the difficulty is real, the growth is real, and the person becomes more capable across time. Most jobs do not offer this consistently. But when they do, the motivational effect is unlike anything a performance bonus can produce.

Why does this taxonomy matter for a manager? Because when something goes wrong in a team — when engagement drops, when turnover climbs, when effort decays — the reflex is to reach for the economic lever. Pay people more. Add a bonus. The research suggests this works mainly when the economic function is the one that is broken. If the social function is broken, a raise fixes nothing. If the identity function is broken — if people feel their work does not demonstrate their capability to anyone — more money is not the diagnosis. This is why the Hawthorne effect was so disorienting. The researchers were trying to improve output through physical conditions. The workers were responding to something else entirely.

## What Managers Actually Do

Let me be precise about what management is, because the word gets used to mean almost everything.

**Management is the process of planning, organizing, directing, and controlling the activities of people in combination with other resources to accomplish organizational objectives.**

Four activities. Planning: setting direction, allocating resources, anticipating what will go wrong. Organizing: structuring work so efforts coordinate rather than cancel each other out. Directing: helping people understand what they are supposed to do and why it matters. Controlling: measuring whether results match expectations, and adjusting when they do not.

Different managers spend different proportions of time on each of these, depending on where they sit in the organization.

An executive — a CEO, a vice president — spends most time planning. They set strategy. They allocate capital. They decide which markets to enter. They spend relatively little time on the details of daily work. A first-line manager — a team lead, a shift supervisor — spends most time directing. They clarify what the task actually is. They remove obstacles. They troubleshoot. They spend almost no time on organizational strategy.

Between them, middle managers translate. They take strategy from executives and break it into work that the people around them can execute. They report results back up. They spend moderate time on each of the four activities.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: Organizational pyramid showing the three levels and how proportion of time shifts across the four management activities at each level] -->

The skills required shift with the level. I find it useful to think about three types.

**Technical skills** are expertise in the work itself. An accounting manager needs to understand accounting. A software engineering manager needs to understand software. You cannot set realistic goals for work you do not understand, and you cannot recognize good work when you see it if you do not know what good looks like. Technical skill is the entry price.

But here is the trap: technical skill alone does not make a manager. You can be the most skilled individual contributor in a department and be a terrible manager. This is one of the most common organizational tragedies — the person promoted on the basis of individual excellence, then failing because they lack what comes next.

**Human skills** are the ability to work with people. To listen carefully enough to understand what someone actually means, not just what they said. To give feedback that produces change rather than defensiveness. To navigate conflict without leaving wreckage on both sides. To build trust across difference. These matter at every level. A CEO who cannot inspire people lacks something essential. A team lead who cannot have a hard conversation with a struggling employee will fail their team and damage the people on it.

**Conceptual skills** are the ability to see the organization as a system. How does a decision in engineering ripple into sales? How does a culture shift in hiring change the team two years out? How does a change in one department's incentives affect what another department can accomplish? These skills matter more as you move up. An executive who cannot think this way will optimize one piece of the system and break another without knowing why. A first-line manager can succeed while thinking mainly about their own team.

This creates a predictable career problem. People tend to be promoted because they excel at one thing. The excellent engineer becomes a manager and tries to manage through technical excellence — reviewing every piece of code, solving problems themselves rather than developing the people around them. The technically capable manager gets promoted to an executive role and keeps optimizing operational details instead of setting direction. Each level requires different skills, not more of the same skills. Organizations that understand this build development programs around it. Organizations that do not keep making the same promotion mistakes.

## How Management Thinking Evolved

Ideas about management have a history, and knowing that history helps you separate the ideas that are genuinely grounded in evidence from the ones that are folk wisdom dressed up in business language.

**Scientific management** is where formal management thinking began, in the 1890s. Frederick Taylor was an engineer who believed that management could and should be scientific: measure the work, find the most efficient procedure, implement it, reward compliance. His methods genuinely improved productivity in factory settings. Assembly lines, standardized procedures, production scheduling — these innovations came from that tradition and produced real gains.

The cost was human. Work became mechanical. Autonomy was stripped out. Taylor believed this was acceptable because he thought most workers wanted only pay and security, and if you gave them enough pay, they would comply with any procedure. The Hawthorne studies showed this was wrong — not everywhere and always, but systematically enough to matter. Workers responded to conditions Taylor's model did not model: the social dimension, the sense of being seen, the quality of relationships at work.

**Human relations** theory came next, largely in response. Mayo and his colleagues argued that worker satisfaction was the variable managers should optimize. Happy workers, the theory went, would be productive workers. Organizations should invest in morale, social cohesion, group identity.

This was a genuine advance. It forced management attention toward the human experience of work. But it had a serious blind spot: it assumed that workers' interests and management's interests naturally aligned. Pay attention to your workers, make them happy, and they will work harder for the organization. In fact, worker interests and management interests often diverge in real ways. Workers want higher wages. Management wants lower costs. Workers want autonomy. Management wants predictability. The human relations school did not have a framework for this conflict because it assumed the conflict away.

**Systems thinking** arrived in the 1960s as a corrective. Organizations cannot be understood one variable at a time. The payment system affects motivation. Motivation affects performance. Performance affects the payment system. Hiring affects culture. Culture affects who applies to work there. Who applies affects culture again. You cannot pull one lever without three other things moving. Systems thinking made this visible in a way earlier schools had not.

The limitation is practical. Systems thinking is powerful as a diagnostic tool but provides limited guidance about what to actually do. It tells you that everything is connected and that simple interventions produce complex results. It does not tell you which lever to pull.

**Contingency theory** followed: maybe there is no single best way to manage. The right approach depends on context. The right leadership style for a five-person startup is different from the right leadership style for a ten-thousand-person institution. The right organizational structure for a hospital is different from the right structure for a software company. This is much closer to truth than any of the earlier schools, each of which claimed universal applicability. Its limitation is that it offers less in the way of concrete guidance: "it depends" is true but often not immediately useful.

**Evidence-based management** is the more recent attempt to say: base your decisions on what the research actually shows, not on intuition or tradition or what a compelling speaker claimed in a keynote. This is harder than it sounds, and rarer in practice than it should be. The research on management is messy. Findings often do not replicate across contexts. Organizations routinely ignore evidence and follow trends. Evidence-based management is less a settled paradigm than an aspiration — the direction the field is trying to move.

One honest example of what this demands: the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator is used in a large proportion of major organizations. Managers give it to teams as a bonding exercise. People take it and believe it has told them something fundamental about themselves. The research shows that MBTI has essentially no predictive validity for job performance. The Big Five trait model — which measures personality on continuous scales rather than categorical types — does predict performance modestly. MBTI does not. Organizations use MBTI because it is popular and intuitive, not because it predicts anything important. An evidence-based manager notices this distinction.

## A Model for Understanding Organizations

We can now put all of this together into a working model — a framework for locating problems in organizations. I think of organizations as composed of five interlocking blocks.

**Block 1: Individuals and groups.** Organizations are made of people with different personalities, values, abilities, and motivations. Some are highly conscientious; some are not. Some seek autonomy; some want clarity and structure. Some are energized by affiliation with others; some work best with sustained independence. Understanding these differences — not assuming uniformity — is the starting point of effective management.

**Block 2: Tasks and technology.** How does the work actually get done? Is it routine and algorithmic, or does it require sustained judgment? Is it done independently or in coordination with others? Does it require specialized expertise or can almost anyone learn it quickly? The nature of the task shapes what kind of person succeeds in it and what kind of management it requires. A highly routine task and a highly judgment-dependent task need different oversight, different feedback loops, different incentive structures.

**Block 3: Organization design.** How do you structure the organization so that effort coordinates rather than conflicts? Should decisions be centralized at the top or distributed to the people closest to the work? Should people be organized by function — all engineers together — or by product, with all the people needed to deliver one thing sitting together? Are reporting lines clear or ambiguous? Design shapes who talks to whom and what information flows where. The same people with the same skills can produce very different results under different designs.

**Block 4: Organizational processes.** Within the structure, how do decisions actually get made? How is power exercised? How do people communicate with each other — honestly or performatively? How are conflicts resolved — through legitimate disagreement or through political maneuvering? Are meetings forums where ideas are genuinely tested, or are they rituals where conclusions are ratified after being decided elsewhere? The same organizational structure can produce very different outcomes depending on the processes that operate within it.

**Block 5: Management.** All of this is held together by the quality of management. Management is the force that connects the other blocks and adjusts them when they are not working. A good manager sees all five blocks and knows which ones need attention. A poor manager focuses on one block — usually individuals — and cannot understand why their interventions do not produce results.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: The five-block model as interlocking puzzle pieces or a circular system diagram — show the connections between blocks, not just a list] -->

Here is how to use this model. An employee is disengaged. Output has dropped. They are not responding to feedback.

The reflex is to locate the problem in Block 1: this person is lazy, or unmotivated, or wrong for the role. Maybe. But before you act on that diagnosis, examine the other blocks.

Is the task (Block 2) giving this person the opportunity to use their strengths, or is it asking them to do something they were not hired for and cannot develop into?

Is the organizational design (Block 3) creating a bottleneck where this person's work sits waiting for something from another department, with no visibility and no resolution?

Are the organizational processes (Block 4) signaling to this person — through exclusion from key conversations, through ignored input, through work that disappears without acknowledgment — that they are not part of the in-group?

Is the management (Block 5) providing this person with the context, feedback, and clarity they need to orient their effort correctly?

If you diagnose Block 1 and the real problem is Block 3, you have applied the wrong intervention. The person's disengagement deepens. Your confidence in your own diagnosis leads you to escalate — harsher consequences, performance management, eventual termination — while the structural problem that caused the disengagement goes untreated and produces the same outcome in the next person who takes that role.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: The disengaged-employee diagnostic — show the same presenting symptom (dropped output, no response to feedback) branching into five different root-cause hypotheses, one per block, with a sample diagnostic question for each] -->

This is why the Hawthorne studies remain important even after the reanalysis has scaled back Mayo's claims. The researchers were testing Block 2 — physical conditions. The workers were responding to Block 4 — the social processes of being observed, cared about, seen. The study did not prove what Mayo said it proved. But it demonstrated that the wrong block was being examined, and that lesson is still worth learning.

## What We Know, What We Are Still Figuring Out

A final word on calibration, because it matters how you hold the ideas in this chapter.

Management is the youngest of the social sciences, and it has inherited tools from psychology, sociology, and economics — all of which are themselves still contested in important ways. Some things in management are well-established. Some are partially supported. Some are folk wisdom that has achieved the status of fact through repetition rather than evidence.

Maslow's hierarchy of needs is everywhere in management education. The idea that people move through needs in a fixed sequence — safety, then belonging, then esteem, then self-actualization — is neat and memorable and widely cited. The research does not support a strict hierarchy. People's motivations are more fluid and context-dependent than the pyramid implies. Someone will sacrifice material security for a sense of belonging. Someone will take a pay cut to do work that challenges them. The pyramid is a useful first approximation, not a law.

The reanalysis of the Hawthorne studies themselves is instructive here. Mayo's original interpretation treated the observation effect as enormous and decisive. Later researchers reexamined the raw data and found that other factors — who was selected to participate, what supervision changes happened mid-study, wage incentives that were introduced — explained more of the variance. The observation effect is real. It is not as large as Mayo claimed, and it does not operate the same way in every context.

This is not a reason to be cynical about management research. It is a reason to read it carefully and hold it proportionally. This course will tell you when evidence is strong, when it is partial, and when you are looking at an appealing idea that has not been adequately tested. The difference matters. A manager who cannot distinguish between those categories will apply folk wisdom with the confidence of evidence, and be surprised when it does not work.

## The Core Idea

Management is not a procedure you apply to people. It is the practice of creating conditions — social, structural, and motivational — in which people are able to do their best work and choose to.

The Hawthorne researchers thought they were testing light bulbs. They discovered that the social system around the work was more powerful than the physical conditions of the work. That discovery took decades to absorb fully. It is still being absorbed.

Every other idea in this book is downstream of it.
