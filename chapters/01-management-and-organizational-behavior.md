# Management and Organizational Behavior

## Three title options

1. **The Hawthorne Discovery: Why Being Watched Changes Everything**
2. **What Managers Actually Do — and Why the Behavioral Sciences Matter**
3. **The Machinery of Work: From Productivity to Purpose**

---

## TL;DR

Management is the process of coordinating human effort toward shared goals. The field emerged from industrial efficiency engineering, but a crucial discovery — that *being observed changes behavior* — shifted it toward understanding people. This chapter traces that shift, defines what managers do at different organizational levels, and introduces the behavioral sciences framework that governs how organizations actually function.

---

## Chapter Opening: Western Electric, 1924

It is November 1924. The Hawthorne Works of Western Electric manufactures telephone relays — intricate assemblies of metal and coils that demand precision and focus. The plant manager has a straightforward question: does lighting affect productivity?

The experimental setup is simple. Researchers isolate a group of women assembly workers in a test room. They record baseline output. Then they increase the light intensity. Output rises.

They decrease the light. Output rises again.

They return the light to its original level. Output increases *once more*.

By every measure of physical intervention, this fails. Dimmer light should reduce output. Returning to baseline should show a decline compared to the improvement phase. Yet at each step, workers produce more relays, more consistently, with fewer errors.

Elton Mayo, the Harvard researcher overseeing the studies, eventually saw what was happening. The variable was not the light. The variable was *attention*. When researchers paid attention to workers — when workers knew someone was taking notes, watching their work, caring about the outcome — those workers behaved differently. They worked more carefully. They helped each other. The effect vanished when observation stopped.

This discovery, now called the **Hawthorne effect**, is small and immense. Small: it seems obvious in retrospect. Of course people behave differently when they are watched. Immense: it revealed that the conditions under which people work are inseparable from the work itself. A factory is not a machine with people attached. It is a social system. The machinery of management is not about levers and pulleys. It is about creating the conditions under which people choose to do their best work.

The Hawthorne studies marked a turning point in how organizations think about themselves. Before, management was mostly about efficiency — finding the right procedure, the right incentive, the right assembly line configuration. After, it had to account for what we now call organizational behavior: the fact that people in groups behave in ways that cannot be predicted from efficiency engineering alone.

## Section 1: What Is Work, and What Does It Provide?

Open with a question: what does work *do* for a person?

Not "what is work." That is easy. Work is activity that produces something of value for others, done in exchange for compensation. But the question beneath is harder: *why does work matter beyond the paycheck?*

In the 1970s, the Chicago writer Studs Terkel conducted interviews with workers across occupations. He asked them what work meant. The answers were not about money. A steelworker said: "I used to be able to point to a house I built, logs I stacked. He built it and he was proud of it." A sanitation truck driver said: "I feel I earn my money. My wife's happy; that's the big thing." A receptionist, rejected at a party for her job title, said she felt worthless not because of what she earned but because of what people thought she was.

Work serves at least five functions that money alone does not capture.

**Economic function.** Work provides the income necessary to support a life — shelter, food, family. For many, this alone is sufficient reason to work. But once basic needs are met, income becomes one variable among several. A person earning $45,000 in a job with meaning will often report greater satisfaction than someone earning $65,000 in meaningless work. The paycheck matters, but it is not the whole story.

**Social function.** The workplace is where most people spend their waking hours. It is where friendships form, where conversation happens, where a person becomes known by others. Remove work and you remove, for many people, the primary site of social connection. The pandemic isolation of 2020 made this visceral — people locked at home worked harder and longer than they had in an office, yet reported greater loneliness. The work itself was identical. The social context was not.

**Status function.** In most societies, your occupation announces your status. A doctor is regarded differently than a janitor, not because of merit but because of cultural convention. This status can feel trivial. It is not. Status shapes how people treat you, how you see yourself, what opportunities are available to you. A job that provides status — or, more painfully, a job that denies status you believe you deserve — shapes daily experience and self-regard in ways that affect everything else.

**Identity and self-esteem function.** Freud noted that "work has a greater effect than any other technique of living in binding the individual more closely to reality." Work answers the question: *what can I do?* A person who works successfully — who accomplishes tasks, solves problems, creates value — accumulates evidence that they are capable. They matter. They have something to offer. Without this, a person risks what Emile Durkheim called **alienation**: a sense of isolation from activity to which they should belong, a feeling that their effort is meaningless. Alienation is not primarily about money. It is about meaninglessness.

**Actualization function.** Beyond proving oneself capable, work can be the arena where a person becomes more fully themselves. It can offer challenge. Growth. The opportunity to discover what one is capable of. This is the rarest function — many jobs do not offer it — but when present, it transforms work from obligation to calling.

When these functions are absent, particularly the identity and actualization functions, something measurable shifts. Productivity declines, but not because incentives are wrong. Turnover increases. Absenteeism rises. People show up physically but are absent mentally. This is the core problem management must solve: how to create conditions where work produces not just outputs but meaning, for the people doing it and the organizations receiving the value.

## Section 2: The Challenges Managers Face Now

Move to the present. The world into which a manager steps is not the world of 1924, or even 1980. Four major challenges reshape what management means.

**First: global competition and distributed work.**

Until the 1980s, many American firms had little meaningful international competition. Japanese automakers changed that. South Korean electronics manufacturers followed. Now a manager in Chicago must compete with manufacturers in Vietnam and engineers in India. The competition is not just about cost — it is about speed, quality, innovation, and the ability to coordinate work across time zones and cultures.

This has broken the old model of the local factory, the local labor market, the promotion path within a single company. A software engineer in San Francisco might spend her career at three companies in different states, each time negotiating relocation or remote work. A manufacturer might assemble products across five countries, with design in California, components from Taiwan, assembly in Mexico, and customer service in the Philippines. A manager must now coordinate not just individuals but distributed teams with different labor markets, different regulatory requirements, and different expectations about work.

The result: the skills that made someone an effective manager in 1990 are insufficient now. They must think globally. They must lead across distance and difference. They must move fast in uncertainty. They must understand how to build trust without daily face-to-face contact.

**Second: technology moves faster than people.**

The first microprocessor, the Intel 4004, launched in 1971. Today an iPhone contains 15 billion transistors. The rate of change has not slowed; it has accelerated. Cloud computing, machine learning, automation, remote collaboration tools — these are not coming. They are here, and the next wave is already arriving.

For management, this creates a particular problem. You hire someone for a role. Two years later, the technology that role depends on is obsolete. The skills are no longer valuable. The tools have changed. The workflows have changed. A manager who does not invest in continuous learning for themselves and their teams will lead people into irrelevance.

Technology also changes what "management" means. Thirty years ago, a manager's job was largely about oversight — watching people do work and correcting errors. Now much work is asynchronous. A manager cannot watch. They must instead build clarity into systems: clear goals, clear decision authority, clear feedback loops. They must trust. This is harder than oversight. Oversight is *certain* — you see what people are doing. Trust requires acceptance of uncertainty.

**Third: the workforce is no longer demographically homogeneous.**

Historically, the American workforce was dominated by white men. They filled most managerial roles. Women and minorities faced barriers. That is changing, and the change is accelerating. In the next decade, 85% of new workforce entrants will be women or people of color. Additionally, the workforce spans four or five generations with different expectations, values, and relationship to work.

This diversity is an organizational asset — diverse teams solve problems better. It is also a management challenge. A manager trained in one culture cannot assume their assumptions are shared. The motivation tactics that worked in 2000 may not work in 2026. A manager must now learn to lead across difference, to understand how personality, values, and cultural background shape what people want from work and how they want to be led. A manager must ask, not assume.

**Fourth: the definition of "results" has expanded.**

Fifty years ago, a manager could define success narrowly: did we hit the revenue target? Did we reduce costs? Now, employees, customers, communities, and regulators ask additional questions. Did you treat your workers well? Did you damage the environment? Did you behave ethically? Did you contribute to the communities you work in?

This is not sentimentality. It is business calculus. The companies that treat employees well have lower turnover and higher productivity — which saves money. The companies that behave ethically avoid scandals, lawsuits, and regulatory trouble — which saves money and reputation. The companies that think about the environment are ahead of regulations that will come — which gives them competitive advantage. A manager who ignores these questions now will face them later, under pressure, in a reactive position.

Additionally, many talented people will not work for companies whose values they find repugnant. A brilliant engineer will leave if the company is building weapons she disagrees with. A talented administrator will leave if she experiences discrimination. The competition for talent means the competition includes values and purpose.

This expanded definition of results makes management harder. It is easier to optimize a single metric than three. But it also makes management more interesting. A manager now gets to grapple with questions that matter beyond the quarterly report.

## Section 3: What Management Actually Is — and What Managers Do

Let me define management precisely, because the term gets used loosely.

**Management is the process of planning, organizing, directing, and controlling the activities of people in combination with other resources to accomplish organizational objectives.**

Unpack this. Planning: setting direction, anticipating obstacles, allocating resources. Organizing: structuring work so efforts coordinate rather than conflict. Directing: helping people understand what they are supposed to do and why it matters. Controlling: measuring results and adjusting when reality diverges from plan.

This is the basic architecture. Different managers spend different proportions of time on each, depending on their level and function.

Consider **levels in the organizational hierarchy**. An executive — a CEO, a VP of engineering — spends most time planning. They set strategy. They anticipate market shifts. They allocate capital. They spend less time on the details of daily work. A first-line manager — a team lead, a department supervisor — spends most time directing. They help people understand the day-to-day work. They remove obstacles. They troubleshoot. They spend little time on organizational strategy.

A middle manager sits between. They translate strategy into executable work. They coach the people doing the work and report on results to executives. They spend moderate time on each of the four functions.

**Now consider the skills required at each level.** Three types matter: technical, human, and conceptual.

**Technical skills** are expertise in the work itself. An accountant manager must know accounting. A software engineering manager must understand software. These skills are the mechanics of the job. The better you understand the work, the better you can set realistic goals and recognize good work when you see it.

But technical skill alone does not make a manager. You can be the best engineer in the world and be terrible at management. This is a common tragedy: the person promoted because of technical excellence, then failing because they lack the other skills.

**Human skills** are the ability to work with people. To listen. To motivate. To navigate conflict. To give feedback that lands rather than bruises. To build trust. These matter at all levels. A CEO who cannot inspire people lacks something essential. A first-line manager who cannot talk to their team about difficult feedback will struggle.

**Conceptual skills** are the ability to see the organization as a system. How does engineering connect to sales? How does culture affect hiring? How do changes in one part ripple through others? These skills matter more as you go up the hierarchy. An executive must think like this or they will optimize one piece and break another. A first-line manager can succeed while thinking mostly about their own team.

This creates a common career trap. A person becomes skilled at one role, earns promotion, and fails in the next. The engineer becomes a manager and focuses only on technical excellence, ignoring the human side. They leave burnout in their wake. Or: the manager who is great with people but struggles with the bigger strategic picture gets promoted to executive and flounders. Organizations should attend to this. Promotion should account for the *type* of skill required at the next level, not just excellence in the current level.

Additionally, different functions require different emphasis. A manufacturing manager focuses heavily on operations — scheduling, quality, efficiency. A marketing manager focuses on customer relationships and external communication. A finance manager focuses on measurement and control. A human resources manager focuses on hiring, development, and culture. The balance of activities shifts with the function.

The most important implication: a manager cannot copy the playbook of another manager. You must understand your level (executive, middle, first-line), your function (operations, marketing, finance, HR, etc.), and which skills need development. A person at your level in your function probably faces similar challenges.

## Section 4: Schools of Thought — Why Management Thinking Shifted

The story so far: work matters to people beyond the paycheck. Managers face complex challenges. Management requires multiple skills at different levels.

Now: where did these ideas come from? How did management thinking evolve?

Management thinking has shifted through several schools. Understanding them helps you understand which ideas are grounded in evidence and which are folk wisdom. This matters because it shapes which ideas you should trust.

**The first school: scientific management (1890s–1920s).**

Frederick Taylor was an engineer. He believed management should be scientific: measure everything, find the most efficient way to do things, implement it, reward people for compliance.

This had real impact. Assembly lines, production scheduling, work standards — these innovations did increase productivity. A factory using scientific management could produce more, cheaper, faster.

But there was a human cost. Work became repetitive, mechanical, stripped of autonomy. A worker became an extension of the procedure. Taylor believed this was fine — that most workers wanted only pay and security. Pay them enough and they will comply with any procedure.

The Hawthorne studies challenged this. They showed that workers responded to attention and social conditions in ways Taylor's framework did not predict. Scientific management was not wrong — it did increase efficiency. But it was incomplete. It ignored the social and psychological dimensions of work.

**The second school: human relations (1930s–1950s).**

Elton Mayo, the Harvard researcher from the Hawthorne studies, proposed that management should focus on worker satisfaction. Happy workers, he reasoned, would be more productive. Organizations should care about morale, social ties, group cohesion.

This was a genuine advance. It moved management attention away from pure efficiency toward the human experience. It recognized that motivation was more complex than pay.

But the human relations school had a blind spot. It assumed that workers' interests aligned with management's. Make workers happy, the theory went, and they will work harder for the organization. In fact, worker interests and management interests often diverge. A worker might want higher wages. Management wants lower costs. The human relations school did not adequately grapple with this conflict.

There is also a methodological question that has become clearer over decades: **the original Hawthorne effect was smaller than Mayo claimed.** Reanalyses of the data suggest that other factors — the selection of which workers participated, changes in supervision practices, wage incentives introduced during the study — played larger roles than Mayo attributed to the effect of being observed. The effect is real. Observation does change behavior. But the original study may have made it seem much larger.

**The third school: systems thinking (1960s–1970s).**

Management could not be purely about efficiency or purely about satisfaction. Managers had to think about organizations as systems. How does the payment system affect motivation? How do hiring practices affect culture? How does organizational structure enable or prevent communication?

Systems thinking recognized that you cannot change one part without affecting others. Pull on one lever and three other things move. This is obvious once stated, but it represented a major shift. Management became about understanding interconnections.

The limitation: systems thinking is elegant in theory and messy in practice. You can draw diagrams showing how everything connects. But actually managing the connections requires making decisions with incomplete information. Perfect system thinking does not exist. You always simplify.

**The fourth school: contingency theory (1970s–1990s).**

Maybe, researchers began to think, there is no single best way to manage. Maybe the best approach depends on context. The right leadership style for a startup differs from the right style for a stable manufacturing firm. The right organizational structure for a software company differs from that of a hospital.

Contingency theory says: the effectiveness of management practices depends on context. This is much closer to truth than the earlier schools, each of which claimed universality. But it is less useful as guidance. It says "it depends" rather than "do this." Useful management guidance says "here is what works in your situation."

**The fifth school: evidence-based management (2000s–present).**

In recent decades, a new school has emerged: management should be grounded in what the research actually shows, not in intuition or tradition.

This is harder than it sounds. The research on management is messy. Findings often do not replicate. Context matters in ways that are hard to account for. And organizations often do not behave based on evidence. They follow trends. They copy competitors. They trust gurus over data.

Evidence-based management says: measure what matters. Test your assumptions. Revise when reality deviates from expectation. This is the frame that governs the rest of this book. It means asking: what does the evidence actually show?

## Section 5: Why the Behavioral Sciences Matter — A Model of Organizational Behavior

We have now arrived at the central insight: **understanding organizations requires the behavioral sciences.**

Not just psychology. Not just sociology. Not just economics. All of them, applied to the specific problem of how people behave in organizations. The reason is simple: organizations are fundamentally human systems. The technical questions — which tools to use, which processes to follow — are secondary to the human questions: who are these people, what do they want, how do they influence each other?

A manager faces a concrete problem: an employee is disengaged. Output has dropped. They are not responding to feedback. What should the manager do?

A psychologist might ask: what is the person's psychological state? Are they depressed? Do they lack clarity about what success looks like? Is the goal too easy or too hard? Does the work align with their values and strengths? This is the individual perspective.

A sociologist might ask: what is the group saying about this person? Is the team signaling that this person is not one of us? Is there conflict between this person and others? Are norms in the group pushing toward disengagement? Has the person been excluded from informal networks? This is the social perspective.

An economist might ask: what are the incentives facing this person? Are they being paid fairly relative to others doing similar work? Does the effort they are being asked to put in justify the compensation they receive? Are there better opportunities elsewhere that they might be considering? This is the incentive perspective.

All three are partially right. The disengagement might stem from individual psychology, group dynamics, incentive misalignment, organizational structure, leadership behavior, or some combination. A manager who brings only one perspective will likely miss the cause. A manager who understands all three — and how they interact — has a much better chance of diagnosing the real problem.

This is where the model comes in. I offer a simple framework. Imagine organizations as composed of five building blocks working together. Understanding these blocks, and how they interact, explains most of what happens in organizations. When something goes wrong, you can locate the problem by examining each block.

**Block 1: Individuals and groups.** Organizations are made of people with different personalities, values, abilities, and motivations. Some are conscientious; some are not. Some are risk-seeking; some are risk-averse. Some want autonomy; some want clarity and structure. Some are driven by mastery; some by affiliation; some by power. A manager must understand these differences and adjust accordingly. Different people respond to different conditions. One size does not fit all.

**Block 2: Tasks and technology.** How does the work actually get done? Is it routine, or does it require judgment? Is it independent, or does it require coordination with others? Is it done by humans, machines, or some combination? Does it require specialized knowledge or can anyone learn it? The nature of the task shapes what kind of person succeeds in it and what kind of management it requires. A task that requires judgment cannot be managed the same way as a task that is completely routine.

**Block 3: Organization design.** How do you structure the organization so that effort coordinates rather than conflicts? Should decisions be centralized or distributed? Should people be organized by function (all engineers together) or by product (all people needed to deliver a product together)? Are there clear reporting lines or is it a matrix? Design shapes who talks to whom and what information flows where. Design also shapes what behaviors are possible. You cannot have a collaborative culture if the structure pits teams against each other for resources.

**Block 4: Organizational processes.** Within the structure, how do decisions actually get made? How does power flow? How do people communicate? How are conflicts resolved? Are meetings where ideas are exchanged honestly, or are they performances? These processes determine whether the organization is collaborative or political, whether good ideas surface or get suppressed, whether people feel trust or suspicion. The same organizational structure can produce very different outcomes depending on the processes that operate within it.

**Block 5: Management.** All of this is held together by the quality of management. Management is the lens through which these other blocks connect and the force that adjusts them when they are not working. A good manager understands all five blocks and knows which ones need attention. A poor manager focuses on one and ignores the others, wondering why their efforts do not produce results.

A complete understanding of an organizational problem requires looking at all five blocks. A person is disengaged. Look at their individual psychology — are they capable? Do they understand what success looks like? Do they have the skills this job requires? Look at the group — is the team healthy? Are they isolating this person? Is the team itself disengaged? Look at the task — is it giving them opportunity to use their strengths? Is it too hard or too easy? Is the work meaningful or tedious? Look at the design — does the organizational structure create obstacles for this person? Are there bottlenecks? Is decision authority unclear? Look at the processes — is leadership giving this person visibility and feedback? Can they speak up safely? Is their work recognized? Look at management — is the manager trying to understand the root cause, or just applying generic motivation tactics? Is the manager present or distant?

The most common management mistake is staying in one block. A manager assumes the person is lazy (Block 1) and applies harsher consequences, not recognizing that the task (Block 2) is impossible given the resources, or the design (Block 3) has created a bottleneck where this person's work sits waiting for something from another department, or the leadership (Block 5) is not providing necessary information, or the group (Block 4) has decided this person is not part of the in-group and is excluding them from key conversations. When you address only Block 1, you miss the real problem in one of the other blocks. The person's disengagement then deepens.

This is why the Hawthorne studies were so important. They revealed that observation (a feature of Block 4: the processes around this work) changed behavior more than any change to the physical conditions (Block 2). The researchers were not trying to test whether observation matters. They were trying to test whether lighting affects productivity. But they stumbled on something more fundamental: the social and psychological context of work shapes behavior as much as the work itself.

## Section 6: Calibration — What We Know, What We Don't, What We Think We Do

Before you apply any of this, a caution. Management is the youngest of the social sciences. It has inherited ideas from psychology, sociology, and economics — all of which are relatively young and uncertain. Some management ideas are well-grounded in evidence. Some are partially supported. Some are folk wisdom.

This book will distinguish. When the evidence is clear, the book says so. When it is partial, the book is honest. When it is largely absent, the book names the gap.

One example: the "Hawthorne effect" as originally described. Mayo concluded that the effect of observation on behavior was large and decisive. Later analyses showed the effect was smaller, and other factors in the study were more influential. This matters because the Hawthorne effect has become a kind of folk wisdom in management — the idea that just paying attention to people transforms productivity. The truth is more modest: attention does affect behavior, but the effect is not as large or universal as the original study suggested.

Another example: personality typing. Myers-Briggs is ubiquitous in organizations. Managers give it to teams as a bonding exercise. People take it and believe it describes something fundamental about them. The research shows that MBTI has essentially no predictive validity for job performance. The Big Five trait model — measuring people on continuous scales rather than discrete types — does predict performance modestly. But MBTI does not. Organizations use MBTI because it is popular and intuitive, not because it predicts anything important about how someone will perform.

A third example: Maslow's hierarchy of needs. Many managers learned that people progress through needs — first safety, then belonging, then esteem, then self-actualization. The research does not support a strict hierarchy. People's motivations are more fluid and context-dependent. Someone might sacrifice safety for belonging. Someone might give up material security to pursue self-actualization. The hierarchy is too neat for how people actually work.

These are not failures of the field. They are features. The field evolves as evidence accumulates. A manager who knows the difference between "this is grounded in solid evidence" and "this is intuitively appealing but we are not sure" will make better decisions than a manager who treats all management ideas as equally valid.

## Section 7: Exercises

### Warm-up

**1. Define work using the definition in this chapter. Give an example of something that meets that definition and something that does not.**

Example: Teaching someone to read is work (it produces value for others). Rearranging your apartment is not (it produces value for you, not for others), though rearranging it for someone else who pays you would be.

**2. Of the five functions of work — economic, social, status, identity, actualization — which ones does your current or ideal job provide? Which ones does it not?**

(Personal reflection. No right answer, but the exercise builds self-knowledge about what you actually value in work, not what you think you should value.)

**3. Identify a manager you know or have worked for. Using the three skill types (technical, human, conceptual), how would you rate them? What accounts for their effectiveness or ineffectiveness?**

(Grounded observation. Often reveals that managers are strong in one type and weak in others.)

### Application

**4. The Hawthorne effect suggests that observation changes behavior. Describe a situation in your own work or study where you believe this has happened — where being observed or knowing someone was tracking your work changed how you approached it.**

**5. Using the five blocks (individuals, tasks, design, processes, management), diagnose a workplace problem you have observed. Is there a block that seems particularly misaligned? How might adjusting that block improve the situation?**

Example: A team is producing poor-quality work despite high salaries and clear goals. Block 1 (individuals) seems fine — they are capable. Block 2 (tasks) seems fine — the work is clear. Block 3 (design) might be the issue — maybe they are restructured so often that ownership is unclear. Block 4 (processes) might be the issue — maybe the leadership does not provide feedback. Block 5 (management) might be the issue — maybe the manager is absent. Which one?

### Synthesis

**6. Contrast the scientific-management view of motivation with the human-relations view. Which assumptions in each do you find yourself believing? Where do you think they might mislead?**

(Integrative thinking. Helps you see which management school's assumptions you carry and whether they serve you.)

**7. This chapter introduces a model of organizational behavior with five blocks. Which block do you think is most commonly neglected by managers? Why?**

(Meta-reflection on management blindness. Most managers neglect at least one block systematically. Knowing which one is the start of improving.)

## What Would Change My Mind

I would reconsider this chapter's framing if longitudinal evidence showed that organizational design or management quality had measurably smaller effects on individual behavior and outcomes than the five-block model suggests. Currently, the literature supports that these factors matter substantially. If that changed, the model would need revision.

## Still Puzzling

I remain uncertain about the causal direction of many relationships. Does good management create organizational culture, or do people predisposed to good culture gravitate to well-managed organizations? Does job satisfaction cause performance, or does performing well cause satisfaction? The correlations are real. The causal stories are still being written.
