# Chapter 8 — Performance Appraisal and Rewards

*How you measure performance shapes what people actually do — and what they stop doing.*

---

Here are two performance reviews happening at the same company on the same afternoon.

Janet has been at the firm eight months. She came in on three separate occasions to cover for team members who called in sick — real coverage, the kind that disrupts your own schedule. She solved a client problem that had been open for weeks. Her work is clean and careful.

Her manager slides the evaluation form across the desk. Nearly all "satisfactory."

"Ken," she says, "I covered for Mike twice and Sarah once. I fixed the Henderson account."

Ken nods. "I know. That's exactly what I expect of my team."

The meeting ends in twenty-four minutes.

Across the hall, Ron is in a two-hour review with his manager Mary. Two weeks ago, Mary emailed him asking him to write down his goals and accomplishments and note any major changes in the job. She's been keeping a log — real examples, not impressions — of his work over the past six months. She reviewed the goals they set together. When Ron leaves, he feels more motivated than when he walked in, and he knows exactly what constitutes good performance.

Same company. Same performance appraisal system on the books. One meeting measures effort carefully and ties it to what happens next. The other measures impressions and calls them data.

## What you measure shapes what people do

The word "appraisal" carries five distinct purposes, and they don't all want the same thing. Organizations appraise performance to give feedback, to enable self-development, to allocate rewards, to make personnel decisions, and to identify training needs. These purposes are in tension.

Feedback that helps people improve requires honesty and specificity. Reward allocation requires consistency and fairness. Legal protection requires documentation. An appraisal system trying to serve all five purposes simultaneously often serves none of them well. Janet's twenty-four-minute meeting was trying to do all five and accomplished approximately zero of them.

The deeper problem is that measurement shapes behavior before anyone notices. If you measure customer satisfaction scores but not the quality of individual conversations, your people will manage satisfaction scores. If you measure sales volume but not client retention, you'll get short-cycle sells and burned relationships. If you measure how hard people work but have no operational definition of "hard," you'll measure whether managers like their employees. The system you build doesn't just assess performance — it creates the performance you get.

This is the first thing Ken got wrong. He didn't choose a measurement system. He inherited a form, filled it out in the time available, and called it a system. What it actually was: an impression translated into a number, dressed up in the vocabulary of assessment.

## The four methods and what each one actually measures

Pick up the form Ken used — a graphic rating scale — and you'll see seven evaluation dimensions: quantity of work, quality of work, knowledge of job, personal qualities, cooperation, dependability, initiative. Each has a five-point scale: Outstanding, Good, Satisfactory, Fair, Unsatisfactory.

The method is popular for two reasons: speed and familiarity. You can rate someone in fifteen minutes. The organization gets a number for each dimension. Numbers feel objective.

Here's what's actually happening. Ken looks at "quantity of work" and thinks, "She's doing fine." He doesn't have a baseline. He doesn't remember specific numbers. He has an impression, and he's translating that impression into a number. When he gets to "personal qualities," the scale offers no definition. He's voting on whether he likes her as a person. When he gets to "cooperation," he's thinking about how easy she is to work with, which has nothing to do with how well she cooperates on actual tasks.

And when he marks "satisfactory," he means "doing what the job requires." He thinks of "satisfactory" as the bar, not as mediocre. By his definition, Janet is at the bar. He's not wrong about the word. He's operating from a definition nobody gave her. She leaves thinking "satisfactory" means "you're nothing special." He intended it to mean "you're exactly where you should be."

This is not dishonesty. This is a bad instrument.

<!-- → [TABLE: graphic rating scale example — sample dimensions and five-point anchors, with a column showing the vague language problems at each anchor level; student should see where interpretation diverges] -->

The alternative is a Behaviorally Anchored Rating Scale — BARS. The goal is to replace vague words with observable behavior.

Instead of "cooperation," you describe what cooperation looks like at each level of the scale:

- "Never raises concerns about another's behavior to that person first." (Unsatisfactory)
- "Discusses concerns with the relevant person in private before involving management." (Satisfactory)
- "Proactively flags team conflicts and works to resolve them before they affect the team's work." (Outstanding)

Ken can't misinterpret "discusses concerns with the relevant person in private." That's either happening or it isn't. The rating becomes an observation, not a judgment. Two different managers watching the same employee will give the same rating, because they're watching for the same specific behavior rather than forming different impressions of the same vague word.

BARS costs time to build. You have to interview experienced workers and managers and ask, "What does excellent performance look like in this job? What does poor performance look like?" Then you write those behaviors down, test them, refine them. The form isn't one-size-fits-all — you need a separate BARS for each distinct job. But when it's done well, raters give similar ratings for similar performance. Janet would get the same score from Ken and from Mary, because they're both observing the same behaviors, not interpreting the same vague word.

<!-- → [TABLE: BARS example for one dimension — "handling client concerns"; rows show five behavioral anchors from outstanding to unsatisfactory, written as specific observable actions; contrast with the graphic scale's vague language] -->

A third method is Management by Objectives — MBO. At the start of a review period, you and your manager agree on what success looks like: "Close eight new accounts. Reduce customer complaints from twelve per month to eight. Complete the product redesign by June 30." At the end of the period, you measure. Did you close eight new accounts? Yes or no.

MBO connects effort directly to results. It removes "what does my manager think of me" and replaces it with "did I hit the target." For jobs with clear quantitative output — sales, production, project delivery — this is powerful. The limitation is that MBO measures what's easy to measure. Customer satisfaction matters, but if you can't measure it in your system, it doesn't count. Team innovation matters, but the MBO forces you to specify it in advance in concrete terms: "Implement three new operational processes." You'll be rated on whether you hit three. You might implement one brilliant process and two mediocre ones and be rated 2 for 3. The system incentivizes you to hit the number, not to hit the spirit.

MBO also assumes goals are realistic and within the person's control. If your goal is "sell $500,000 in a year" but your market contracts by 20 percent, you weren't the problem. Your performance rating will say otherwise.

360-degree feedback takes input from supervisors, peers, direct reports, and sometimes customers. The reasoning is that no single rater sees the whole picture. Your manager sees you in meetings. Your peers see you on project teams. Your reports see you in delegation. Your customers see you in delivery. All four views together produce a more honest picture than any one person's impression.

The catch is organizational maturity. 360 feedback works when the culture is high-trust and people are accustomed to honest critique. In organizations where feedback is political and people fear retaliation, 360 feedback becomes a weapon. Someone gives you low ratings and you'll never know who. You'll suspect your rival on the other team. You'll become political too. Implementing 360 without building the trust first makes the organization more political, not less.

<!-- → [TABLE: comparison of four appraisal methods — rows: graphic rating scale, BARS, MBO, 360 feedback; columns: accuracy, cost to implement, cost to run, best suited for, failure mode] -->

The honest conclusion: there's no best appraisal method. There's the right method for your situation. If your jobs are stable and observable behavior is the core of performance, a well-built BARS works best. If your jobs have variable outputs and you can set clear goals in advance, MBO is flexible enough to adapt. If someone reports to multiple managers or stakeholders, 360 captures what one boss misses. The method determines what people pay attention to. Janet's manager chose a method that pays attention to impressions. She heard the message.

## Why the method isn't enough: the rater's brain

Say Ken switches to a BARS system. He's now watching for specific behaviors. He should give Janet a fair appraisal.

Except Ken's brain is running patterns beneath his conscious awareness, and those patterns distort what he sees.

The halo effect: if Ken thinks Janet is smart — and he does, because she solved the Henderson account — he'll tend to rate her as hardworking. He'll assume the quality of her work is high because one piece of work was impressive. He'll rate her communication as strong because she was clear in one conversation. One good thing casts a halo over everything else. Janet gets a high rating across dimensions she wasn't actually tested on.

Recency bias: Ken's memory prioritizes what happened recently. Two weeks ago, Janet had a rough week — she made a mistake on a proposal and had to redo it. Two months ago, she finished three projects flawlessly. But the review is now, and the recent rough patch looms larger. Her annual rating reflects September, not the full year. Workers know this. Ask how they behave the month before their review. Many put on a show.

Central tendency error: Ken looks at the form and clusters everyone in the middle. "Outstanding" feels like favoritism. "Unsatisfactory" feels like an admission that his team is failing. So everyone is "satisfactory" or "good." The scale collapses to a narrow band. Janet, who did something concrete and valuable, gets the same rating as the person who got by.

Similarity bias: Ken likes people who remind him of himself. He's spontaneous; she's cautious. He doesn't give her credit for seeing risks he misses, because caution doesn't read to him as a skill — it reads as hesitation. The appraisal becomes partly a test of whether the person is the manager's kind of person.

Confirmatory bias: once Ken forms an opinion of Janet, his memory starts confirming it. She made a mistake on the Henderson account in her first week. Ken remembers that. When she later fixed it perfectly, he remembers it as "she cleaned up her own mess" — less impressive than "she solved a problem nobody else could." His memory is unconsciously editing the story to fit the initial judgment.

These are not character flaws. They are how human brains work. Your brain is constantly predicting based on pattern recognition. It shortcuts to save energy. These shortcuts usually work. They sometimes fail completely.

Research on rater training is instructive. You can train Ken to recognize the halo effect. You can show him the recency error. He will nod, understanding. And then, the next time he evaluates someone, his brain will run the same patterns. The training helps; it doesn't eliminate the bias.

What does work is structure. BARS works not because it's perfect but because it forces Ken to find a specific example and match it to the scale, not to form an overall impression and translate it to a number. If he rates Janet as "does not proactively flag team conflicts," he has to think: can I remember a time she didn't flag a conflict? He might actually recall a time she did. The form is forcing him to find evidence. Evidence is harder to distort than impressions.

The more specific the appraisal system, the less room for unconscious bias to hide.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: five rater biases — each bias named, described in one sentence, and paired with the structural countermeasure; designed as a reference card a manager could keep; halo → anchored rubric per criterion; recency → continuous incident log; central tendency → calibration session; similarity → structured criteria; confirmatory → designate a contrarian on each review] -->

The single most powerful thing a manager can do — more than any system change — is to keep a log. Every few weeks, note something a person did. Date it. Write it down. Be specific: not "worked hard" but "debugged the authentication system without prompting." Not "had a bad day" but "missed the product meeting without notice." When you do the appraisal, you're reading from a record, not from memory. The record doesn't lie the way memory does.

## Three myths that cost organizations money

**Myth: More frequent reviews are always better.** The instinct makes sense. Annual reviews accumulate too much time, too much recency bias, too much weight on a single conversation. So organizations move to quarterly, then monthly, then continuous feedback. But the benefit of frequency depends on the job. For complex, creative work — strategy, design, research — annual deep reviews with quality feedback beat frequent shallow ones. You can't assess strategic judgment in thirty minutes. You can catch up on urgent issues, but that's not appraisal. For routine, well-defined work — data entry, basic customer service — frequent feedback works better because the feedback can be specific and immediate. Confusing these two jobs and applying one rhythm to both is a category error. Janet's job has both routine and judgment components. She needs some feedback on specific deliverables and one real conversation a year about where she's headed. She's getting neither.

**Myth: 360-degree feedback automatically improves performance.** It can. In high-trust organizations where people are accustomed to giving and receiving honest critique, 360 feedback lowers self-ratings ("my peers think I'm more arrogant than I thought"), and when managers take it seriously, they change. But in organizations where feedback is political, 360 becomes a weapon. Someone gives you low ratings anonymously. You suspect your rival. You become strategic about who you rate highly and why. You get a more political organization, not a more honest one. The tool requires the culture to be ready for it. Most organizations implement the tool and skip building the culture.

**Myth: Pay-for-performance works for all jobs.** This is the costliest myth. Edward Deci and Richard Ryan's research established the mechanism: extrinsic rewards can crowd out intrinsic motivation. In a classic experiment, college students were asked to solve puzzles. Half were told they'd be paid. When the payment condition ended and the task became voluntary, the paid group solved significantly fewer puzzles than the group who was never paid. The payment had converted an intrinsically motivated activity into an extrinsically motivated one. When the money stopped, motivation stopped.

The effect is conditional. Payment undermines intrinsic motivation when the task is something the person already enjoys, when the payment is tied tightly to performance metrics, and when the payment feels controlling. Payment supports motivation when the task is unpleasant or dull, when the payment is informational ("you're good at this, we're compensating that"), and when it doesn't feel coercive.

For an engineer who loves solving hard problems, tying their pay tightly to tickets closed converts their intrinsic motivation into extrinsic. They optimize for the metric. They close tickets quickly and stop caring whether they're closed well.

The research does not say don't pay people. It says be intentional about what you're tying pay to.

## When performance pay works and when it doesn't

Edward Lawler, who studied compensation across hundreds of organizations, identified four conditions under which performance pay actually drives performance. Without all four, the system fails or creates unexpected behavior.

**Trust between management and employees must be high.** If employees believe management is manipulating ratings to avoid paying bonuses, they stop believing pay is tied to performance. Janet watches Ken make ratings and suspects the system is rigged. Why work harder if the raise won't come anyway? The trust is built through transparency — showing people how the formula works, paying when promised, never moving the goalposts after the fact.

**Individual performance must be measurable.** If the appraisal system can't reliably distinguish good performance from poor, you can't tie pay to it. An engineer's code quality can be measured. A manager's strategic judgment is harder. If your measurement is unreliable, your pay system is rewarding something else — impressions, politics, recent behavior — dressed up as performance.

**The pay difference between high and poor performers must be substantial.** Research suggests 15–20 percent minimum to create a meaningful incentive. A high performer who gets a 3 percent raise and a poor performer who gets a 1 percent raise sees almost no difference. Neither is motivated. Organizations frequently compress pay ranges for political reasons and then wonder why performance pay isn't working.

**Few negative consequences for high performance must be perceived.** If people see high performers ostracized or burned out, they won't try hard. If they see individual performance rewarded at the expense of cooperation, they'll hoard information. GE's stack ranking — forced distribution, bottom 10 percent out annually — created an internally competitive culture where teams stopped sharing information because if your colleague looked good, you looked worse. Microsoft ran the same experiment and got the same result: engineers stopped helping each other. Both companies eventually abandoned the system. Adobe in 2012 replaced annual ratings with "check-ins" — regular conversations about what the person was working on, where they wanted to go, what support they needed. No final rating. No forced curve. Voluntary turnover decreased. Development conversations happened. Performance improved.

These cases show the same pattern: systems that optimize for individual performance at the expense of cooperation eventually damage the organization they were designed to improve.

## The reward side: what people actually value

Rewards come in two flavors. Extrinsic rewards are things the organization gives: money, bonuses, promotions, recognition. Intrinsic rewards come from doing the work itself: feeling accomplished, solving a hard problem, getting better at your craft.

The relationship between them is not additive. When extrinsic rewards are well-designed and meaningful, they can confirm and amplify intrinsic motivation — a raise that says "your organization values your work" is also feedback. But when extrinsic rewards are tight and narrow, they crowd out intrinsic motivation. The person stops asking "am I doing good work?" and starts asking "am I hitting the number?"

Organizations now often talk about "total rewards" — everything of value you get from working there. Salary is one part. Health insurance, retirement contributions, vacation, flexible work, development, recognition, learning opportunities are others. The research shows people value different rewards differently depending on where they are in life. A person starting out prioritizes income. A parent with young children prioritizes flexibility and health insurance. A person mid-career prioritizes learning and growth. An older worker prioritizes security and pension. A one-size-fits-all reward package misses most people most of the time.

The fundamental trade-off is this: you get what you measure, reward, and attend to. If you measure individual output and reward it generously, you'll get individual output and less cooperation. If you measure team outcomes and reward them, you'll get teamwork and less individual star performance. If you measure nothing carefully and reward based on politics, you'll get politics. The system delivers exactly what it measures. No more, no less.

## The two meetings, reconsidered

Return to Janet and Ron.

Janet's manager used a graphic rating scale, had no definition of "satisfactory," translated his impression into a number, and gave her twenty-four minutes. The organization sent a message: we measure performance based on what we think of you, not what you do. Janet's motivation — which was intrinsic, she wanted to do well — gets replaced by a political question: how do I make my manager like me?

Ron's manager set clear goals at the start of the year. She kept a log. She structured the conversation around what was accomplished, what wasn't, and why. Ron leaves knowing what good performance looks like, how his manager evaluates it, and what comes next. The intrinsic motivation — I want to do good work — is supported, not undermined.

The difference is not the company, the pay, or the market. It is the system.

When Ken later gets frustrated — "I don't understand why Janet doesn't try harder" — he's looking in the wrong place. The system he chose is telling her that hard work doesn't change the outcome. When Mary sees Ron develop, she's reaping what her system creates. Clear measurement, clear feedback, clear consequences. The gap between the two meetings is not a gap in managers. It's a gap in machinery.

The performance appraisal system your organization chooses doesn't just measure performance. It shapes what people believe your organization actually values. Janet believes her organization values impressions and politics. Ron believes his values results and development. Both beliefs are self-fulfilling. Janet spends energy managing her manager. Ron spends energy getting results. The appraisal created both outcomes. It will keep creating them until the system changes.

## What would change my mind

If evidence shows that graphic rating scales with good rater training produce the same behavioral outcomes as BARS in high-trust organizations, I'd revise the claim that structure reduces bias. The research would have to show that Ken's gut feeling, with training, is as accurate as behavioral observation.
