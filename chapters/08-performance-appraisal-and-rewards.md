# Performance Appraisal and Rewards

## TL;DR

How you measure performance shapes what people actually do. Whether that measurement is fair, timely, and tied to meaningful rewards determines whether the system motivates honest effort or game-playing. The machinery matters more than the label.

---

## Three possible titles

1. **Measuring What Matters: How Organizations Appraise Performance and Why It's So Hard to Get Right**
2. **The Feedback Loop: What Annual Reviews Actually Tell Us About Performance, Bias, and Motivation**
3. **Performance, Rewards, and the Gap Between What We Intend and What We Measure**

## Cold open

It's late September at a Fortune 500 financial services company. Janet sits across from her manager Ken in his office overlooking the trading floor. She's been here eight months. She came in on three separate occasions to cover for team members who called in sick — real coverage, the kind that disrupts your own schedule. She solved a client problem that had been open for weeks. Her work is clean and careful.

Ken slides the evaluation form across his desk. Janet's ratings are nearly all "satisfactory."

"Ken," she says, "I covered for Mike twice and for Sarah once. I fixed the Henderson account."

Ken nods. "I know. That's exactly what I expect of my team. You're right on track for someone in their first year. But you can't expect to jump to the top right away."

The meeting ends in twenty-four minutes. Janet leaves confused. Her manager said he saw the work. He said it was what he expected. Then why didn't the rating reflect it?

Two weeks before his review, across the hall, Ron gets an email from his manager Mary. She's asking him to write down his goals and accomplishments for the last six months and note any major changes in the job. Mary is also digging into the file where she keeps a log — real examples, not impressions — of Ron's performance over the past half year. She's reviewing the goals they set together and thinking about what should come next. They'll meet for two hours. Ron will go in prepared. Mary will too. When Ron leaves, he feels more motivated than when he walked in, and he knows exactly what constitutes good performance.

Same company. Same performance appraisal system on the books. One system measures effort carefully and ties it to what happens next. The other measures impressions and calls them data.

This chapter is about why that gap exists, what it costs, and how organizations close it.

## Concept 1 — The machinery of measurement: what we count and why it matters

The word "appraisal" carries a weight that deserves unpacking. Organizations appraise performance for five reasons, and they matter differently. Understanding which one is driving your system is where clarity starts.

**Feedback to employees.** Without knowing how well they are doing, people cannot improve. This is not optional. A worker who doesn't know her work contains errors will repeat them. A manager who doesn't know his communication is unclear will keep being unclear. Feedback is the basic condition for learning.

**Self-development.** Performance appraisals also show individuals their strengths and weaknesses as others see them. Janet could look at her appraisal and think, "My manager doesn't see my extra effort." Ron can look at his and understand exactly where his skills need work. The appraisal becomes a mirror — sometimes a fair mirror, sometimes a distorted one.

**Reward allocation.** This is where things get complicated. If your pay, your bonus, your chance at promotion depends on your appraisal, the appraisal is no longer just feedback. It becomes a high-stakes judgment. The stakes change what people do.

**Personnel decisions.** Appraisals inform hiring, promotions, transfers, and terminations. A manager using an appraisal to justify firing an employee had better have documented the performance problem carefully and over time. Courts look at these files. So do lawyers. Appraisals are not just HR documents — they are evidence.

**Training and development.** An appraisal that identifies specific gaps in skill drives training decisions. If the company sees that three people struggle with data analysis, that's a signal to run a training program. If the appraisal is vague — "communication needs work" — nobody learns anything actionable.

Here's the problem: these five purposes are in tension. Feedback that helps people improve requires honesty and detail. Reward allocation requires fairness and consistency. Legal protection requires documentation. They need different kinds of data. An appraisal system trying to do all five often does none of them well.

### The measurement choice: what method mirrors reality?

Pick up a graphic rating scale — the form Janet's manager used — and you'll see seven evaluation dimensions: quantity of work, quality of work, knowledge of job, personal qualities, cooperation, dependability, initiative. Each one has a five-point scale: Outstanding, Good, Satisfactory, Fair, Unsatisfactory. You mark a box for each dimension and you're done.

This method is popular for two reasons: speed and familiarity. You can rate someone in fifteen minutes. The organization gets a number for each dimension. Numbers feel objective.

But here's what's actually happening. Ken looks at quantity and thinks, "She's doing fine, a decent amount of work." He doesn't have a baseline. He doesn't remember specific numbers. He has an impression, and he's translating that impression into a number. When he gets to "personal qualities," he's not measuring anything — the scale offers no definition. He's voting on whether he likes her as a person. When he gets to "cooperation," he's thinking about how easy she is to work with, which has nothing to do with how well she cooperates on actual tasks.

The raters aren't being dishonest. They're using the only tool they have. The problem is the tool assumes there's a scale from "outstanding" to "unsatisfactory" that every rater will interpret the same way. There isn't.

Janet's manager thinks "satisfactory" means "doing what the job requires" — and by that standard, Janet is satisfactory. She does what he asked. He doesn't think of "satisfactory" as "mediocre with room to improve." He thinks of it as "meeting the bar." To him, she is at the bar. He's not wrong, exactly. He's operating from a different definition of the word than the company intended.

This is the central tendency error in action. Different raters define the scale differently. The form collapses actual performance into a number, and loses the specificity that would let a worker know what comes next.

Enter the Behaviorally Anchored Rating Scale — BARS. The goal is to replace vague words with actual observable behavior.

Instead of "cooperation," you describe what cooperation looks like:
- "Never raises concerns about another's behavior to that person first."
- "Discusses concerns with the relevant person in private before involving management."
- "Proactively flags team conflicts and works to resolve them."

Instead of one scale called "cooperation," you have one scale called "handles interpersonal issues," and each point on the scale is a specific action you either see or you don't. Ken can't misinterpret "discusses concerns with the relevant person in private." That's either happening or it isn't. The rating becomes an observation, not a judgment.

BARS costs time to build. You have to interview experienced workers and managers and ask them, "What does great performance look like in this job?" Then you write those behaviors down. Then you test them. The form is not one-size-fits-all — you need a separate BARS for each distinct job. But when it's done well, raters give similar ratings for the same performance. Janet would get the same score from Ken and from Mary, because they're seeing the same behaviors, not interpreting the same vague word.

A third choice is Management by Objectives — MBO. Here, the appraisal isn't about general qualities like "cooperation." It's about specific, measurable goals. At the start of a review period, you and your manager agree on what success looks like: "Close eight new accounts. Reduce customer complaints from twelve per month to eight. Complete the product redesign by June 30." At the end of the period, you measure: Did you close eight new accounts? Yes or no. Did you reduce complaints? Let's count.

MBO works beautifully for jobs with clear quantitative output — sales, production, project delivery. It connects effort directly to results. It removes the "what does my manager think of me" anxiety and replaces it with "did I hit the target?"

But MBO has its own costs. It emphasizes what's easy to measure. Customer satisfaction matters, but if you can't measure it in your system, it doesn't count. Team innovation matters, but the goal is written in concrete terms: "Implement 3 new operational processes." The system incentivizes you to hit the number, not to hit the spirit. If you implement one brilliant process and one mediocre one, you're still 2 for 3 on the goal, and you'll be rated accordingly.

MBO also assumes goals are realistic and within the person's control. If your goal is "sell $500,000 in a year" but the economy contracts and your market shrinks by 20 percent, you weren't the problem. Your performance rating will be bad anyway. Some firms use what's called "calibration" — adjusting goals for market conditions. It requires honesty and constant attention. Most firms don't bother.

A fourth option is 360-degree feedback — getting input from supervisors, peers, direct reports, and sometimes customers. The idea is elegant: no single rater can see the whole picture. Your manager sees you in meetings and in reviews. Your peers see you on project teams. Your reports see you in delegation and feedback. Your customers see you in delivery. All four views together give a more honest picture than any single person's impression.

The catch is speed and cost. Getting meaningful feedback from eight people takes time. Each person has to know the appraisal is anonymous (otherwise they won't be honest). The appraisal has to be designed so that overlapping feedback creates a coherent picture, not a collection of unrelated scores. And the person being reviewed has to hear contradictory feedback — "My boss thinks I'm strategic, my peers think I'm in the weeds, my reports think I'm hands-off" — and figure out what it means. This works if the organization has the time and culture to do it right. In many firms, 360 feedback is a checkbox. It generates data nobody uses.

### Trade-offs baked in

Each method trades off accuracy against cost, speed against detail, consistency against adaptability.

Graphic rating scales are cheap and fast. They're also unreliable — Ken's "satisfactory" is not the same as Mary's "satisfactory." A worker can get a "satisfactory" rating while doing excellent work, and she'll never know why there's a ceiling on her career.

BARS is expensive to build — the work of interviewing and refinement takes weeks for each job. But it's reliable. Two raters using the same BARS will rate the same performance similarly. And it's specific — Janet gets feedback on actual behaviors, not on vagueness.

MBO is reliable when goals are objective and achievable. It's unreliable when goals are aspirational or when markets change. It's also incomplete — it measures what's quantifiable, not necessarily what matters most.

360 feedback is comprehensive but slow. It requires organizational maturity to use well. Many companies adopt it because it looks sophisticated, then ignore the results because the results are messy.

One more choice is worth naming: assessment centers. These are used less for ongoing performance evaluation and more for identifying high-potential managers. Over two days, trained observers watch candidates through structured exercises — in-basket exercises, role-plays, case analyses, group problems. The observers are looking for patterns: this person makes decisions quickly but doesn't listen; this person builds consensus but is slow to act; this person is clear under pressure. The technique is expensive (you need trained raters and dedicated facilities), and the evidence for how well it predicts future success is mixed. But it's comprehensive — you see how someone behaves under stress, not just how they perform routine tasks.

The honest reading is this: there's no best appraisal method. There's the right method for your situation. If you need to distinguish high performers from others quickly, and your jobs are stable and standardized, a well-designed BARS for each job works well. If your jobs have variable outputs and you can't predict what's important until partway through the year, MBO is flexible enough to adapt. If you're in a matrix organization where someone reports to multiple managers, 360 feedback captures what one boss misses. If you're trying to develop a pipeline of future leaders, assessment centers give you the depth of observation that matters.

The jobs determine the method. The method determines what people pay attention to. Janet's manager chose the method (graphic rating scales) and didn't think about what message it sends. The message is: I'm evaluating you on my impressions, not on specific behaviors or results. Janet hears that and stops trying to distinguish herself.

## Concept 2 — The rater's brain: why appraisals fail even when the method is right

Let's say Ken switches to a BARS system. Now he's watching for specific behaviors. He should give Janet a fair appraisal.

Except that Ken's brain is running patterns beneath his conscious awareness, and those patterns distort what he sees.

**The halo effect.** If Ken thinks Janet is smart — and he does, because she solved that Henderson account problem — he'll tend to rate her as hardworking too. He'll assume the quality of her work is high because one piece of work was impressive. He'll rate her communication as strong because she was clear in one conversation. One good thing about a person casts a halo over everything else. Janet gets a high rating across the board, even dimensions she wasn't tested on.

This cuts the other direction too. If Ken thinks someone is lazy, every task they produce is lazily done, in Ken's mind, whether it is or not. One time the person missed a deadline, and now everything is viewed through the lens of "this person is unreliable."

**Recency error.** Ken's memory is working with recent events. Two weeks ago, Janet had a rough week. She made a mistake on a proposal and had to redo it. Two months ago, she finished three projects flawlessly. But Ken is doing the appraisal now, and the recent mistake looms larger in his mind than the earlier successes. Her annual rating reflects what happened in September, not what happened across the whole year. Workers know this. Ask them how they behave the month before their review. Many "put on a show."

**Central tendency error.** Ken looks at the form. Most of his team gets "satisfactory." It feels wrong to mark anyone as "outstanding" — that seems like favoritism. It also feels wrong to mark anyone as "unsatisfactory" — he's not doing his job if his people are that bad. So he clusters everyone in the middle. The scale collapses to a narrow band. Janet, who did something concrete and valuable, gets the same "satisfactory" rating as the person who gets by. The scale tells managers nothing.

**Similarity bias.** Ken likes people who remind him of himself. A manager who thinks of himself as strategic, high-energy, and quick to move will rate people with those same traits higher. A manager who values stability and process will rate the careful, methodical worker higher. The appraisal becomes less about actual performance and more about whether the person is the manager's kind of person. Janet didn't have much in common with Ken at first. She's more cautious. He's more spontaneous. He doesn't give her credit for seeing risks he misses.

**Confirmatory bias.** Once Ken forms an opinion of Janet, his brain looks for evidence that supports it. She made a mistake on the Henderson account in her first week. Ken remembers that. When she later fixed it perfectly, he remembers it as "she cleaned up her own mess" — less impressive than "she solved a problem nobody else could." His memory is unconsciously editing the story to fit the initial judgment.

These aren't character flaws. They're how human brains work. Your brain is constantly predicting based on pattern recognition. It shortcuts to save energy. If Janet is smart, your brain thinks, then probably her work is good. If she made a mistake early on, your brain files her under "careful but not sharp." These shortcuts usually work. They sometimes fail spectacularly.

The research on rater training is instructive. You can train Ken to recognize the halo effect. You can show him the recency error. You can tell him to watch for similarity bias. He will nod, understanding. And then, the next time he evaluates someone, his brain will run the same patterns. The training helps, but it doesn't eliminate the bias.

What does work, research shows, is structure. The BARS method works not because it's perfect but because it forces Ken to observe specific behaviors and match them to the scale, not to observe a person and form an overall impression. The Behavioral Observation Scale works similarly — Ken rates specific behaviors at specific frequencies. MBO works because Ken is measuring outcomes against preset targets, not comparing performance to his gut feeling.

Structure doesn't eliminate bias. It channels it. Ken still sees what he's inclined to see. But the form forces him to find an example. If he rates Janet as "does not proactively flag team conflicts," he has to think, "Can I recall her not flagging a conflict?" He might actually remember a time she did. If he rates her as "satisfactory," he's at least constrained by the definition of satisfactory that's on the form.

The more specific the appraisal system, the less room for unconscious bias to hide.

### Three myths that cost organizations money

**Myth 1: More frequent reviews are always better.** The response has been to shift from annual reviews to quarterly check-ins to monthly one-on-ones. Continuous feedback sounds enlightened. But research shows the benefit of frequency depends on the task. For complex, creative work where the person's judgment matters — strategy, design, research — annual deep reviews with quality feedback beat frequent shallow ones. You can't give meaningful feedback on strategic judgment in thirty minutes. You can catch up on urgent issues, but that's not the same as appraisal. For routine, well-defined work — data entry, basic customer service — frequent feedback works better because the feedback can be specific and immediate. The work itself is simple enough to discuss quickly. Confusing these two jobs and applying one appraisal rhythm to both tells you what the organization thinks the job is worth. Janet's job is partly judgment, partly execution. She needs both — some feedback on specific deliverables, and one conversation a year about where she's headed.

**Myth 2: 360-degree feedback automatically improves performance.** The research is mixed. 360 feedback can lower self-rated performance ("My peers think I'm arrogant; I didn't know that"), and when managers take it seriously, they do change behavior. But 360 feedback is also expensive to run well, it's slow, and the results can be demoralizing if the culture isn't ready for it. In high-trust organizations where people are used to giving and receiving criticism, 360 works. In organizations where feedback is political and people fear retaliation, 360 feedback becomes a weapon. Someone gives you low ratings and you'll never know who. You'll suspect your rival on the other team. You'll become political too. Implementing 360 without building the trust first makes the organization more political, not less.

**Myth 3: Pay-for-performance works for all jobs.** This is the biggest and costliest myth. The research from Deci, Ryan, and other motivation researchers is clear: pay-for-performance works well for narrow, measurable tasks where the goal is to maximize output quantity. It works poorly for complex tasks requiring creativity, judgment, or cooperation. Why? Because when pay is tied closely to performance, the person focuses on the metrics. If your pay is tied to the number of sales calls made, you make calls and don't spend time on relationship-building. If your pay is tied to customer satisfaction, you're incentivized to focus on the metrics you're measured on and deprioritize everything else. The motivation shifts from "do the job well" to "hit the target." For a salesperson, this can align. For a researcher, a designer, or a manager, the target and the actual goal diverge.

GE used stack ranking — forced distribution — under Jack Welch. The company rated the bottom 10 percent of employees as unsatisfactory, the top 20 percent as excellent, and everyone else in the middle. The method was reliable: raters couldn't cluster everyone in the middle. It was also brutal. If you worked at GE and were in a strong team, you were in trouble. Your manager had to rate one person out of ten as unsatisfactory, whether they were performing well or not. The result was a culture where people competed fiercely, didn't share information, and left ambitious colleagues off projects so they wouldn't look good. GE later abandoned the system. Microsoft did the same in 2013 — they'd used forced ranking, and it created the same culture of hoarding and competition. Some organizations still do it. The evidence is clear: forced ranking creates internal competition that damages cooperation.

Adobe, in 2012, completely eliminated annual reviews and moved to "check-ins" — regular conversations between manager and employee, without a final rating and no forced curve. Employees were no longer worried that to get a raise, a colleague had to be rated low. The conversation became about what the person was working on, where they wanted to go, what support they needed. Adobe didn't stop measuring performance — it just stopped turning measurement into a forced rating. The result, according to the company, was that people stayed longer, development conversations happened, and performance actually improved. You can argue about causation, but the result is consistent with the research on motivation.

## Concept 3 — Motivation and reward: intrinsic, extrinsic, and the gap between them

Now the organization has measurement figured out. Janet gets a fair appraisal on a BARS scale. She sees specific, observable behaviors that constitute good performance. She knows what comes next. And then the organization decides how to reward her.

Rewards come in two flavors. Extrinsic rewards are things the organization gives: money, bonuses, promotions, recognition. Intrinsic rewards are things that come from doing the work: feeling accomplished, solving a hard problem, getting better at your craft.

The relationship between the two is more complicated than it appears. When extrinsic rewards are well-designed and meaningful, they can amplify intrinsic motivation. A raise tells you that your organization values your work. Recognition from your manager tells you that your effort matters. But when extrinsic rewards are tight and narrow — "you get a 2 percent raise if you hit your targets, 0 percent if you don't" — they can crowd out intrinsic motivation.

Here's why. Imagine a child who loves helping her neighbor wash the car. One day you offer to pay her. Now she helps because you're paying her, not because she enjoys the activity. The intrinsic motivation — I like helping — has been replaced by extrinsic motivation — I'm getting paid. When you stop paying, she stops helping. You've converted a willing volunteer into someone who works for money.

This happens in organizations too. An engineer who loved solving problems can, if you shift her entirely to performance pay, become focused on counting solved problems and moving to the next ticket. The intrinsic satisfaction of solving one problem *well* gets crowded out by the extrinsic incentive to solve many problems *quickly*.

This doesn't mean reward people poorly. It means be intentional about what you're incentivizing.

### How money actually works as a motivator

Edward Lawler, one of the most careful researchers on compensation, identified the conditions under which pay actually drives performance:

**Trust levels between management and employees must be high.** If employees suspect that management is manipulating the performance ratings to avoid paying bonuses, the system collapses. Janet sees her manager manipulate ratings to keep the distribution on the bell curve. She stops believing that pay is tied to performance. Why work harder? It won't change your rating.

**Individual performance must be measurable.** If the appraisal system can't distinguish good performance from poor, pay-for-performance can't work. You can't tie pay to performance you haven't measured accurately.

**Pay rewards to high performers must be substantially higher than to poor performers.** A high performer who gets a 3 percent raise and a poor performer who gets a 1 percent raise sees almost no difference. Neither is motivated. Research suggests the difference needs to be at least 15–20 percent to create a meaningful incentive.

**Few, if any, negative consequences for good performance must be perceived.** If people see someone get ostracized for outperforming their peers, they won't try hard. If they see cooperation rewarded less than individual performance, they'll hoard information. The system has to feel like winning doesn't come at a cost to the group.

When these four conditions are met, performance pay works. When they're not, it either fails or creates unexpected behaviors. Janet watches her manager make the ratings and doesn't trust the system, so she stops trying. A salesperson hits the target by doing whatever the metric measures and ignores what's not measured. A team stops cooperating because the bonus is individual.

### Total rewards and the lived experience

Organizations now often talk about "total rewards" — everything of value you get from working there. Salary is one part. Health insurance, retirement contributions, vacation, flexible work, development, recognition, learning opportunities are others.

The research shows people value different rewards differently depending on where they are in life. A young person starting out might prioritize income. A parent with kids might prioritize health insurance and flexibility. A person mid-career might prioritize learning and growth. An older worker might prioritize security and pension. A one-size-fits-all reward package misses the mark.

Some companies try to solve this with flexible benefits programs — you get a budget and you choose. Want more vacation? Take it. Want more money? You get it. Want to redirect your health insurance to a different plan? That's available. The approach works when the organization is sophisticated enough to manage the complexity and when employees understand the tradeoffs. The cost is administrative. The benefit is that people get something closer to what matters to them.

None of this changes the fundamental trade-off: you get what you measure, reward, and attend to. If you measure individual output and reward it well, you'll get individual output and less cooperation. If you measure team outcomes and reward them well, you'll get teamwork and less individual star power. If you measure nothing carefully and reward based on politics and favoritism, you'll get politics and favoritism.

The question isn't which reward system is best. It's which system matches your strategy. If you want individual innovation, pay for it individually. If you want collaborative problem-solving, tie rewards to team outcomes. If you want people to stay and build institutional knowledge, give security and long-term incentives. If you want short-term extraction and don't care about retention, you can squeeze harder. The system will deliver exactly what it measures.

## Integration — the machinery working

Return to the two review meetings. Janet and Ron are experiencing different systems.

Janet's manager uses a graphic rating scale. There's no definition of "satisfactory." There's no behavioral standard. Ken translates his impression into a number, and Janet leaves with no idea what comes next. The organization is sending a message: we measure performance based on what we think of you, not on what you do. Janet's motivation, which was intrinsic (she wanted to do well), gets replaced by political motivation (how can I make my manager like me?).

Ron's manager uses a combination. Mary sets clear goals at the start of the year. She logs specific examples of Ron's work over time. She reads them before the review. She structures the conversation around what was accomplished, what wasn't, and why. Ron leaves knowing exactly what good performance looks like, how his manager evaluates it, and what comes next. The intrinsic motivation — I want to do good work — is supported, not undermined.

The difference isn't in the company, the pay, or the market. It's in the system. One system measures behavior carefully. The other measures impression. One system ties feedback to specific observations. The other ties feedback to gut feeling.

When Janet's manager gets frustrated later — "I don't understand why she doesn't try harder" — he's looking at the wrong place. The system he chose is telling her that hard work doesn't matter. When Ron's manager sees development, she's reaping what the system creates. Clear measurement, clear feedback, clear consequences. It works.

The performance appraisal system your organization chooses doesn't just measure performance. It shapes what people believe about what the organization values. Janet believes her organization values impressions and politics. Ron believes his organization values results and development. Both beliefs are self-fulfilling. Janet will spend energy managing her manager. Ron will spend energy getting results. The appraisal system created both.

## Chapter summary

Performance appraisal is the most important and most mishandled aspect of management. Organizations use appraisals to provide feedback, enable development, allocate rewards, make personnel decisions, and identify training needs. These five purposes are in tension. A system that solves for speed doesn't solve for accuracy. A system that solves for legal protection doesn't solve for development.

The appraisal method shapes what gets measured. Graphic rating scales are fast but unreliable. BARS is reliable but expensive. MBO is flexible but incomplete. 360 feedback is comprehensive but slow. Assessment centers are deep but resource-intensive. The right method depends on your job, your strategy, and your organization.

Raters' brains distort what they see through halo effects, recency bias, central tendency, similarity bias, and confirmatory bias. Training helps but doesn't eliminate these patterns. Structure — specific behavioral definitions, observable examples, measured outcomes — channels bias by forcing raters to find evidence, not impressions.

Rewards come in two kinds: extrinsic (money, promotion, recognition) and intrinsic (accomplishment, growth, mastery). When extrinsic rewards are well-designed and tied tightly to performance, they amplify motivation. When they're tight and narrow, they crowd out intrinsic motivation. The conditions for performance pay to work are high trust, measurable performance, substantial differentials between good and poor, and few negative consequences for high performance. Without these, performance pay creates unexpected behaviors — game-playing, politics, deprioritization of what's not measured.

The appraisal system you choose sends a signal about what your organization actually values. It shapes whether people invest in real performance or in managing their manager. The system matters more than the label.

---

## What would change my mind

If evidence shows that graphic rating scales with good rater training produce the same behavioral outcomes as BARS in high-trust organizations, I'd revise the claim that structure reduces bias. The research would have to show that Ken's gut feeling, with training, is as accurate as behavioral observation.

---

## Still puzzling

I don't fully understand why organizations continue using methods known to be less effective (graphic rating scales, forced ranking) when better methods are available (BARS, MBO, check-ins). The inertia is real. The cost explanation doesn't fully account for it. There's something about how organizations approach change — risk aversion, or fear of looking incompetent if you admit the old system was wrong — that I haven't quite captured.

# Companions

## Companion 1: Historical cases — GE stack ranking, Adobe check-ins, Microsoft abandonment

### Jack Welch and GE's forced distribution (1980s–2000)

Jack Welch made stack ranking famous. The system was simple: divide employees into three groups. Top 20 percent got excellent ratings, best bonuses, best advancement. Middle 70 percent got satisfactory ratings and steady raises. Bottom 10 percent got unsatisfactory ratings, no bonuses, and pressure to improve or leave. The percentage was rigid — every year, one person out of ten had to be rated as the worst performer, whether they were performing poorly or not.

The theory was appealing: it prevented central tendency error. It forced discrimination between good and bad performers. It kept a culture of high performance by constantly removing bottom performers.

The result, in practice, was that GE developed an intensely competitive culture. Teams stopped sharing information because if your colleague looked good, you looked bad by comparison. People spent energy on impression management and office politics. High performers left for companies where success didn't require making someone else fail. The company attributed much of its success during this era to the management system. It's harder to know what it would have achieved with a different system.

By the 2000s, even GE was seeing the costs. In some units, the system had calcified into tribal politics. Managers were hitting the 10 percent number by pushing out good performers from teams, not by identifying bad ones. GE eventually abandoned the practice. Welch, in later years, acknowledged it was damaging. But the cultural impact lasted for years.

### Adobe's abandonment of ratings and embrace of check-ins (2012)

In 2012, Adobe's chief people officer Donna Morris made a decision that was countercultural at the time. The company eliminated the annual performance review system and all its rankings and ratings. In its place, they introduced "check-ins" — regular, informal conversations between a manager and employee about what they're working on, what they need, and where they want to go.

The change was driven by data. Adobe was seeing that talented people were leaving the company. Exit interviews showed that the performance management system was a driver. People felt that being evaluated on an annual basis, with a final rating, created anxiety. The forced distribution meant that some good performers were rated lower so others could be rated higher. People were gaming the system. Development conversations were impossible because the conversation was about justifying a rating, not about growth.

Check-ins removed the judgment. There was no final rating. There was no bell curve. The conversation could be about what the person was learning, what they were struggling with, what support they needed. The result, according to Adobe, was that voluntary turnover decreased, engagement increased, and performance improved. The company didn't stop measuring performance — it just stopped formalizing it into a rating.

The check-in model has since been adopted by other companies. It's not free — it requires more managerial time, and it requires that managers actually show up to do the work. But the model shows that you can have performance management without a rating system.

### Microsoft's 2013 abandonment of stack ranking

Microsoft used stack ranking for years. Every team had to rate employees on a curve. The top performers were exceptional, the next group was good, the next was acceptable, and the bottom had to be unsatisfactory. The system was designed to maintain excellence and remove weak performers.

The result was that Microsoft developed an internal culture of competition. Teams didn't share code because sharing made it harder to look exceptional individually. People didn't help each other because helping someone else succeed made you less exceptional by comparison. The talented people who thrived in competitive environments did well. The people who wanted to build things with others left.

By 2013, new leadership recognized the cost. The company eliminated stack ranking and moved to a goal-based system where performance is measured against what you said you'd accomplish, not against your colleagues. The change took years to embed in the culture, but the result was that information sharing increased, cross-team collaboration increased, and the rate of internal mobility (people moving between teams) increased.

These three cases show the same pattern: systems that incentivize individual performance over collaboration, that measure against a curve rather than against absolute standards, eventually damage the organization they were meant to improve.

## Companion 2: The bias research — halo, recency, central tendency, confirmatory bias

### The halo effect in practice

The halo effect is one of the most researched and most resilient biases in performance appraisal. The core finding: if you think someone is strong in one dimension, you tend to rate them as strong across all dimensions. If you think someone is weak in one dimension, you rate them as weak across the board.

Research by Landy and Farr (1980) showed raters are poor at keeping dimensions separate. If a salesperson has excellent relationships with customers, the rater tends to rate that person as having good judgment, good communication, good initiative — even if the evidence doesn't support those ratings. One strong point becomes a halo that covers everything else.

Why does this happen? Because the brain is making predictions based on limited information. If you're smart, probably you're competent across domains. If you're creative, probably you're good at problem-solving. These inferences are usually right. Sometimes they're wildly wrong. An engineer can be excellent at technical work and terrible at mentoring. A manager can be strategic and poor at execution. The halo effect causes the rater to miss these gaps.

What reduces it? Behavioral specificity. If the appraisal asks, "Does this person mentor effectively?" instead of relying on a general impression of whether they're a good leader, the rater has to find evidence. They might think this person is a great leader, but when asked for specific mentoring examples, they come up empty. The cognitive demand to find evidence breaks the halo's spell.

### Recency bias and the problem of memory

When you're doing an annual review, your memory is working with what happened recently. The review is in September. What happened in February is hard to recall. What happened in August is vivid.

This is neurologically real. Your brain prioritizes recent information. It's evolutionarily adaptive — the most recent threat is the most relevant. In performance appraisal, it causes appraisals to overweight recent months and underweight earlier months.

Research by Ilgen and Feldman (1983) showed that managers doing annual reviews could barely recall specific incidents from the first six months of the evaluation period. Their ratings were dominated by incidents from the last two months. A person could have a strong six months and a rough two months, and the annual rating would reflect the recent rough patch.

One solution is the log — exactly what Mary did. If you write down specific examples as they happen, you have a record that doesn't depend on memory. The organization has to build this habit into the manager's routine, but it works.

### Central tendency error and the collapsing scale

Most managers, when evaluating their team, cluster everyone in the middle of the scale. Not "satisfactory" defined narrowly, but a broad middle band that includes most people. This is called central tendency error — the rater fails to use the full scale.

Why? Partly because managers want to be liked and rating someone as "unsatisfactory" feels harsh. Partly because most people are doing an acceptable job — if you hire reasonably well, most people are in the acceptable-to-good range. Partly because "outstanding" feels like favoritism and "unsatisfactory" feels like a failure on the manager's part.

The result is that the scale collapses. Everyone is "satisfactory" or "good." There's no differentiation. Janet, who did something concrete and valuable, gets the same rating as someone who got by. An appraisal system where everyone is in the middle tells you nothing about who the strong performers are.

Forcing a distribution (the stack ranking approach) eliminates central tendency error but creates different problems — it forces poor ratings on good performers. The structural solution is to use specific behavioral anchors (BARS) that make it harder to collapse the scale because you're rating specific behaviors, not forming an overall impression.

### Confirmatory bias and the story we tell

Once a manager forms an opinion of an employee, the manager's memory starts confirming that opinion. If Ken thinks Janet is careful but not creative, he'll remember times she was careful. He'll forget times she was innovative. If Ken thinks another employee is unfocused, he'll remember times that person got sidetracked. He'll discount times that person was intensely focused.

This is confirmatory bias — the tendency to search for, interpret, favor, and recall information in ways that confirm a prior belief. It's not conscious dishonesty. It's how memory works. Your brain is constantly editing the story to be coherent with what you already believe.

Research by Nickerson (1998) showed that once formed, initial impressions are remarkably sticky. Even when given information that contradicts the initial impression, people edit the information to fit the impression. A manager who thinks someone is unfocused will interpret "spent three hours on one problem" as "got stuck on details" rather than "showed persistence." The same behavior gets interpreted differently based on the initial judgment.

What breaks this cycle? Documented evidence. If the appraisal system requires specific examples, and those examples are written down as they happen, the manager is working with record, not memory. The record doesn't lie the way memory does.

## Companion 3: The motivational research — Deci and Ryan, Lawler's conditions

### Deci and Ryan on intrinsic and extrinsic motivation

Edward Deci and Richard Ryan's research, beginning in the 1970s, showed something that challenged conventional management wisdom: extrinsic rewards can undermine intrinsic motivation.

In a classic experiment, college students were asked to solve puzzles. Half were told they'd be paid for solving puzzles correctly. Half were told they could solve puzzles for fun. When paid, students solved puzzles more quickly. But when the payment condition ended and the task became voluntary, the paid group solved significantly fewer puzzles. The payment had converted an intrinsically motivated activity (I enjoy solving puzzles) into an extrinsically motivated one (I solve puzzles for money). When the money stopped, motivation stopped.

The effect is real but conditional. Payment undermines intrinsic motivation when:
- The task is something the person enjoys
- The payment is tied tightly to performance (you get paid only if you hit a target)
- The payment feels controlling (you must solve this puzzle this way to get paid)

Payment supports motivation when:
- The task is unpleasant or dull
- The payment is a bonus or reward, not a condition for participation
- The payment is informational ("You're good at this; we're compensating that") rather than controlling ("Do this or you don't get paid")

The implication for organizations: be careful about what you tie pay to. If you're hiring a creative engineer who loves solving hard problems, and you tie their pay tightly to solving problems quickly, you risk converting their intrinsic motivation into extrinsic. They'll optimize for speed, not quality or creativity.

The research doesn't say "don't pay people well." It says "be intentional about what you're paying for and how you're paying it."

### Lawler's conditions for effective performance pay

Edward Lawler studied compensation across hundreds of organizations and identified the conditions under which performance pay actually works. Without all four, the system either fails or creates unexpected behaviors.

**Condition 1: Trust levels between management and employees must be high.** If employees believe management is manipulating ratings to avoid paying bonuses, they'll stop believing that pay is tied to performance. Why work harder if the raise won't come anyway? The trust can be built through transparency — showing people how the formula works, paying when promised, not moving the goalposts.

**Condition 2: Individual performance must be measurable.** If you can't measure performance accurately, you can't tie pay to it. An engineer's code quality can be measured. A manager's strategic judgment is harder. If the appraisal system can't distinguish good performance from poor, performance pay falls apart.

**Condition 3: Pay rewards to high performers must be substantially higher than to poor performers.** The research suggests 15–20 percent is the minimum to create a meaningful incentive. A high performer who gets a 3 percent raise and a low performer who gets a 1 percent raise sees almost no difference. Neither is motivated.

**Condition 4: Few, if any, negative consequences for good performance must be perceived.** If people see high performers ostracized, burned out, or pushed out, they won't try hard. If they see cooperation rewarded less than individual performance, they'll hoard. The system has to feel like winning doesn't come at a cost.

Lawler's research showed that organizations that meet all four conditions do see performance improvements with performance pay. Organizations that meet some conditions see mixed results. Organizations that meet none see game-playing and turnover. The research is clear: performance pay is not a universal motivator. It depends on the conditions.

## Companion 4: Practical design — how to build an appraisal system that actually works

### Start with job analysis

Before you choose a method, understand the job. What's actually being asked of this person? Is it routine, with clear output? Or is it complex, with judgment required? Is it individual work? Or is it collaborative? Is the output measurable? Or is it qualitative?

A graphic rating scale works fine for a routine job with clear standards. BARS works for a job where observable behavior is the core of performance. MBO works for a job where outcomes are what matter. 360 feedback works for a job where multiple stakeholders see the person's work.

If you build an appraisal system without understanding the job, you'll choose a method that doesn't fit. You'll then force the job to fit the method. Janet's job has both routine and judgment components. A graphic rating scale treats it as routine only. BARS could work if it captured the judgment part. MBO could work if the goals included qualitative ones.

### Define behavior or outcomes, not traits

"Communication skills" is vague. "Explains technical decisions in plain English to non-technical audiences" is observable. "Leadership" is vague. "Develops coaching relationships with direct reports" is observable. "Initiative" is vague. "Proposes and leads process improvements" is observable.

The more specific your definition, the less room for bias. If you're asking raters to evaluate "initiative," different raters will interpret the word differently. If you're asking raters to identify "people who proposed and led process improvements," they can find examples or they can't.

This is true whether you're using BARS, MBO, or behavioral observation scales. The specificity reduces the rater's degrees of freedom. The rater has to find evidence, not form an impression.

### Train raters on the method and the biases

Rater training improves reliability. It doesn't eliminate bias, but it helps. The training should cover:
- How to use the method (what counts as an example? how specific does it have to be?)
- Common rater biases (halo, recency, central tendency, similarity bias)
- How to avoid them (keep a log, find specific examples, use the form's definitions)
- What the appraisal is for (feedback, development, compensation, or all three?)

Training improves compliance and consistency. Most organizations skip it because it takes time. It's worth doing.

### Keep a log, not a memory

The single most powerful thing a manager can do to improve appraisal accuracy is to keep a log. Every few weeks, note something a person did — positive or negative. Date it. Write it down. Be specific. Not "worked hard" but "debugged the authentication system without prompting." Not "had a bad day" but "missed the product meeting without notice."

This log eliminates memory bias. When you do the appraisal, you're reading from a record, not from memory. The record is more accurate, more specific, and less subject to recency bias. It also makes the appraisal conversation different — you're discussing documented examples, not impressions.

### Tie feedback to specific examples

In the appraisal conversation, don't say "you're not a strong communicator." Say "In the August client meeting, you didn't explain the delay in the timeline before presenting the new estimate. The client was confused. Here's what clarity would look like: you'd say 'The timeline slipped because of X. That's now pushed the estimate to Y.' Then present the new number." Specific, observable, actionable. Janet would know what to do differently.

### Match the method to the strategy

If your strategy is to develop a culture of individual excellence, use MBO with individual goals. If your strategy is to develop collaborative problem-solving, use team MBO with shared goals. If your strategy is to develop future leaders, use assessment centers. If your strategy is to develop people where they are, use check-ins with development focus. Don't import a method because another company uses it. Match the method to what you're actually trying to build.

### Decide what the appraisal is for

Decide which of the five purposes — feedback, development, compensation, personnel decisions, training identification — your appraisal system is primarily for. You can try to do all five, but they trade off. If compensation is the primary driver, development conversations become anxious. If legal protection is the primary driver, people feel like they're being built a case against. Be honest about what's driving it. Then build a system that serves that purpose.

The organizations that do appraisal well are usually doing it for development or feedback. The appraisal isn't primarily to decide who gets paid or who gets fired. It's to help people understand what's expected and how they're doing. When that's the purpose, the system can be lighter, faster, and more honest. The compensation and personnel decisions follow from that clarity, not from the appraisal itself.
