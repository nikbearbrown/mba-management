## TL;DR

Learning is a relatively permanent change in behavior through experience and reinforcement. The study of how reinforcement shapes behavior at work — operant conditioning, schedules of reinforcement, behavioral modification — reveals why some reward systems create sustained effort while others produce only compliance. The single most important discovery: intermittent rewards (especially variable-ratio schedules) create stronger, longer-lasting behavior than frequent ones. This is why slot machines are addictive and why paying salespeople on a fixed schedule is less effective than paying them on a variable one.

## The Cold Open: A Sales Floor Running on Commission

It's 9 a.m. on a Tuesday at a mid-sized financial services firm. Twenty-five salespeople arrive at their desks. Some have already made three calls. Others are still settling in with coffee. One person is talking to a prospect. Another is filling out paperwork from yesterday's failed pitch.

On the wall, a leaderboard shows year-to-date sales figures. The top three names are circled in red. Below that, the rest. The compensation structure is simple: base salary of $35,000, plus a commission of 1.5% of any deal closed. The commission varies — a small product nets $150, a large enterprise contract nets $4,000. The timing varies too. Some salespeople close a deal almost every week. Others go a month between closes.

Watch what happens at 2 p.m. when Marcus, a mid-tier performer, closes a deal worth $2,800. He gets up, walks past the leaderboard, and sits back down. No celebration. He's already thinking about the next call. He *expects* deals. The closing is not rare enough to register as special.

Now watch what happens to Jennifer, who has been struggling. She makes her forty-seventh call of the month — she's been counting. It's a small contract, $300. She stands up. She actually stands up at her desk. Another salesperson notices and nods. Jennifer sits down again and makes the next call with visible energy.

The difference is not the money. Jennifer makes less than Marcus. The difference is what B.F. Skinner would have recognized immediately: Jennifer is on a variable-ratio reinforcement schedule. She does not know when the next reward will come. She only knows that if she keeps doing the behavior, a reward will eventually arrive. Marcus is on a fixed-ratio schedule — he knows roughly how many calls it takes him to close a deal. The uncertainty in Jennifer's schedule produces effort that predictability does not.

This chapter is about why this works. It is about the machinery of behavior at work — why some reward systems create sustained effort and others create only surface compliance. It is about three concepts that will follow you through your entire career: how behavior gets learned, how timing of rewards shapes that learning, and how to design systems that actually change what people do. None of it is intuitive. All of it is visible if you know where to look.

## Concept 1: The Law of Effect and the Machinery of Operant Conditioning

### The puzzle: Why do some behaviors repeat?

Begin with a simple question: Why do you do what you do at work?

The obvious answer — because I am paid — is only partially true. Payment is part of it. But consider two scenarios.

**Scenario 1:** You work in customer service. Your supervisor has a habit. Whenever you mention a new problem-solving approach, she pauses, looks at you, and says, "That's interesting. I hadn't thought of that." This happens roughly every two weeks. You notice it happening. Gradually, you start bringing up new ideas more often. You prepare examples before meetings. You think about the job differently when you're not at work.

**Scenario 2:** You work in the same role. Your supervisor pays you a $0.50/hour bonus if you suggest ideas, and it's paid monthly. The money is real. But you do not feel more inclined to speak up. The feedback is too delayed. The amount too small relative to your salary. The behavior does not stick the way it does in Scenario 1.

This difference is the machinery that E.L. Thorndike discovered in the 1890s and B.F. Skinner made visible a generation later. Thorndike stated it simply: **behavior that is followed by satisfaction tends to be repeated; behavior followed by discomfort tends not to be repeated**. He called this the **law of effect**. Skinner called the mechanism **operant conditioning** — the learning that happens when behavior operates on the environment and produces consequences.

The key insight: the *timing and variability of the consequence* matter more than its magnitude.

### The mechanism: Drive, Habit, and Reinforcement

Skinner's model of operant conditioning has three parts.

**Drive** is an internal state of need or desire. You come to work. You have a drive to succeed, to earn money, to be recognized, to solve problems, or some combination. The drive activates your motivation. The stronger the drive, the more receptive you are to learning.

**Habit** is the learned connection between a situation (stimulus) and an action (response). Cue: you see a customer email. Response: you answer it promptly. This S-R bond strengthened because of what happened next. Cue: your supervisor praises your response. The bond gets stronger. Cue: you notice the email on your list. Response: you check it again because the bond is now strong.

**Reinforcement** is anything that strengthens this S-R bond. Praise strengthened it. Recognition strengthens it. A bonus might, though it depends on the timing. Avoiding reprimand strengthens it. The definition is behavioral, not emotional: a reinforcer is whatever causes the behavior to increase. If your supervisor's praise does not increase your tendency to answer emails promptly, the praise is not a reinforcer, no matter how nice it sounds.

Here is the distinction that will matter in your career: **reinforcement is not the same as motivation**. Motivation is internal — your conscious desire to succeed. Reinforcement is external — the consequence of your action. A supervisor can reinforce behavior without knowing why you do it. You might answer emails promptly because you want to solve problems for customers. Your supervisor might assume you are motivated by fear of reprimand. The reinforcer works regardless. The internal story does not matter. The consequence does.

This is why the $0.50/hour bonus fails. It is not a reinforcer *for that behavior*. It does not follow the behavior quickly enough. It is not salient enough relative to what you are already making. The consequence is not tied tightly enough to the action. A true reinforcer would be immediate, specific ("I'm praising this idea because it directly solved the problem"), and contingent ("this praise comes only when you do this thing").

### The trade-off: Speed of learning vs. lasting change

Operant conditioning produces fast initial learning. You try a behavior. A good consequence follows. You are more likely to try it again. If this happens repeatedly, the behavior becomes a habit. But there's a problem: habits formed through immediate, reliable feedback *also extinguish quickly* when the feedback stops.

Here's the trade-off: 

**Continuous reinforcement** (reinforcing every single occurrence of the behavior) produces the *fastest learning*. You do the behavior, reward comes immediately. You learn it quickly. This is good when you need someone to learn something fast — a new software system, a safety procedure, a customer script.

**Intermittent reinforcement** (reinforcing some occurrences but not all) produces *slower initial learning* but *much stronger and longer-lasting behavior*. You do the behavior, sometimes a reward comes, sometimes it doesn't. You cannot predict which time will produce the reward, so you keep trying. When the reinforcement eventually stops (because the supervisor gets busy, the program ends, the budget runs out), you continue the behavior longer because you are used to doing it sometimes without a reward.

This is the hidden discovery that shapes every reward system in business. The behavior that lasts is not the behavior that was always reinforced. It is the behavior that was reinforced unpredictably.

### Worked example: Teaching a new technique to an assembly line

Suppose your plant needs workers to use a new assembly technique that reduces defects by 15%. You have three days to establish the habit.

*Continuous reinforcement approach:* Every time a worker completes a unit using the new technique, you check it, praise them immediately, or give them a small token reward. Day 1: the workers try it because you're watching and the feedback is immediate. Day 2: habit is forming. Day 3: the new technique is now the dominant behavior. Workers are doing it automatically.

*But then:* You assign someone else to do the checking because you are busy with something else. Suddenly, the immediate feedback disappears. Within a week, workers drift back to the old faster technique because they were getting reinforced for *compliance with your watching*, not for the actual behavior.

*Intermittent reinforcement approach:* You check and praise some pieces, not all. Randomly — workers do not know which pieces will be checked. Day 1: workers try the new technique, unsure if it will be caught. Some pieces are praised, some are not. Day 2: this is more effort than the old way, but it's unpredictable whether I'll get feedback. The uncertainty is uncomfortable. Day 3: a few workers have drifted back to the old way; the unpredictability was too much.

*But then:* You assign someone else to do the checking, and you tell the workers in advance: "Someone will be checking, randomly." Now workers continue the new technique because they learned it under unpredictable reinforcement. They are used to doing it sometimes without immediate feedback. The behavior is more robust.

The first approach works faster. The second works longer. You choose based on how long you need the behavior to stick.

### Common misconception: "Reinforcement is just a reward"

Not all rewards are reinforcers. A reinforcer is defined by its effect: does the behavior increase? If the behavior does not increase, it is not a reinforcer, even if it is objectively nice.

You give an employee a $500 bonus for hitting a quarterly target. In your mind, this is a reward. But if the employee does not increase effort next quarter, the bonus was not a reinforcer. Maybe they assumed the bonus was one-time. Maybe they did not believe their effort caused the bonus. Maybe $500 is irrelevant to them. Maybe the target felt arbitrary. The bonus was a reward, but not a reinforcer — it did not reinforce the behavior.

## Concept 2: Schedules of Reinforcement — Why Slot Machines and Sales Commissions Produce Different Persistence

### The puzzle: Why do some people keep trying when others give up?

Walk into a casino. Slot machines are not designed around generosity. The house margin is 2-4%. Yet people feed machines for hours. Now walk into a workplace where the incentive structure is objectively generous — 10% of pay can be earned as bonus if targets are hit — and watch some people make a few attempts and then stop trying.

The difference is not the math. The difference is *how the reward is scheduled*.

### The mechanism: Four schedules of reinforcement

Skinner identified four schedules of partial reinforcement, each producing different patterns of behavior.

**Fixed-interval schedule:** The reinforcer comes after a fixed amount of time, regardless of how much behavior occurred. Example: a weekly paycheck. You show up, you work, Friday comes, you get paid. This schedule produces moderate performance with a characteristic dip right after reinforcement — you get paid Friday afternoon, and Monday morning you are slightly less productive because another paycheck is a whole week away.

**Fixed-ratio schedule:** The reinforcer comes after a fixed number of behaviors. Example: piece-rate pay — you make five widgets, you get $10. This schedule produces rapid, sustained behavior while the reinforcer is available, but extinction is fast when it stops. Workers on piece-rate systems often produce at high speed until the pay ends, then stop abruptly.

**Variable-interval schedule:** The reinforcer comes at unpredictable times, but averaging to some interval. Example: a manager does performance reviews monthly, but not on any set date — sometimes it's the first week, sometimes the third. Workers produce steady effort throughout the month because they never know when the next review (reinforcement) will come.

**Variable-ratio schedule:** The reinforcer comes after an unpredictable number of behaviors, averaging to some ratio. Example: sales commission where the deal size varies. You make thirty calls averaging to one close per 20 calls, but you never know which call will close. This schedule produces the highest, most persistent behavior because the reinforcer is both uncertain and contingent on your action. You only earn it by behaving. The more calls you make, the more likely you are to get a close.

This is the slot machine. You insert a token. Something random happens. Rarely, you win. The pattern of behavior it produces is distinctive: high rate, long persistence even without reinforcement, and strong resistance to extinction.

Here's what makes this dangerous and brilliant simultaneously: **variable-ratio schedules are the most addictive reinforcement pattern we know**. They produce behavior that persists longer when reinforcement stops. They produce behavior that is harder to break. They produce obsessive repetition. This is why people keep gambling. This is why notification systems on phones are addictive — they reinforce checking the phone on a variable schedule. This is why some commission structures work so well and sometimes trap workers in unhealthy patterns.

### The comparison: Which schedule produces what?

| Schedule | Speed of learning | How long behavior lasts | Behavior pattern | What happens when rewards stop |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Continuous** | Fastest | Short | Steady | Behavior stops quickly |
| **Fixed-interval** | Moderate | Moderate | Dips after reinforcement | Stops fairly quickly |
| **Fixed-ratio** | Fast | Moderate | High and stable | Stops quickly, sometimes abruptly |
| **Variable-interval** | Slow | Long | Steady | Fades gradually |
| **Variable-ratio** | Moderate | Longest | Very high, sometimes obsessive | Persists the longest |

The key insight: if you need lasting behavior change, *intermittent is better than continuous, and variable is better than fixed*. If you need someone to learn something new fast, *continuous is better, but you must plan for how to fade the continuous reinforcement* (because abruptly stopping it will kill the behavior).

### Worked example: The sales manager's dilemma

A regional sales manager runs a 25-person team. The previous manager used a fixed-interval approach: everyone received a bonus pool review on March 15 and September 15. Bonuses were paid if targets were hit. Results: solid, predictable sales with a visible dip in productivity right before and right after bonus periods.

The new manager wants higher performance. She switches to a fixed-ratio system: salespeople get a commission check for each deal closed, calculated within days. The structure is cleaner — you earn, you get paid. Initial results: sales spike. People are thrilled with the immediacy. Commissions are paid weekly now instead of semi-annually.

But six months in, she notices something else. When the company had cash-flow problems and paused the commission checks for two weeks, sales activity *collapsed*. In the old system, people kept working steadily even when a bonus period was months away. Now, with no commission check expected soon, the work stops. Salespeople were used to reinforcement every few days. Without it, they lose momentum.

She shifts to a variable-ratio approach. Commissions are still tied to closed deals, but the size of deals varies. Most deals close for 1-2% commission, but occasionally a large deal closes for 5%. Salespeople do not know in advance which calls will land big deals. Results: sales activity becomes steadier. Even when bonuses are delayed or missed for a few weeks, people keep calling. They have learned that persistence is the variable that matters, not the predictability of the payoff.

The trade-off: the fixed-interval system was predictable and fair. The variable-ratio system produces more behavior but also more pressure. Some salespeople thrive in variability. Others find it stressful. The manager must choose based on what kind of effort she wants and what kind of people she is managing.

### The myth to correct: "Paying people more always works"

This is false. The relationship between pay and performance is weak to moderate when the performance is complex or when people are already above the survival threshold. A person making $60,000 per year does not necessarily increase effort when offered a bonus of $1,000 (1.7% of salary). The bonus is not a strong reinforcer.

What *does* matter for this person: the *contingency* (does my effort actually produce the bonus?), the *timing* (when will I find out if I succeeded?), and the *salience* (does this bonus matter relative to what I actually care about?). A small bonus paid monthly with clear metrics might be a better reinforcer than a large annual bonus paid once a year with ambiguous metrics.

The research is clear: pay-for-performance systems work well for simple, measurable tasks (sales, units produced) where the worker controls the metric. They work poorly for complex, interdependent tasks (team performance, innovation, strategic planning) where effort does not map cleanly to results. In complex work, the reward structure matters less than the existence of psychological safety and autonomy.

## Concept 3: Behavior Modification and the Limits of External Reinforcement

### The puzzle: Why do some behavior-change programs fail while others work?

An organization decides to reduce tardiness. It implements a program: employees who arrive late lose $5 from their next paycheck. After three months, tardiness drops 40%. The program works. But six months later, tardiness is creeping back up. The program is not maintaining its effect. Meanwhile, morale has declined, and turnover is up slightly among the best performers.

Meanwhile, a different organization implements a behavior-change program for the same problem. It involves three elements: clarifying expectations (here is what on-time arrival means), providing feedback (you have been on time 18 of 22 days this month, which is your highest rate), and gradually raising the standard (next month, the goal is 20 of 22). No punishment. No big rewards. Just clarity, feedback, and incremental progress. After six months, tardiness is down 35% and people report that they feel like they are improving.

The difference is not the reinforcement. Both programs use consequences. The difference is in how the program is designed and what assumptions it makes about people.

### The mechanism: Behavior Modification at work

**Behavior modification** (or organizational behavior modification, OBM) is the systematic application of operant conditioning principles to workplace behavior. It assumes that behavior can be shaped through clear goals, feedback, and consequences. When it works, it works well. When it fails, the failure is instructive.

The typical process has five steps:

1. **Define the desired behavior in objective terms.** Not "better attitude" or "more engagement." Something measurable: "arrival time between 8:00 and 8:05 a.m." or "response to customer email within 2 hours." This specification is harder than it sounds because many behaviors we care about are fuzzy.

2. **Measure current performance.** How many arrivals are on time now? How many emails are answered within 2 hours? This baseline matters because it tells you how much room there is for improvement and what "success" should look like.

3. **Set a clear goal that is achievable but challenging.** Not "never be late" (almost no one can achieve that) but "90% on time over the next month." The goal matters because it focuses effort and provides a target for feedback.

4. **Provide feedback and reinforce progress.** This is where timing matters. Feedback should come quickly and in specific terms. "You have been on time 18 of 22 days — that's 82%, up from last month's 75%." The reinforcement does not have to be big. It often works just as well to celebrate the improvement directly.

5. **Gradually increase the standard (shaping).** Once people are hitting 90%, move the target to 95%. This incremental approach prevents the frustration that comes from a suddenly-raised bar.

The beauty of this approach: it works. Meta-analyses show that OBM programs produce average improvements of 25-40% in targeted behaviors. But there are limits.

### The trade-off: External control vs. intrinsic motivation

Behavior modification produces fast, measurable changes in specific behaviors. But there's a cost: it can shift focus from the underlying purpose to compliance with the metric.

Here's the risk: a customer-service team is measured on call handling time. The behavior-modification program reinforces speed — fewer than 6 minutes per call. Call time drops from 8 minutes to 5.5 minutes. Success. But customer satisfaction scores, which nobody is measuring, decline because people are rushing callers off the phone.

Or: an assembly line is reinforced for units produced per hour. Production increases. But defects also increase because the measurement does not include quality.

Or: salespeople are reinforced for number of calls made, and calls increase from 20 to 35 per day. But deal-close rate drops because they are making more low-quality calls.

This is the behavior-modification trap: *when you measure and reinforce a specific behavior, people optimize for that behavior, sometimes at the expense of the broader goal*.

The deeper concern comes from research by Edward Deci and colleagues on intrinsic motivation. Extrinsic reinforcement (cash, prizes, recognition) can actually *decrease* intrinsic motivation (the internal drive to do something for its own sake) when the task is already engaging. The mechanism: when I earn money for doing something I already found interesting, I change my attribution. I start thinking "I do this because of the money" instead of "I do this because I care." The external motivation crowds out the internal one.

This does not happen for all tasks. For boring or aversive work (making phone calls, data entry, repetitive manufacturing), external reinforcement does not crowd out intrinsic motivation because there is little intrinsic motivation to begin with. For engaging work (problem-solving, creative projects, meaningful contribution), adding external rewards can actually reduce persistence and quality.

### The mechanism: Behavioral Self-Management (when people reinforce themselves)

There is a counterweight to this concern: behavioral self-management. Instead of the supervisor designing the reinforcement schedule, the employee does. The employee identifies a behavior they want to change, tracks it, and provides their own reinforcement (or punishment).

The mechanics use the same operant principles. A person who wants to exercise more might:
- **Identify the behavior:** going to the gym for 30 minutes
- **Track it:** keeping a log to see current frequency (maybe 2 times per week)
- **Set a goal:** 4 times per week over the next month
- **Identify a reinforcer:** a favorite activity or small treat, contingent on hitting the goal
- **Implement it:** only watch a favorite show after a gym session

The person is using the same reinforcement schedule on themselves, but the locus of control is internal. They chose the goal. They chose the reinforcer. They control the feedback.

Research shows that self-managed behavior change has two advantages: it is more sustainable (people maintain the behavior longer) and it is more intrinsically motivated (people feel like they chose the change, not that it was imposed).

The limitation: self-management works best for behaviors where the person is motivated to change. A smoker who genuinely wants to quit can use self-management effectively. A smoker who is being pressured to quit by their doctor might go through the motions but face constant temptation.

### Worked example: A quality-improvement program that almost failed, then worked

A manufacturing plant had a defect rate of 3.2% on a particular assembly process. Management decided to reduce it to 1% within six months using a behavior-modification approach. They measured defects daily. They set a goal of 2% by month 2, 1.5% by month 4, 1% by month 6. They provided feedback to each production team daily. For defect reductions beyond the target, teams received a small bonus pool.

Month 1: defect rate dropped to 2.8%. Good start. But production speed also dropped because people were slowing down to be more careful. Overall output was down 8%.

Month 2: the plant realized that rewarding defect reduction without accounting for speed was creating the wrong incentive. They restructured: now the metric was defects per unit produced, not absolute defects. This separated the two variables.

Month 3-4: defect rate continued to improve (now at 1.4%), and production rate recovered. But employee satisfaction surveys showed frustration. People felt like they were working harder for constant measurement and marginal bonuses.

Month 5: the plant introduced a shift. Instead of just telling teams what the target was, they invited teams to set their own targets. Teams could aim for 1% if they thought they could get there, or could propose a different target if they had other constraints. Teams started owning the goal instead of resisting it.

Month 6: defect rate hit 0.98%. On-time, with better morale than month 1.

The shift from externally-imposed reinforcement to self-managed goal-setting changed the psychology from "they are making us do this" to "we decided this matters." The behavior was the same. The internal experience was different. Long-term compliance was higher.

### The myth to correct: "Everyone is motivated by the same thing"

This is one of the biggest misconceptions about behavior modification. The research is clear: what is reinforcing for one person is not reinforcing for another. Money reinforces some people. Recognition reinforces others. Autonomy reinforces still others. Time off matters to some and not at all to others.

The only way to know what is reinforcing for a particular person is to watch what they actually do when given options. If someone consistently turns down high-bonus roles to stay in autonomous roles, money is not their primary reinforcer. If someone trades advancement opportunities to keep a flexible schedule, autonomy is not the driver — control over their time is.

Effective behavior-modification programs individualize the reinforcers. This is why it is worth asking people what would make them want to do something more, instead of assuming.

## Integration: The whole system

Return to the sales floor from the cold open. You now understand the machinery beneath what you see.

Jennifer is on a variable-ratio schedule. Her behavior is high and persistent because she does not know which call will close. This is brilliant incentive design if the goal is maximum effort. It is also stress-inducing if Jennifer is the kind of person who needs predictability.

Marcus is on a fixed-ratio schedule relative to his experience level. He knows roughly how many calls it takes him to close. His effort is high and stable, but it would drop quickly if the commission structure changed to a monthly bonus instead of per-deal. He has learned to expect feedback frequently, and intermittent feedback without frequent cycles would be demotivating.

The leaderboard creates a variable-interval element. People never know when they will see their rank move, but they know roughly that it updates regularly. This creates steady effort throughout the month.

The plant manager who implemented behavior modification for defect reduction is using operant conditioning principles systematically. The manager has learned that the most sustainable improvement came when people moved from external reinforcement to self-managed goal-setting.

Each of these is leverage. The ability to design a system that reinforces the behavior you actually want — not the behavior that is easy to measure — is a core managerial skill. The ability to recognize when external reinforcement is crowding out the intrinsic motivation you need — creativity, caregiving, ethical behavior — is equally important.

The trap is treating reinforcement as a universal tool. It works brilliantly for some behaviors and some people. It fails for others. Your job as a manager is to diagnose which is which, to design the reinforcement schedule that matches your goal and your people, and to know when not to use external reinforcement at all because it would undermine what you actually need.

## Chapter Summary

You can now do five things you could not do at the start of this chapter.

**You understand the law of effect and operant conditioning.** Behavior that is followed by a consequence is more or less likely to repeat depending on whether the consequence is reinforcing. This is not intuition; it is mechanism. A supervisor's immediate praise can be a stronger reinforcer than a large annual bonus because of timing. Knowing what is actually reinforcing a behavior requires watching what the person does, not guessing.

**You understand the power of schedules.** Continuous reinforcement produces fast learning but quick extinction. Intermittent reinforcement produces slower learning but much longer-lasting behavior. Variable schedules produce higher effort and more persistence than fixed schedules. Slot machines work because they use the schedule that produces the most addictive behavior: variable-ratio. Understanding this means you can design incentive systems that work for as long as you need them to.

**You understand the difference between reinforcement and motivation.** A consequence can reinforce a behavior whether or not the person is "motivated" in the emotional sense. A person can perform well because they are reinforced, even if they do not care about the task. This distinction is pragmatic: it means you can change behavior without requiring the internal shift that motivation implies.

**You understand behavior modification as a systematic process.** Defining behavior, measuring it, setting clear goals, providing feedback, and reinforcing progress produces measurable improvements. But you also understand its limits: it works best for simple, measurable behaviors and can fail for complex, interdependent work or when external reinforcement crowds out intrinsic motivation.

**You understand the spectrum from external reinforcement to self-management.** Systems can range from supervisor-controlled reinforcement to self-managed goal-setting. Self-management is more sustainable and intrinsically motivating, but it requires that the person wants to change the behavior. The most effective systems often shift from external management (to establish the behavior quickly) to self-management (to maintain it long-term).

The thing to watch for, going forward, is the **hidden measurement problem**. When you measure and reinforce a specific behavior, people optimize for that behavior, sometimes at the expense of what actually matters. The customer-service metric that rewards speed without measuring satisfaction. The sales metric that rewards volume without measuring repeat business. The quality metric that rewards defect-counting without measuring whether customers actually prefer the product. The solution is not to stop measuring. It is to measure what actually matters, even when it is harder.

Evidence that variable-ratio schedules do not actually produce longer-lasting behavior than fixed schedules, or that behavior modification does not produce sustained change beyond six months, would require substantial revision. Also: evidence that pay-for-performance systems work as well for complex, interdependent tasks as for simple, individual tasks would reshape the practical recommendations.

## Still puzzling

I do not yet fully understand the boundary conditions of the intrinsic-motivation crowding-out effect. When does external reinforcement enhance intrinsic motivation, and when does it suppress it? The research shows it depends on whether the task was already engaging, but the mechanism beneath that distinction is not clear to me.
