# Chapter 13 — Organizational Power and Politics

## Suggested titles

1. **The Invisible Org Chart: How Power Actually Works Inside Organizations**
2. **Power and Politics in Organizations: What the Data Actually Shows**
3. **Beyond Authority: Understanding How Decisions Really Get Made**

---

## TL;DR

Power — the ability to make someone do something they wouldn't otherwise do — is not the same as authority or leadership, and understanding the five bases of power reveals why some managers influence through inspiration while others rely on fear. Political behavior in organizations is not inherently bad; it emerges predictably when goals are ambiguous, resources are scarce, and decisions are made under uncertainty. The key is learning to recognize when politics serves the organization and when it corrodes it.

---

## Chapter opening

You are sitting in a budget meeting at a mid-sized technology firm. The CFO is presenting Q3 spend projections. The numbers are clear. The case is logical. The room seems to agree. Then, forty minutes later, in a hallway conversation between the VP of Product and the CTO, the actual decision gets made — not the one the CFO proposed, but a modified version that splits the difference and protects the Product team's headcount while cutting Engineering's training budget. Nobody in that hallway intended to subvert the formal process. Both of them believed they were arguing for what was best for the company. But neither of them could have made that call in the conference room in front of the CFO and the entire leadership team. Something about power shifted between those two settings.

This chapter is about that shift. It is about the gap between the org chart and the influence map — between who has the authority to make a decision and who actually shapes what that decision will be. For a long time, management theory pretended the org chart and the influence map were the same thing. They never are. Understanding power and politics is the difference between a manager who thinks people follow because of their title and a manager who knows why people actually follow — and who can work effectively in both settings.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- **Identify** the five bases of power (French and Raven) and explain how each one generates different employee responses — commitment, compliance, or resistance.
- **Distinguish** power from authority and leadership, and explain why the three often get confused but matter differently.
- **Predict** when political behavior will flourish in an organization (ambiguous goals, scarce resources, uncertainty, change, nonprogrammed decisions) and what conditions minimize it.
- **Recognize** the strategic contingencies model of power and explain how control over uncertainty, nonsubstitutability, and centrality shift power among units.
- **Apply** at least three power tactics (information control, agenda control, coalitions) and explain their ethical boundaries.
- **Design** organizational systems that reduce destructive political behavior without eliminating the political engagement that organizations actually need.

### Prerequisites

Chapter 2 (individual differences), Chapter 6 (decision-making under uncertainty), Chapter 9 (group dynamics). Familiarity with the idea that formal organizational structures often differ from informal ones.

### Why this chapter matters

Power and politics are either invisible or all anyone talks about, depending on the organization. Managers who understand power dynamics can work effectively in both worlds. You will see, in this chapter and beyond, that many of the formal "rational" management systems (performance appraisal, resource allocation, hiring) are actually political processes running under a rational label. Recognizing this is not cynicism; it is clarity. The alternative — pretending power doesn't exist — leaves you blindsided when it does.

## Concept 1 — What power is, and how it differs from authority and leadership

### Cold open: The power no one talks about

You are a junior manager at a human-resources department in a large financial-services firm. A new senior VP arrives from outside. He is well-intentioned, competent, credentialed. He wants to streamline the hiring process, cut cycle time from six weeks to three. It is a reasonable goal. He announces it at an all-hands meeting.

Three weeks later, the hiring cycle is still five to six weeks. He asks the recruiting team why. "The background checks take four weeks," they tell him. "We can't control that." He pushes back. "Other firms do it in two weeks." They agree. Nothing changes. He brings in a consultant. The consultant interviews people. The consultant recommends a new vendor for background checks. The recommendation lands in the VP's inbox and quietly disappears. 

Six months later, the cycle time is still five weeks, and the VP has stopped pushing. He has not lost authority — his title is the same, his budget is the same, the org chart shows him in charge of HR. What he has lost is the ability to move the organization. He has hit what researchers call a *power dependency*: he is formally in charge of a system that, to work, depends on people and resources he does not actually control. The background-check company is not reporting to him. The recruiting team's compliance depends not on whether they fear his punishment, but on whether they believe they can actually comply. And they don't.

This is power, distinct from both authority and leadership, and it is the beginning of understanding how organizations actually work.

### The mechanism: Power, authority, leadership — three different things

Max Weber, the German sociologist who arguably invented organizational studies, defined power as "the probability that one actor within a social relationship will be in a position to carry out his own will despite resistance." It's a good definition. Notice what it does not say: it does not talk about the right to exercise power. It does not assume the person has a formal title. It says: ability, despite resistance.

Let's sharpen that. Power is the ability to cause another person or group to take an action they would not otherwise take. It is about *change in behavior*. Two people matter: the person trying to influence (the power holder) and the person being influenced. And notice the word "otherwise" — power means actual change, not just agreement or compliance with what someone would have done anyway.

Authority, by contrast, is about *rights*. When the CFO tells an accountant to reconcile the month-end books, that is authority. The CFO has the *right* to give that order; the organization says so. The accountant's compliance is based not on fear of the CFO and not on admiration for the CFO, but on shared acceptance that the CFO holds that particular position and therefore has that particular right. Authority is legitimate power. It is power that the group agrees is appropriate.

Leadership is the third thing, and it is the hardest of the three to pin down. Leadership is the ability to elicit responses from others that go *beyond* required or mechanical compliance. It is voluntary. A department head can require people to attend a meeting (authority). A leader gets people to care about the meeting's outcome. Both things might be done by the same person, but they are not the same thing. We sometimes call the former "headship" — the position — and the latter "leadership" — the pull.

Here is the key trade-off: authority is durable and constrained. It comes with the role. But it is legitimate only as long as the group consents to the arrangement. That consent can be withdrawn — revolutions happen, restructures happen, elections happen. Power, on the other hand, can come from anywhere. It can be built on knowledge, on control of scarce resources, on personal appeal, on the ability to hurt someone, on the credibility you have earned. It is more fragile in some ways (if you leave the organization, some of it goes with you) and more durable in others (it is harder to formally revoke). Leadership is the rarest of the three and the most valuable, but also the hardest to systematize. You cannot policy your way to leadership. Authority you can — you write the role in the org chart. But leadership cannot be mandated.

The VP in the opening scene had authority (the right to set HR policy) but lacked power (the ability to change what people actually do) and failed to build leadership (the willingness of people to engage with the vision). That is why he failed.

### The five bases of power (French and Raven, 1959)

John French and Bertram Raven, two mid-century social psychologists studying influence, identified five distinct bases of power — five sources from which power can come. Each generates different behavioral responses from the person being influenced. This framework has held up remarkably well for over sixty years.

**Referent power** is power that comes from personal appeal — from someone looking up to you. When you do something because you admire the person asking you to do it, that is referent power. It has also been called charisma, though that is a loaded term. The mechanism is simpler: I want to be like you, so I do what you ask because I want your approval or because doing it makes me more like you in my own mind. In organizations, you see this when junior employees imitate senior employees — not because they are told to, but because they admire them. You see it with celebrities endorsing products; the hope is that the star power rubs off. You see it with founders and early employees at startups — the founder's vision pulls people to work eighty-hour weeks because they believe in what the founder believes. Referent power is the one that generates *commitment* — genuine buy-in, not just behavioral compliance.

**Expert power** is power that comes from knowledge or skill that others need. A professor has expert power in the classroom because the professor knows the material and the students want to know it. An accountant has expert power in tax season. A specialist surgeon has expert power in her operating room. The boundary is important: expert power is narrow and domain-specific. A brilliant neurosurgeon has expert power in neurosurgery and almost nowhere else. You cannot expert-power your way to changing someone's behavior in a domain where you are not actually expert. Expert power is durable as long as your expertise is relevant and scarce. If what you know becomes common knowledge or automated, the power evaporates.

**Legitimate power** is the official right to ask for compliance — authority, in other words. A supervisor has legitimate power to assign work because the organization says that is the supervisor's job. A police officer has legitimate power to stop you on the street. A judge has legitimate power to sentence you. The boundaries of legitimate power are crucial: a supervisor's legitimate power to assign work does not extend to ordering a subordinate to run personal errands or to commit an illegal act. Where legitimate power ends, the person must either choose to comply (under some other form of power or influence) or refuse. If a manager asks an employee to clean the manager's car and the employee says no, the manager's legitimate power provides no grounds to enforce it.

**Reward power** is power that comes from control over things people want — money, promotion, status, good assignments, new equipment, responsibility. Research has consistently shown that reward power tends to increase job performance, at least when people believe there is a clear link between their performance and the reward. The trade-off is that in many organizations, supervisors do not actually control as many rewards as they think they do. Salary is often locked by market rates or labor contracts. Promotions are often decided by committees. So reward power, while it looks simple in organizational charts, often does not work the way the chart suggests.

**Coercive power** is power that comes from the ability to punish — to fire someone, to demote them, to give them an undesirable assignment, to withhold promotion, to reduce responsibilities. Coercive power does not require physical force. It can be as subtle as social exclusion, humiliation, or withdrawal of emotional support. David Kipnis, who spent his career studying power, made a dark observation: of all the bases of power available to people, coercive power — the power to hurt — is probably the most used and most difficult to control. The reason is simple: it works in the short term. Fear is a reliable motivator. But the behavioral response it generates is not commitment or compliance — it is resistance and resentment.

### The mechanism: How different bases of power generate different employee responses

Here is what matters organizationally: the base of power you use shapes how people respond.

If you use referent power — if people do things because they admire you and want your approval — you get *commitment*. The employee will be motivated to help you succeed. They will apply discretionary effort. They will suggest ideas. They will warn you about problems before they blow up. This is the response every manager wants.

If you use expert power, you also tend to get commitment, because the person recognizes that what you are asking for actually makes sense and they want to learn. A teacher giving difficult feedback gets commitment when the student trusts that the teacher actually knows what good work looks like.

If you use legitimate power or reward power, you typically get *compliance*. The employee will do what you ask, either because they see it as their duty or because they expect a reward. But they will not do more than asked. They will not suggest improvements. They will not stay late unless explicitly asked. They will do the job, not more.

If you use coercive power, you get *resistance*. The employee will do the minimum required to avoid punishment. They will look for ways to get around the rule. If they can find another job, they will leave. They will do the job but also quietly undermine it. If you have ever worked for someone who relied on threat and humiliation, you know this response in your bones.

This is not a moral judgment; it is an empirical observation. It is built into the psychology of motivation and the rational self-interest of employees. Commitment requires either genuine agreement or genuine admiration. Compliance requires either a sense of duty or a clear exchange (do this and get paid). Resistance is what happens when you rely on threat.

### Common misconceptions

**"If I have authority, people will do what I ask."** They will comply with explicit requests. But they will not grant you the discretionary effort, the suggestion-making, the early warning about problems that makes an organization actually work. Authority keeps basic rules in place. It does not build organizations that move.

**"Power is bad and should be eliminated."** Power is not bad. It is how things actually get done. The question is not whether power exists but whether it is used toward legitimate organizational goals or diverted to personal ends.

**"The person with the most authority has the most power."** False, and it causes surprises. An executive assistant often has more power over what a CEO actually does than many people several rungs higher on the org chart. A gatekeeper at a hospital emergency department has more practical power over patient care than many physicians. Power is not the same as rank.

## Concept 2 — How power is distributed among organizational units

### Cold open: The department that ran out of luck

In the 1970s, electric utilities were among the most powerful corporations in America. They were growing. They were building. The central contingency of the business was *technical capability* — could you design and build the next power plant? Could you keep the grid stable? So the senior leadership of most utilities was full of engineers. The engineering department had power. It controlled the essential function. It understood the complexity.

By the 1990s, that had flipped. Utilities faced litigation, regulation, environmental lawsuits, nuclear-safety concerns, climate policy battles. The contingency shifted. What mattered was no longer can we build it, but can we *defend ourselves when we build it*? The senior leadership became full of lawyers. The legal department gained power. The engineering department lost it, not because the engineers got worse at their jobs, but because the external environment shifted what was strategically essential to survival.

This is not the exception. This is how power actually distributes itself in organizations. It follows need.

### The mechanism: Strategic contingencies and resource dependence

There are two major models that explain how power distributes itself among departments and units in organizations. Both have survived decades of research. Both explain different pieces of the puzzle.

**The resource dependence model** says this: if Department A controls resources that Department B needs, and those resources are scarce and valued, then Department A has power over Department B. The mechanism is simple: *you need me for something I control, and you cannot easily get it elsewhere.* A purchasing department has power over production because it controls the flow of raw materials. A finance department has power over capital investment because it controls the capital. In a university, a doctoral program has power if it can reliably attract prestigious faculty and top-tier graduate students — because the university values those things and cannot produce them without that unit.

The evidence for this model is strong. Salancik and Pfeffer studied university budget decisions and found that departments with more external grant funding got larger budget allocations, even controlling for the size of the department. Why? Because the department brought in money the university valued. Once a department had power from securing resources, it used that power to secure *more* resources — what Pfeffer calls "the rich get richer." This pattern shows up everywhere: in corporations, in nonprofits, in government agencies.

**The strategic contingencies model** adds something crucial. It is not just *what resources you control*, but *whether controlling those resources reduces uncertainty for others.* A contingency is a requirement for one unit's work that depends on another unit's work. A strategic contingency is when one unit can help another unit manage the unpredictable parts of its job.

Consider a manufacturing firm. The production line has inherent uncertainty: will the supplier deliver materials on time? Will the machinery break down unexpectedly? Will demand suddenly surge? The purchasing department reduces some of that uncertainty by guaranteeing material supply. The maintenance department reduces uncertainty by keeping equipment running. In successful manufacturing firms, both purchasing and maintenance have real power because they *cope with the uncertainty that would otherwise disrupt production*. That is the strategic contingency model: power accrues to whoever helps you manage what you cannot predict.

Three specific things determine how much power a unit has under this model:

First, *the ability to reduce uncertainty.* If my group can forecast what is about to happen to your group, we have power. If we can prevent disruptions from reaching you, we have power. If we can absorb shocks that would otherwise hit you, we have power. In the utility example: when growth and construction were the primary uncertainty, engineering had power because they could manage technical complexity. When litigation and regulation became the primary uncertainty, legal had power because they could manage that complexity.

Second, *nonsubstitutability* — whether your group can get what we provide from someone else. If there are many vendors who can provide the same service, our power weakens. If we are the only source, our power strengthens. Apple's power in personal computing partly comes from the fact that if you want an iPad ecosystem, you can only get it from Apple. IBM's power in mainframes used to work the same way — if you wanted mainframe capability, you bought from IBM. When IBM-compatible personal computers arrived, IBM's power collapsed because suddenly people had alternatives. Exclusivity is power.

Third, *centrality* — how interconnected is your work with everyone else's? An assembly line at the end of the line depends entirely on the work of the line at the beginning. The units at the end have low power because they are in the middle of a workflow. The units at the beginning have more power because so much depends on them. IT infrastructure has power in modern organizations not because IT people are brilliant (though some are) but because almost everything runs on technology now. IT is central.

### Common misconceptions

**"The department with the biggest budget has the most power."** Budget size is often a consequence of power, not a cause. The powerful departments attract resources because they are powerful. But a small department with exclusive expertise or control over essential uncertainty can have enormous power.

**"Power distribution in organizations is stable."** It is not. As the utility example shows, power shifts when the environment shifts. When your organization's strategic contingencies change — whether due to market shifts, technological change, competition, or regulatory changes — power redistributes. Departments that were essential become peripheral. New departments emerge as essential. This is why organizational change is so political: people in newly peripheral departments often fight to maintain relevance and power even when the organization needs them to shrink.

## Concept 3 — How political behavior works and how to manage it

### Cold open: A budget meeting that was really a power play

You are the VP of Engineering at a software company. The company has grown from 50 people to 200. The CEO calls a quarterly business review to discuss resource allocation. The sales VP presents her case: she needs three more account executives to close the deals in the pipeline. The product VP presents her case: she needs two more product managers to handle the complexity of feature prioritization across three platforms. You present your case: you need to pay down technical debt and hire two senior architects to reduce the risk in the system.

The CEO says: "All of that sounds important. Here is what we are going to do. Sales, you get two. Product, you get one. Engineering, you get one architect but you're going to have to move a mid-level engineer to technical debt work — call it a reallocation, not a headcount." 

The sales VP walks out disappointed but basically okay — she got two-thirds. The product VP is upset — she got half. You are furious because you didn't get the structural reinforcement you asked for, and now you have to choose which mid-level engineer to pull off a feature team, which will slow down the product.

But what just happened? What looked like a rational resource-allocation decision was actually a political power play. The CEO did not have perfect information about what each department actually needs (nobody does). The CEO was not making a pure "merit" decision (if she were, each argument would have been evaluated on its merits). Instead, the CEO was making a *power* decision: balancing the influence of three departments, the relative strength of each relationship, the CEO's confidence in each leader, and the perceived political cost of disappointing each one.

This is organizational politics.

### The mechanism: When and why political behavior emerges

Politics — defined by Jeffrey Pfeffer as "those activities taken within organizations to acquire, develop, and use power and other resources to obtain one's preferred outcomes in a situation in which there is uncertainty or dissensus about choices" — is not a departure from normal organizational life. It is organizational life, at least under specific conditions.

Political behavior intensifies under five conditions:

**Ambiguous goals.** If the organization's goal is clear — make a specific product for a specific market at a target cost — there is less room for people to pursue their own agenda under the cover of pursuing organizational goals. But in many organizations, especially larger ones, goals are genuinely ambiguous. Should we prioritize growth or profitability? Market share or customer satisfaction? Innovation or efficiency? When goals are ambiguous, people can pursue incompatible objectives while claiming to pursue organizational interests.

**Scarce resources.** If there is enough budget for everyone to do what they think is important, there is no need for politics to decide allocation. But when resources are limited, someone wins and someone loses. In that situation, it is rational for people to engage in political behavior to protect their area. This is why you see more political intensity in downturns than in upturns — scarcity triggers it.

**Nonprogrammed decisions.** If a decision is routine and governed by policy, there is little room for political maneuvering. "What is the salary for a Software Engineer, level 3?" Check the policy. "Who gets to use the conference room on Tuesday at 2 p.m.?" Check the calendar. But "Should we invest in a new market or double down on an existing one?" or "Should we acquire that startup or build in-house?" — these decisions have lots of ambiguity and lots of opportunity for different units to advocate for different outcomes.

**Organizational change.** During restructures, new product launches, market entries, or strategic shifts, normal power structures are temporarily malleable. People compete to secure territory and influence in the new structure. This is why you see more political behavior during change than during stability. It is not that change makes people more political; it is that change makes the stakes visible. Power battles that were hidden in stable times become explicit.

**Complex or rapidly changing environment.** When technology is uncertain, markets shift fast, or regulations change, the organization cannot predict what it will need. That creates opportunity for different units to argue for different strategies, each claiming their approach is what the turbulent environment demands. A decade ago, a manufacturing company's IT department could argue that stability and predictability were paramount; now the same department argues that agility and rapid iteration are paramount. Both claims sound reasonable because the environment is genuinely uncertain.

Here is what matters: political behavior is not inherently bad. Some political activity is necessary. The departments that do not advocate for themselves get starved of resources. The units that do not compete for influence fall behind. The organizations that *eliminate* all political behavior often do so by making decisions so rigid and programmed that they cannot adapt to change. The question is not whether to have politics (you will), but how to channel it toward organizational goals rather than personal ones.

### The mechanism: How power tactics actually work

People exercise power through specific tactics. Understanding these helps you recognize when they are being used on you and when you might use them appropriately.

**Controlling access to information.** Most decisions depend on information. If you control what information people see, you shape what they decide. A common version: only the personnel department and senior management know what people actually earn. This information asymmetry gives both of them power over salary discussions, because they can claim "market data" that others cannot independently verify. In politics broadly, this is called controlling the narrative. In organizations, it is controlling the data.

**Controlling access to persons.** Who gets to talk to the decision-maker? That is power. In the Nixon White House, the President's senior advisers controlled who could see the President and who could not. This isolation contributed to some of Nixon's worst decisions. In corporations, gatekeeping still works: an executive assistant controls the executive's calendar. Whoever can get on the calendar can influence the executive. Whoever cannot, cannot.

**Selective use of objective criteria.** Few organizational decisions have one objectively correct answer. Instead, decisions depend on choosing the criteria by which to evaluate options. Should we hire the candidate with the most experience or the best cultural fit? The cheapest vendor or the most reliable vendor? If you can choose the criteria, you can predetermine the outcome. A famous case: in faculty hiring debates, one group emphasizes teaching excellence and chooses hiring criteria based on teaching; the other emphasizes research and chooses criteria based on research quality. Both groups claim to be selecting the best candidate. What they are really doing is selecting the criteria that favor their preferred candidate.

**Controlling the agenda.** If an unwanted proposal never comes up for a vote, it never loses. A simple tactic: order meeting topics so the controversial item is last, when people are tired and want to leave. If that fails, raise objections to delay the vote until the next meeting. Repeat until the proposal dies of neglect.

**Using outside experts.** A unit with a particular agenda can commission a study or hire a consultant known to support that agenda. The consultant issues a report. The report becomes "objective evidence" of the preferred position, even though the consultant was hired specifically because they were likely to reach that conclusion.

**Coalitions and alliances.** One unit cannot always move the organization alone. But if several units with overlapping interests band together, they have more collective power. Multiple labor unions in the same corporation form coalitions to bargain for contract concessions. Business groups form trade associations to lobby for favorable policies. In organizations, similar coalition-building happens: "If you support my budget request this quarter, I will support yours next quarter." That is politics in its basic form.

**Bureaucratic gamesmanship.** An organization's own rules can become weapons. "Work to rule" — following every policy and rule to the letter — often grinds an organization to a halt because most organizations have conflicting rules. A group wanting to resist a decision can drag its feet, create red tape, file formal objections, and in the process slow a decision until either it dies or the group gets what it wants.

### Common misconceptions

**"Politics is always bad for the organization."** Some political behavior is necessary and healthy. The engineering department advocating for better infrastructure is engaging in politics, and that advocacy often prevents bigger problems later. The question is not politics yes or no, but politics serving what ends.

**"The answer is to eliminate politics."** You cannot. You can reduce it by clarifying goals, reducing ambiguity, and allocating resources transparently. But in any organization with multiple goals, scarce resources, and imperfect information, some political behavior will happen. The organizations that have eliminated it are often the ones that cannot adapt, because nobody dares to challenge the status quo.

**"People who do politics are manipulative and bad at their real jobs."** False. Some of the most effective managers are politically skilled. They understand power, build coalitions, negotiate resource allocation, and still get their core work done. Political skill is not separate from management skill; it is part of it.

## Integration and synthesis

Let's walk back through what we have seen. 

At the interpersonal level, power is the ability to change someone's behavior. It comes from five sources: personal appeal (referent power), knowledge (expert power), legitimate authority, control over rewards, or ability to punish. Each source generates different behavioral responses — commitment, compliance, or resistance. Authority, which is one specific base of power, is not enough. You can have authority and still lack the actual power to move an organization. This happens when people depend on resources you do not control, or when they do not believe you have the right to give certain orders, or when they simply do not care about your reward or your punishment.

At the unit or departmental level, power is not distributed by size or seniority. It is distributed by strategic importance. Two models explain this: the resource dependence model (power goes to whoever controls scarce, valued resources) and the strategic contingencies model (power goes to whoever helps others manage uncertainty). When the organization's strategic contingencies shift — when the environment changes and different capabilities become essential — power redistributes. Departments that were central become peripheral. This creates political conflict because people in those departments fight to maintain relevance even as the organization needs them to shrink.

At the organizational level, political behavior is a normal response to specific structural conditions: ambiguous goals, scarce resources, uncertainty, and change. Politics is how organizations make decisions when rational criteria cannot settle the question. The problem is not that politics exists. The problem is when political behavior serves individual or departmental interests at the expense of organizational ones. This happens when the system does not sharply distinguish between success and failure, or when the reward system encourages people to maximize their own position rather than organizational performance.

What does this mean for you as a manager? 

First, recognize power and politics as real. The org chart is not the influence map. Some of the most powerful people in your organization do not have authority. Some of the people with authority have little power. Understanding why is the beginning of effectiveness.

Second, know your own bases of power. Are you expert? Referent? Relying on coercive power? Legitimate authority? The answer shapes what you can and cannot move. If you are expert but lack authority, you will influence through knowledge, not command. That is fine — it is often more durable — but you need to know it.

Third, manage organizational politics by managing the conditions that spawn it. Clarify goals. Reduce ambiguity in decision-making and reward systems. Make resource allocation transparent and tied to performance, not to politics. When you cannot eliminate uncertainty, acknowledge it and make decisions explicitly political rather than pretending they are rational. "We are going to do X because it aligns with our strategic priority, even though the data does not settle it" is better than "The data shows we should do X" when the data does not actually show that. The second creates cynicism. The first creates buy-in.

Fourth, limit unethical political behavior by making it costly. People who use information control, coalitions, and agenda-setting to advance organizational goals are doing politics in service of legitimate outcomes. People who do the same things to advance only personal interests are doing something different. Organizations with strong ethics, clear consequences for violations, and transparent promotion and reward systems tend to see less of the second kind.

The evidence is clear: power and politics cannot be eliminated from organizations. Nor should they be. They are how decisions actually get made, especially in complex, uncertain situations. The key is shaping them toward legitimate ends rather than personal ones.

## Graduated exercises

### Warm-up

1. Identify a decision that was made at an organization you have been part of (school, work, volunteer group). Describe the decision and the formal reason given for it. Then, separately, describe what you now think was the real political dynamics behind it — what power was being exercised, by whom, and toward what end. What are you now uncertain about?

2. Name someone you know (fictional or real) who has legitimate authority but does not actually have much power. What would it take for that person to gain actual power?

3. Think of a time you changed someone's behavior through personal appeal or admiration (referent power) rather than because they had to. What made that work?

### Application

4. A manufacturing firm has a procurement department that has historically been administrative — they buy what engineering specifies, from vendors that manufacturing approves. Suddenly the firm faces a supply-chain crisis. Procurement becomes critical: they are the ones who can find alternative vendors, negotiate contracts quickly, and solve urgent sourcing problems. Using the strategic contingencies model, explain why procurement's power in the organization will likely increase. What would happen to their power if the crisis ended and the supply chain stabilized again?

5. You are taking over as VP of a division that has been struggling. The division has four main departments. Based on the resource dependence and strategic contingencies models, what information would you gather to understand which departments have power and why? What would you ask about? Who would you talk to?

6. An organization wants to reduce political behavior in its annual budget cycle. Currently, every department advocates hard for maximum resources, and the final allocation often looks like an attempt to satisfy everyone a little rather than fund actual priorities. Using the conditions that trigger political behavior (ambiguous goals, scarce resources, nonprogrammed decisions, uncertainty, change), propose three specific changes to the budget process that might reduce destructive political behavior. Explain why each change addresses one or more of the conditions.

### Synthesis and challenge

7. Research or describe a real case where a unit in an organization lost power as strategic contingencies shifted (the utility-and-engineers example from this chapter is one; you could find others in business, nonprofits, or government). What happened to the people in that unit? How did they respond? What did the organization learn?

8. Consider the five bases of power (referent, expert, legitimate, reward, coercive). In what organizational context might each one be most appropriate? When would using a particular base of power be inappropriate? Create a two-by-two matrix with "appropriate context" on one axis and "the five bases" on the other. Fill in examples.

9. You notice that a colleague is using information control and selective criteria to advance a position you think is wrong for the organization. What would you do? Walk through the ethical and practical considerations. How does your answer change if the colleague is your boss? If the colleague is a peer?

## Chapter summary

Power — the ability to change someone's behavior — is not the same as authority (the right to ask for compliance) or leadership (the ability to inspire voluntary engagement). The five bases of power identified by French and Raven — referent, expert, legitimate, reward, and coercive — generate different employee responses. Referent and expert power tend to produce commitment; legitimate and reward power produce compliance; coercive power produces resistance.

Power at the organizational unit level follows strategic importance, not size. The resource dependence model explains that units controlling scarce, valued resources gain power. The strategic contingencies model explains that units gain power by reducing uncertainty for others. When strategic contingencies shift (due to environmental change, technological disruption, or market shifts), power redistributes, often creating political conflict.

Political behavior in organizations is predictable and often necessary. It intensifies under five structural conditions: ambiguous goals, scarce resources, nonprogrammed decisions, organizational change, and uncertain environments. Politics is how organizations make decisions when rational criteria cannot settle questions. The answer is not to eliminate politics but to manage it by clarifying goals, reducing ambiguity, aligning reward systems with organizational outcomes, and making political decisions explicit rather than disguising them as rational ones.

Power tactics — information control, agenda control, selective criteria, coalitions, and others — are tools for exercising power. Whether they are ethical depends not on the tool but on the ends served. Tactics used to advance organizational goals are legitimate. Tactics used only for personal interest are not.

## What would change my mind

I have presented both the resource dependence and strategic contingencies models as established frameworks. What would shift my confidence: evidence that power distribution in organizations does not actually follow strategic contingency patterns, or that strategic contingencies do not shift as environments change. If you found an organization where the least important units had the most power, or where power did not shift when strategic contingencies shifted, you would have found something important. I do not think that is likely, but the framework is testable.

## Still puzzling

What I do not fully understand: why some managers become trapped by their own power. A person gains power by mastering a domain (IT, finance, operations) and then becomes unable to pivot when the strategic contingency shifts. They see the threat but cannot let go of the behavior that made them powerful. This is a psychological and organizational phenomenon I see repeatedly, and I do not have a satisfying explanation for it. It may be that power itself creates a kind of blindness.
