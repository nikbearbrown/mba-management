# Chapter 13 — Organizational Power and Politics
*Why the org chart and the influence map are never the same document.*

---

Here is something that happens in every organization, every day, and almost never gets discussed directly.

A CFO presents Q3 spend projections at a budget meeting. The numbers are clear. The logic is sound. The room appears to agree. Forty minutes later, in a hallway conversation between the VP of Product and the CTO, the actual decision gets made — a modified version that protects Product's headcount while cutting Engineering's training budget. Neither person intended to subvert the formal process. Both believed they were arguing for what was best for the company. But something about the setting of the conference room made that conversation impossible there. In the hallway it was natural.

This is not exceptional. This is how organizations actually work. The conference room is where formal authority operates. The hallway is where power operates. They are not the same thing, and the gap between them — between the org chart and the influence map, between who has the right to make a decision and who actually shapes what that decision will be — is the subject of this chapter.

Management theory spent a long time pretending the two were identical. You have authority, therefore you have power. You have the title, therefore you have the influence. This is comforting and false. Understanding why it is false — and what power actually is, where it comes from, and how it distributes itself across organizations — is the difference between a manager who is perpetually surprised by outcomes and one who understands what is actually happening.

---

## What Power Is (and What It Is Not)

Max Weber defined power as "the probability that one actor within a social relationship will be in a position to carry out his own will despite resistance." Notice what that definition does not say. It says nothing about rights. It says nothing about formal titles. It says: ability, despite resistance.

Power is the ability to cause another person or group to take an action they would not otherwise take. The word "otherwise" is doing real work. Power means actual change in behavior — not agreement with something someone would have done anyway, not compliance with a request that was welcome to begin with.

**Authority** is different. Authority is about rights. When a CFO tells an accountant to reconcile the month-end books, that is authority — the CFO has the right to give that order, and the accountant's compliance is based on shared acceptance that the CFO holds that position. Authority is legitimate power. It is power that the organization formally endorses and that the group consents to as appropriate.

**Leadership** is the third thing, and the hardest to pin down. Leadership is the ability to elicit responses that go beyond required compliance. A department head can require people to attend a meeting — that is authority. A leader gets people to care about the meeting's outcome. We sometimes call the former "headship" — the position — and the latter "leadership" — the pull. The same person can exercise both, but they are not the same thing, and which one is operating at any moment determines the quality of what you get.

Here is why the distinction matters in practice. Consider a senior VP hired from outside a large financial-services firm to streamline a hiring process — cut cycle time from six weeks to three. He has authority: his title is clear, his budget is intact, the org chart shows him in charge. He announces the goal at an all-hands. Three weeks later nothing has changed. The background checks still take four weeks. He pushes back. They agree. Nothing changes. He brings in a consultant. The recommendation lands in his inbox and quietly disappears. Six months later the cycle time is still five weeks and he has stopped pushing.

He lost, not because he lacked authority — he never lost that — but because he encountered a *power dependency*. The system he was formally in charge of depended on people and resources he did not actually control. The background-check vendor was not reporting to him. The recruiting team's compliance depended not on fear of punishment but on whether they believed they could comply. They didn't. He had authority. He never acquired power. He certainly never built leadership — the voluntary engagement of people in a shared vision.

The tragedy of management is how often this exact pattern repeats, at every organizational level.

## The Five Sources

John French and Bertram Raven, studying influence in the 1950s, identified five distinct bases from which power can come. Each generates a different behavioral response from the person being influenced — and that difference is the most practically important thing in this chapter.

**Referent power** comes from personal appeal — from someone looking up to you. When you do something because you admire the person asking, because you want their approval or because doing it makes you feel more like them, that is referent power at work. It shows up when junior employees imitate senior ones not because they are told to but because they want to. It shows up in the early days of a startup when a founder's vision pulls people to work extreme hours not because the pay demands it but because they genuinely believe in what the founder believes. Referent power generates *commitment* — genuine buy-in, discretionary effort, early warning about problems, suggestions no one asked for. It is the rarest and most valuable.

**Expert power** comes from knowledge or skill that others need. A specialist surgeon has expert power in her operating room. An accountant has expert power during an audit. The boundary is narrow and important: expert power is domain-specific. A brilliant neurosurgeon has expert power in neurosurgery and almost nowhere else. And expert power is durable only as long as the expertise is relevant and scarce. When what you know becomes common knowledge or gets automated, the power evaporates.

**Legitimate power** is the official right to ask for compliance — authority. A supervisor has legitimate power to assign work because the organization has defined that as the supervisor's role. But the limits matter enormously. A supervisor's legitimate power to assign work does not extend to ordering personal errands or requiring illegal acts. Where legitimate power ends, the person must either comply for some other reason or refuse. Legitimate power generates compliance when people see the request as within the legitimate scope of the role. It generates resistance when the request exceeds that scope, even if the person technically cannot prevent the order from being given.

**Reward power** comes from control over things people want — money, promotion, good assignments, recognition, status. The assumption in most org charts is that managers have substantial reward power over their reports. The reality is often more constrained: salary ranges are set by HR, promotions are decided by committees, desirable projects are allocated through processes managers do not fully control. Reward power tends to increase performance when people believe there is a clear and reliable link between their behavior and the reward. When that link is ambiguous or the decisions look arbitrary, reward power weakens considerably even though the formal structure is unchanged.

**Coercive power** comes from the ability to punish — to fire, to demote, to withhold, to humiliate, to exclude. David Kipnis spent his career studying power and noted something disturbing: of all five bases, coercive power is probably the most used and the most difficult to control. The reason is that it works in the short term. Fear is a reliable motivator. But the behavioral response it generates is resistance and resentment, not commitment or willing compliance. People do the minimum required to avoid punishment. They look for ways around the rule. If they can find another job, they leave. If they cannot, they stay and quietly undermine.

<!-- → [TABLE: Five bases of power — columns: power base, source, behavioral response generated (commitment/compliance/resistance), durability, when it fails — rows: referent, expert, legitimate, reward, coercive] -->

The relationship between the base and the response is empirical, not moral. It is built into the psychology of motivation. Commitment requires either genuine agreement or genuine admiration. Compliance requires either a sense of duty or a clear exchange. Resistance is what happens when the only reason to comply is to avoid something bad, and the badness is not large enough or certain enough to override the resentment. Most managers who rely heavily on coercive power eventually discover that their formal power and their actual influence have diverged — they can command compliance but cannot generate commitment, and in knowledge work, compliance without commitment is worth very little.

## How Power Distributes Itself Across Units

The above is about interpersonal power — the relationship between individuals. But power also distributes itself across departments and units, and the pattern of that distribution is equally important and equally counterintuitive.

The intuitive assumption is that larger departments have more power, or older departments, or departments with larger budgets. None of these reliably predicts actual influence. Power follows a different logic entirely.

Consider what happened to electric utilities in America. In the 1960s and 1970s, the central strategic challenge was technical: could you design and build the next power plant? Could you keep the grid stable? Senior leadership was full of engineers. The engineering department had power because it controlled the capability that determined whether the company survived.

By the 1990s, utilities faced a different reality: litigation, environmental lawsuits, nuclear-safety challenges, regulatory battles, climate policy. The question was no longer "can we build it" but "can we defend ourselves when we build it." Senior leadership became full of lawyers. Legal gained power. Engineering's power diminished — not because engineers got worse at their jobs, but because the strategic challenge that determined organizational survival had shifted, and engineering was no longer the department that could manage it.

This pattern is not an accident or an anomaly. It is the operating logic of organizational power. Two complementary models explain it.

**The resource dependence model** starts from a simple observation: if your department controls resources that other departments need, and those resources are scarce and valued, you have power over those departments. A purchasing department has power over production because it controls the flow of materials. A finance department has power over capital projects because it controls capital. Salancik and Pfeffer studied university budget decisions and found that departments with more external grant funding received larger budget allocations, controlling for department size. The departments that brought in money the university valued were rewarded with resources, which they then used to bring in more money, which produced more resources. Power begets power when the mechanism is resource control.

**The strategic contingencies model** adds precision. It is not only what resources you control but whether controlling those resources helps others manage uncertainty. A contingency is a requirement for one unit's work that depends on another's. A *strategic* contingency is when one unit's work reduces the unpredictability in another's.

Consider a manufacturing firm. The production line has inherent uncertainty: will the supplier deliver on time? Will machinery break down? Will demand suddenly surge? The purchasing department reduces some of that uncertainty by guaranteeing material supply. The maintenance department reduces it by keeping equipment running. Both accumulate power because they *cope with the uncertainty that would otherwise disrupt production*. That is the strategic contingency model: power accrues to whoever helps you manage what you cannot predict.

Three specific factors determine how much power a unit has under this model. The first is the *ability to reduce uncertainty*: if your group can forecast, prevent, or absorb disruptions that would otherwise hit another group, you have power. The second is *nonsubstitutability*: if other units can get what you provide from someone else, your power weakens; if you are the only source, it strengthens. Apple's power in personal computing partly comes from the fact that if you want an iPad ecosystem, you can only get it from Apple. IBM's mainframe power collapsed when IBM-compatible PCs arrived — because suddenly people had alternatives. The third is *centrality*: how interconnected is your unit's work with everyone else's? IT infrastructure has enormous power in modern organizations not because technologists are especially impressive but because almost everything runs on technology now. Centrality is power.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: Strategic contingencies model — show three factors (uncertainty reduction, nonsubstitutability, centrality) feeding into unit power, with the utility example annotated to show how each factor shifted between the 1970s and 1990s as the strategic challenge changed from technical to regulatory] -->

The practical implication is that power is not stable. When your organization's strategic challenges shift — when the environment changes what capabilities are essential — power redistributes. Departments that were central become peripheral. New departments gain influence. This creates political conflict because people in newly peripheral departments fight to maintain relevance and resources even when the organization needs to shift investment elsewhere. The most contentious moments in organizational life are usually the ones where power is redistributing — where the organization needs one department to grow and another to shrink, but the shrinking department still has enough residual power to resist.

## Political Behavior: When It Emerges and What It Actually Is

Power shapes individual relationships and distributes itself across departments. The interplay between these two levels, under specific structural conditions, produces what we call organizational politics.

Politics — defined carefully by Pfeffer as "activities taken within organizations to acquire, develop, and use power and other resources to obtain one's preferred outcomes in situations of uncertainty or dissensus" — is not a departure from normal organizational life. It is normal organizational life, under the right conditions.

Political behavior intensifies reliably under five conditions. **Ambiguous goals** create room for people to pursue their own agendas under cover of pursuing organizational ones. When the goal is clear, it is harder to argue that your preferred approach serves it. When goals are genuinely ambiguous — grow or profit? market share or customer satisfaction? innovation or efficiency? — incompatible objectives can coexist under the same banner. **Scarce resources** mean that someone wins and someone loses, and rational actors fight for their side. **Nonprogrammed decisions** — the non-routine, high-stakes choices that policy cannot govern — leave space for advocacy, coalition-building, and agenda manipulation that routine decisions do not. **Organizational change** makes normally hidden power structures temporarily visible and malleable; people compete to secure position in the new arrangement. **Uncertain environments** mean that different units can credibly argue for different strategies, each claiming to be what the turbulent moment demands.

Consider what this looks like in practice. A software company growing from fifty to two hundred people holds a quarterly resource review. The sales VP needs three account executives to close the pipeline. The product VP needs two product managers to handle feature complexity. The engineering VP needs to pay down technical debt and hire two senior architects. The CEO allocates: sales gets two, product gets one, engineering gets one architect plus a reallocation that slows feature development. Everyone walks out frustrated to varying degrees.

What just happened? The CEO did not have perfect information about what each department actually needs. The decision was not made on pure merit — if it were, each argument would have been evaluated against the same standard. Instead, the CEO was balancing the influence of three departments, the relative strength of each relationship, confidence in each leader, and the perceived political cost of disappointing each one. That is organizational politics. It is not pathological. It is how allocation decisions actually get made when rational criteria cannot fully settle the question.

<!-- → [TABLE: Five conditions that intensify political behavior — columns: condition, mechanism, example, what reduces it — rows: ambiguous goals, scarce resources, nonprogrammed decisions, organizational change, uncertain environment] -->

The tools of political behavior are worth naming because understanding them is the first step toward using them appropriately and recognizing when others are using them on you.

**Controlling access to information** is the most fundamental. Most decisions depend on information. If you shape what information people see, you shape what they decide. Information asymmetry is a structural feature of organizations — HR and senior management know what everyone earns, which gives them power in salary discussions that others cannot easily counterbalance.

**Controlling the agenda** is simpler and remarkably effective. If an unwanted proposal never comes to a vote, it never loses. Ordering agenda items so the controversial one is last — when people are tired and want to leave — is a classic tactic. If that fails, raise technical objections to delay the vote until next meeting. Repeat until the proposal dies of neglect.

**Selective use of objective criteria** is almost invisible when used well. Few decisions have one objectively correct answer. The criteria by which you evaluate options determine the outcome before the evaluation begins. In faculty hiring debates, one group emphasizes teaching excellence and writes criteria based on teaching; another emphasizes research and writes criteria based on research quality. Both claim to be selecting the best candidate. What they are really doing is selecting the criteria that favor their preferred candidate.

**Coalitions** multiply individual power. One unit cannot always move the organization alone. If several units with overlapping interests band together, they have collective leverage that none would have independently. Coalition-building is how most significant organizational decisions actually get made — not through the formal authority of a single actor but through the assembled influence of aligned interests.

**Bureaucratic gamesmanship** uses the organization's own rules as weapons. Most organizations have conflicting rules. Following every policy to the letter often grinds things to a halt. A group wanting to slow or stop a decision can create procedural friction until the decision either dies or is renegotiated.

None of these tactics is inherently ethical or unethical. Each is a tool. Whether it corrodes or serves the organization depends on the ends being pursued. A manager who uses information transparency to ensure all decision-makers have the facts is using information control constructively. A manager who withholds information to prevent a decision that would benefit the organization but harm their department is using it destructively. The same tactic, different orientation, different organizational outcome.

## What Managers Can Actually Do With This

The chapter opened with a hallway conversation. Two leaders, in a setting where formal authority was absent, making the decision that the formal process had not produced. That conversation was not a failure of organizational governance. It was the organization doing what organizations do — using informal power to navigate a situation where formal authority was insufficient.

Understanding this, rather than pretending it does not happen, is the starting point for managing effectively.

**Know that the org chart is not the influence map.** Some of the most powerful people in your organization have modest titles. Some people with impressive titles have little actual influence. Understanding which bases of power are operative in your specific context — who has expert power over what, who has built referent power with whom, whose reward and coercive power is actually credible — is not cynicism. It is clarity. The alternative is being repeatedly surprised.

**Know your own bases of power and be honest about what they will and will not move.** Expert power is useful and durable but narrow. Legitimate power generates compliance but not commitment. Referent power generates commitment but must be earned through behavior over time and cannot be faked. Coercive power works in the short term and costs you in the long term, every time. If you are relying primarily on coercive power or on legitimate authority, you are operating with the bases that produce the weakest behavioral responses.

**Manage the structural conditions that generate destructive politics.** Goals that are genuinely clear reduce the space for competing agendas. Resource allocation processes that are transparent and tied to organizational priorities reduce the political returns to information manipulation and coalition-building for personal gain. Decision rights that are explicit — who can decide what, with what input from whom — reduce the ambiguity that political behavior rushes to fill. The budget process that produces horse-trading rather than prioritization is usually the one with the most ambiguity about what the organization actually values. Make the values concrete, make the criteria explicit, make the process visible, and the political returns to gaming it shrink.

**Be honest when decisions are political rather than pretending they are purely rational.** "We are going to do X because it aligns with our strategic priority, even though the data does not definitively settle it" produces more organizational trust than "the data shows we should do X" when the data does not actually show that. People know when a decision was political. Claiming rationality for a political decision does not make it look more rational — it makes the decision-maker look less trustworthy.

The hallway conversation at the opening of this chapter was not a scandal. It was how organizations navigate situations where formal authority is insufficient and the people involved have the power, the relationship, and the judgment to move. What would have made it a scandal is if the decision had served the two VPs at the expense of the organization, rather than serving the organization through an informal channel that the formal process had failed to provide.

Power and politics cannot be eliminated from organizations, and the attempt to eliminate them usually produces organizations that cannot adapt — where nobody challenges the status quo because the political cost of doing so is too high. The goal is not eliminating politics. The goal is pointing it toward legitimate ends.

That requires understanding what it actually is.
