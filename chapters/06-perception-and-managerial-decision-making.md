# Chapter 6 — Perception and Managerial Decision Making

**TL;DR:** Managers rarely make decisions the way textbooks say they should. Your brain runs on two systems — fast and slow — and the slow one uses more fuel than you have to spend. What makes managers effective is not perfect rationality but deliberate structures that catch the errors your wiring will make automatically.

---

## Chapter opening — The Coin Flip Nobody Takes

It is 1980 and Daniel Kahneman, a psychologist who will win a Nobel Prize without ever publishing an experimental paper (that part of the story is odd, but true), is sitting across from a group of investment professionals. He describes a scenario. A company is facing two options for a new product line. Option A will generate a guaranteed profit of $240,000. Option B is a coin flip: heads, they make $500,000; tails, they make zero.

The math is not close. The expected value of the coin flip is $250,000. That is $10,000 more than the sure thing. This is not a close call. A rational actor — the kind of actor that appears in economics textbooks — takes the bet.

Nobody takes the bet.

This is not naive risk-aversion. Kahneman runs the scenario again, reversed. Now both options are losses. Option A means a guaranteed loss of $240,000. Option B is a coin flip: heads, they lose $500,000; tails, they lose nothing. The expected loss is still $250,000, which is worse than the sure thing.

The investors take the coin flip. They reject the sure loss and gamble.

This is the moment Kahneman and his collaborator Amos Tversky realized that the human brain is not a calculator that got better at math over time. It is a machine that trades away accuracy to make decisions fast. It feels loss more sharply than gain — which made sense in the savanna, where losing what you have is more dangerous than missing what you might get — and that feeling has been baked into your neurology so deep that knowing the math does not override it.

This discovery cracked open how managers actually make decisions. Because if the people making the biggest decisions in the largest organizations are using the same equipment you are using when you grab a cookie instead of an apple, then understanding how that equipment works is not optional. It is the entire job.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- **Identify** the two systems your brain uses to make decisions and **diagnose** when each is appropriate.
- **Distinguish** between programmed and nonprogrammed decisions and **apply** the correct decision-making structure to each.
- **Name** the seven major barriers to effective decision-making (bounded rationality, escalation of commitment, time pressure, uncertainty, confirmation bias, relationship conflict, and the "similar to me" bias) and **build defenses** against each.
- **Execute** the six-step decision-making process for high-stakes decisions.
- **Recognize** when your own emotions are signals worth listening to, and when they are static that drowns out the data.
- **Conduct** group decision-making in a way that captures the benefits of diverse perspectives without slipping into groupthink.

## Prerequisites

Comfort with the idea that your intuitions are not always correct, even when they feel certain. A willingness to be skeptical of your own judgment. Familiarity with the basics of probability (the idea that something can have a 50% chance) helps but is not required.

## Why this chapter matters

Every decision you make as a manager ripples. A first-line supervisor's scheduling error costs overtime and frustrates employees. A mid-level manager's choice about which technology to adopt might lock the organization into five years of maintenance costs. A CEO's bet on a new market can save the company or sink it. The quality of managerial decisions compounds — good ones build confidence and momentum; bad ones accumulate drag.

The difference between a manager who makes good decisions and one who doesn't is rarely that the good one has more information. It is that the good one has built systems that compensate for how your brain actually works, not how you wish it worked.

## Concept 1 — Two systems, one brain: The fast track and the careful lane

You are in the produce section and you reach for an apple. You don't conduct a cost-benefit analysis. You see something red, you recognize apple, you grab it. That is System 1. Fast. Automatic. Effortless. It runs all the time, underneath your awareness.

You are told that the apple costs $4.99 and you need to decide whether it is worth it. Now you think. You compare it to the price you paid last week at a different store. You consider whether you need an apple or whether a banana would do as well. You deliberate. That is System 2. Slow. Deliberate. It takes fuel — metabolic fuel, in the form of glucose in your brain. System 2 is how you do algebra. It is how you write an email when you want to persuade someone. It is how you make decisions that matter.

The brain does not run both systems at once. You have to choose. And because System 2 is expensive, your brain prefers to let System 1 run things. System 1 makes millions of small choices for you every day — which foot to put forward when you walk, whether a sound is worth paying attention to, whether the person in the room seems trustworthy or suspicious. Most of the time this works fine. System 1 has been shaped by experience and evolution to get things right most of the time.

But most of the time is not all the time. And a decision that goes wrong at the managerial level does not go wrong in isolation. It goes wrong *repeatedly*, because the decision shapes how the organization moves.

### System 1 — The reactive brain

When you feel fear, you run. You don't think. Your amygdala — a part of your brain involved in emotional response — activates before the parts of your brain that do deliberation have time to weigh options. This is a feature, not a bug. In a situation where something is actually trying to eat you, thinking is a good way to become lunch.

Experienced managers often use System 1 effectively without realizing it. A veteran software engineer walks into a code review and sees an off-by-one error in three seconds — not because they are counting the code carefully, but because their pattern-recognition machinery has run enough code reviews that the error jumps out. An experienced finance director hears a deal structured in a particular way and says "no" before consciously articulating why — because they have seen that structure fail before. Expert intuition is System 1 running on a track worn smooth by years of feedback-rich experience.

The key phrase is "feedback-rich experience." Your brain learns when you make a decision and then *see the consequences*. If you are a tennis player, you see the ball go in or miss the court hundreds of times a week. Your System 1 calibrates to this feedback loop. If you are a CEO making a major acquisition, you might make five big decisions in your entire career. Your System 1 does not get the feedback it needs to calibrate.

### System 2 — The reflective brain

When the stakes are high and the situation is novel, System 1 is not enough. A company deciding whether to adopt a new technology that will require six months of disruption and millions of dollars in retraining cannot rely on gut feeling. You need to think — deliberately, methodically, with attention to the evidence.

This is what System 2 does. It is housed primarily in your prefrontal cortex, the newest part of your brain evolutionarily. It has access to language. It can follow a logical argument. It can weigh competing claims and calculate expected values. It is the engine of reason.

It is also exhausting. When your glucose levels drop, your System 2 performance falls off. When you are angry or afraid, your System 2 performance falls off — the emotional activation pulls you toward System 1, toward reactivity. When you have been making decisions all day, your System 2 has less fuel left.

This is why experienced managers make their most important decisions early in the day, or first thing after a break. It is why they know that meetings scheduled at the end of Friday afternoon tend to produce worse decisions than meetings at 9 a.m. on Tuesday. It is not that people suddenly become stupid. It is that System 2 — the careful, deliberative system — has run out of fuel and System 1 has to take over.

### Emotions are data, not noise

A common misconception is that good decision-making means removing emotions and running pure logic. The evidence does not support this. Emotions are your System 1 running on experience. When you walk into a meeting with a potential business partner and feel something is off — that the person is being evasive or the numbers don't quite add up — your emotional system is detecting patterns your conscious mind has not yet articulated. If you ignore that signal, you often regret it.

The distinction is this: emotions *are* data, but they are not *all* the data. A manager who feels anxious about a decision and then digs deeper to understand *why* they feel anxious has just extracted useful information from System 1. A manager who feels anxious and therefore makes a decision in the opposite direction, without asking whether the anxiety is based on real pattern recognition or just on how hungry they are, has made the mistake.

Effective managers toggle between System 1 and System 2. They notice their emotions — what Psychologist Daniel Goleman calls *emotional intelligence* — they ask themselves whether the emotion is a signal or static, and they use that information to decide whether to trust their gut or to slow down and think carefully.

### Common misconception: "Trusting your gut is good decision-making"

This one is so deeply embedded in startup culture and leadership folklore that it deserves its own moment. You have heard it a hundred times: the best decisions come from intuition, from knowing without knowing, from trusting yourself. There is a reason this myth persists. The people who say it loudest are often the ones who have made good decisions by intuition, and they have become successful, and therefore we listen to them.

But here is the catch: the successful gut-truster has *usually* had feedback-rich experience in the domain where they are trusting their gut. An experienced venture capitalist who has sat on the boards of thirty startups has seen what founders succeed and which ones stumble. Their intuition about a pitch is calibrated to reality. But a venture capitalist with three deals under their belt? Their intuition is calibrated to nothing. They just feel confident. And confidence is not correlated with accuracy.

The research on expert intuition, done primarily by psychologist Gary Klein, shows that intuition is reliable *only* in environments with regular, unambiguous feedback about the consequences of your decisions. In medicine, chess, fire-fighting — places where you make a decision and then see whether it worked — intuition can be trustworthy. In fields like financial forecasting, long-term strategic planning, or hiring — where you might never find out whether you made the right call — intuition is just as fallible as anyone's.

The safest assumption is this: if you have not made thousands of similar decisions and seen the consequences, your gut is not an instrument. It is a beginning of an inquiry. Ask yourself what your gut is reacting to. Look for evidence. Then decide.

### Worked example — choosing a supplier

You are a manufacturing manager. Two suppliers have bid on a contract. Supplier A is the one you have used for three years. They are reliable, familiar, and they know your processes. The bid is $50,000 above Supplier B's bid. Supplier B is new to you, their facility is three thousand miles away, and the bid comes in much lower.

Your System 1 says: use Supplier A. I know them. They are safe. I do not have to learn something new.

Your System 2 says: the $50,000 difference is significant. It might be that Supplier B has a cost advantage that is sustainable, or it might be that they are bidding low to get the contract and will raise prices once you are locked in.

Here is what you should actually do. You say to yourself: "I am feeling pull toward Supplier A because I know them. That is System 1 using the 'similar to me / familiar is safe' heuristic. That might be the right call, but I cannot trust my familiarity feeling as evidence. Let me spend System 2 time on this." Then you do the work. You visit Supplier B's facility if you can. You call references. You talk to their other customers. You build a cost model that shows what happens if Supplier B's prices rise by 10% or 20% after year one. You talk to colleagues about what has happened when they switched suppliers. You examine the contract for exit clauses and penalty structures.

After all that, you might still choose Supplier A. The work might show that the switching risk is not worth the savings. But at least you are choosing with your eyes open, not just following the feeling.

### Implications for managers

This matters most in three settings.

**First, when you are hiring.** You meet a candidate and they remind you of yourself — same alma mater, same background, similar communication style. System 1 says: they are good. But research by organizational psychologist Sheila Sittenfeld shows that the "similar to me" bias leads to homogeneous teams that are less innovative and less likely to catch each other's errors. When you feel that pull, slow down. Hire diverse.

**Second, when you are stuck in an escalation.** You have committed to a course of action and new evidence suggests you were wrong. Admitting it feels like losing, so you double down. Your System 1 does not want to feel foolish. Your System 2 — if you give it the fuel and the time — will see that the sunk cost is gone, it cannot be recovered, and the only question is what to do going forward. This particular error costs organizations billions. We will come back to it in the next section.

**Third, when you are tired or hungry.** Your System 2 performance is degraded. Do not make big decisions. Schedule the hard choices for when you have fuel.

## Concept 2 — Bounded rationality and the structures that work anyway

In 1947, Herbert Simon published a book that changed how we think about decision-making. Simon was observing actual managers, not hypothetical rational actors. He noticed that managers almost never gathered complete information. They did not consider every possible option. They did not calculate the mathematical optimum. They did this not because they were lazy or irrational, but because doing so would be *impossible*.

The amount of information in the world is infinite. The number of possible decisions is infinite. Human minds are finite. You cannot compare every option. You cannot process all the information. So what do you do instead?

You use *satisficing* — one of Simon's best contributions to our vocabulary. You do not hunt for the *best* option. You hunt for an option that is *good enough*, given the time and information you have. You satisfice.

This is not a second-class way to decide. It is a recognition of reality. A manufacturing manager does not spend six months analyzing suppliers to find the absolute mathematically optimal choice. They gather enough information to find a supplier that will work well, meets the criteria, and can deliver on time. They order from that supplier and move on to the next problem. That is not a failure of rationality. That is rationality applied to a world where analysis has a cost.

Satisficing works because it is not actually the enemy of good decisions. The difference between a decision that is 85% optimal and one that is 95% optimal is usually much smaller than the cost of the analysis it would take to find the 95% solution. You get more organizational impact by making seventy 85%-optimal decisions than by making five 99%-optimal decisions.

But satisficing only works in one condition: the option you choose has to be actually good enough. It has to work. And here is where things get subtle. Your brain has developed a set of shortcuts — heuristics — that usually help you satisfice successfully. But in some situations, these same shortcuts lead you into traps.

### Heuristics: The mental shortcuts that usually work

When you see a person in a white coat in a hospital, you assume they are a doctor. This assumption is usually right, and it saves you from having to ask every time. When you hear a name and guess the person's gender, you are using a heuristic based on patterns. When you meet someone at a conference and they seem knowledgeable and confident, you trust what they say more than you would if they seemed uncertain — even if the uncertainty is actually a mark of intellectual honesty.

These shortcuts are heuristics. They are fast. They are efficient. They usually work. And they fail spectacularly in the situations where the pattern is misleading.

A classic study had participants read a description of a person: Steve is introverted and solitary, interested in philosophy. The study then asked, "Is Steve more likely to be a librarian or a farmer?" Most people said librarian, because Steve's description matches the stereotype of a librarian. But there are far more farmers in the United States than librarians. By pure base-rate math, Steve is more likely to be a farmer, because farmers outnumber librarians twenty to one. The people answering the question relied on how well Steve matched the stereotype (representativeness heuristic), not on how many farmers and librarians there actually are. They answered correctly by accident.

In management, heuristics become dangerous when the pattern is misleading. You promote the person who is most similar to your successful leaders, without noticing that you are now less diverse. You invest in the technology that has always worked, not noticing that the market has shifted. You trust the salesperson who is most confident, not noticing that confidence and competence are different things.

### Bounded rationality as a hard limit

Here is the core insight: you cannot make a perfectly rational decision because you do not have access to perfect information, you do not have unlimited time to analyze, and your brain cannot hold all possible options in mind at once. This is not something you can train away. This is not something that smarter people can overcome. This is a feature of how humans work.

Herbert Simon called this *bounded rationality* — the recognition that you are bounded by the amount of information you can access, the time you have to decide, and the processing power you have available.

This matters because it means the goal of decision-making is not perfection. The goal is to make good decisions *given your bounds*. And the way you do that is to build structures that help you work within those bounds.

Here is what this looks like in practice. You are a manager facing a nonprogrammed decision — something new and complex, not a routine choice. You do not try to gather all the information in the world. You gather the *relevant* information. You do not consider infinite options. You generate *several* good options and compare them. You do not try to mathematically prove which is best. You use a *structured process* that accounts for common errors.

The six-step decision-making process, which we will examine in Concept 3, is exactly this kind of structure. It does not guarantee a perfect decision. It makes a good decision much more likely by walking you through the steps where errors usually happen.

### Common misconception: "Perfect information would solve this"

If managers just had more data, the thinking goes, they could make better decisions. This is true to a point, and then it inverts. There is a sweet spot where more information improves your decision. Beyond that point, more information is just noise. You are not smarter about the decision. You are more confident about the wrong thing.

The solution is not more information. The solution is *the right information*, and the discipline to stop gathering and start deciding. There is a cost to waiting for perfect information. You can miss the market window. You can miss the opportunity. You can watch a problem grow because you were still analyzing.

Effective managers know when to stop gathering and start acting. They have a gut sense — a System 1 sense — for when additional analysis will change the decision and when it will just make them more confused. That gut sense is calibrated through experience and reflection. When you find yourself thinking "I need just one more report," ask: would this report actually change what I am going to do? If the answer is no, you have all the information you need.

### Worked example — the decision to hire or promote from outside

You need a new department head. You have an internal candidate who is competent, familiar to the team, and would probably do fine. You also have an external candidate who has impressive credentials and comes from a competitor.

If you wait for perfect information, you will never decide. You cannot know for certain how either person will perform. You cannot predict whether the internal candidate would have grown into the role over time, or whether the external candidate will suffer from culture shock. You could keep interviewing, checking references, analyzing organizational charts. You will always find more to analyze.

What you do instead is establish the *relevant* information. What skills are genuinely critical for this role? What evidence do you have about each candidate on those dimensions? Are there any warnings signs? Does one candidate have experience specifically with the challenges this department is facing? Then you decide. You do not wait for certainty. You decide on the best available evidence and you move on.

## Concept 3 — The seven barriers and how to defend against them

Decision-making goes wrong in predictable ways. If you can learn to recognize these patterns — in yourself, in your organization, in moments of high stress — you can build defenses. You do not need to be smarter than the pattern. You just need to be prepared for it.

### Barrier 1: Confirmation bias and the echo chamber problem

You believe something. Then you notice all the evidence that supports your belief. You do not notice — or you actively discount — the evidence against it.

A manager decides that a particular employee has potential. When that employee does good work, the manager notices it. When the employee fails, the manager attributes it to circumstances outside the employee's control. The opposite is also true: a manager who has decided an employee is weak will dismiss strong performance as luck or help from others. This is confirmation bias. Your brain is preferring information that confirms what you already believe.

This is not stupidity. It is how your pattern-recognition system works. Your brain organizes the world into categories — "this person is competent" or "this approach will fail" — and then it filters information to match those categories. It does this automatically, before you are aware of it.

The defense is structural, not willpower. You cannot just decide to stop having confirmation bias. It is too deep in your neurology. What you *can* do is build a process that forces you to look for disconfirming evidence.

**Structured approach:** When you are making a big decision about something you care about, deliberately ask yourself: What would have to be true for me to be wrong about this? Then go *look* for that evidence. Do not sit back and wait for it to come to you. Actively search. Call the people who disagree. Read the analyst reports that say the opposite of what you believe. Ask your critics, "What am I missing?" This is not comfortable. It feels like you are looking for reasons to doubt yourself. But it is the only way to compensate for confirmation bias.

### Barrier 2: The "similar to me" bias and homogeneity

Humans prefer people who are similar to them. This is not malice. It is a pattern-recognition shortcut your brain uses. Similar patterns feel safe. Different patterns feel suspicious.

In hiring, this shows up as managers hiring people who look like them, who went to the same schools, who have the same backgrounds. In promotion, it shows up as fast-tracking people who remind you of yourself. In leadership meetings, it shows up as listening more carefully to people who communicate the way you do.

This has two costs. First, homogeneous teams are less innovative. They miss patterns that diverse teams catch. Second, homogeneous organizations have less capacity to learn about their own blind spots. If everyone thinks like you, no one can tell you what you are missing.

**Structural approach:** Form hiring committees with deliberate diversity. Make sure the committee includes people with different backgrounds, different ages, different genders, different career paths. Require that the hiring decision involve multiple people who bring different perspectives. Impose a formal check: if the person you are about to hire is very similar to you on multiple dimensions (same alma mater, same career path, same gender), explicitly ask: why is that? Are we making the best hiring decision, or are we following the pattern?

### Barrier 3: Escalation of commitment and the sunk cost fallacy

You have committed to a course of action. You have invested time, money, reputation. New evidence comes in suggesting you were wrong. But admitting it feels like loss. So you double down. You invest more. You ignore warning signs. You keep going because reversing course would mean admitting you were mistaken.

This kills organizations from the inside. A company invests in a technology platform that is not delivering. By year two, there is clear evidence that switching platforms would be cheaper long-term than staying the course. But by year two, so much has been invested — sunk costs — that the leadership cannot bring themselves to switch. They continue investing, and five years later, they are out of business, having poured hundreds of millions into the wrong platform.

The mechanism is clear. Your brain experiences the sunk cost as a loss. Losses feel more painful than gains feel good — that is the loss aversion Kahneman discovered. So you are willing to take worse odds going forward just to avoid admitting the loss. You will risk much more money to try to recover what you have already lost.

The sunk cost fallacy is not a failure of analysis. It is a failure of *emotional* regulation. You feel the weight of the sunk cost and you cannot move past it.

**Defense:** Separate the decision from the emotion. Explicitly tell yourself: the money I have already spent is gone. It cannot be recovered. It is not part of this decision. The only question is: going forward, what is the best use of my resources? This works better if you have a framework. Some organizations use a "premortem" — you imagine it is a year from now and the decision you made turned out to be a disaster. Now work backward to figure out what went wrong. This forces you to confront the possibility of failure before you are emotionally invested in success.

### Barrier 4: Overconfidence and the illusion of control

A study of Swedish drivers found that over 90% of them rated themselves as above-average. This is mathematically impossible, unless Swedish roads are very forgiving. This is overconfidence. Your brain believes you are more capable, more skilled, more accurate than you actually are. And the more confident you are, the less likely you are to check your work.

In business, overconfidence shows up as underestimating how long a project will take, overestimating how much you know about a market, believing you can manage risks that are actually quite substantial.

Overconfidence is particularly dangerous for new managers or people entering a new domain. You do not have enough experience to know what you do not know. So you are maximally confident about things you are maximally wrong about. As you accumulate experience, you often become *less* confident, because you start to see the complexity.

**Defense:** When making a big decision, explicitly estimate the confidence interval. Do not just guess a number. Guess the range where you think the real number is. If you are forecasting how long a project will take and your gut says "four months," force yourself to name the range: "It could take as little as three months if everything goes perfectly, and as much as eight months if X or Y happens." That range captures something your gut estimate was missing — the complexity and variability that overconfidence ignores.

### Barrier 5: Time pressure and decision quality

When time is short, you have no choice but to use System 1 — fast, intuitive decision-making. This is sometimes necessary. But it is also how you make expensive mistakes.

Time pressure pushes you toward the first option that seems acceptable. It pushes you away from deliberation. It pushes you toward the option you know, rather than the one that might be better. It makes you rely on heuristics rather than evidence.

If you have ever watched a team under deadline, you see this in action. The thorough analysis goes out the window. The creative options disappear. You end up using the approach you have always used.

**Defense:** Do not make big decisions under time pressure. This is easier said than done. But it is important enough that some managers build it into their process. If you get a proposal that requires a decision "immediately," your first instinct should be skeptical. Real opportunities usually allow 48 hours of thinking. If the opportunity evaporates in less time, it was probably not that good to begin with.

### Barrier 6: Anchoring and the power of numbers

The first number you hear sticks to your brain. It becomes the reference point for everything you think afterward.

A negotiation study asked judges to set sentences for a crime. Before discussing the case, some judges were told the prosecutor had asked for eight months. Others were told the prosecutor had asked for two years. The judges who heard "eight months" gave average sentences of five months. The judges who heard "two years" gave average sentences of seven months. The actual facts of the case were the same. The difference was the anchor.

In business, anchoring shows up everywhere. A startup raising money names a valuation. Everyone in the room anchors to that number. A vendor quotes a price. You assume that is the real price and any discount feels like a win. A forecaster predicts a market size. Everyone treats that prediction as the baseline, even if the forecaster has no real basis for the estimate.

**Defense:** Be aware of anchors and question them. When you walk into a negotiation, do not let the other side's first offer anchor you. Counter-anchor with your own number, based on your own analysis. When you see a forecast, ask where it came from. Is it based on data or on tradition? When you hear a price, research what the actual market range is.

### Barrier 7: Relationship conflict and the emotional hijack

There are two kinds of conflict in organizations. Process conflict is about how to do something. It can actually improve decision-making. "Should we use approach A or approach B?" is a good conflict to have. People disagree, they surface different options, they make a better decision.

Relationship conflict is personal. It is attacking a person rather than debating an idea. And it breaks decision-making. When someone feels personally attacked, their amygdala activates. They revert to System 1 — emotional reaction. They stop listening to data. They stop thinking. They just fight.

A manager gives feedback to an employee: "Your analysis was incomplete. You missed the impact on customer acquisition cost." That is process feedback about the work. An employee can hear it and think about whether it is true.

The same manager says: "You never think things through. You are always sloppy." That is relationship feedback. It attacks the person. The employee stops listening to whether the analysis was incomplete. They are just defending themselves.

**Defense:** When giving feedback, keep it focused on the behavior and the work, not the person. "This analysis is missing these three elements" is much different from "You are bad at analysis." When you are receiving feedback that feels personal, pause. Ask: is there a real point about my work here, underneath the emotional tone? Separating the content from the delivery takes discipline, but it is worth it.

### Worked example — defending against barriers in a real situation

You are the head of operations. Your company has been using an inventory management system for four years. It costs $500,000 per year to maintain. A consultant proposes a new system that would cost $2 million upfront and $200,000 per year to run.

Your gut reaction is negative. You have four years of sunk costs in the current system. The staff knows it. Training people on a new system is a hassle. The new system is from an outside vendor you do not know.

Here is the decision-making minefield you are walking into:

- **Sunk cost fallacy:** The $500,000 you have already spent is gone. It is not part of this decision.
- **Confirmation bias:** You are probably noticing the problems with switching and ignoring the benefits.
- **Similar-to-me bias:** You trust the vendor you already know, even though they might be overcharging.
- **Overconfidence:** You might believe you understand the true cost of the current system better than you actually do.
- **Anchoring:** The $2 million upfront number anchors you downward. Any cost less than that feels reasonable.

What you should actually do:

1. Separate the decision from the emotional weight of the sunk cost. Say this explicitly: "We have spent $500,000 so far. That does not change whether we should switch. The question is: going forward, which system produces better value?"

2. Deliberately search for evidence that contradicts your initial judgment. Call the new vendor's other clients. Call companies that chose to stay with their current system. Ask both: what surprised you?

3. Build the full economic model. How much time do employees spend working around the limitations of the current system? What is that worth? What are the switching costs? What are the benefits of the new system? Do the full calculation.

4. Talk to diverse perspectives. Do not just listen to the people who know the current system (who have sunk-cost incentives). Talk to the people who will use the new system if you switch.

5. Set a decision deadline. When will you have enough information to decide? Not "when all information is in," but "by what date?" and then stick to it.

After all that, you might decide to stay with the current system. The switching costs might genuinely outweigh the benefits. But at least you are deciding with your eyes open, and you are defending against the predictable ways your brain would lead you astray.

## Concept 4 — The six-step process and when to use it

For programmed decisions — the ones you make repeatedly using known criteria — you can rely on heuristics and mental shortcuts. You schedule the warehouse based on historical patterns. You reorder supplies based on stock levels. You process a customer service complaint using the known playbook. You have developed these scripts because they work.

For nonprogrammed decisions — the ones that are novel, complex, with unclear criteria and uncertain outcomes — you cannot rely on scripts. A decision to merge with another company. A decision to expand into a new market. A decision about whether to fire a senior leader. These decisions do not have a playbook.

This is where the six-step process comes in. It is a structured approach designed to work around the barriers we discussed. It does not guarantee a good decision. But it makes a good decision more likely.

### Step 1: Recognize that a decision needs to be made

This sounds obvious. It is not. Managers often avoid decisions because they are not sure what to do. Or they hope the problem will solve itself. Or they do not want to disappoint someone.

The defense: have a low threshold for escalation. If you notice a problem and you are not sure what to do about it, bring it to someone who has more authority. Do not let problems sit. Small problems grow into big problems, and by the time you finally address them, they have done damage.

### Step 2: Generate multiple alternatives

Managers often spend about thirty seconds on this step. They think of two options and then move on. But the quality of your final decision is limited by the quality of your options. If you only consider bad options, you will choose a bad option.

The defense: force yourself to generate at least four or five alternatives. If you cannot come up with more than two, you have not thought hard enough. Talk to other people. What would a customer do? What would a competitor do? What is the option nobody is talking about? What would you do if money were not a constraint? Generate options first; evaluate them later.

### Step 3: Analyze the alternatives

This is where you do the work. You gather evidence on each option. You consider the costs and benefits. You think about second-order effects. What happens if you choose option A? What problems does that create? How would you solve those problems?

You also think about your biases. For each option, what would be the best-case scenario? The worst-case scenario? Are you being overconfident about one option? Underconfident about another?

And you consider the ethical implications. This matters. A lot of corporate disasters could have been prevented if someone had slowed down at this step and asked: if this decision blows up, will I be okay with what I did? Will the organization be okay?

### Step 4: Select an alternative

Once you have analyzed your options, you have to choose. This is often harder than it seems, because you do not have perfect information about which option is truly best. Some of your analyses will be wrong. Some of the unknowns will remain unknown.

Effective managers recognize that perfection is impossible. They look at the evidence they have and they make a call. Sometimes they will be wrong. That is okay. What is not okay is never deciding because you are waiting for certainty.

One tactic: write down the reasoning. "I am choosing option B because [three reasons]." If you do this, and the decision turns out badly, you can go back and see whether your reasoning was flawed or whether you were just unlucky. This helps you calibrate your decision-making process over time.

### Step 5: Implement the decision

Deciding is not the same as doing. You have to actually execute. And implementation often reveals things that the analysis missed. You might need to modify the decision as you learn more.

This is where many organizations stumble. The leadership team makes a decision, but the people who have to live with it do not understand it or do not believe in it. They do not implement with conviction.

The defense: involve the people who will implement in the analysis phase. Do not wait until the decision is made to tell them. When people have input into the decision, they are more likely to support it.

### Step 6: Evaluate the effectiveness

Many managers skip this step. They make a decision, implement it, and then move on to the next problem. They never ask: did that work? How do I know? What would I do differently next time?

This is how you build decision-making skill. You only improve if you close the feedback loop.

## Concept 5 — Group decision-making and the dangers of consensus

Decisions made by groups can be better than decisions made by individuals, but only if the group dynamic is right. A diverse group that openly discusses trade-offs and honestly exposes disagreement will usually make a better decision than an individual. A group that is trying to be harmonious and reaches agreement quickly will usually make a worse decision than an individual.

### The benefits of group decision-making

When you have multiple people in the room, you have multiple perspectives. Perspective A sees the market opportunity. Perspective B sees the execution risk. Perspective C sees the regulatory constraint. All three perspectives are true. An individual decision-maker might miss one or all of them.

A group also generates more options. Four people in a room will come up with more creative solutions than one person thinking alone. That is not because the group is smarter. It is because the group has more experience, more knowledge, more patterns to draw from.

A group also commits more to a decision if they have been part of making it. If the CEO makes a decision and announces it, people might follow it, but they will not be enthusiastic. If a cross-functional team helped design the decision, they will fight for it, because they are invested in it.

### The dangers: Groupthink and suppression of dissent

A classic study by social psychologist Irving Janis examined high-stakes government decisions. The Bay of Pigs invasion. The escalation in Vietnam. The decision to go forward with the Space Shuttle Challenger launch despite engineering concerns. In each case, a group of intelligent, well-intentioned people made a terrible decision.

The pattern was the same each time. The group was very cohesive. They had been together for a long time. They had a strong leader. And they fell into *groupthink* — a state where the group prioritizes agreement over accuracy. Nobody wants to rock the boat. Nobody wants to damage the friendly feeling of the group. So when someone has doubts, they keep quiet.

This is often masked by agreement. Everyone says yes. The group feels unified. But the unity is superficial. People are holding back their true concerns to preserve harmony.

The other danger is *suppression of dissent* — when a powerful person actively prevents others from speaking up. A manager says, "Here is what I think we should do," and the room goes quiet because nobody is willing to contradict the boss. The boss walks out thinking they have built consensus. In fact, they have just prevented anyone from telling them they are wrong.

### How to create the conditions for good group decision-making

**Build in diversity.** The group needs to have different perspectives, different experiences, different backgrounds. If everyone is the same, you just get groupthink with more people.

**Explicitly invite dissent.** Say this out loud: "I want to hear disagreement. I want to know what you think might go wrong. Do not just agree with me." And then you have to prove you mean it by listening seriously to the objections and not retaliating against people who raise concerns.

**Assign a devil's advocate.** Someone takes on the role of critic. Their job is to poke holes in the emerging consensus. To challenge the group's assumptions. This is not their real job. It is a temporary role. But it creates a safe space for disagreement because it is explicitly sanctioned.

**Focus on the goal.** When conflict starts to become personal, redirect to the shared goal. "We are all trying to build a successful product. We disagree on the path, but we agree on the destination. Let's focus there."

**Make it safe to speak up about evidence.** A manager says, "I have evidence that contradicts what we are leaning toward. Can I share it?" The group should say yes. Every time. Without exception. This requires a culture shift. Many organizations punish the person who brings bad news. If you want good group decision-making, you have to reward the person who brings bad news.

## Integration — Bringing it together

The rational decision model exists in economics textbooks. It assumes you have perfect information, unlimited time, and complete rationality. It assumes decisions are made by individuals, not groups. In this model, there is a single right answer, and the job of the decision-maker is to find it.

The real world is not the rational model. You have bounded information, time pressure, emotional attachments, cognitive biases, and group dynamics. You are making decisions in the midst of uncertainty.

But you are not helpless. You cannot overcome the bounds of your rationality. But you can recognize where your thinking is most likely to go wrong and build systems to catch it.

You use System 1 — fast intuitive judgment — when you have feedback-rich experience and the stakes are moderate. You switch to System 2 — deliberate analysis — when the stakes are high and the situation is novel. You generate multiple alternatives instead of accepting the first option. You deliberately search for evidence that contradicts your beliefs. You make group decisions with actual diversity and actual permission to disagree.

None of these are complicated. The complication is doing them consistently, especially under pressure. But that is where the payoff is. Consistent application of simple decision-making structures produces better outcomes than trying to be brilliant in the moment.

## Chapter summary

Managers make decisions within bounds. They do not have perfect information or unlimited time. They have two mental systems — fast and slow — and the pressure of real decisions pushes them toward the fast system too often.

The brain makes predictable errors: confirmation bias, escalation of commitment, overconfidence, anchoring, the "similar to me" bias, relationship conflict, and time pressure all distort judgment. None of these can be overcome by willpower. They require structures: diverse groups that are genuinely permitted to disagree, processes that force deliberation on big decisions, defenses against sunk-cost thinking, and habits of gathering disconfirming evidence.

Effective managers know their own biases. They have developed decision-making processes they use consistently. They build teams with different perspectives. They close the feedback loop by actually evaluating whether their decisions worked. They do not try to be right every time. They try to be right more often than they are wrong, and then they learn from the times they are wrong.

The research on decision-making is clear: process matters more than intelligence. A process that is imperfect but consistent will outperform intelligent ad-hoc judgment. This is not a glamorous conclusion. But it is the one that holds across decades of research.
