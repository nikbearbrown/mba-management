# Chapter 3 — Perception and Job Attitudes
*The machinery that decides what you see, and what gets decided about you.*

---

Here is something that should bother you.

Two managers watch the same employee give the same presentation. One walks out thinking: *she's a builder, strategic, ready for the next level.* The other walks out thinking: *she's detail-oriented, careful, a good executor.* Neither manager is lying. Neither is particularly biased by the usual suspects — they would both pass a bias workshop quiz without breaking a sweat. They saw the same thirty minutes. They just didn't see the same thing.

How does that happen? And once it happens, what does it do?

This chapter is about that question. The perceptual machinery underneath the answer turns out to be the same machinery that determines attribution — who gets credit, who gets blamed, and why — and it connects, in ways that aren't obvious at first, to whether people feel satisfied with their jobs, whether they stay or leave, and whether they bring their full effort to work or quietly withhold it. These topics look separate in a textbook table of contents. They're not. They're stages in a single loop. I'll show you the loop.

---

## The construction problem

Start with the simplest version of the question: why does perception vary?

The naive answer is that people see different things because they have different information. Fix the information problem — put both managers in the room at the same time — and you fix the perception problem. This answer is wrong, and understanding why it's wrong is where things get interesting.

Perception is not a recording. Your brain receives a torrent of signals — light, sound, pressure, temperature, the cadence of someone's voice, the microexpression that flashed before they said "yes" — and it cannot possibly process all of it. It has to choose. It selects. It organizes the selected signals into categories. It interprets those categories. By the time perception becomes conscious thought, most of the original input has been discarded, and what remains has been shaped by what you needed, what you expected, and what you've learned to recognize.

The machinery has three stages: **selection**, **organization**, and **interpretation**. Each stage introduces a gap between what's there and what you perceive.

## Stage one: selection

Your visual system is processing about 11 million bits per second. Your conscious attention handles roughly 40. Something has to decide which 40 bits matter.

Some of what drives selection lives in the stimulus itself. Loud things get attention. Moving things get attention. Things that contrast with their surroundings get attention. That's why a fire alarm pulls you out of concentration: intensity and contrast are built into the selection rules. Organizations use this: the foreman who raises his voice is performing a hack on the selection system, and it works, at least until the nervous system habituates and stops tagging the raised voice as signal.

But a lot of what drives selection lives *in you*. This is the part that's genuinely surprising.

Your immediate needs shape what you notice. A tired worker scans the room for the clock. A worried worker scans the news for layoff signals. An ambitious worker notices which meetings they're not being invited to. All four are in the same environment. They're each pulling different information from it, not because they're looking in different directions, but because their attention is tuned by what they need.

Your past experience shapes selection too. A manager who has built teams before notices team coordination problems faster than one who hasn't — they know the early signatures. A negotiator who has been burned reads tonal shifts that look like nothing to a novice. This *response disposition* is how expertise works: you've learned to notice the right things. It's also how expertise traps you: you notice what you've learned to notice, which means you can miss the thing that doesn't fit the template.

Here's a concrete demonstration. Psychologists showed people ordinary playing cards — but some of the cards had their colors reversed, hearts and diamonds printed in black, spades and clubs in red. When the cards were shown briefly, people consistently reported the cards as they expected them to be, not as they were. The brain filed the stimulus under the familiar category and reported the category, not the input. The expectation overwrote the observation.

This is not a bug. It's how the system is supposed to work. You build models of the world and run predictions off those models; you don't re-derive the world from scratch every moment, because that would be impossibly slow. The cost is that when the world deviates from your model, you sometimes don't notice.

---

## Stage two: organization

Once you've selected stimuli, your brain has to do something with them. It organizes them into categories. This worker seems ambitious. That manager is political. This team is siloed. These categories are functional — they let you treat new situations as familiar cases and act quickly.

The problem is that categories compress information. Once you've categorized someone, you process their subsequent behavior through that category. A worker you've categorized as ambitious gets credit for results — your brain infers that they drove the outcome. The same results from someone you've categorized as cautious get filed as "they did what they were supposed to." The label reorganizes what you see.

This is how Sarah's story actually works. Her boss filed her as "detail-oriented." That wasn't neutral description — it was a category with a boundary drawn around it. Detail-oriented people notice problems. They don't generate vision. They execute; they don't lead. Once the category was assigned, her boss began processing her contributions through it. When she built the joint venture architecture in Southeast Asia — a strategic act — it was filed as careful execution. When she spotted the regulatory gap in Myanmar — a strategic insight — it was filed as problem-detection. The category filtered out the part of the signal that would have revised it.

## Stage three: interpretation, and why it gets self-fulfilling

Once you've organized stimuli into categories, you assign meaning. You decide what the category implies about future behavior, capability, and potential. That assignment drives action. The action changes the outcome.

This is where perception becomes a mechanism, not just a filter.

In 1968, Robert Rosenthal and Lenore Jacobson ran a study in an elementary school. They told teachers that certain students — randomly selected — were about to undergo dramatic intellectual growth. "Intellectual bloomers," they called them. By the end of the year, those students had actually gained more IQ points than their peers. Not because they were inherently different. Because the teachers who believed they were exceptional gave them more attention, more wait time to answer questions, more encouragement, more feedback. The belief became self-fulfilling through the mechanism of treatment.

The same mechanism runs in organizations, and the consequences are larger because organizations have longer time horizons and more resources to allocate.

If your manager believes you're a builder, you get builder assignments — projects with scope, visibility, strategic complexity. Those assignments develop the capabilities that builders need. They're also the assignments that get noticed, that generate the kind of results that accelerate promotion. By contrast, if your manager believes you're detail-oriented, you get detail-oriented assignments. You execute well, because that's what the role requires. Your manager's original perception is confirmed. Neither of you is doing anything dishonest. The prophecy just runs.

What's worth noticing about the Pygmalion effect is that it's not primarily about belief — it's about the mechanism that converts belief into treatment into outcome. The belief matters only insofar as it changes how you treat someone. Change the belief, change the treatment, and the outcomes shift. This is an intervention point.

## Attribution: how people explain what they see

Perception selects and organizes; attribution interprets causation. When something happens — a project succeeds, an employee misses a deadline, a team collapses in coordination — people immediately ask *why*. The answer to *why* determines what happens next: who gets promoted, who gets put on a performance plan, what the organization learns or fails to learn.

Harold Kelley, building on earlier work by Fritz Heider, proposed that attribution judgments use three pieces of information. **Consensus**: did everyone do this, or only this person? **Consistency**: does this person always do this, in similar situations? **Distinctiveness**: does this person do this in every context, or only this one?

The logic runs like this. If only this person behaved this way (low consensus), they always behave this way in similar situations (high consistency), and they behave this way across all contexts (low distinctiveness) — that pattern points inward. It's about the person. Their ability, their motivation, their character.

If everyone behaved this way (high consensus), this person doesn't usually behave this way (low consistency), and they only behave this way in this particular situation (high distinctiveness) — that pattern points outward. It's about the situation. The task was unusually hard. The market shifted. The deadline was unrealistic.

In clean laboratory conditions, people use these factors reasonably well. In real organizations, with real ambiguity and real stakes, the attribution process gets distorted by two systematic biases that are among the most robustly documented findings in social psychology.

---

## The two biases that trap you

The **fundamental attribution error** is the tendency to explain other people's behavior by their character rather than their situation. Your employee misses the deadline — your first impulse is to reach for a personality explanation: they're disorganized, they can't prioritize, they're not committed. You're less likely to ask whether they were blocked waiting for information from another team, whether the timeline was set by someone who didn't understand the dependencies, whether three other priorities landed on their desk at the same moment. Situational causes are real, they're common, and they require more cognitive work to reconstruct. Character explanations are faster, and your brain prefers them.

The error has a particular cost in management. If you explain a missed deadline as a character defect, you respond with training, coaching, or a performance conversation. If the real cause was a broken process or an unrealistic timeline, none of those responses fix anything. The problem recurs. You grow increasingly certain that the character explanation is right, because the behavior keeps happening. You've built a confirmation machine out of a misattribution.

The **self-serving bias** runs in the other direction, applied to yourself. Your successes you attribute to your skill and effort. Your failures you attribute to circumstance. Meanwhile, you tend to reverse that logic for others: their successes get attributed to favorable conditions; their failures to their deficiencies.

Listen to how salespeople discuss closed deals versus lost ones. "I closed that account because I understood what they needed and earned their trust." "We lost the deal because the budget got frozen." Not "I closed it partly because they were ready to buy." Not "We lost it partly because our product had a gap." The pattern is automatic, not dishonest — your brain protects your self-image by biasing the explanation. The cost is that you don't learn accurately from failure, and you don't accurately assess other people's contributions.

Both biases are efficient, in the narrow sense: they let you make fast judgments and move on. The price is systematic error. You misread your people, you misread your own performance, and you build an organizational story about cause and effect that diverges from reality.

---

## What attitudes actually are, and why the distinctions matter

Attribution shapes treatment. Treatment shapes opportunity. Opportunity shapes how people feel about their situation. The feeling is what attitude researchers are trying to measure.

An *attitude* is a predisposition — a lean toward responding favorably or unfavorably to something. Attitudes have three components: what you believe about something, how you feel about it, and what you're inclined to do. In workplace research, four distinct attitudes have gotten the most attention, and the research literature suffers from treating them as interchangeable when they're not.

**Job satisfaction** is the emotional state that results from evaluating your job experience. It's the answer to "Do I like this?" It's generated by the gap between what you expected and what you got — interesting work, decent pay, reasonable boss, collegial teammates. When expectations meet reality, satisfaction follows. When they don't, dissatisfaction does.

**Job involvement** is narrower. It's the degree to which you care about doing the work well, independent of how you feel about the job overall. You can be uninvolved in a job you enjoy — coasting through easy work — and deeply involved in a job you dislike, because your standards require it.

**Organizational commitment** is your identification with and attachment to the organization itself, distinct from your feelings about the work or the people. The research distinguishes three varieties. *Affective commitment* is the emotional attachment — you want to be here. *Continuance commitment* is the calculation — you stay because leaving would cost too much. *Normative commitment* is the sense of obligation — you stay because you feel you ought to. These predict different behaviors. People with high affective commitment work harder and stay longer. People with high continuance commitment stay physically while leaving psychologically — they do the minimum because the alternative is costly, not because they care about the outcome. Normative commitment produces adequate performance and vigilant exit-scanning.

**Engagement** is absorption — the degree to which you're focused, energized, and connected to the work in the moment. An engaged worker is present; a disengaged one is elsewhere.

Organizations have spent decades measuring satisfaction, partly because satisfaction surveys were developed first and are easy to administer. The problem is that satisfaction predicts performance weakly. The correlation is real — roughly *r* = 0.30 across hundreds of studies — but that explains only about nine percent of performance variation. Ninety-one percent is driven by other things: skill, resources, process, organizational clarity, the clarity of what good looks like.

The causal direction also runs both ways. People who perform well feel more satisfied — winning feels good. But satisfaction doesn't reliably cause performance; it's more of a byproduct than a driver. The stronger predictors of discretionary effort — the extra work people do when no one is requiring it — are engagement and affective commitment, not satisfaction.

Retention is a different story. The strongest predictor of whether people leave isn't satisfaction with their current conditions — it's whether they can see a path forward from where they are. People who believe they're growing stay. People who stop believing they have a future leave, even when they're reasonably satisfied with the present.

## How attitudes form — and how they're more social than you think

Here's a finding that organizational behavior researchers have documented but managers rarely internalize: your attitude toward your job is partly determined by the attitudes of the people around you, independently of what your actual conditions are.

A new employee walks into a team that has, collectively, decided the manager is unfair, the pay is below market, and advancement is illusory. That collective judgment was formed over time, from real experiences. But it's also being broadcast constantly in the team's language, its jokes, its interpretations of every company communication. The new employee hears this broadcast every day. Within months, they begin to believe it — not because they've independently evaluated the evidence, but because attitudes form partly through social construction. The team taught them what to think about this place.

This is the *social-information-processing model*, and it has a counterintuitive implication: if you want to change someone's attitude, changing their conditions is not sufficient. You have to change the social environment that's interpreting those conditions. One person's positive experience in a negative team will usually be reframed by the team rather than revising the team's belief.

The model doesn't mean individual conditions don't matter — they do. Three things shape workplace attitudes: personality (some people are dispositionally more satisfied regardless of conditions), the situation (change the work, the pay, or the manager and attitudes shift), and social context (the collective interpretation of conditions matters as much as the conditions themselves). The implication for managers is that cultural change requires changing the social fabric, not just the terms.

---

## The loop

Here is the loop this chapter has been building toward.

A manager forms an early perception of you — accurately or not, through selection and organization that reflect their experience, their needs, and their priors. They attribute your early performance to something about you rather than your situation — the fundamental attribution error runs in their direction. They file you in a category. That category shapes how they treat you. The treatment is visible to you — you can feel whether you're being given strategic work or tactical work, whether you're being invited into conversations or excluded from them. That signal shapes your organizational commitment: are they investing in me? Your commitment shapes your engagement: if the answer is no, you gradually withhold. And your engagement shapes your actual performance over time, confirming the manager's original perception.

The loop closes. And it's almost entirely invisible to everyone inside it.

The manager believes they're reading you accurately. You believe you're responding rationally to the situation. You're both right, in a narrow sense. The machinery is just running.

Where does it break? It breaks when the attribution becomes explicit. When someone names it: "I want to tell you what I think you're capable of, and I want to be honest that my early take might be wrong." When the treatment signal changes: you're given an assignment that doesn't match the category you've been filed in. When the social context shifts: someone in the team who has been reading the situation differently says so out loud.

None of this requires organizational reform or a new theory. It requires seeing the machinery. That's the point of this chapter.

---

## What remains genuinely uncertain

I should be honest about what the research doesn't settle.

The Pygmalion effect is real, but smaller than the original 1968 study suggested. Effect sizes in replications hover around one-third of a standard deviation — meaningful, but not miraculous. And most replications are with children and teachers, where the power asymmetry is larger and the impressionability higher. Whether the same mechanism runs with the same force in adult organizations is less well established than I'd like.

The three-factor attribution model (consensus, consistency, distinctiveness) is a good framework for thinking about attribution, but people don't actually use it like a formula. The factors work better in simple, well-defined cases than in the messy, ambiguous situations where attribution errors actually matter.

The relationship between satisfaction and performance is robust — weakly positive, causally bidirectional — but explaining *why* it's so weak requires more than the research has settled. The explanation I find most convincing is that satisfaction reflects what you receive and performance reflects what you give, and those are driven by different things. But that's an interpretation of the data, not a finding.

What I'm more confident about: the social-information-processing model is real and underappreciated. The distinction between affective commitment and continuance commitment matters for how you think about retention. And the path-forward problem — whether people believe they're growing — is a better predictor of voluntary turnover than most satisfaction surveys are designed to measure.

1. **The reversed-cards experiment, applied.** Think of a belief you held confidently about a colleague that turned out to be wrong. 7. **Run the loop forward.** Pick a person you manage or have managed. What would you do, and what would you *not* do, to change it? *(Tests: Three-Component Model, engagement vs. commitment distinction)*

## Summary

Perception is constructed from selection, organization, and interpretation. The construction is efficient and often accurate, but it introduces systematic distortions: you see what you're primed to see, you file stimuli into categories that affect how you process subsequent information, and you assign meaning through a lens shaped by your experience and your needs. Early perceptions shape treatment, which shapes opportunity, which shapes outcome — sometimes in ways that become self-fulfilling.

Attribution is how people explain causation. The three-factor model provides a framework; the fundamental attribution error and self-serving bias describe the systematic ways that framework misfires in practice. The FAE produces explanations that blame the person when situations are the real driver. Self-serving bias produces explanations that protect your self-image at the expense of accurate learning.

Job satisfaction, commitment, engagement, and involvement are distinct attitudes with different antecedents and different behavioral consequences. Satisfaction is a weak predictor of performance. Affective commitment and engagement are stronger predictors of discretionary effort. The strongest predictor of voluntary departure is whether people can see a path forward from where they are. Attitudes form through personality, situation, and social context — you can't change one without at least attending to the other two.

The loop connecting perception to attribution to treatment to attitude to behavior is the governing mechanism of organizational life. It's invisible when you don't know it's there. It becomes manageable when you do.
