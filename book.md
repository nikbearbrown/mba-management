# Principles of Management

## Title
Principles of Management — Organizational Behavior, the Workplace, and the Science (and Art) of Leading People

## Subtitle
The introductory survey of organizational behavior — what we know about how individuals, groups, and organizations actually function, what we don't, and how to lead anyway.

## Author
Nik Bear Brown.

## Audience

This book is written for the undergraduate business student taking their first organizational-behavior course — typically a sophomore or junior in a business, management, or human-resources program. They will become managers, founders, HR partners, consultants, or analysts who depend on workplace research without becoming behavioral scientists themselves. They have heard a lot of management folklore. The book's job is to separate which parts are supported by careful research, which parts are partially supported under specific conditions, and which parts are corporate ritual.

Specifically:

- Business school students taking PRINCIPLES OF MANAGEMENT or ORGANIZATIONAL BEHAVIOR.
- HR, OD, and I-O psychology students who need the introductory survey.
- Engineering and CS students whose firms send them to people-management workshops.
- Pre-MBA and adult-learner students preparing for graduate study.
- New managers reading on their own.

This is not a clinical-psychology textbook (the *psychology* book in this workshop covers that ground). It is not a strategic-management textbook (this is about people, not portfolios). It is the standard intro OB sequence: 19 chapters covering individual differences and motivation, group and team dynamics, leadership and influence, organization-level structure and culture, and the workplace as the laboratory.

## Scope

The nineteen chapters cover, in order:

1. **Management and organizational behavior** — what management is; the major perspectives; the Hawthorne studies and the start of OB.
2. **Individual and cultural differences** — personality, values, Hofstede dimensions, GLOBE study.
3. **Perception and job attitudes** — perceptual processes; job satisfaction; commitment; engagement.
4. **Learning and reinforcement** — operant conditioning at work; reinforcement schedules; behavior modification.
5. **Diversity in organizations** — the business case; legal frameworks; group differences and individual variation.
6. **Perception and managerial decision making** — the rational model and its limits; bounded rationality; heuristics and biases.
7. **Work motivation for performance** — content and process theories; equity, expectancy, goal-setting.
8. **Performance appraisal and rewards** — measurement methods, biases, total rewards systems.
9. **Group and intergroup relations** — group dynamics, social identity, intergroup conflict.
10. **Understanding and managing work teams** — Tuckman stages, team effectiveness, virtual teams.
11. **Communication** — process, channels, barriers, organizational communication networks.
12. **Leadership** — trait, behavioral, contingency, transformational, servant; LMX; the leader-follower system.
13. **Organizational power and politics** — French and Raven bases of power; political behavior; impression management.
14. **Conflict and negotiations** — sources of conflict, conflict styles, negotiation strategies.
15. **External and internal organizational environments and corporate culture** — the organization as system; culture as construct.
16. **Organizational structure and change** — design choices; resistance to change; Lewin's three-stage and Kotter's eight-step models.
17. **Human resource management** — hiring, training, performance management, total rewards.
18. **Stress and well-being** — sources, consequences, interventions, wellness programs.
19. **Entrepreneurship** — what entrepreneurs do; opportunity recognition; new-venture creation.

Out of scope: detailed strategy and corporate finance (those have their own courses), advanced research methods, advanced labor law.

## Voice notes for this book

The Attenborough × Feynman v1.1 voice applies, with adjustments for organizational behavior's particular shape.

**Cold opens earn their place.** A chapter on motivation opens at a startup choosing equity vs salary. A chapter on conflict opens at a contract negotiation in a paneled boardroom. A chapter on leadership opens with a CEO during a crisis. Real workplace scenes, recognizable to working students.

**Methods made visible — and methodological humility.** OB has its own version of psychology's replication crisis. Many "classic" findings — the Hawthorne effect's original magnitude, Maslow's strict hierarchy, Mischel's marshmallow test, the original Stanford Prison Experiment as evidence about workplace power — have been substantially revised. The book names the original finding, the revision, and the practical implication. Students leave with calibrated confidence rather than memorized frameworks.

**Frameworks earn their place.** Maslow, Herzberg, Vroom, McClelland, Hofstede, Tuckman, Mintzberg, Kotter — each gets the same treatment: name the framework, name what it explains, name what it doesn't, name the evidence. The chapter that introduces the framework also names how it gets misused.

**Grounded examples in workplace contexts.** Real companies (named or anonymized when source is anonymous), real cases. Where the source uses an abstract example, the rewrite replaces with a workplace scenario a student would recognize.

**Honest about what management is.** Management is the youngest social science and depends on a mix of empirical research, case-study tradition, and corporate folklore. The chapters say so. Where the literature is genuinely settled, the book is direct. Where it isn't, the book is direct about that too.

**Pop-management myths corrected by name.** "Different generations want different things at work" (modest support, mostly stereotype). Personality typing as a hiring tool (Big Five yes, MBTI no). The "10x engineer" claim. The "70-20-10 leadership-development rule." Each gets named, situated against the actual evidence, and corrected — not in a sidebar but in the prose where the related correct mechanism is taught.

## Prerequisites assumed

- Reading comprehension at college level.
- Basic familiarity with research vocabulary (sample, control, hypothesis) — re-introduced where needed.
- Willingness to revise prior beliefs about how organizations work.
- No prior management experience required, though it helps.
